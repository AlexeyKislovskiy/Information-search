<p><b>Lakota</b> (<span title="Lakota-language text"><i lang="lkt">Lakȟótiyapi</i></span> <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="lkt-Latn-fonipa"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Lakota" title="Help:IPA/Lakota">&#91;laˈkˣɔtɪjapɪ&#93;</a></span>), also referred to as <b>Lakhota</b>, <b>Teton</b> or <b>Teton Sioux</b>, is a <a href="/wiki/Siouan_languages" title="Siouan languages">Siouan language</a> spoken by the <a href="/wiki/Lakota_people" title="Lakota people">Lakota people</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Sioux" title="Sioux">Sioux</a> tribes. Lakota is mutually intelligible with the two dialects of the <a href="/wiki/Dakota_language" title="Dakota language">Dakota language</a>, especially <a href="/wiki/Dakota_language#Comparison_of_the_dialects" title="Dakota language">Western Dakota</a>, and is one of the three major <a href="/wiki/Variety_(linguistics)" title="Variety (linguistics)">varieties</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Sioux_language" title="Sioux language">Sioux language</a>.
</p><p>Speakers of the Lakota language make up one of the largest <a href="/wiki/Native_American_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Native American language">Native American language</a> speech communities in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, with approximately 2,000 speakers, who live mostly in the northern plains states of <a href="/wiki/North_Dakota" title="North Dakota">North Dakota</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_Dakota" title="South Dakota">South Dakota</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-e25_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-e25-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Many communities have immersion programs for both children and adults.
</p><p>The language was first put into written form by <a href="/wiki/European_Americans" title="European Americans">European American</a> missionaries around 1840. The orthography has since evolved to reflect contemporary needs and usage.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_and_origin">History and origin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lakota_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History and origin"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Lakota_people" title="Lakota people">Lakota people</a>'s creation stories say that language originated from the creation of the tribe.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Andrews_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Andrews-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Other creation stories say language was invented by <a href="/wiki/Iktomi" title="Iktomi">Iktomi</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Walker_DeMallie_Jahner_1980_p.106_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Walker_DeMallie_Jahner_1980_p.106-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Phonology">Phonology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lakota_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Phonology"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1033289096">.mw-parser-output .hatnote{font-style:italic}.mw-parser-output div.hatnote{padding-left:1.6em;margin-bottom:0.5em}.mw-parser-output .hatnote i{font-style:normal}.mw-parser-output .hatnote+link+.hatnote{margin-top:-0.5em}</style><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable plainlinks selfreference noprint">For assistance with IPA transcriptions of Lakota for Wikipedia articles, see <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Lakota" title="Help:IPA/Lakota">Help:IPA/Lakota</a>.</div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vowels">Vowels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lakota_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Vowels"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Lakota has five oral vowels, <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/i<span class="wrap"> </span>e<span class="wrap"> </span>a<span class="wrap"> </span>o<span class="wrap"> </span>u/</span>, and three nasal vowels, <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ĩ<span class="wrap"> </span>ã<span class="wrap"> </span>ũ/</span> (phonetically <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ɪ̃<span class="wrap"> </span>ə̃<span class="wrap"> </span>ʊ̃]</span>). Lakota <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/e/</span> and <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/o/</span> are said to be <a href="/wiki/Open-mid_vowel" title="Open-mid vowel">more open</a> than the corresponding cardinal vowels, perhaps closer to <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ɛ]</span> and <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ɔ]</span>. Orthographically, the nasal vowels are written with a following <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;ƞ&#x27e9;</span>, <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;ŋ&#x27e9;</span>, or <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;n&#x27e9;</span>; historically, these were written with <a href="/wiki/Ogonek" title="Ogonek">ogoneks</a> underneath, <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;į ą ų&#x27e9;</span>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> No syllables end with consonantal <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/n/</span>.
</p>

So the clan name written phonemically as <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;Oglala&#x27e9;</span> has become the place name <i><a href="/wiki/Ogallala,_Nebraska" title="Ogallala, Nebraska">Ogallala</a></i>.

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Voiced_uvular_fricative" title="Voiced uvular fricative">voiced uvular fricative</a> <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ʁ/</span> becomes a <a href="/wiki/Uvular_trill" class="mw-redirect" title="Uvular trill">uvular trill</a> (<span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ʀ]</span>) before <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/i/</span><sup id="cite_ref-rood96_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rood96-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-lako04_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lako04-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> and in fast speech it is often realized as a <a href="/wiki/Voiced_velar_fricative" title="Voiced velar fricative">voiced velar fricative</a> <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ɣ]</span>. The voiceless aspirated plosives have two allophonic variants each: those with a delay in voicing <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">([pʰ<span class="wrap"> </span>tʰ<span class="wrap"> </span>kʰ])</span>, and those with velar friction <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">([pˣ<span class="wrap"> </span>tˣ<span class="wrap"> </span>kˣ])</span>, which occur before <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/a/</span>, <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ã/</span>, <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/o/</span>, <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ĩ/</span>, and <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ũ/</span> (thus, <span title="Lakota-language text"><i lang="lkt">lakhóta</i></span>, <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/laˈkʰota/</span> is phonetically <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[laˈkˣota]</span>). For some speakers, there is a phonemic distinction between the two, and both occur before <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/e/</span>. No such variation occurs for the affricate /tʃʰ/. Some orthographies mark this distinction; others do not. The uvular fricatives <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/χ/</span> and <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ʁ/</span> are commonly spelled <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;ȟ&#x27e9;</span> and <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;ǧ&#x27e9;</span>.
</p><p>All <a href="/wiki/Morpheme" title="Morpheme">monomorphemic</a> words have one vowel which carries primary <a href="/wiki/Stress_(linguistics)" title="Stress (linguistics)">stress</a> and has a higher tone than all other vowels in the word. This is generally the vowel of the second syllable of the word, but often the first syllable can be stressed, and occasionally other syllables as well. Stress is generally indicated with an acute accent: <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;á&#x27e9;</span>, etc. Compound words will have stressed vowels in each component; proper spelling will write compounds with a hyphen. Thus <span title="Lakota-language text"><i lang="lkt">máza-ská</i></span>, literally "metal-white", i.e. "silver; money" has two stressed vowels, the first <i>a</i> in each component. If it were written without the hyphen, as <span title="Lakota-language text"><i lang="lkt">mazaska</i></span>, it would imply a single main stress.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Phonological_processes">Phonological processes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lakota_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Phonological processes"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A common phonological process which occurs in rapid speech is <i>vowel contraction</i>, which generally results from the loss of an intervocalic glide. Vowel contraction results in phonetic long vowels (<a href="/wiki/Phoneme" title="Phoneme">phonemically</a> a sequence of two identical vowels), with falling pitch if the first underlying vowel is stressed, and rising pitch if the second underlying vowel is stressed: <i>kê:</i> (falling tone), "he said that", from <span title="Lakota-language text"><i lang="lkt">kéye</i></span>; <i>hǎ:pi</i> (rising tone), "clothing", from <span title="Lakota-language text"><i lang="lkt">hayápi</i></span>. If one of the vowels is nasalized, the resulting long vowel is also nasalized: <i>čhaŋ̌:pi</i>, "sugar", from <span title="Lakota-language text"><i lang="lkt">čhaŋháŋpi</i></span>.<sup id="cite_ref-rood96_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rood96-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>When two vowels of unequal height contract, or when feature contrasts exist between the vowels and the glide, two new phonetic vowels, <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[æː]</span> and <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ɔː]</span>, result:<sup id="cite_ref-rood96_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rood96-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> <i>iyæ̂:</i>, "he left for there", from <span title="Lakota-language text"><i lang="lkt">iyáye</i></span>; <i>mitȟa:</i>, "it's mine", from <span title="Lakota-language text"><i lang="lkt">mitȟáwa</i></span>.
</p><p>The plural <a href="/wiki/Clitic" title="Clitic">enclitic</a> <i>=pi</i> is frequently changed in rapid speech when preceding the enclitics <i>=kte</i>, <i>=kiŋ</i>, <i>=kštó</i>, or <i>=na</i>. If the vowel preceding <i>=pi</i> is high/open, <i>=pi</i> becomes [u]; if the vowel is non-high (mid or closed), <i>=pi</i> becomes [o] (if the preceding vowel is nasalized, then the resulting vowel is also nasalized): <i>hi=pi=kte</i>, "they will arrive here", [hiukte]; <i>yatkáŋ=pi=na</i>, "they drank it and...", <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[jatkə̃õna]</span>.<sup id="cite_ref-rood96_6-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rood96-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Lakota also exhibits some traces of sound symbolism among fricatives, where the <a href="/wiki/Point_of_articulation" class="mw-redirect" title="Point of articulation">point of articulation</a> changes to reflect intensity: <span title="Lakota-language text"><i lang="lkt">zí</i></span>, "it's yellow", <span title="Lakota-language text"><i lang="lkt">ží</i></span>, "it's tawny", <span title="Lakota-language text"><i lang="lkt">ǧí</i></span>, "it's brown".<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> (Compare with the similar examples in <a href="/wiki/Mandan_language#Vocabulary" title="Mandan language">Mandan</a>.)
</p>
<h2><span id="Orthographies.2C_standardization.2C_and_teaching_materials"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Orthographies,_standardization,_and_teaching_materials">Orthographies, standardization, and teaching materials</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lakota_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Orthographies, standardization, and teaching materials"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Several orthographies as well as <i><a href="/wiki/Ad_hoc" title="Ad hoc">ad hoc</a></i> spelling are used to write the Lakota language, with varying perspectives on whether standardization should be implemented.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> In 2002, Rosebud Cultural Studies teacher Randy Emery argued that standardization of the language could cause problems "because the language is utilized diversely. If standardization is determined to be the approach... then the question is whose version will be adopted? This will cause dissent and politics to become a factor in the process."<sup id="cite_ref-LakotaJournal2002_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LakotaJournal2002-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Also in 2002, <a href="/wiki/Sinte_Gleska_University" title="Sinte Gleska University">Sinte Gleska University</a> rejected a partnership with the European-owned Lakota Language Consortium.<sup id="cite_ref-LakotaJournal2002_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LakotaJournal2002-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Sinte Gleska uses the orthography developed by <a href="/wiki/Albert_White_Hat" title="Albert White Hat">Albert White Hat</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Hirschfelder_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hirschfelder-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> which on December 13, 2012, was formally adopted by the <a href="/wiki/Rosebud_Sioux_Tribe" class="mw-redirect" title="Rosebud Sioux Tribe">Rosebud Sioux Tribe</a> per Tribal Resolution No. 2012–343. This resolution also banned the Lakota Language Consortium and its "<a href="/wiki/Czech_orthography" title="Czech orthography">Czech orthography</a>" from the reservation and its educational system.<sup id="cite_ref-John98_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-John98-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> This ban was a response to a series of protests by community members and grassroots language preservation workers, at Rosebud and other Lakota communities, against the Lakota Language Consortium (LLC).<sup id="cite_ref-LakotaJournal2002_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LakotaJournal2002-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-John86-87_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-John86-87-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Despite its name, the LLC is an organization formed by two Europeans.<sup id="cite_ref-John86-87_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-John86-87-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Concerns arose due to the LLC's promotion of their <i>New Lakota Dictionary</i>, websites and other Internet projects aimed at revising and standardizing their new spelling of the Lakota language. "Lakota first language speakers and Lakota language teachers criticize the "Czech orthography" for being overloaded with markings and – foremost – for the way it is being brought into Lakota schools"; it has been criticized as "<a href="/wiki/Neocolonialism" title="Neocolonialism">neocolonial</a> domination."<sup id="cite_ref-John86-87_15-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-John86-87-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Sonja John writes that "The new orthography the Czech linguist advocates resembles the Czech orthography – making it easier for Czech people to read. The Europeans predominantly use the internet to give the impression that this "Czech orthography" is a Lakota product and the standard for writing Lakota."<sup id="cite_ref-John73_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-John73-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> "The Rosebud Sioux Tribe was the first of the Lakota tribes to take legal action against the self-authorizing practices the LLC committed by utilizing names of Lakota language experts without their consent to obtain funding for their projects."<sup id="cite_ref-John97-98_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-John97-98-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Rosebud Resolution No. 2008–295 goes further and compares these actions to what was done to children taken from their families by the <a href="/wiki/American_Indian_residential_schools" class="mw-redirect" title="American Indian residential schools">residential schools</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-John97-98_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-John97-98-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2006 some of the Lakota language teachers at <a href="/wiki/Standing_Rock_Indian_Reservation" title="Standing Rock Indian Reservation">Standing Rock</a> chose to collaborate with <a href="/wiki/Sitting_Bull_College" title="Sitting Bull College">Sitting Bull College</a>, and the Lakota Language Consortium (LLC), with the aim of expanding their language curriculum. Teachers at Standing Rock use several different orthographies.<sup id="cite_ref-Tausha_Hauff_PhD_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tausha_Hauff_PhD-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Language activists at Standing Rock also refer to it as simply the "SLO" or even "Suggested Lakota Orthography."<sup id="cite_ref-Tausha_Hauff_PhD_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tausha_Hauff_PhD-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Tasha Hauff writes,
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r996844942">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Choosing a writing system, or orthography, is often a serious point of contention in Indigenous communities engaging in revitalization work (Hinton, 2014). While writing a traditionally oral language can itself be considered a colonial act, standardizing a writing system is fraught with political as well as pedagogical complications. Because teachers at Standing Rock were in need of language-teaching materials, and the LLC was one of the few organizations developing such resources, Standing Rock adopted the new orthography, but not without resistance from members of the community. ... The new writing system at Standing Rock was often criticized or even rejected within the community. Some fluent speakers at Standing Rock have not accepted the new writing system. There are some who continue to work in language education and who use the LLC materials but do not write in the orthography. These are usually Elders who remain in the habit of writing the way they learned. A few people at Standing Rock, however, have been offended by the notion of a standard way of writing Lakota/Dakota, especially one that seems unlike any of the systems used by Elders. Community members have been particularly wary of the SLO ["Standard Lakota Orthography"], which appears to be developed by outsiders who are not fluent speakers and would require considerable study for a fluent speaker to use.<sup id="cite_ref-Tausha_Hauff_PhD_18-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tausha_Hauff_PhD-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<p>In 2013 Lakota teachers at <a href="/wiki/Red_Cloud_Indian_School" title="Red Cloud Indian School">Red Cloud Indian School</a> on <a href="/wiki/Pine_Ridge_Indian_Reservation" title="Pine Ridge Indian Reservation">Pine Ridge Indian Reservation</a> discussed their use of orthography for their K-12 students as well as adult learners. The orthography used at Red Cloud "is meant to be more phonetic than other orthographies... That means there are usually more 'H's than other versions. While many orthographies use <a href="/wiki/Tipi" title="Tipi">tipi</a>... Red Cloud spells it thípi." He continues, "the orthography also makes heavy use of diacritical marks... that is not popular among some educators and academics".  Delphine Red Shirt, an Oglala Lakota tribal member and a lecturer on languages at <a href="/wiki/Stanford_University" title="Stanford University">Stanford University</a>, disagrees and prefers a Lakota orthography without diacritical marks. "I'm very against any orthography that requires a special keyboard to communicate," she said. First language speaker and veteran language teacher at Red Cloud, the late Philomine Lakota, had similar concerns with the orthography, and argues against changing the spelling forms she learned from her father. However, she did consider that, a shared curriculum could "create consistency across the region and encourage the long-term viability of the language. However, Philomine is also cognizant that it will take more than a school curriculum to preserve the language."<sup id="cite_ref-Rapid_City_Journal_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rapid_City_Journal-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> She added, "In order for a language to survive, it can't simply be taught from the top. A language is a living thing and students need to breathe life into it daily; talking with friends, family and elders in Lakota".<sup id="cite_ref-Rapid_City_Journal_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rapid_City_Journal-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2018, at the <a href="/wiki/Cheyenne_River_Indian_Reservation" title="Cheyenne River Indian Reservation">Cheyenne River Indian Reservation</a>, Lakota speaker Manny Iron Hawk and his wife Renee Iron Hawk discussed opening an immersion school and the difficulties around choosing an orthography to write Lakota; Mr. Iron Hawk voiced support for the LLC (SLO) Orthography, saying it was accessible to second language learners, but know not all agreed with him.<sup id="cite_ref-West_River_Eagle_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-West_River_Eagle-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> Others in the community voiced a preference for the tribe creating their own orthography. While Mr. Iron Hawk supports this approach, Renee Iron Hawk also expressed a sense of urgency, saying "We should just use what we have, and then fix and replace it, but we need to start speaking it now". The Iron Hawks both agreed that too much time has been spent arguing over which orthography to use or not use, and not enough time is spent teaching and speaking the language.<sup id="cite_ref-West_River_Eagle_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-West_River_Eagle-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On May 3, 2022, the Tribal Council of the <a href="/wiki/Standing_Rock_Sioux" class="mw-redirect" title="Standing Rock Sioux">Standing Rock Sioux</a>, in a near-unanimous vote, banished the Lakota Language Consortium (and specifically, LLC linguist Jan Ullrich and co-founder Wilhelm Meya) from ever again setting foot on the reservation. The council's decision was based on the LLC's history with not only the Standing Rock community, but also with at least three other communities that also voiced concerns about Meya and the LLC, "saying he broke agreements over how to use recordings, language materials and historical records, or used them without permission."<sup id="cite_ref-NBC-LLCBanished_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NBC-LLCBanished-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Res150-22_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Res150-22-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="LLC_alphabet">LLC alphabet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lakota_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: LLC alphabet"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The "Standard Lakota Orthography" as the LLC calls it, is in principle phonemic, which means that each character (<a href="/wiki/Grapheme" title="Grapheme">grapheme</a>) represents one distinctive sound (<a href="/wiki/Phoneme" title="Phoneme">phoneme</a>), except for the distinction between glottal and velar aspiration, which is treated phonetically.
</p><p>Lakota vowels are <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;a, e, i, o, u&#x27e9;</span> nasal vowels are <i>aŋ, iŋ, uŋ.</i> <a href="/wiki/Pitch_accent" class="mw-redirect" title="Pitch accent">Pitch accent</a> is marked with an <a href="/wiki/Acute_accent" title="Acute accent">acute accent</a>: <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;á, é, í, ó, ú, áŋ, íŋ, úŋ&#x27e9;</span> on stressed vowels (which receive a higher tone than non-stressed ones)<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The following consonants approximate their IPA values: <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;b, g, h, k, l, m, n, ŋ, p, s, t, w, z&#x27e9;</span>. <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;Y&#x27e9;</span> has its English value of <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/j/</span>. An apostrophe, <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;'&#x27e9;</span>, is used for the <a href="/wiki/Glottal_stop" title="Glottal stop">glottal stop</a>.
</p><p>A <a href="/wiki/Caron" title="Caron">caron</a> is used for sounds, other than <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ŋ/</span>, which are not written with Latin letters in the IPA: <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;č&#x27e9;</span> <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/tʃ/</span>, <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;ǧ&#x27e9;</span> <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ʁ/</span>, <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;ȟ&#x27e9;</span> <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/χ/</span>, <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;š&#x27e9;</span> <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ʃ/</span>, <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;ž&#x27e9;</span> <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ʒ/</span>. Aspirates are written with <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;h&#x27e9;</span>: <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;čh, kh, ph, th,&#x27e9;</span> and <a href="/wiki/Velar_fricative" title="Velar fricative">velar frication</a> with <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;ȟ&#x27e9;</span>: <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;kȟ, pȟ, tȟ.&#x27e9;</span> Ejectives are written with an apostrophe: <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;č', ȟ', k', p', s', š', t'&#x27e9;</span>.
</p><p>The spelling used in modern popular texts is often written without <a href="/wiki/Diacritic" title="Diacritic">diacritics</a>. Besides failing to mark stress, this also results in the confusion of numerous consonants: <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/s/</span> and <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ʃ/</span> are both written <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;s&#x27e9;</span>, <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/h/</span> and <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/χ/</span> are both written <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;h&#x27e9;</span>, and the aspirate stops are written like the unaspirates, as <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;p, t, c, k&#x27e9;</span>.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Curley_alphabet">Curley alphabet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lakota_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Curley alphabet"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1982, Lakota educator Leroy Curley (1935-2012) devised a 41-letter circular alphabet.<sup id="cite_ref-Curley_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Curley-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Grammar">Grammar</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lakota_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Grammar"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Word_order">Word order</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lakota_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Word order"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The basic word order of Lakota is <a href="/wiki/Subject%E2%80%93object%E2%80%93verb" class="mw-redirect" title="Subject–object–verb">subject–object–verb</a>, although the order can be changed for expressive purposes (placing the object before the subject to bring the object into focus or placing the subject after the verb to emphasize its status as established information). It is <a href="/wiki/Adposition#Postpositions" class="mw-redirect" title="Adposition">postpositional</a>, with adpositions occurring after the head nouns: <span title="Lakota-language text"><i lang="lkt">mas'óphiye él</i></span>, "at the store" (literally 'store at'); <span title="Lakota-language text"><i lang="lkt">thípi=kiŋ ókšaŋ</i></span>, "around the house" (literally 'house=the around') (Rood and Taylor 1996).
</p><p>Rood and Taylor (1996) suggest the following template for basic word order. Items in parentheses are optional; only the verb is required. It is therefore possible to produce a grammatical sentence that contains only a verb.
</p>
<blockquote><p>(interjection) (conjunction) (adverb(s)) (nominal) (nominal) (nominal) (adverb(s)) verb (enclitic(s)) (conjunction)</p></blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Interjections">Interjections</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lakota_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Interjections"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>When <a href="/wiki/Interjection" title="Interjection">interjections</a> are used, they begin the sentence or end it. A small number of interjections are used only by one gender, for instance the interjection expressing disbelief is <span title="Lakota-language text"><i lang="lkt">ečéš</i></span> for women but <span title="Lakota-language text"><i lang="lkt">hóȟ</i></span> for men; for calling attention women say <span title="Lakota-language text"><i lang="lkt">máŋ</i></span> while men use <span title="Lakota-language text"><i lang="lkt">wáŋ</i></span>. Most interjections, however, are used by both genders.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Conjunctions">Conjunctions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lakota_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Conjunctions"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>It is common for a sentence to begin with a conjunction. Both <span title="Lakota-language text"><i lang="lkt">čhaŋké</i></span> and <span title="Lakota-language text"><i lang="lkt">yuŋkȟáŋ</i></span> can be translated as <i>and</i>; <span title="Lakota-language text"><i lang="lkt">k’éyaš</i></span> is similar to English <i>but</i>. Each of these conjunctions joins clauses. In addition, the conjunction <span title="Lakota-language text"><i lang="lkt">na</i></span> joins nouns or phrases.
</p>
<h3><span id="Adverbs.2C_postpositions_and_derived_modifiers"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Adverbs,_postpositions_and_derived_modifiers">Adverbs, postpositions and derived modifiers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lakota_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Adverbs, postpositions and derived modifiers"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Lakota uses <a href="/wiki/Preposition_and_postposition" title="Preposition and postposition">postpositions</a>, which are similar to English prepositions, but follow their noun complement. <a href="/wiki/Adverb" title="Adverb">Adverbs</a> or postpositional phrases can describe manner, location, or reason. There are also <a href="/wiki/Interrogative_word" title="Interrogative word">interrogative</a> adverbs, which are used to form questions.
</p>
<h4><span id="Synonymity_in_the_postpositions_.C3.A9l_and_ekt.C3.A1"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Synonymity_in_the_postpositions_él_and_ektá">Synonymity in the postpositions <span title="Lakota-language text"><i lang="lkt">él</i></span> and <span title="Lakota-language text"><i lang="lkt">ektá</i></span></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lakota_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Synonymity in the postpositions él and ektá"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>To the non-Lakota speaker, the postpositions <span title="Lakota-language text"><i lang="lkt">él</i></span> and <span title="Lakota-language text"><i lang="lkt">ektá</i></span> sound like they can be interchangeable, but although they are full synonyms of each other, they are used in different occasions. Semantically (word meaning), they are used as locational and directional tools. In the English language they can be compared to prepositions like "at", "in", and "on" (when used as locatives) on the one hand, and "at", "in", and "on" (when used as directionals), "to", "into", and "onto", on the other. (Pustet 2013)
</p><p>A pointer for when to use <span title="Lakota-language text"><i lang="lkt">él</i></span> and when to use <span title="Lakota-language text"><i lang="lkt">ektá</i></span> can be determined by the concepts of location  (motionless) or motion; and space vs. time. These features can produce four different combinations, also called semantic domains, which can be arranged as follows (Pustet 2013):
</p>
<ol><li>space / rest: "in the house" [thípi kiŋ él] (This sentence is only describing location of an object, no movement indicated)</li>
<li>space / motion: "to the house [thípi kiŋ ektá] (This sentence is referring to movement of a subject, it is directional in nature)</li>
<li>time / rest: "in the winter" [waníyetu kiŋ él] (This sentence refers to a static moment in time, which happens to be during winter)</li>
<li>time / motion: "in/towards the winter" [waníyetu kiŋ ektá] (Pustet 2013) (This sentence is delegated to time, but time which is soon to change to another season)</li></ol>
<p>Summed up, when a context describes no motion, <span title="Lakota-language text"><i lang="lkt">él</i></span> is the appropriate postposition; when in motion, <span title="Lakota-language text"><i lang="lkt">ektá</i></span> is more appropriate. They are both used in matters of time and space.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nouns_and_pronouns">Nouns and pronouns</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lakota_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Nouns and pronouns"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As mentioned above, <a href="/wiki/Noun_phrase" title="Noun phrase">nominals</a> are optional in Lakota, but when nouns appear the basic word order is subject–object–verb. Pronouns are not common, but may be used <a href="/wiki/Contrast_(linguistics)" title="Contrast (linguistics)">contrastively</a> or emphatically.
</p><p>Lakota has four <a href="/wiki/Article_(grammar)" title="Article (grammar)">articles</a>: <span title="Lakota-language text"><i lang="lkt">waŋ</i></span> is indefinite, similar to English <i>a</i> or <i>an</i>, and <span title="Lakota-language text"><i lang="lkt">kiŋ</i></span> is definite, similar to English <i>the</i>. In addition, <span title="Lakota-language text"><i lang="lkt">waŋží</i></span> is an indefinite article used with hypothetical or <a href="/wiki/Irrealis_mood" title="Irrealis mood">irrealis</a> objects, and <span title="Lakota-language text"><i lang="lkt">k’uŋ</i></span> is a definite article used with nouns that have been mentioned previously.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Verbs">Verbs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lakota_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Verbs"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Verbs are the only word class that are obligatory in a Lakota sentence. Verbs can be active, naming an action, or <a href="/wiki/Stative_verb" title="Stative verb">stative</a>, describing a property. (In English, such descriptions are usually made with <a href="/wiki/Adjective" title="Adjective">adjectives</a>.)
</p><p>Verbs are <a href="/wiki/Inflection" title="Inflection">inflected</a> for first-, second- or third <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_person" title="Grammatical person">person</a>, and for singular, <a href="/wiki/Dual_(grammatical_number)" title="Dual (grammatical number)">dual</a> or plural <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_number" title="Grammatical number">grammatical number</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Morphology">Morphology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lakota_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Morphology"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Verb_inflection">Verb inflection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lakota_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Verb inflection"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>There are two paradigms for verb <a href="/wiki/Inflection" title="Inflection">inflection</a>. One set of <a href="/wiki/Morpheme" title="Morpheme">morphemes</a> indicates the <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_person" title="Grammatical person">person</a> and <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_number" title="Grammatical number">number</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Subject_(grammar)" title="Subject (grammar)">subject</a> of active verbs. The other set of morphemes <a href="/wiki/Agreement_(linguistics)" title="Agreement (linguistics)">agrees</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Object_(grammar)" title="Object (grammar)">object</a> of <a href="/wiki/Transitive_verb" title="Transitive verb">transitive</a> action verbs <i>or</i> the subject of stative verbs.<sup id="cite_ref-rood96_6-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rood96-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Most of the morphemes in each paradigm are prefixes, but plural subjects are marked with a suffix and third-person plural objects with an <a href="/wiki/Infix" title="Infix">infix</a>.
</p><p>First person arguments may be singular, <a href="/wiki/Dual_(grammatical_number)" title="Dual (grammatical number)">dual</a>, or plural; second or third person arguments may be singular or plural.
</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Enclitics">Enclitics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lakota_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Enclitics"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Lakota has a number of <a href="/wiki/Enclitic" class="mw-redirect" title="Enclitic">enclitic</a> <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_particle" title="Grammatical particle">particles</a> which follow the verb, many of which differ depending on whether the speaker is male or female.
</p><p>Some enclitics indicate the aspect, mood, or number of the verb they follow. There are also various <a href="/wiki/Interrogative" title="Interrogative">interrogative</a> enclitics, which in addition to marking an utterance as a question show finer distinctions of meaning. For example, while <i>he</i> is the usual question-marking enclitic, <i>huŋwó</i> is used for <a href="/wiki/Rhetorical_question" title="Rhetorical question">rhetorical questions</a> or in formal <a href="/wiki/Public_speaking" title="Public speaking">oratory</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Dubitative_mood" title="Dubitative mood">dubitative</a> <i>wa</i> functions somewhat like a <a href="/wiki/Tag_question" title="Tag question">tag question</a> in English (Rood and Taylor 1996; Buchel 1983). (See also the section below on men and women's speech.)
</p>
<h4><span id="Men.27s_and_women.27s_speech"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Men's_and_women's_speech">Men's and women's speech</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lakota_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Men&#039;s and women&#039;s speech"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>A small number of enclitics (approximately eight) differ in form based on the gender of the speaker. <i>Yeló</i> (men) <i>ye</i> (women) mark mild assertions. <i>Kštó</i> (women only according to most sources) marks strong assertion. <i>Yo</i> (men) and <i>ye</i> (women) mark neutral commands, <i>yetȟó</i> (men) and <i>nitȟó</i> (women) mark familiar, and <i>ye</i> (both men and women) and <i>na</i> mark requests. <i>He</i> is used by both genders to mark direct questions, but men also use <i>hųwó</i> in more formal situations. <i>So</i> (men) and <i>se</i> (women) mark <a href="/wiki/Dubitative_mood" title="Dubitative mood">dubitative</a> questions (where the person being asked is not assumed to know the answer).
</p><p>While many <a href="/wiki/First_language" title="First language">native speakers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Linguistics" title="Linguistics">linguists</a> agree that certain enclitics are associated with particular <a href="/wiki/Gender" title="Gender">genders</a>, such usage may not be exclusive. That is, individual men sometimes use enclitics associated with women, and vice versa (Trechter 1999).
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ablaut">Ablaut</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lakota_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Ablaut"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li>All examples are taken from the <i>New Lakota Dictionary</i>.</li></ul>
<p>The term "<a href="/wiki/Ablaut" class="mw-redirect" title="Ablaut">ablaut</a>" refers to the tendency of some words to change their final vowel in certain situations. Compare these sentences.
</p>
<ul><li><i>Šúŋka kiŋ sápa čha waŋbláke.</i></li>
<li><i>Šúŋka kiŋ sápe.</i></li>
<li><i>Šúŋka kiŋ sápiŋ na tȟáŋka.</i></li></ul>
<p>The last vowel in the word "<i>SápA</i>" changed each time. This vowel change is called "ablaut". Words which undergo this change are referred to as A-words, since, in dictionary citations, they are written ending in either -A or -Aŋ. These words are never written with a final capital letter in actual texts. Derivatives of these words generally take the ablaut as well, however there are exceptions.
</p><p>There are three forms for ablauted words: <i>-a/-aŋ</i>, <i>-e</i>, <i>-iŋ</i>. These are referred to as a/aŋ-ablaut, e-ablaut, and iŋ-ablaut respectively. Some words are ablauted by some and not others, like "gray" <i>hóta</i> or <i>hótA</i>. Ablaut always depends on what word follows the ablauted word.
</p>
<h4><span id="A.2Fa.C5.8B-ablaut"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="A/aŋ-ablaut">A/aŋ-ablaut</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lakota_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: A/aŋ-ablaut"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>This is the basic form of the word, and is used everywhere in which the other forms are not utilized.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="E-ablaut">E-ablaut</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lakota_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: E-ablaut"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>There are two cases in which e-ablaut is used.
</p>
<ol><li>Last word in the sentence</li>
<li>Followed by a word which triggers e-ablaut</li></ol>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="1._Last_word_in_sentence">1. Last word in sentence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lakota_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: 1. Last word in sentence"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<dl><dt>Examples</dt>
<dd><i>Héčhiya yé</i> He went there. (e-ablaut of the verb <i>yÁ</i>)</dd>
<dd><i>Yúte</i> She ate it. (e-ablaut of the verb <i>yútA</i>)</dd>
<dd><i>Thípi kiŋ pahá akáŋl hé.</i> The house stands on a cliff. (e-ablaut of the verb <i>hÁŋ</i>)</dd></dl>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="2._Followed_by_a_word_which_triggers_e-ablaut">2. Followed by a word which triggers e-ablaut</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lakota_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: 2. Followed by a word which triggers e-ablaut"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>There are three classes of words which trigger e-ablaut
</p>
<ol><li>various enclitics, such as <i>ȟča, ȟčiŋ, iŋčhéye, kačháš, kiló, kštó, któk, lakȟa, -la, láȟ, láȟčaka, ló, séčA, sékse, s’eléčheča, so, s’a, s’e, šaŋ, šni, uŋštó</i></li>
<li>some conjunctions and articles, such as <i>kiŋ, kiŋháŋ, k’éaš, k’uŋ, eháŋtaŋš</i></li>
<li>some auxiliary verbs, such as <i>kapíŋ, kiníča (kiníl), lakA (la), kúŋzA, phiča, ši, wačhíŋ, -yA, -khiyA</i></li></ol>
<dl><dt>Examples</dt></dl>
<dl><dd><i>Škáte šni.</i> He did not play. (enclitic)</dd>
<dd><i>Škáte s’a.</i> He plays often. (enclitic)</dd>
<dd><i>Škáte ló.</i> He plays. (enclitic (marking assertion))</dd>
<dd><i>Okȟáte háŋtaŋ...</i> If it is hot... (conjunctive)</dd>
<dd><i>Sápe kiŋ</i> The black one (definite article)</dd>
<dd><i>Glé kúŋze.</i> He pretended to go home. (auxiliary verb)</dd>
<dd><i>Yatké-phiča.</i> It is drinkable. (auxiliary verb)</dd></dl>
<h4><span id="I.C5.8B-ablaut"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Iŋ-ablaut">Iŋ-ablaut</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lakota_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Iŋ-ablaut"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The <i>iŋ</i>-ablaut (pronounced <i>i</i> by some) occurs only before the following words:
</p>
<dl><dd><i>ktA</i> (irrealis enclitic)</dd>
<dd><i>yetȟó</i> (familiar command enclitic)</dd>
<dd><i>na, naháŋ</i> (and)</dd>
<dd><i>naíŋš</i> (or, and or)</dd>
<dd><i>yé</i> (polite request or entreaty enclitic)</dd></dl>
<dl><dt>Examples</dt></dl>
<dl><dd><i>Waŋyáŋkiŋ yetȟó.</i> Take a look at this, real quick.</dd>
<dd><i>Yíŋ kte.</i> She will go.</dd>
<dd><i>Skúyiŋ na wašté.</i> It was sweet and good.</dd>
<dd><i>Waŋyáŋkiŋ yé.</i> Please, look at it.</dd></dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Phrases">Phrases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lakota_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Phrases"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>"<i>Háu kȟolá</i>", literally "Hello, friend", is the most common greeting, and was transformed into the generic motion picture American Indian "<a href="/wiki/How_(greeting)" title="How (greeting)">How</a>!", just as the traditional feathered headdress of the Teton was "given" to all movie Indians. As <i>háu</i> is the only word in Lakota which contains a <a href="/wiki/Diphthong" title="Diphthong">diphthong</a>, <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/au/</span>, it may be a loanword from a non-Siouan language.<sup id="cite_ref-rood96_6-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rood96-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Language_revitalization_efforts">Language revitalization efforts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lakota_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Language revitalization efforts"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Assimilating Indigenous peoples into the expanding American society of the late 19th and early 20th centuries depended on suppression or full eradication of each tribe's unique language as the central aspect of its culture. <a href="/wiki/Indian_residential_schools" class="mw-redirect" title="Indian residential schools">Indian residential schools</a> in <a href="/wiki/American_Indian_residential_schools" class="mw-redirect" title="American Indian residential schools">the US and Canada</a> that separated Indigenous children from their parents and relatives enforced this assimilation process with beatings and other forms of violence for speaking tribal languages(Powers).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources#What_information_to_include" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="A complete citation is needed. (December 2021)">full citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> The Lakota language survived this suppression. "Lakota persisted through the recognized natural immersion afforded by daily conversation in the home, the community at reservation-wide events, even in texts written in the form of letters to family and friends. people demonstrated their cultural resilience through the positive application of spoken and written Lakota." (Powers)<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources#What_information_to_include" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="A complete citation is needed. (December 2021)">full citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Even so, employment opportunities were based on speaking English; a Lakota who was bilingual or spoke only English was more likely to be hired.<sup id="cite_ref-Andrews_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Andrews-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1967, the <a href="/wiki/Red_Cloud_Indian_School" title="Red Cloud Indian School">Red Cloud Indian School</a> at <a href="/wiki/Pine_Ridge_Indian_Reservation" title="Pine Ridge Indian Reservation">Pine Ridge</a> began offering Lakota language classes. This was over two decades before the <a href="/wiki/Native_American_Languages_Act_of_1990" title="Native American Languages Act of 1990">Native American Languages Act of 1990</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-TimelineRedCloud_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TimelineRedCloud-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the mid-1970s the Rosebud Reservation established their Lakota Language and Culture department at the <a href="/wiki/Sinte_Gleska_University" title="Sinte Gleska University">Sinte Gleska University</a> under the chairmanship of Ben Black Bear, Jr., who employed textbooks and orthography developed by the Colorado University Lakota Project (CULP). A few years later Black Bear was replaced as a chair of the department by <a href="/wiki/Albert_White_Hat" title="Albert White Hat">Albert White Hat</a>, who discontinued the use of the Colorado University textbooks. In 1992 White Hat published a textbook with his own orthography, for use at all levels of language learning on Rosebud. Sinte Gleska University now uses the orthography developed by Albert White Hat.<sup id="cite_ref-Hirschfelder_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hirschfelder-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2002 Sinte Gleska University rejected the Lakota Language Consortium invitation to support their organization. Rosebud Cultural Studies teacher Randy Emery spoke to the <i>Lakota Journal</i>, stating, "The Lakota Language Consortium has created the misleading impression that Sinte Gleska University is one of the schools that supports their organization," and that the LLC had circulated a document to this effect with other misleading information about the state of the language, "The (LLC) documentation strongly implies that there are no fluent speakers younger than the elder age group and the presentation implies that the Lakota cannot deal with the problem themselves; therefore outside help must be brought in to lead the program. This appears to us as a sugar coated attack on sovereignty".<sup id="cite_ref-LakotaJournal2002_12-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LakotaJournal2002-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2008, the Red Cloud School at Pine Ridge launched their Lakota language curriculum for K-12 students.<sup id="cite_ref-TimelineRedCloud_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TimelineRedCloud-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> In November 2012, the incoming president of the Oglala Sioux Tribe, Bryan Brewer, announced that he intended "to lead a Lakota Language Revitalization Initiative that will focus on the creation and operation of Lakota language immersion schools and identifying all fluent Lakota speakers."<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> A Lakota language immersion daycare center is scheduled to open at <a href="/wiki/Pine_Ridge_Indian_Reservation" title="Pine Ridge Indian Reservation">Pine Ridge</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> Also in 2012, Lakota <a href="/wiki/Language_immersion" title="Language immersion">immersion classes</a> were provided for children in an experimental program at <a href="/wiki/Sitting_Bull_College" title="Sitting Bull College">Sitting Bull College</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Standing_Rock_Reservation" class="mw-redirect" title="Standing Rock Reservation">Standing Rock Reservation</a>, where children speak only Lakota for their first year.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> Tom Red Bird is a Lakota teacher at the program who grew up on the Cheyenne River Reservation. He believes in the importance of teaching the language to younger generations as this would close the gap in the ages of speakers.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> In 2014, it is estimated that about five percent of children age four to six on <a href="/wiki/Pine_Ridge_Indian_Reservation" title="Pine Ridge Indian Reservation">Pine Ridge Indian Reservation</a> speak Lakota.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> Language Revitalization efforts continued to be strengthened by the establishment of several independent, grassroots Lakota language immersion schools and camps, such as those at the <a href="/wiki/Dakota_Access_Pipeline_protests" title="Dakota Access Pipeline protests">Dakota Access Pipeline protests</a> camps at <a href="/wiki/Standing_Rock" class="mw-redirect" title="Standing Rock">Standing Rock</a> in 2016.<sup id="cite_ref-StandingRockSchool_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-StandingRockSchool-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On May 3, 2022, the <a href="/wiki/Standing_Rock_Sioux" class="mw-redirect" title="Standing Rock Sioux">Standing Rock Sioux</a> Tribal Council passed Resolution Number 150-22, which, along with banishing the LLC, contains provisions to protect the Nation's <a href="/wiki/Intellectual_property_rights" class="mw-redirect" title="Intellectual property rights">intellectual property rights</a> and <a href="/wiki/Data_sovereignty" title="Data sovereignty">data sovereignty</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-NBC-LLCBanished_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NBC-LLCBanished-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Res150-22_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Res150-22-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> 
</p>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r996844942"><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>It reclaimed copyrights over all language materials created by the consortium and called for their immediate return, to be placed in the care of “the first-language speakers and knowledge-keepers in our communities."<sup id="cite_ref-NBC-LLCBanished_21-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NBC-LLCBanished-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Res150-22_22-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Res150-22-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<h3><span id="Lakota_Language_Education_Program_.28LLEAP.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Lakota_Language_Education_Program_(LLEAP)">Lakota Language Education Program (LLEAP)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lakota_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Lakota Language Education Program (LLEAP)"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2011, Sitting Bull College (Fort Yates, North Dakota, Standing Rock) and the University of South Dakota began degree programs to create effective Lakota language teachers. By earning a Bachelor of Arts in education at the University of South Dakota or a Bachelor of Science in education at Sitting Bull College, students can major in "Lakota Language Teaching and Learning" as part of the Lakota Language Education Action Program, or LLEAP.
</p><p>LLEAP is a four-year program designed to create at least 30 new Lakota language teachers by 2014, and was funded by $2.4 million in grants from the U.S. Department of Education. At the end of the initial phase, SBC and USD will permanently offer the Lakota Language Teaching and Learning degree as part of their regular undergraduate Education curriculum. The current LLEAP students' tuition and expenses are covered by the grant from the U.S. Department of Education. LLEAP is the first program of its kind, offering courses to create effective teachers in order to save a Native American language from going extinct, and potentially educate the 120,000 prospective Lakota speakers in the 21st century.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Government_support">Government support</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lakota_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Government support"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1990, Senator <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Inouye" title="Daniel Inouye">Daniel Inouye</a> (D-HI) sponsored the <a href="/wiki/Native_American_Languages_Act_of_1990" title="Native American Languages Act of 1990">Native American Languages Act</a> in order to preserve, protect, and promote the rights and freedoms of Native people in America to practice, develop and conduct business in their native language. This law, which took effect on October 30, 1990, reversed over 200 years of American policy that would have otherwise eliminated the indigenous languages of the United States. This legislation gave support to tribal efforts to fund language education programs.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Lakota_influences_in_English">Lakota influences in English</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lakota_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=33" title="Edit section: Lakota influences in English"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/American_Indian_English" title="American Indian English">American Indian English</a></div>
<p>Just as people from different regions of countries have accents, Lakota who speak English have some distinct speech patterns. These patterns are displayed in their grammatical sequences and can be heard through some phonological differences. These unique characteristics are also observed in Lakota youth, even those who only learned English.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
