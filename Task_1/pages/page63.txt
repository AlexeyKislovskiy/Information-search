<p><b>Malaysian Sign Language</b> (<a href="/wiki/Malay_language" title="Malay language">Malay</a>: <i lang="ms"><b>Bahasa Isyarat Malaysia</b></i>, or <b>BIM</b>) is the principal language of the deaf community of <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a>. It is also the official sign language used by the Malaysian government to communicate with the deaf community and was officially recognised by the Malaysian government in 2008 as a means to officially communicate with and among the deaf, particularly on official broadcasts and announcements. BIM has many dialects, differing from state to state.<sup id="cite_ref-Omar2009_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Omar2009-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Malaysian Sign Language was created with the establishment of the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Malaysian_Federation_of_the_Deaf&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Malaysian Federation of the Deaf (page does not exist)">Malaysian Federation of the Deaf</a> in 1998, and its use has expanded among deaf leaders and participants. It is based on <a href="/wiki/American_Sign_Language" title="American Sign Language">American Sign Language</a> (ASL), but the two are considered different languages.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Kod_Tangan_Bahasa_Malaysia" class="mw-redirect" title="Kod Tangan Bahasa Malaysia">Kod Tangan Bahasa Malaysia</a> or Manually Coded Malay (KTBM) is another teaching method created by hearing educators and linguists in between 1980 and 1986 and remains the only form of sign recognized by the <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Education_(Malaysia)" title="Ministry of Education (Malaysia)">Malaysian Ministry of Education</a> as a method to teach Malay to deaf students in formal education settings rather than act as an official language. However, it is not a language in itself, but a means of manually coding the Malay language. The use of KTBM supposedly makes it easier for teachers to teach the Malay language to deaf students.
</p><p>Sign languages which predate BIM in Malaysia are <a href="/wiki/Penang_Sign_Language" title="Penang Sign Language">Penang Sign</a> (PSL) and <a href="/wiki/Selangor_Sign_Language" class="mw-redirect" title="Selangor Sign Language">Selangor Sign</a> (Kuala Lumpur Sign, SSL or KLSL). Additionally, every parent of deaf children uses unique created signs, called <a href="/wiki/Home_sign" title="Home sign">home signs</a>, for gestural communication. The use of such home signs among <i><a href="/wiki/Peranakan" class="mw-redirect" title="Peranakan">peranakan</a></i> or <a href="/wiki/Malaysian_Chinese" title="Malaysian Chinese">ethnic Chinese</a> users of BIM may be responsible for the controversy over the supposed influence of <a href="/wiki/Chinese_Sign_Language" title="Chinese Sign Language">Chinese Sign Languages</a>, which is not well documented and may merely be based on <a href="/wiki/Ethnic_stereotype" title="Ethnic stereotype">ethnic stereotyping</a>.
</p><p>To further educate and promote the use of BIM, MFD implemented the BIM Sign Bank as the official source of reference for the sign language to the community, including students, teachers, parents, and the general users. In collaboration with Guidewire Gives Back, the BIM Sign Bank application, "BIM Sign Bank by MFD", was developed and launched in July 2021.
</p>

Natural sign languages are not related to the spoken language used in the same region. For example, French Sign Language originated in France, but is not related to French.
