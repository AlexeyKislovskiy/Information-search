<p><b>Innu-aimun</b> or <b>Montagnais</b> is an <a href="/wiki/Algonquian_languages" title="Algonquian languages">Algonquian language</a> spoken by over 10,000 <a href="/wiki/Innu" title="Innu">Innu</a><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> in <a href="/wiki/Labrador" title="Labrador">Labrador</a> and <a href="/wiki/Quebec" title="Quebec">Quebec</a> in Eastern <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>. It is a member of the <a href="/wiki/Cree_language" title="Cree language">Cree</a>–Montagnais–<a href="/wiki/Naskapi" title="Naskapi">Naskapi</a> <a href="/wiki/Dialect_continuum" title="Dialect continuum">dialect continuum</a> and is spoken in various dialects depending on the community.
</p>

<figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:20050607-092209_NatashquanQC_TsehekapetishukNamatueminishuk.jpeg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/20050607-092209_NatashquanQC_TsehekapetishukNamatueminishuk.jpeg/220px-20050607-092209_NatashquanQC_TsehekapetishukNamatueminishuk.jpeg" decoding="async" width="220" height="138" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/20050607-092209_NatashquanQC_TsehekapetishukNamatueminishuk.jpeg/330px-20050607-092209_NatashquanQC_TsehekapetishukNamatueminishuk.jpeg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/20050607-092209_NatashquanQC_TsehekapetishukNamatueminishuk.jpeg/440px-20050607-092209_NatashquanQC_TsehekapetishukNamatueminishuk.jpeg 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="640" /></a><figcaption>"Buckle up your children" sign in Innu-aimun, in the Nutashkuan reserve near <a href="/wiki/Natashquan,_Quebec_(township)" class="mw-redirect" title="Natashquan, Quebec (township)">Natashquan, Quebec</a>.</figcaption></figure>
<p>Since the 1980s, Innu-aimun has had considerable exposure in the popular culture of <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> and <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> due to the success of the rock music band <a href="/wiki/Kashtin" title="Kashtin">Kashtin</a> and the later solo careers of its founders <a href="/wiki/Claude_McKenzie" title="Claude McKenzie">Claude McKenzie</a> and <a href="/wiki/Florent_Vollant" title="Florent Vollant">Florent Vollant</a>. Widely heard hit songs with Innu-language lyrics have included "<span title="Innu-language text"><i lang="moe">Ish-kuess</i></span>" ("Girl"), "<span title="Innu-language text"><i lang="moe">E Uassiuian</i></span>" ("My Childhood"), "<span title="Innu-language text"><i lang="moe">Tipatshimun</i></span>" ("Story") and in particular "<span title="Innu-language text"><i lang="moe">Akua tuta</i></span>" ("Take care of yourself"), which appeared on soundtrack compilations for the television series <i><a href="/wiki/Due_South" title="Due South">Due South</a></i> and the documentary <i><a href="/wiki/Music_for_The_Native_Americans" title="Music for The Native Americans">Music for The Native Americans</a></i>. The lyrics of Akua Tuta are featured on over 50 websites, making this one of the most broadly accessible pieces of text written in any native North American language. Florent Vollant has also rendered several well-known <a href="/wiki/Christmas_carols" class="mw-redirect" title="Christmas carols">Christmas carols</a> into Innu in his 1999 album <span title="Innu-language text"><i lang="moe">Nipaiamianan</i></span>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2013, "a comprehensive pan-Innu dictionary, covering all the Innu dialects spoken in Quebec and Labrador [was] published in Innu, <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a> and <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>."<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Grammar">Grammar</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Innu-aimun&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Grammar"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Innu-aimun is a <a href="/wiki/Polysynthetic_language" title="Polysynthetic language">polysynthetic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Head-marking_language" title="Head-marking language">head-marking</a> language with relatively free <a href="/wiki/Word_order" title="Word order">word order</a>. Its three basic parts of speech are <a href="/wiki/Noun" title="Noun">nouns</a>, <a href="/wiki/Verb" title="Verb">verbs</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Particle_(linguistics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Particle (linguistics)">particles</a>. Nouns are grouped into two <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_gender" title="Grammatical gender">genders</a>, animate and inanimate, and may carry <a href="/wiki/Affix" title="Affix">affixes</a> indicating <a href="/wiki/Plural" title="Plural">plurality</a>, <a href="/wiki/Possession_(linguistics)" title="Possession (linguistics)">possession</a>, <a href="/wiki/Obviative" title="Obviative">obviation</a>, and location. Verbs are divided into four classes based on their <a href="/wiki/Transitivity_(grammatical_category)" class="mw-redirect" title="Transitivity (grammatical category)">transitivity</a>: animate intransitive (AI), inanimate intransitive (II), transitive inanimate (TI), and transitive animate (TA). Verbs may carry affixes indicating agreement (with both subject and object <a href="/wiki/Verb_argument" class="mw-redirect" title="Verb argument">arguments</a>), <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_tense" title="Grammatical tense">tense</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_mood" title="Grammatical mood">mood</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Inversion_(linguistics)" title="Inversion (linguistics)">inversion</a>. Two different sets, or <i>orders</i>, of verbal affixes are used depending on the verb's <a href="/wiki/Syntax" title="Syntax">syntactic context</a>. In simple main clauses, the verb is marked using affixes of the <i>independent order</i>, whereas in subordinate clauses and content-word questions, affixes of the <i>conjunct order</i> are used.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dialects">Dialects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Innu-aimun&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Dialects"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Innu-aimun is related to <a href="/wiki/East_Cree_language" class="mw-redirect" title="East Cree language">East Cree</a> (<span title="Northern East Cree-language romanization"><i lang="crl-Latn">Īyiyū Ayimūn</i></span> – Northern/Coastal dialect and <span title="Southern East Cree-language romanization"><i lang="crj-Latn">Īnū Ayimūn</i></span> – Southern/Inland dialect) spoken by the <a href="/wiki/Grand_Council_of_the_Crees" title="Grand Council of the Crees">James Bay Cree</a> of the <a href="/wiki/James_Bay" title="James Bay">James Bay</a> region of Quebec and <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">Ontario</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Atikamekw_language" title="Atikamekw language">Atikamekw</a> (<span title="Atikamekw-language romanization"><i lang="atj-Latn">Nēhinawēwin</i></span> and <span title="Atikamekw-language romanization"><i lang="atj-Latn">Nehirâmowin</i></span>) of the <a href="/wiki/Atikamekw" title="Atikamekw">Atikamekw</a> (<span title="Atikamekw-language romanization"><i lang="atj-Latn">Nehiraw</i></span>, <span title="Atikamekw-language romanization"><i lang="atj-Latn">Nehirowisiw</i></span>) in the upper <a href="/wiki/Saint-Maurice_River" title="Saint-Maurice River">Saint-Maurice River</a> valley of <a href="/wiki/Quebec" title="Quebec">Quebec</a>. Innu-aimun is divided into four dialects – Southern Montagnais (Mashteuiatsh, Betsiamites), Eastern Montagnais (Ekuanitshit, Nutashkuan, Unamen Shipu, Pakuashipi), Central Montagnais (Uashat and Maliotenam, Matimekosh) and Labrador-Montagnais (Sheshatshit).<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The speakers of the different dialects can communicate well with each other. The <a href="/wiki/Naskapi_language" title="Naskapi language">Naskapi language</a> and culture are quite different from those of the Montagnais,<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> in which the dialect changes from y to n as in <span title="Naskapi-language romanization"><i lang="nsk-Latn">Iiyuu</i></span> versus <span title="Innu-language romanization"><i lang="moe-Latn">Innu</i></span>.
</p>
