<p>The <b>Hmu language</b> (<i>hveb Hmub</i>), also known as <b>Qiandong Miao</b> (黔东, <b>Eastern Guizhou Miao</b>), <b>Central Miao</b> (中部苗语), <b>East Hmongic</b> (Ratliff 2010<sup id="cite_ref-Ratliff_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ratliff-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>), or (somewhat ambiguously) <b>Black Miao</b>, is a <a href="/wiki/Dialect_cluster" class="mw-redirect" title="Dialect cluster">dialect cluster</a> of <a href="/wiki/Hmongic_languages" title="Hmongic languages">Hmongic languages</a> of China. The best studied dialect is that of Yǎnghāo (养蒿) village, <a href="/wiki/Taijiang_County" title="Taijiang County">Taijiang County</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guizhou" title="Guizhou">Guizhou</a> Province.<sup id="cite_ref-Ratliff_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ratliff-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Qanu (咯努), a Hmu variety, had 11,450 speakers as of 2000, and is spoken just south of <a href="/wiki/Kaili_City" title="Kaili City">Kaili City</a>, Guizhou.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The Qanu are ethnoculturally distinct from the other Hmu.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Subdivisions_and_distribution">Subdivisions and distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hmu_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Subdivisions and distribution"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span id="Wang_.281985.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Wang_(1985)">Wang (1985)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hmu_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Wang (1985)"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Wang Fushi (1985)<sup id="cite_ref-jianzhi_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jianzhi-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> groups the Qiandong Miao languages as follows.
</p>
<ul><li><b>Northern</b>: 1,000,000 speakers in Kaili, Majiang, Nandan, Longlin, Leishan, Taijiang, Huangping, Jianhe, Zhenyuan, Sansui, Shibing, Sandu, Fuquan, Pingba, Zhenning, Xingren, Zhenfeng, Anlong, Wangmo, etc.</li>
<li><b>Eastern</b>: 250,000 speakers in Jinping, Liping, Jianhe, Jingzhou, Tongdao, Huitong, etc.</li>
<li><b>Southern</b>: 350,0000 speakers in Rongjiang, Congjiang, Nandan, Sandu, Rongshui, Sanjiang, etc. Includes <a href="/wiki/N%C3%A1-Meo_language" title="Ná-Meo language">Na Meo</a> of northern Vietnam.<sup id="cite_ref-Hsiu2015_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hsiu2015-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Western</b> (<a href="/wiki/Raojia_language" title="Raojia language">Raojia</a>): 15,000 speakers in Heba of Majiang, Mianluo of Duyun, Sandu, Rongjiang, parts of Nandan</li></ul>
<h3><span id="Wu_.282009.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Wu_(2009)">Wu (2009)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hmu_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Wu (2009)"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Wu Zhengbiao (2009)<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> divides Hmu into seven different dialects. Past classifications usually included only three or four dialects. For example, Li Jinping &amp; Li Tianyi (2012),<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> based on past classifications, divide Hmu into the three dialects of Northern,<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Southern,<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> and Eastern.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Datapoint locations of representative dialects are from Li Yunbing (2000).<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><b>Eastern</b> (<i>Representative dialect</i>: Sanjiang township 三江乡, <a href="/wiki/Jinping_County,_Guizhou" title="Jinping County, Guizhou">Jinping County</a>, Guizhou)
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Jinping_County,_Guizhou" title="Jinping County, Guizhou">Jinping County</a>, <a href="/wiki/List_of_administrative_divisions_of_Guizhou" title="List of administrative divisions of Guizhou">Guizhou</a> (in Ouli 偶里寨 of Ouli Township 偶里乡, etc.)
<ul><li><b>Hekou 河口 dialect</b> (10,000+ speakers): spoken in Hekou 河口乡, Wenniu 文牛乡, and Zhanghua 彰化乡 townships<sup id="cite_ref-TuYang2008_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TuYang2008-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Ouli 偶里 dialect</b> (20,000+ speakers): spoken in Pinglve 平略乡, Ouli 偶里乡, Zhaizao 寨早乡, Jiaosan 皎三乡, Maoping 茅坪乡, Guazhi 挂治乡, Pingjin 平金乡, and Suijiang 稳江乡, Loujiang 娄江乡, and Tongpo 铜坡乡 townships<sup id="cite_ref-TuYang2008_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TuYang2008-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Yuhe 裕河 dialect</b> (about 3,000 speakers): spoken in Yuhe 裕河乡, Xinmin 新民乡, and Guben 固本乡 townships<sup id="cite_ref-TuYang2008_14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TuYang2008-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup></li></ul></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Jingzhou_Miao_and_Dong_Autonomous_County" title="Jingzhou Miao and Dong Autonomous County">Jingzhou County</a>, <a href="/wiki/List_of_administrative_divisions_of_Hunan" title="List of administrative divisions of Hunan">Hunan</a> (in Caidiwan 菜地湾,<sup id="cite_ref-Chen2013_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chen2013-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> etc.)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Huitong_County" title="Huitong County">Huitong County</a>, <a href="/wiki/List_of_administrative_divisions_of_Hunan" title="List of administrative divisions of Hunan">Hunan</a></li></ul></li>
<li><b>Northern</b> (<i>Representative dialect</i>: Yanghao village 养蒿村, Guading town 挂丁镇, <a href="/wiki/Kaili_City" title="Kaili City">Kaili city</a>, Guizhou)

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Others">Others</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hmu_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Others"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><b><a href="/wiki/Sanqiao_language" title="Sanqiao language">Sanqiao</a> 三锹</b> (三橇) is a <a href="/wiki/Mixed_language" title="Mixed language">mixed</a> <a href="/wiki/Kam_language" title="Kam language">Kam</a>–Hmu language spoken in <a href="/wiki/Liping_County" title="Liping County">Liping County</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jinping_County,_Guizhou" title="Jinping County, Guizhou">Jinping County</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guizhou" title="Guizhou">Guizhou</a>, China by about 6,000 people.<sup id="cite_ref-Yu2017_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Yu2017-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">Classification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hmu_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Classification"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Hmu has been recognized as a branch of <a href="/wiki/Hmongic_languages" title="Hmongic languages">Hmongic</a> since the 1950s. Wang (1985) recognized three varieties. Matisoff (2001) treated these as distinct languages, which is reflected in <i>Ethnologue</i>. Lee (2000) added a fourth variety, Western Hmu (10,000 speakers), among the <a href="/wiki/Yao_people" title="Yao people">Yao</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> and Matisoff (2006) lists seven (Daigong, Kaili [N], Lushan, Taijiang [N], Zhenfeng [N], Phö, Rongjiang [S]).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Writing">Writing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hmu_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Writing"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Northern Qiandong Miao, also known as Central Miao and as Eastern Guizhou Hmu (黔东方言 <i>Qián-Dōng fāngyán</i>), was chosen as the standard for Hmu-language textbooks in China, based on the pronunciation of Yǎnghāo (养蒿) village.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Wang1985_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wang1985-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p><span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">&#91;<a href="/wiki/Glottal_stop" title="Glottal stop">ʔ</a>&#93;</span> is not distinct from a zero initial (that is, if we accept <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ʔ/</span> as a consonant, there are no vowel-initial words in Hmu), and only occurs with tones 1, 3, 5, 7.
</p><p>The aspirated nasals and fricatives do not exist in Southern or Eastern Hmu; cognates words use their unaspirated homologues. Further, in Eastern Hmu, <i>di, ti</i> merge into <i>j, q</i>; <i>c</i> merges into <i>x</i>; <i>r</i> (Northern <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/z/</span>) merges into <i>ni</i>; and <i>v</i> is pronounced <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[w]</span>. In Southern Hmu, words cognate with <i>hni</i> (and some with <i>ni</i>) are pronounced <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[nʲʑ]</span>; those with <i>r</i> are <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[nz]</span>; and some words exchange <i>s</i> and <i>x</i>.
