<p><b>Zay</b> (also <b>Lak'i</b>, <b>Laqi</b>) is an <a href="/wiki/Afroasiatic_languages" title="Afroasiatic languages">Afroasiatic</a> language of the <a href="/wiki/Semitic_languages" title="Semitic languages">Semitic</a> branch spoken in <a href="/wiki/Ethiopia" title="Ethiopia">Ethiopia</a>. It is one of the <a href="/wiki/Gurage_languages" title="Gurage languages">Gurage languages</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Ethiopian_Semitic_languages" title="Ethiopian Semitic languages">Ethiopian Semitic</a> group. The Zay language has around 5,000 speakers known as the <a href="/wiki/Zay_people" title="Zay people">Zay</a>, who inhabit <a href="/wiki/Gelila,_Ethiopia" title="Gelila, Ethiopia">Gelila</a> and the other five islands and shores of <a href="/wiki/Lake_Zway" class="mw-redirect" title="Lake Zway">Lake Zway</a> in the southern part of the country.
</p>

<p>Zay is an unwritten language.  Most speakers are <a href="/wiki/Multilingualism" title="Multilingualism">multilingual</a> in other <a href="/wiki/Gurage_languages" title="Gurage languages">Gurage languages</a>, in the <a href="/wiki/Oromo_language" title="Oromo language">Oromo language</a>, and in <a href="/wiki/Amharic" title="Amharic">Amharic</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The language is geographically concentrated around Lake Zway; specifically, in Herera, <a href="/wiki/Meki" title="Meki">Meki</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ziway" class="mw-redirect" title="Ziway">Ziway</a>, and the five islands: Fundurro Island (Famat or Getesemani Island) the smallest island; Tsedecha Island (Aysut Island), next to the biggest island; Debre-Tsion Island, the largest island; Gelila Island; and Debre Sina Island. It is an <a href="/wiki/Endangered_language" title="Endangered language">endangered language</a>, with speakers migrating to the mainland adopting the <a href="/wiki/Oromo_language" title="Oromo language">Oromo language</a>, and increasing use of Oromo by the younger generations on the Zay islands.
</p><p>Zay is 70% lexically similar with the <a href="/wiki/Silt%CA%BCe_language" title="Siltʼe language">Siltʼe language</a>, and 60% with <a href="/wiki/Harari_language" title="Harari language">Harari</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Grammar">Grammar</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zay_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Grammar"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The word order of Zay is <a href="/wiki/Subject%E2%80%93object%E2%80%93verb" class="mw-redirect" title="Subject–object–verb">SOV</a> (subject–object–verb). Attributive <a href="/wiki/Adjective" title="Adjective">adjectives</a> precede the <a href="/wiki/Noun" title="Noun">nouns</a> they modify. Possessives also precede nouns.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Zay is a  <a href="/wiki/Pro-drop_language" title="Pro-drop language">pro-drop language</a>, with required subject-marking on the verb.
</p><p>Zay has been greatly affected by contact it has had with the <a href="/wiki/Gurage_languages" title="Gurage languages">Gurage languages</a>. This contact has created a significant amount of lexical and grammatical change in Zay.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
