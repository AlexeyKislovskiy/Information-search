<p>The <b>Mixe–Zoque</b> <span class="rt-commentedText nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt" lang="en-fonipa"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˌ/: secondary stress follows">ˌ</span><span title="&#39;m&#39; in &#39;my&#39;">m</span><span title="/iː/: &#39;ee&#39; in &#39;fleece&#39;">iː</span><span title="&#39;h&#39; in &#39;hi&#39;">h</span><span title="/eɪ/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;face&#39;">eɪ</span><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="&#39;s&#39; in &#39;sigh&#39;">s</span><span title="/oʊ/: &#39;o&#39; in &#39;code&#39;">oʊ</span><span title="&#39;k&#39; in &#39;kind&#39;">k</span><span title="/eɪ/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;face&#39;">eɪ</span></span>/</a></span></span><sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> (also: <b>Mixe–Zoquean</b>, <b>Mije–Soke</b>, <b>Mije–Sokean</b>) languages are a <a href="/wiki/Language_family" title="Language family">language family</a> whose living members are spoken in and around the <a href="/wiki/Isthmus_of_Tehuantepec" title="Isthmus of Tehuantepec">Isthmus of Tehuantepec</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>. The Mexican government recognizes three distinct Mixe–Zoquean languages as official: <a href="/wiki/Mixe_languages" title="Mixe languages">Mixe</a> or <i>ayook</i> with 188,000 speakers, <a href="/wiki/Zoque_languages" title="Zoque languages">Zoque</a>  or <i>o'de püt</i> with 88,000 speakers, and the <a href="/wiki/Popoluca" title="Popoluca">Popoluca</a> languages of which some are Mixean and some Zoquean with 69,000 speakers. However, the internal diversity in each of these groups is great. <a href="/wiki/Glottolog" title="Glottolog">Glottolog</a> counts 19 different languages, whereas the current classification of Mixe–Zoquean languages by <a href="/wiki/S%C3%B8ren_Wichmann" title="Søren Wichmann">Wichmann</a> (1995) counts 12 languages and 11 dialects. <a href="/wiki/Extinct_languages" class="mw-redirect" title="Extinct languages">Extinct languages</a> classified as Mixe–Zoquean include <a href="/wiki/Tapachultec_language" title="Tapachultec language">Tapachultec</a>, formerly spoken in <a href="/wiki/Tapachula" title="Tapachula">Tapachula</a>, along the southeast coast of <a href="/wiki/Chiapas" title="Chiapas">Chiapas</a>.
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mixe%E2%80%93Zoque_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Historically the Mixe–Zoquean family may have been much more widespread, reaching into the Guatemalan Pacific coast (i.e. the <a href="/wiki/Soconusco" title="Soconusco">Soconusco</a> region). <a href="/wiki/Terrence_Kaufman" title="Terrence Kaufman">Terrence Kaufman</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lyle_Campbell" title="Lyle Campbell">Lyle Campbell</a> have argued, based on a number of widespread loanwords in other <a href="/wiki/Mesoamerican_languages" title="Mesoamerican languages">Mesoamerican languages</a>, that it is likely that the <a href="/wiki/Olmec" class="mw-redirect" title="Olmec">Olmec</a> people, generally seen as the earliest dominating culture of <a href="/wiki/Mesoamerica" title="Mesoamerica">Mesoamerica</a>, spoke a Mixe–Zoquean language.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>  Kaufman and John Justeson also claim to have deciphered a substantial part of the text written in <a href="/wiki/Isthmian_script" title="Isthmian script">Isthmian script</a> (called also by them and some others 'Epi-Olmec') which appears on <a href="/wiki/La_Mojarra_Stela_1" title="La Mojarra Stela 1">La Mojarra Stela 1</a>, based upon their deciphering of the text as representing an archaic Mixe–Zoquean language.
</p><p>Both of these claims have been criticized: <a href="/wiki/Michael_D._Coe" title="Michael D. Coe">Michael D. Coe</a> and <a href="/wiki/David_Stuart_(Mayanist)" title="David Stuart (Mayanist)">David Stuart</a> argue that the surviving corpus of the few known examples of Isthmian inscriptions is insufficient to securely ground any proposed decipherment. Their attempt to apply Kaufman's and Justeson's decipherments to other extant Isthmian material failed to produce any meaningful results. Wichmann (1995) criticizes certain proposed Mixe–Zoquean loans into other Mesoamerican languages as being only Zoquean, not Mixean, which would put the period of borrowing much later than the <a href="/wiki/Proto-Mixe%E2%80%93Zoquean_language" title="Proto-Mixe–Zoquean language">Proto-Mixe–Zoquean</a> time-frame in which the Olmec culture was at its height. The date of the Mixe–Zoque split has however since been pushed back, and the argument is therefore much weaker than it once was thought to be.<sup id="cite_ref-Wichmann,_Beliaev_2008_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wichmann,_Beliaev_2008-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Later, Kaufman (2001), again on the basis of putative loans from Mixe–Zoque into other Mesoamerican languages, argued a Mixe–Zoquean presence at <a href="/wiki/Teotihuacan" title="Teotihuacan">Teotihuacan</a>, and he ascribed to Mixe–Zoquean an important role in spreading a number of the linguistic features that later became some of the principal commonalities used in defining the <a href="/wiki/Mesoamerican_Linguistic_Area" class="mw-redirect" title="Mesoamerican Linguistic Area">Mesoamerican Linguistic Area</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Genetic_relations_with_other_families">Genetic relations with other families</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mixe%E2%80%93Zoque_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Genetic relations with other families"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Mixe–Zoque languages have been included in several long-range classification proposals, e.g. in <a href="/wiki/Edward_Sapir" title="Edward Sapir">Edward Sapir</a>'s "Mexican Penutian" branch of his proposed <a href="/wiki/Penutian_languages" title="Penutian languages">Penutian</a> linguistic superfamily, <sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESapir1929_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESapir1929-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> or as part of the <a href="/wiki/Macro-Mayan_languages" title="Macro-Mayan languages">Macro-Mayan</a> proposal by Norman McQuown which groups together the Mixe–Zoque languages with the <a href="/wiki/Mayan_languages" title="Mayan languages">Mayan languages</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Totonacan_languages" title="Totonacan languages">Totonacan languages</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMcQuown1942_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMcQuown1942-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> At the end of the last century, <a href="/wiki/Lyle_Campbell" title="Lyle Campbell">Lyle Campbell</a> dismissed most earlier comparisons as methodologically flawed, but considered the Macro-Mayan proposal the most promising, but yet unproven hypothesis.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECampbell1997323–324_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECampbell1997323–324-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> In two more recently published articles, evidence is presented for linking the Mixe–Zoque languages either with the <a href="/wiki/Totonacan_languages" title="Totonacan languages">Totonacan languages</a> ("<a href="/wiki/Totozoquean_languages" title="Totozoquean languages">Totozoquean</a>"),<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBrownBeckKondrakWatters2011_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBrownBeckKondrakWatters2011-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> or with the <a href="/wiki/Mayan_languages" title="Mayan languages">Mayan languages</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMora-Marín2016_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMora-Marín2016-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Phonology">Phonology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mixe%E2%80%93Zoque_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Phonology"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The phoneme inventory of <a href="/wiki/Proto-Mixe%E2%80%93Zoquean" class="mw-redirect" title="Proto-Mixe–Zoquean">Proto-Mixe–Zoquean</a> as <a href="/wiki/Historical_linguistics" title="Historical linguistics">reconstructed</a> by Wichmann (1995) can be seen to be relatively simple, but many of the modern languages have been innovative; some have become quite vowel rich, and some also have introduced a <a href="/wiki/Fortis_and_lenis" title="Fortis and lenis">fortis–lenis</a> contrast in the stop series. Although the lateral phoneme <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/l/</span> is found in a few words in some of the languages, these are probably of <a href="/wiki/Onomatopoeia" title="Onomatopoeia">onomatopoeic</a> origin.

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Syllables">Syllables</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mixe%E2%80%93Zoque_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Syllables"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Mixe–Zoquean languages are characterized by complex syllabic nuclei made up of combinations of vowels together with the <a href="/wiki/Glottal_stop" title="Glottal stop">glottal stop</a> and <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/h/</span> in the proto-language. Complex syllable-final consonant clusters are also typical in the daughter languages and can be reconstructed for the proto-language.
</p><p>Proto-Mixe–Zoquean syllable nuclei could be either:
</p>
<dl><dd>V – short vowel</dd>
<dd>V' – short vowel with glottal stop</dd>
<dd>VV – long vowel</dd>
<dd>V'V – long vowel with medial glottal stop</dd>
<dd>VV' – long vowel with final glottal stop</dd>
<dd>Vh – short vowel with h</dd></dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Grammatical_features">Grammatical features</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mixe%E2%80%93Zoque_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Grammatical features"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Mixe–Zoquean languages are <a href="/wiki/Head-marking_language" title="Head-marking language">head-marking</a> and <a href="/wiki/Polysynthetic_language" title="Polysynthetic language">polysynthetic</a>, with morphologically complex verbs and simple nouns. Grammatical subjects as well as objects are marked in the verb. <a href="/wiki/Ergative%E2%80%93absolutive_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Ergative–absolutive language">Ergative</a> <a href="/wiki/Morphosyntactic_alignment" title="Morphosyntactic alignment">alignment</a> is used, as well as <a href="/wiki/Direct%E2%80%93inverse_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Direct–inverse language">direct–inverse systems</a> triggered by <a href="/wiki/Animacy" title="Animacy">animacy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Topic_(linguistics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Topic (linguistics)">topicality</a>. In Mixe–Zoquean verbs, a morphological distinction is made between two basic <a href="/wiki/Clause" title="Clause">clause</a>-types, independent and dependent; verbs take different aspectual and personal affixes, depending on the type of clause in which they appear. There are two different sets of <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_aspect" title="Grammatical aspect">aspect</a>-markers, one used in dependent clauses and another used in independent clauses. Three aspects are distinguished within each clause-type: incompletive, completive, and irrealis.
</p>
