<p>The <b>Mongolic languages</b> are a <a href="/wiki/Language_family" title="Language family">language family</a> spoken by the <a href="/wiki/Mongolic_peoples" title="Mongolic peoples">Mongolic peoples</a> in <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Europe" title="Eastern Europe">Eastern Europe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Central_Asia" title="Central Asia">Central Asia</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_Asia" title="North Asia">North Asia</a> and <a href="/wiki/East_Asia" title="East Asia">East Asia</a>, mostly in <a href="/wiki/Mongolia" title="Mongolia">Mongolia</a> and surrounding areas and in <a href="/wiki/Kalmykia" title="Kalmykia">Kalmykia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Buryatia" title="Buryatia">Buryatia</a>. The best-known member of this language family, <a href="/wiki/Mongolian_language" title="Mongolian language">Mongolian</a>, is the primary language of most of the residents of <a href="/wiki/Mongolia" title="Mongolia">Mongolia</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Mongols" title="Mongols">Mongol</a> residents of <a href="/wiki/Inner_Mongolia" title="Inner Mongolia">Inner Mongolia</a>, with an estimated 5.7+ million speakers.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mongolic_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<figure class="mw-default-size mw-halign-right" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:MongolicLanguagesGraph.svg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/MongolicLanguagesGraph.svg/220px-MongolicLanguagesGraph.svg.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="113" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/MongolicLanguagesGraph.svg/330px-MongolicLanguagesGraph.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/MongolicLanguagesGraph.svg/440px-MongolicLanguagesGraph.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="1009" data-file-height="519" /></a><figcaption>A timeline-based graphical representation of the Mongolic and <a href="/wiki/Para-Mongolic_languages" title="Para-Mongolic languages">Para-Mongolic languages</a></figcaption></figure>
<p>The possible precursor to Mongolic is the <a href="/wiki/Xianbei#Language" title="Xianbei">Xianbei language</a>, heavily influenced by the <a href="/wiki/Proto-Turkic_language" title="Proto-Turkic language">Proto-Turkic</a> (later, the <a href="/wiki/Oghuric_languages" title="Oghuric languages">Lir-Turkic</a>) language.
</p><p>The stages of <a href="/wiki/Mongolian_language#Linguistic_history" title="Mongolian language">historical Mongolic</a> are:
</p>
<ul><li>Pre-Proto-Mongolic, from approximately the 4th century AD until the 12th century AD, influenced by <a href="/wiki/Turkic_languages#Geographical_expansion_and_development" title="Turkic languages">Shaz-Turkic</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Proto-Mongolic_language" title="Proto-Mongolic language">Proto-Mongolic</a>, from approximately the 13th century, spoken around the time of <a href="/wiki/Genghis_Khan" title="Genghis Khan">Chinggis Khan</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Middle_Mongol_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Middle Mongol language">Middle Mongol</a>, from the 13th century until the early 15th century<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> or late 16th century,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> depending on classification spoken. (Given the almost entire lack of written sources for the period in between, an exact cutoff point cannot be established.) Again influenced by <a href="/wiki/Turco-Mongol_tradition" title="Turco-Mongol tradition">Turkic</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Classical_Mongolian_language" title="Classical Mongolian language">Classical Mongolian</a>, from approximately 1700 to 1900.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Mongolian_language" title="Mongolian language">Standard Mongolian</a> The standard Mongolian language has been in official use since 1919, and this form of the language is used in the economic, political, and social fields.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pre-Proto-Mongolic">Pre-Proto-Mongolic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mongolic_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Pre-Proto-Mongolic"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Pre-Proto-Mongolic</i> is the name for the stage of Mongolic that precedes Proto-Mongolic. Proto-Mongolic can be clearly identified chronologically with the language spoken by the Mongols during <a href="/wiki/Genghis_Khan" title="Genghis Khan">Genghis Khan</a>'s early expansion in the 1200-1210s. Pre-Proto-Mongolic, by contrast, is a continuum that stretches back indefinitely in time. It is divided into Early Pre-Proto-Mongolic and Late Pre-Proto-Mongolic.
</p><p>Late Pre-Proto-Mongolic refers to the Mongolic spoken a few centuries before Proto-Mongolic by the Mongols and neighboring tribes like the <a href="/wiki/Merkit" title="Merkit">Merkits</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kerait" class="mw-redirect" title="Kerait">Keraits</a>. Certain archaic words and features in Written Mongolian go back past Proto-Mongolic to Late Pre-Proto-Mongolic (Janhunen 2006).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Relationship_with_Turkic">Relationship with Turkic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mongolic_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Relationship with Turkic"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1033289096">.mw-parser-output .hatnote{font-style:italic}.mw-parser-output div.hatnote{padding-left:1.6em;margin-bottom:0.5em}.mw-parser-output .hatnote i{font-style:normal}.mw-parser-output .hatnote+link+.hatnote{margin-top:-0.5em}</style><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Turco-Mongols" class="mw-redirect" title="Turco-Mongols">Turco-Mongols</a></div>
<p>Pre-Proto-Mongolic has borrowed various words from <a href="/wiki/Turkic_languages" title="Turkic languages">Turkic languages</a>.
</p><p>In the case of Early Pre-Proto-Mongolic, certain loanwords in the Mongolic languages point to early contact with <a href="/wiki/Oghur_languages" class="mw-redirect" title="Oghur languages">Oghur</a> (Pre-Proto-Bulgaric) Turkic, also known as r-Turkic. These loanwords precede <a href="/wiki/Common_Turkic" class="mw-redirect" title="Common Turkic">Common Turkic</a> (z-Turkic) loanwords and include:
</p>
<ul><li>Mongolic <i>ikere</i> (twins) from Pre-Proto-Bulgaric <i>ikir</i> (versus Common Turkic <i>ekiz</i>)</li>
<li>Mongolic <i>hüker</i> (ox) from Pre-Proto-Bulgaric <i>hekür</i> (Common Turkic <i>öküz</i>)</li>
<li>Mongolic <i>jer</i> (weapon) from Pre-Proto-Bulgaric <i>jer</i> (Common Turkic <i>yäz</i>)</li>
<li>Mongolic <i>biragu</i> (calf) versus Common Turkic <i>buzagu</i></li>
<li>Mongolic <i>siri-</i> (to smelt ore) versus Common Turkic <i>siz-</i> (to melt)</li></ul>
<p>The above words are thought to have been borrowed from Oghur Turkic during the time of the <a href="/wiki/Xiongnu" title="Xiongnu">Xiongnu</a>.
</p><p>Later <a href="/wiki/Turkic_peoples" title="Turkic peoples">Turkic peoples</a> in Mongolia all spoke forms of Common Turkic (z-Turkic) as opposed to <a href="/wiki/Oghur_languages" class="mw-redirect" title="Oghur languages">Oghur</a> (Bulgharic) Turkic, which withdrew to the west in the 4th century. The <a href="/wiki/Chuvash_language" title="Chuvash language">Chuvash language</a>, spoken by 1 million people in European Russia, is the only living representative of Oghur Turkic which split from Proto Turkic around the 1st century AD.
</p><p>Words in Mongolic like <i><span title="Mongolian-language text"><i lang="mn">dayir</i></span></i> (brown, Common Turkic <i>yagiz</i>) and <i>nidurga</i> (fist, Common Turkic <i>yudruk</i>) with initial *d and *n versus Common Turkic *y are sufficiently archaic to indicate loans from an earlier stage of Oghur (Pre-Proto-Bulgaric). This is because Chuvash and Common Turkic do not differ in these features despite differing fundamentally in rhotacism-lambdacism (Janhunen 2006). Oghur tribes lived in the Mongolian borderlands before the 5th century, and provided Oghur loanwords to Early Pre-Proto-Mongolic before Common Turkic loanwords.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGolden201131_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGolden201131-5">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Proto-Mongolic">Proto-Mongolic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mongolic_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Proto-Mongolic"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Proto-Mongolic_language" title="Proto-Mongolic language">Proto-Mongolic language</a></div>
<p>Proto-Mongolic, the ancestor language of the modern Mongolic languages, is very close to Middle Mongol, the language spoken at the time of <a href="/wiki/Genghis_Khan" title="Genghis Khan">Genghis Khan</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Mongol_Empire" title="Mongol Empire">Mongol Empire</a>. Most features of modern Mongolic languages can thus be reconstructed from Middle Mongol. An exception would be the voice suffix like -caga- 'do together', which can be reconstructed from the modern languages but is not attested in Middle Mongol.
</p><p>The languages of the historical <a href="/wiki/Donghu_people" title="Donghu people">Donghu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wuhuan" title="Wuhuan">Wuhuan</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Xianbei" title="Xianbei">Xianbei</a> peoples might have been related to Proto-Mongolic.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> For <a href="/wiki/Tuoba_language" title="Tuoba language">Tabghach</a>, the language of the founders of the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Wei" title="Northern Wei">Northern Wei</a> dynasty, for which the surviving evidence is very sparse, and Khitan, for which evidence exists that is written in the two Khitan scripts (<a href="/wiki/Khitan_large_script" title="Khitan large script">large</a> and <a href="/wiki/Khitan_small_script" title="Khitan small script">small</a>) which have as yet not been fully deciphered, a direct affiliation to Mongolic can now be taken to be most likely or even demonstrated.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Middle_Mongol">Middle Mongol</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mongolic_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Middle Mongol"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Middle_Mongol" title="Middle Mongol">Middle Mongol</a></div>
<p>The changes from Proto-Mongolic to Middle Mongol are described below.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Changes_in_phonology">Changes in phonology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mongolic_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Changes in phonology"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Consonants">Consonants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mongolic_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Consonants"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>Research into reconstruction of the consonants of Middle Mongol has engendered several controversies. Middle Mongol had two series of plosives, but there is disagreement as to which phonological dimension they lie on, whether aspiration<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> or voicing.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The early scripts have distinct letters for velar plosives and uvular plosives, but as these are in complementary distribution according to vowel harmony class, only two back plosive phonemes, *<i>/k/</i>, *<i><span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/kʰ/</span></i> (~ *<i>[k]</i>, *<i><span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[qʰ]</span></i>) are to be reconstructed.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> One prominent, long-running disagreement concerns certain correspondences of word medial consonants among the four major scripts (<i>UM</i>, <i>SM</i>, <i>AM</i>, and <i>Ph</i>, which were discussed in the preceding section). Word-medial <i>/k/</i> of Uyghur Mongolian (UM) has not one, but two correspondences with the three other scripts: either /k/ or zero. Traditional scholarship has reconstructed *<i>/k/</i> for both correspondences, arguing that *<i>/k/</i> was lost in some instances, which raises the question of what the conditioning factors of those instances were.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> More recently, the other possibility has been assumed; namely, that the correspondence between UM <i>/k/</i> and zero in the other scripts points to a distinct phoneme, <i>/h/</i>, which would correspond to the word-initial phoneme <i>/h/</i> that is present in those other scripts.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> <i>/h/</i> (also called <i>/x/</i>) is sometimes assumed to derive from *<i><span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/pʰ/</span></i>, which would also explain zero in <i>SM</i>, <i>AM</i>, <i>Ph</i> in some instances where <i>UM</i> indicates /p/; e.g. <i><a href="/wiki/Deel_(clothing)" title="Deel (clothing)">debel</a></i> &gt; Khalkha <i>deel</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The palatal affricates *<i>č</i>, *<i>čʰ</i> were fronted in Northern Modern Mongolian dialects such as Khalkha. <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">*<i>kʰ</i></span> was <a href="/wiki/Spirantization" class="mw-redirect" title="Spirantization">spirantized</a> to <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/x/</span> in Ulaanbaatar Khalkha and the Mongolian dialects south of it, e.g. Preclassical Mongolian <i>kündü</i>, reconstructed as <i><span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">*kʰynty</span></i> 'heavy', became Modern Mongolian <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/xunt/</span><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> (but in the vicinity of <a href="/wiki/Bayankhongor" title="Bayankhongor">Bayankhongor</a> and <a href="/wiki/Baruun-Urt" title="Baruun-Urt">Baruun-Urt</a>, many speakers will say <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[kʰunt]</span>).<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Originally word-final *<i>n</i> turned into /ŋ/; if *<i><span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">n</span></i> was originally followed by a vowel that later dropped, it remained unchanged, e.g. <i><span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">*kʰen</span></i> became <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/xiŋ/</span>, but <i><span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">*kʰoina</span></i> became <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/xɔin/</span>. After i-breaking, <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">*[ʃ]</span> became phonemic. Consonants in words containing back vowels that were followed by <i><span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">*i</span></i> in Proto-Mongolian became <a href="/wiki/Palatalization_(phonetics)" title="Palatalization (phonetics)">palatalized</a> in Modern Mongolian. In some words, word-final <i><span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">*n</span></i> was dropped with most case forms, but still appears with the ablative, dative and genitive.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Only foreign origin words start with the letter <i>L</i> and none start with the letter <i>R</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Ramsey1987_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ramsey1987-17">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Vowels">Vowels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mongolic_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Vowels"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>The standard view is that Proto-Mongolic had <i><span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">*i,<span class="wrap"> </span>*e,<span class="wrap"> </span>*y,<span class="wrap"> </span>*ø,<span class="wrap"> </span>*u,<span class="wrap"> </span>*o,<span class="wrap"> </span>*a</span></i>. According to this view, <i><span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">*o</span></i> and <i><span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">*u</span></i> were <a href="/wiki/Pharyngealization" title="Pharyngealization">pharyngealized</a> to <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ɔ/</span> and <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ʊ/</span>, then <i><span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">*y</span></i> and <i><span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">*ø</span></i> were <a href="/wiki/Velarization" title="Velarization">velarized</a> to <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/u/</span> and <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/o/</span>. Thus, the vowel harmony shifted from a velar to a pharyngeal paradigm. <i><span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">*i</span></i> in the first syllable of back-vocalic words was <a href="/wiki/Assimilation_(linguistics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Assimilation (linguistics)">assimilated</a> to the following vowel; in word-initial position it became <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ja/</span>. <i><span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">*e</span></i> was rounded to <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">*ø</span> when followed by <i><span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">*y</span></i>. VhV and VjV sequences where the second vowel was any vowel but <i><span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">*i</span></i> were monophthongized. In noninitial syllables, short vowels were deleted from the phonetic representation of the word and long vowels became short;<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> e.g. <i><span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">*imahan</span></i> (<i><span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">*i</span></i> becomes <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ja/</span>, <i><span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">*h</span></i> disappears) &gt; <i><span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">*jamaːn</span></i> (unstable <i>n</i> drops; vowel reduction) &gt; /jama(n)/ 'goat', and <i><span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">*emys-</span></i> (regressive rounding assimilation) &gt; <i><span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">*ømys-</span></i> (vowel velarization) &gt; <i><span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">*omus-</span></i> (vowel reduction) &gt; /oms-/ 'to wear'
</p><p>This reconstruction has recently<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (November 2016)">when?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> been opposed, arguing that vowel developments across the Mongolic languages can be more economically explained starting from basically the same vowel system as Khalkha, only with <i><span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">*[ə]</span></i> instead of <i>*[e]</i>. Moreover, the sound changes involved in this alternative scenario are more likely from an <a href="/wiki/Articulatory_phonetics" title="Articulatory phonetics">articulatory</a> point of view and early Middle Mongol loans into <a href="/wiki/Korean_language" title="Korean language">Korean</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Changes_in_morphology">Changes in morphology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mongolic_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Changes in morphology"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Nominal_system">Nominal system</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mongolic_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Nominal system"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<figure class="mw-default-size mw-halign-right" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Secret_history.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img alt="white page with several lines of black Chinese characters running top-down and separated into small groups by spaces. To the left of some of the characters there are small characters such as 舌　and 中. To the right of each line, groups of characters are indicated as such by a &quot;]]&quot;-shaped bracket, and to the right of each such bracket, there are other medium-sized characters" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/Secret_history.jpg/250px-Secret_history.jpg" decoding="async" width="250" height="371" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/Secret_history.jpg/375px-Secret_history.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/Secret_history.jpg/500px-Secret_history.jpg 2x" data-file-width="607" data-file-height="900" /></a><figcaption><i><a href="/wiki/The_Secret_History_of_the_Mongols" class="mw-redirect" title="The Secret History of the Mongols">The Secret History of the Mongols</a></i> which goes back to a lost Mongolian script original is the only document that allows the reconstruction of agreement in social gender in Middle Mongol.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup></figcaption></figure>
<p>In the following discussion, in accordance with a preceding observation, the term "Middle Mongol" is used merely as a cover term for texts written in any of three scripts, Uighur Mongolian script (UM), Chinese (SM), or Arabic (AM).
</p><p>The case system of Middle Mongol has remained mostly intact down to the present, although important changes occurred with the comitative and the dative and most other case suffixes did undergo slight changes in form, i.e., were shortened.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> The Middle Mongol comitative -<i>luγ-a</i> could not be used attributively, but it was replaced by the suffix -<i>taj</i> that originally derived adjectives denoting possession from nouns, e.g. <i>mori-tai</i> 'having a horse' became <i>mor'toj</i> 'having a horse/with a horse'. As this adjective functioned parallel to <i>ügej</i> 'not having', it has been suggested that a "privative case" ('without') has been introduced into Mongolian.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> There have been three different case suffixes in the dative-locative-directive domain that are grouped in different ways: -<i>a</i> as locative and -<i>dur</i>, -<i>da</i> as dative<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> or -<i>da</i> and -<i>a</i> as dative and -<i>dur</i> as locative,<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> in both cases with some functional overlapping. As -<i>dur</i> seems to be grammaticalized from <i>dotur-a</i> 'within', thus indicating a span of time,<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> the second account seems to be more likely. Of these, -<i>da</i> was lost, -<i>dur</i> was first reduced to -<i>du</i> and then to -<i>d</i><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> and -<i>a</i> only survived in a few frozen environments.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Finally, the directive of modern Mongolian, -<i>ruu</i>, has been innovated from <i>uruγu</i> 'downwards'.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> Social gender agreement was abandoned.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Verbal_system">Verbal system</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mongolic_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Verbal system"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>Middle Mongol had a slightly larger set of declarative finite verb suffix forms<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> and a smaller number of participles, which were less likely to be used as finite predicates.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> The linking converb -<i>n</i> became confined to stable verb combinations,<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> while the number of converbs increased.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> The distinction between male, female and plural subjects exhibited by some finite verbal suffixes was lost.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Changes_in_syntax">Changes in syntax</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mongolic_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Changes in syntax"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Neutral word order in clauses with pronominal subject changed from object–predicate–subject to subject–object–predicate; e.g.
</p>
<div class="interlinear" style="margin-left:3em"><div style="float: left; margin-bottom: 0.3em;margin-right: 1em;"><p style="margin: 0px;font-style: italic;">Kökseü</p><p style="margin: 0px;">Kökseü</p></div><div style="float: left; margin-bottom: 0.3em;margin-right: 1em;"><p style="margin: 0px;font-style: italic;">sabraq</p><p style="margin: 0px;">sabraq</p></div><div style="float: left; margin-bottom: 0.3em;margin-right: 1em;"><p style="margin: 0px;font-style: italic;">ügü.le-run</p><p style="margin: 0px;">speak-<abbr class="gloss-abbr" title="converb" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-variant-numeric: oldstyle-nums; text-transform: lowercase;">CVB</abbr></p></div><div style="float: left; margin-bottom: 0.3em;margin-right: 1em;"><p style="margin: 0px;font-style: italic;">ayyi</p><p style="margin: 0px;">alas</p></div><div style="float: left; margin-bottom: 0.3em;margin-right: 1em;"><p style="margin: 0px;font-style: italic;">yeke</p><p style="margin: 0px;">big</p></div><div style="float: left; margin-bottom: 0.3em;margin-right: 1em;"><p style="margin: 0px;font-style: italic;">uge</p><p style="margin: 0px;">word</p></div><div style="float: left; margin-bottom: 0.3em;margin-right: 1em;"><p style="margin: 0px;font-style: italic;">ugu.le-d</p><p style="margin: 0px;">speak-<abbr class="gloss-abbr" title="past tense" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-variant-numeric: oldstyle-nums; text-transform: lowercase;">PAST</abbr></p></div><div style="float: left; margin-bottom: 0.3em;margin-right: 1em;"><p style="margin: 0px;font-style: italic;">ta</p><p style="margin: 0px;">you</p></div><div style="float: left; margin-bottom: 0.3em;margin-right: 1em;"><p style="margin: 0px;font-style: italic;">...</p><p style="margin: 0px;">...</p></div><div style="float: left; margin-bottom: 0.3em;margin-right: 1em;"><p style="margin: 0px;font-style: italic;">kee-jüü.y</p><p style="margin: 0px;">say-<abbr class="gloss-abbr" title="nonfuture" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-variant-numeric: oldstyle-nums; text-transform: lowercase;">NFUT</abbr></p></div><p style="display: none;">Kökseü sabraq ügü.le-run ayyi yeke uge ugu.le-d ta ... kee-jüü.y</p><p style="display: none;">Kökseü sabraq speak-CVB alas big word speak-PAST you ... say-NFUT</p><p style="clear: left;">"Kökseü sabraq spoke saying, 'Alas! You speak a great boast....' "<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup></p><div style="clear: left; display: block;"></div>
</div>
<p>The syntax of verb negation shifted from negation particles preceding final verbs to a negation particle following participles; thus, as final verbs could no longer be negated, their paradigm of negation was filled by particles.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> For example, Preclassical Mongolian <i>ese irebe</i> 'did not come' v. modern spoken Khalkha Mongolian <i>ireegüi</i> or <i>irsengüi</i>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">Classification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mongolic_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Classification"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Para-Mongolic_languages" title="Para-Mongolic languages">Para-Mongolic languages</a> and <a href="/wiki/Altaic_languages" title="Altaic languages">Altaic languages</a></div>
<p>The Mongolic languages have no convincingly established living relatives. The closest relatives of the Mongolic languages appear to be the <a href="/wiki/Para-Mongolic_languages" title="Para-Mongolic languages">para-Mongolic languages</a>, which include the extinct <a href="/wiki/Khitan_language" title="Khitan language">Khitan</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Janhunen_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Janhunen-37">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Tuyuhun_language" title="Tuyuhun language">Tuyuhun</a>, and possibly also <a href="/wiki/Tuoba_language" title="Tuoba language">Tuoba</a> languages.<sup id="cite_ref-Shimunek2017_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Shimunek2017-38">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Alexander_Vovin" title="Alexander Vovin">Alexander Vovin</a> (2007) identifies the extinct Tabγač or <a href="/wiki/Tuoba_language" title="Tuoba language">Tuoba language</a> as a Mongolic language.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> However, Chen (2005)<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> argues that Tuoba (Tabγač) was a <a href="/wiki/Turkic_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Turkic language">Turkic language</a>. Vovin (2018) suggests that the <a href="/wiki/Rouran_language" title="Rouran language">Rouran language</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Rouran_Khaganate" title="Rouran Khaganate">Rouran Khaganate</a> was a Mongolic language, close but not identical to Middle Mongolian.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Altaic">Altaic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mongolic_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Altaic"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A few linguists have grouped Mongolic with <a href="/wiki/Turkic_languages" title="Turkic languages">Turkic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tungusic_languages" title="Tungusic languages">Tungusic</a> and possibly <a href="/wiki/Koreanic_languages" title="Koreanic languages">Koreanic</a> or <a href="/wiki/Japonic_languages" title="Japonic languages">Japonic</a> as part of the widely discredited <a href="/wiki/Altaic_languages" title="Altaic languages">Altaic family</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Following <a href="/wiki/Sergei_Starostin" title="Sergei Starostin">Sergei Starostin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Martine_Robbeets" title="Martine Robbeets">Martine Robbeets</a> suggested that Mongolic languages belong to a "<a href="/wiki/Transeurasian" class="mw-redirect" title="Transeurasian">Transeurasian</a>" superfamily also comprising <a href="/wiki/Japonic_languages" title="Japonic languages">Japonic languages</a>, <a href="/wiki/Korean_language" title="Korean language">Korean</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tungusic_languages" title="Tungusic languages">Tungusic languages</a> and <a href="/wiki/Turkic_languages" title="Turkic languages">Turkic languages</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> but this view has been severely criticized.<sup id="cite_ref-Tian_2022_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tian_2022-44">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<li>Southern Mongolic (part of a Gansu–Qinghai <a href="/wiki/Sprachbund" title="Sprachbund">Sprachbund</a>)

<p>In another classificational approach,<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> there is a tendency to call Central Mongolian a language consisting of Mongolian proper, Oirat and Buryat, while Ordos (and implicitly also Khamnigan) is seen as a variety of Mongolian proper. Within Mongolian proper, they then draw a distinction between Khalkha on the one hand and the <a href="/wiki/Mongolian_language_in_Inner_Mongolia" title="Mongolian language in Inner Mongolia">Mongolian language in Inner Mongolia</a> (containing everything else) on the other hand. A less common subdivision of Central Mongolic is to divide it into a Central dialect (Khalkha, Chakhar, Ordos), an Eastern dialect (Kharchin, Khorchin), a Western dialect (Oirat, Kalmyk), and a Northern dialect (consisting of two Buryat varieties).<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The broader delimitation of Mongolian may be based on <a href="/wiki/Mutual_intelligibility" title="Mutual intelligibility">mutual intelligibility</a>, but an analysis based on a <a href="/wiki/Phylogenetic_tree#Limitations_of_phylogenetic_analysis" title="Phylogenetic tree">tree diagram</a> such as the one above faces other problems because of the close contacts between, for example, Buryat and Khalkha Mongols during history, thus creating or preserving a <a href="/wiki/Dialect_continuum" title="Dialect continuum">dialect continuum</a>. Another problem lies in the sheer comparability of terminology, as Western linguists use <i>language</i> and <i>dialect</i>, while Mongolian linguists use the <a href="/wiki/Brothers_Grimm" title="Brothers Grimm">Grimmian</a> trichotomy <i>language</i> (kele), <i>dialect</i> (nutuγ-un ayalγu) and <i>Mundart</i> (aman ayalγu).
</p><p>Rybatzki (2003: 388–389)<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> recognizes the following 6 areal subgroups of Mongolic.
</p>
<ul><li>Northeastern Mongolic (NE) = <a href="/wiki/Dagur_language" title="Dagur language">Dagur</a></li>
<li>Northern Mongolic (N) = <a href="/wiki/Khamnigan_Mongol" title="Khamnigan Mongol">Khamnigan Mongol</a>–<a href="/wiki/Buryat_language" title="Buryat language">Buryat</a></li>
<li>Central Mongolic (C) = <a href="/wiki/Mongolian_language" title="Mongolian language">Mongol proper</a>–<a href="/wiki/Ordos_Mongolian" title="Ordos Mongolian">Ordos</a>–<a href="/wiki/Oirat_language" title="Oirat language">Oirat</a></li>
<li>South-Central Mongolic (SC) = <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Yugur_language" title="Eastern Yugur language">Shira Yughur</a></li>
<li>Southeastern Mongolic (SE) = <a href="/wiki/Monguor_language" title="Monguor language">Mongghul</a>–<a href="/wiki/Monguor_language" title="Monguor language">Mangghuer</a>–<a href="/wiki/Bonan_language" title="Bonan language">Bonan</a>–<a href="/wiki/Santa_language" title="Santa language">Santa</a> – <a href="/wiki/Kangjia_language" title="Kangjia language">Kangjia</a></li>
<li>Southwestern Mongolic (SW) = <a href="/wiki/Moghol_language" title="Moghol language">Moghol</a></li></ul>
<p>Additionally, the <a href="/wiki/Max_Planck_Institute_for_Evolutionary_Anthropology" title="Max Planck Institute for Evolutionary Anthropology">Max Planck Institute for Evolutionary Anthropology</a> refers to Central Mongolic as "Eastern Mongolic" and classifies the group as follows, using data from Rybatzki (2003) as the basis:<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Writing_systems">Writing systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mongolic_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Writing systems"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Mongolian_script" title="Mongolian script">Mongolian script</a></div>
<ul><li>The traditional <a href="/wiki/Mongolian_script" title="Mongolian script">Mongolian script</a> (based on the <a href="/wiki/Old_Uyghur_alphabet" title="Old Uyghur alphabet">Old Uyghur alphabet</a>) was first developed for Proto-Mongolic, possibly as early as the 7th century.</li>
<li>In 1931, the <a href="/wiki/Mongolian_People%27s_Republic" title="Mongolian People&#39;s Republic">Mongolian People's Republic</a> adopted a <a href="/wiki/Mongolian_Latin_alphabet" title="Mongolian Latin alphabet">Mongolian version of the Latin alphabet</a> as the official script for Mongolian.</li>
<li>Under <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet</a> influence, in 1941 Mongolia switched to a version of the Russian alphabet called <a href="/wiki/Mongolian_Cyrillic" class="mw-redirect" title="Mongolian Cyrillic">Mongolian Cyrillic</a>.</li>
<li>In March 2020, the Mongolian government announced plans to use both Cyrillic and the traditional Mongolian script in official documents by 2025.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
