<p><b>Siltʼe</b> (ስልጥኘ <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[siltʼiɲɲə]</span> or የስልጤ አፍ <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[jəsiltʼe<span class="wrap"> </span>af]</span>) is an <a href="/wiki/Ethiopian_Semitic_languages" title="Ethiopian Semitic languages">Ethiopian Semitic language</a>  spoken in South <a href="/wiki/Ethiopia" title="Ethiopia">Ethiopia</a>. A member of the <a href="/wiki/Afroasiatic_languages" title="Afroasiatic languages">Afroasiatic family</a>, its speakers are the <a href="/wiki/Silt%CA%BCe_people" title="Siltʼe people">Siltʼe</a>, who mainly inhabit the <a href="/wiki/Silt%CA%BCe_Zone" title="Siltʼe Zone">Siltʼe Zone</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Nations,_Nationalities,_and_Peoples%27_Region" title="Southern Nations, Nationalities, and Peoples&#39; Region">Southern Nations, Nationalities, and Peoples' Region</a>. Speakers of the Wolane dialect mainly inhabit the <a href="/wiki/Kokir_Gedebano" title="Kokir Gedebano">Kokir Gedebano</a> district of <a href="/wiki/Gurage_Zone" title="Gurage Zone">Gurage Zone</a>, as well as the neighbouring <a href="/wiki/Seden_Sodo" title="Seden Sodo">Seden Sodo</a> district of the <a href="/wiki/Oromia_Region" class="mw-redirect" title="Oromia Region">Oromia Region</a>. Some have also settled in urban areas in other parts of the country, especially <a href="/wiki/Addis_Ababa" title="Addis Ababa">Addis Ababa</a>.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Consonants">Consonants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Silt%CA%BCe_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Consonants"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Siltʼe has a fairly typical set of consonants for an <a href="/wiki/Ethiopian_Semitic_languages" title="Ethiopian Semitic languages">Ethiopian Semitic</a> language. There are the usual <a href="/wiki/Ejective_consonant" title="Ejective consonant">ejective consonants</a>, alongside plain voiceless and voiced consonants and all of the consonants, except <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/h/</span> and <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ʔ/</span>, can be <a href="/wiki/Gemination" title="Gemination">geminated</a>, that is, lengthened.
</p><p>The charts below show the phonemes of Siltʼe. For the representation of Siltʼe consonants, this article uses a modification of a system that is common (though not universal), among linguists who work on Ethiopian Semitic languages, but differs somewhat from the conventions of the <a href="/wiki/International_Phonetic_Alphabet" title="International Phonetic Alphabet">International Phonetic Alphabet</a>. When the IPA symbol is different, it is indicated in brackets in the charts.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vowels">Vowels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Silt%CA%BCe_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Vowels"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Siltʼe vowels differ considerably from the typical set of seven vowels in languages such as <a href="/wiki/Amharic" title="Amharic">Amharic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tigrinya_language" title="Tigrinya language">Tigrinya</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ge%CA%BDez" title="Geʽez">Geʽez</a>. Siltʼe has the set of five short and five long vowels that are typical of the nearby Eastern Cushitic languages, which may be the origin of the Siltʼe system. There is considerable <a href="/wiki/Allophone" title="Allophone">allophonic</a> variation within the short vowels, especially for <i>a</i>; the most frequent allophone of <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/a/</span>, <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ə]</span>, is shown in the chart. All of the short vowels may be <a href="/wiki/Vowel#Phonation" title="Vowel">devoiced</a> preceding a pause.

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Orthography">Orthography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Silt%CA%BCe_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Orthography"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Since at least the 1980s, Siltʼe has been written in the <a href="/wiki/Ge%CA%BDez_script" title="Geʽez script">Geʽez script</a>, originally developed for the now-extinct Geʽez language and most familiar today in its use for <a href="/wiki/Amharic" title="Amharic">Amharic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tigrinya_language" title="Tigrinya language">Tigrinya</a>.
</p><p>This orthographic system makes distinctions among only seven vowels. Some of the short-long distinctions in Siltʼe are therefore not marked. In practice, this probably does not interfere with comprehension because there are relatively few <a href="/wiki/Minimal_pair" title="Minimal pair">minimal pairs</a> based on vowel length.
In written Siltʼe, the seven Geʽez vowels are mapped onto the ten Siltʼe vowels as follows:
</p>
<ul><li><i>ä</i> → <i>a</i>: አለፈ <i>alafa</i> 'he passed'</li>
<li><i>u</i> → <i>u</i>, <i>uu</i>: ሙት <i>mut</i> 'death', <i>muut</i> 'thing'</li>
<li><i>i</i> →
<ul><li><i>ii</i>: ኢን <i>iin</i> 'eye'</li>
<li>word-final <i>i</i>: መሪ <i>mari</i> 'friend'</li>
<li><i>i</i> ending a noun <a href="/wiki/Word_stem" title="Word stem">stem</a>: መሪከ <i>marika</i> 'his friend'</li>
<li>impersonal perfect verb <i>i</i> suffix: ባሊ <i>baali</i> 'people said'; በባሊም <i>babaalim</i> 'even if people said'</li></ul></li>
<li><i>a</i> → <i>aa</i>: ጋራሽ <i>gaaraaš</i> 'your (f.) house'</li>
<li><i>e</i> → <i>e</i>, <i>ee</i>: ኤፌ <i>eeffe</i> 'he covered'</li>
<li><i>ǝ</i> →
<ul><li><i>i</i> (except as above): እንግር <i>ingir</i> 'foot'</li>
<li>consonant not followed by a vowel: አስሮሽት <i>asroošt</i> 'twelve'</li></ul></li>
<li><i>o</i> → <i>o</i>, <i>oo</i>: ቆጬ <i>kʼočʼe</i> 'tortoise', <i>kʼoočʼe</i> 'he cut'</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Language_vitality">Language vitality</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Silt%CA%BCe_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Language vitality"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Meshesha Make Jobo reports that the use of the Siltʼe language is being replaced by the use of Amharic by some speakers for some domains. He points to large political and social factors, many from the national level. He also points out smaller, local factors, such as the lack of creative genres.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
