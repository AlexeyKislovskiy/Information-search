<p><b>Papiamento</b> (<span class="rt-commentedText nowrap"><small>English:  </small><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt" lang="en-fonipa"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˌ/: secondary stress follows">ˌ</span><span title="&#39;p&#39; in &#39;pie&#39;">p</span><span title="/ɑː/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;father&#39;">ɑː</span><span title="&#39;p&#39; in &#39;pie&#39;">p</span><span title="/i/: &#39;y&#39; in &#39;happy&#39;">i</span><span title="/ə/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;about&#39;">ə</span><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="&#39;m&#39; in &#39;my&#39;">m</span><span title="/ɛ/: &#39;e&#39; in &#39;dress&#39;">ɛ</span><span title="&#39;n&#39; in &#39;nigh&#39;">n</span><span title="&#39;t&#39; in &#39;tie&#39;">t</span><span title="/oʊ/: &#39;o&#39; in &#39;code&#39;">oʊ</span></span>/</a></span></span>)<sup id="cite_ref-lpd_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lpd-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> or <b>Papiamentu</b> (<span class="rt-commentedText nowrap"><small>English: </small><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt" lang="en-fonipa"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˌ/: secondary stress follows">ˌ</span><span title="&#39;p&#39; in &#39;pie&#39;">p</span><span title="/ɑː/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;father&#39;">ɑː</span><span title="&#39;p&#39; in &#39;pie&#39;">p</span><span title="/i/: &#39;y&#39; in &#39;happy&#39;">i</span><span title="/ə/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;about&#39;">ə</span><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="&#39;m&#39; in &#39;my&#39;">m</span><span title="/ɛ/: &#39;e&#39; in &#39;dress&#39;">ɛ</span><span title="&#39;n&#39; in &#39;nigh&#39;">n</span><span title="&#39;t&#39; in &#39;tie&#39;">t</span><span title="/uː/: &#39;oo&#39; in &#39;goose&#39;">uː</span></span>/</a></span></span>; <a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">Dutch</a>: <i lang="nl">Papiaments</i> <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="nl-Latn-fonipa"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Dutch" title="Help:IPA/Dutch">&#91;ˌpaːpijaːˈmɛnts&#93;</a></span>) is a <a href="/wiki/Portuguese-based_creole_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Portuguese-based creole language">Portuguese-based creole language</a> spoken in the <a href="/wiki/Dutch_Caribbean" title="Dutch Caribbean">Dutch Caribbean</a>. It is the most widely spoken language on the Caribbean <a href="/wiki/ABC_islands_(Leeward_Antilles)" title="ABC islands (Leeward Antilles)">ABC islands</a> (<a href="/wiki/Aruba" title="Aruba">Aruba</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bonaire" title="Bonaire">Bonaire</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cura%C3%A7ao" title="Curaçao">Curaçao</a>), with official status in Aruba and Curaçao.
</p><p>The language, spelled <span title="Papiamento-language text"><i lang="pap">Papiamento</i></span> in Aruba and <span title="Papiamento-language text"><i lang="pap">Papiamentu</i></span> in Bonaire and Curaçao, is largely based on <a href="/wiki/Portu%C3%B1ol" title="Portuñol">colonial-era Portuguese and Spanish</a> (including <a href="/wiki/Judaeo-Portuguese" title="Judaeo-Portuguese">Judaeo-Portuguese</a>), and has been influenced considerably by <a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">Dutch</a> and <a href="/wiki/Venezuelan_Spanish" title="Venezuelan Spanish">Venezuelan Spanish</a>. Due to <a href="/wiki/Comparison_of_Portuguese_and_Spanish" title="Comparison of Portuguese and Spanish">lexical similarities between Spanish and Portuguese</a>, it is difficult to pinpoint the exact origin of some words. Though there are different theories about its origins, most linguists now believe that Papiamento emerged from the Portuguese creole languages that developed in the West African coasts,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> as it has many similarities with <a href="/wiki/Cape_Verdean_Creole" title="Cape Verdean Creole">Cape Verdean Creole</a> and <a href="/wiki/Guinea-Bissau_Creole" title="Guinea-Bissau Creole">Guinea-Bissau Creole</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-papia_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-papia-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Papiamento&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Monument_and_crypt_in_Morada_Santa,_Bottelier.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/Monument_and_crypt_in_Morada_Santa%2C_Bottelier.jpg/220px-Monument_and_crypt_in_Morada_Santa%2C_Bottelier.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/Monument_and_crypt_in_Morada_Santa%2C_Bottelier.jpg/330px-Monument_and_crypt_in_Morada_Santa%2C_Bottelier.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/Monument_and_crypt_in_Morada_Santa%2C_Bottelier.jpg/440px-Monument_and_crypt_in_Morada_Santa%2C_Bottelier.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1600" data-file-height="1067" /></a><figcaption>Burial site and monument to Doctor <a href="/wiki/Moises_Frumencio_da_Costa_Gomez" title="Moises Frumencio da Costa Gomez">Moises Frumencio da Costa Gomez</a>, the first prime minister of the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands_Antilles" title="Netherlands Antilles">Netherlands Antilles</a>, with a message inscribed in Papiamento: <span title="Papiamento-language text"><i lang="pap">No hasi ku otro loke bo no ke pa otro hasi ku bo</i></span>, roughly meaning: "<a href="/wiki/Golden_Rule" title="Golden Rule">Do not do unto others what you do not want others do unto you</a>"</figcaption></figure>
<figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Catecismo_Corticu_-_Title_page.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/Catecismo_Corticu_-_Title_page.jpg/220px-Catecismo_Corticu_-_Title_page.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="305" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/Catecismo_Corticu_-_Title_page.jpg/330px-Catecismo_Corticu_-_Title_page.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/Catecismo_Corticu_-_Title_page.jpg/440px-Catecismo_Corticu_-_Title_page.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2401" data-file-height="3334" /></a><figcaption>Catecismo Corticu – the first printed book in Papiamento in 1837</figcaption></figure>
<p>There are various theories about the origin and development of the Papiamento language, and precise history has not been established. Its parent language is surely <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Romance_languages" title="Iberian Romance languages">Iberian</a>, but scholars disputed whether Papiamento was derived from Portuguese and its derived <a href="/wiki/Portuguese-based_creole_languages" title="Portuguese-based creole languages">Portuguese-based creole languages</a> or <a href="/wiki/Spanish-based_creole_languages" title="Spanish-based creole languages">from Spanish</a>. Historical constraints, core vocabulary, and grammatical features that Papiamento shares with <a href="/wiki/Cape_Verdean_Creole" title="Cape Verdean Creole">Cape Verdean Creole</a> and <a href="/wiki/Guinea-Bissau_Creole" title="Guinea-Bissau Creole">Guinea-Bissau Creole</a> are far less than those shared with Spanish, even though the Spanish and Dutch influences occurred later, from the 17th century onwards. Jacoba Bouschoute conducted a study on the various Dutch influences in Papiamento. An example of a such word is <span title="Papiamento-language text"><i lang="pap">verfdó</i></span><i>,</i> which is a combination of a Dutch root <span title="Dutch-language text"><i lang="nl">verf</i></span> (meaning "paint") and the Spanish suffix <i>-dor</i> (used for a person who performs an action, like "painter") The transformation from <span title="Dutch-language text"><i lang="nl">verver</i></span> to <span title="Papiamento-language text"><i lang="pap">verfdó</i></span> involved shortening the <i>-r</i> to -dó due to a linguisitic process called <a href="/wiki/Apocopation" class="mw-redirect" title="Apocopation">apocopation</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The name of the language itself originates from <i lang="pt"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/papia#Portuguese" class="extiw" title="wikt:papia">papia</a></i>, from Portuguese and Cape Verdean Creole <i lang="pt"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/papear#Portuguese" class="extiw" title="wikt:papear">papear</a></i> ("to chat, say, speak, talk"), added by the noun-forming suffix <i lang="pt"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/-mento#Portuguese" class="extiw" title="wikt:-mento">-mento</a></i>.
</p><p>Spain claimed dominion over the islands in the 15th century but made little use of them. Portuguese merchants had been trading extensively in the West Indies and with the <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Union" title="Iberian Union">Iberian Union</a> between Portugal and Spain during 1580–1640 period, their trade extended to the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_West_Indies" title="Spanish West Indies">Spanish West Indies</a>. In 1634, the <a href="/wiki/Dutch_West_India_Company" title="Dutch West India Company">Dutch West India Company</a> (WIC) took possession of the islands, deporting most of the small remaining <a href="/wiki/Arawak_peoples" class="mw-redirect" title="Arawak peoples">Arawak</a> and Spanish population to the continent (mostly to the Venezuelan west coast and the Venezuelan plains, as well as all the way east to the Venezuela Orinoco basin and Trinidad), and turned them into the hub of the Dutch slave trade between Africa and the Caribbean.
</p><p>The first evidence of widespread use of Papiamento in Aruba and Curaçao can be seen in official documents in the early 18th century. In the 19th century, most materials in the islands were written in Papiamento including <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholic">Roman Catholic</a> school books and hymnals. In 1837, the <i>Catecismo Corticu pa uso di catolicanan di Curaçao</i> was printed, the first printed book in Papiamento. In 2009 the Catecismo Corticu was added to the <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a> <a href="/wiki/Memory_of_the_World" class="mw-redirect" title="Memory of the World">Memory of the World</a> register.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The first Papiamento newspaper was published in 1871 and was called <i>Civilisadó</i> (The Civilizer).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Local_development_theory">Local development theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Papiamento&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Local development theory"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>One local development theory proposes that Papiamento developed in the Caribbean from an original Portuguese-African <a href="/wiki/Pidgin" title="Pidgin">pidgin</a>, with later Dutch and Spanish (and even some Arawak) influences.
</p><p>Another theory is that Papiamento first evolved from the use in the region since 1499 of 'lenguas' and the first repopulation of the ABC Islands by the Spanish by the Cédula real decreed in November 1525 in which Juan Martinez de Ampués, factor of Española, had been granted the right to repopulate the depopulated Islas inútiles of Oroba, Islas de los Gigantes, and Buon Aire.
</p><p>The evolution of Papiamento continued under the Dutch colonisation under the influence of 16th-century Dutch, Portuguese (Brazilian) and Native American languages (Arawak and Taíno), with the second repopulation of the ABC islands with immigrants who arrived from the ex-Dutch Brazilian colonies.
</p><p>The Judaeo-Portuguese population of the ABC islands increased substantially after 1654, when the Portuguese recovered the <a href="/wiki/Dutch_Brazil" title="Dutch Brazil">Dutch-held territories in Northeast</a> <a href="/wiki/Colonial_Brazil" title="Colonial Brazil">Brazil</a>, causing most Portuguese-speaking Jews and their Portuguese-speaking Dutch allies and Dutch-speaking Portuguese Brazilian allies in those lands to flee from religious persecution. The precise role of <a href="/wiki/Sephardic_Jews" title="Sephardic Jews">Sephardic Jews</a> in the early development is unclear, but <a href="/wiki/Jews" title="Jews">Jews</a> certainly played a prominent role in the later development of Papiamento. Many early residents of Curaçao were Sephardic Jews from Portugal, Spain, Cape Verde or Portuguese Brazil. Also, after the <a href="/wiki/Eighty_Years%27_War" title="Eighty Years&#39; War">Eighty Years' War</a>, a group of Sephardic Jews immigrated from <a href="/wiki/Amsterdam" title="Amsterdam">Amsterdam</a>. Therefore, it can be assumed that <a href="/wiki/Judaeo-Portuguese" title="Judaeo-Portuguese">Judaeo-Portuguese</a> was brought to the island of Curaçao, where it gradually spread to other parts of the community. The Jewish community became the prime merchants and traders in the area and so business and everyday trading was conducted in Papiamento. While various nations owned the island, and official languages changed with ownership, Papiamento became the constant language of the residents.
</p><p>When the Netherlands opened economic ties with Spanish colonies in what are now Venezuela and Colombia in the 18th century<sup id="cite_ref-Putte_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Putte-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> students on Curaçao, Aruba, and Bonaire were taught predominantly in Spanish, and Spanish began to influence the creole language.<sup id="cite_ref-papia_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-papia-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
Since there was a continuous Latinisation process (Hoetink, 1987), even the elite Dutch-Protestant settlers eventually communicated better in Spanish than in Dutch, as a wealth of local Spanish-language publications in the 19th century testify.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="European_and_African_origin_theory">European and African origin theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Papiamento&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: European and African origin theory"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>According to the European and African origin theory the origins of Papiamento lie in the Afro-Portuguese creoles that arose in the 16th century in the west coast of Africa and in the <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Cape_Verde" title="Portuguese Cape Verde">Portuguese Cape Verde</a> islands. From the 16th to the late 17th centuries, most of the slaves taken to the Caribbean came from Portuguese <a href="/wiki/Factory_(trading_post)" title="Factory (trading post)">trading posts</a> (<span title="Portuguese-language text"><i lang="pt">feitorias</i></span>, <abbr title="translation">transl.</abbr><span>&#8201;factories</span>) in those regions. Around those ports, several Portuguese-African pidgin and creole languages developed, such as <a href="/wiki/Cape_Verdean_Creole" title="Cape Verdean Creole">Cape Verdean Creole</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guinea-Bissau_Creole" title="Guinea-Bissau Creole">Guinea-Bissau Creole</a>, <a href="/wiki/Angolar_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Angolar language">Angolar</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Forro_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Forro language">Forro</a> (from São Tomé).<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The sister languages bear strong resemblance with Papiamento. According to this theory, Papiamento was derived from one or more of these older creoles or their predecessors, which were brought to the ABC islands by slaves and traders from Cape Verde and West Africa.
</p><p>The similarity between Papiamento and the other Afro-Portuguese creoles can be seen in the same pronouns used, <span title="Papiamento-language text"><i lang="pap">mi</i></span>, <span title="Papiamento-language text"><i lang="pap">bo</i></span>, <span title="Papiamento-language text"><i lang="pap">el</i></span>, <span title="Papiamento-language text"><i lang="pap">nos</i></span>, <span title="Papiamento-language text"><i lang="pap">bos(o)</i></span>, being Portuguese-based.
Afro-Portuguese creoles often have a shift from "v" to "b" and from "o" to "u": <span title="Papiamento-language text"><i lang="pap">bientu</i></span> (<abbr title="translation">transl.</abbr><span>&#8201;wind</span>), instead of <span title="Papiamento-language text"><i lang="pap">viento</i></span>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="Are these sound changes or spelling changes? Use IPA for sound and angle brackets for spelling. (July 2022)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> In creole and also in Spanish, <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;v&#x27e9;</span> and <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;v&#x27e9;</span> are pronounced the same. In creole, it is also written as a <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;b&#x27e9;</span>. Just as in Portuguese, an unaccented final <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;o&#x27e9;</span> is pronounced as <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/u/</span>.
</p><p>Guene (the name comes from "Guinea") was a secret language that was used by slaves on the plantations of the landhouses of West Curaçao.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> There were about a hundred Guene songs that were sung to make the work lighter.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> However, because of the secret character of Guene, it never had much influence on Papiamento.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Linguistic_and_historical_ties_with_Upper_Guinea_Portuguese_Creole">Linguistic and historical ties with Upper Guinea Portuguese Creole</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Papiamento&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Linguistic and historical ties with Upper Guinea Portuguese Creole"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Since the late 1990s, research has been done that shines light on the ties between Papiamento and Upper Guinea Portuguese Creole. Martinus (1996), Quint (2000)<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> and Jacobs (2008,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJacobs2008_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJacobs2008-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> 2009a,<sup id="cite_ref-academia1_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-academia1-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> 2009b<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>) focus specifically on the linguistic and historical relationships with the Upper Guinea Portuguese Creole, as spoken on the Santiago island of Cape Verde and in Guinea-Bissau and Casamance.   
</p><p>In Bart Jacob's study <i>The Upper Guinea Origins of Papiamento</i><sup id="cite_ref-academia1_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-academia1-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> he defends the hypothesis that Papiamento is a <a href="/wiki/Relexification" title="Relexification">relexified</a> offshoot of an early Upper Guinea Portuguese Creole variety that was transferred from Senegambia to Curaçao in the second half of the 17th century, when the Dutch controlled the island of <a href="/wiki/Gor%C3%A9e" title="Gorée">Gorée</a>, a slave trading stronghold off the coast of <a href="/wiki/Senegal" title="Senegal">Senegal</a>. The Creole was used for communication among slaves and between slaves and slave holders.
</p><p>On Curaçao, this variety underwent internal changes as well as contact-induced changes at all levels of the grammar, but particularly in the lexicon, due to contact with Spanish and, to a lesser extent, Dutch. Despite the changes, the morphosyntactic framework of Papiamento is still remarkably close to that of the Upper Guinea Creoles of Cape Verde and Guinea-Bissau. Parallels have also been identified between the development of Papiamento and <a href="/wiki/Catholicism" class="mw-redirect" title="Catholicism">Catholicism</a>.)<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> 
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Present_status">Present status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Papiamento&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Present status"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Papiamento is spoken in all aspects of society throughout <a href="/wiki/Aruba" title="Aruba">Aruba</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cura%C3%A7ao" title="Curaçao">Curaçao</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bonaire" title="Bonaire">Bonaire</a>. 
</p><p>Papiamento has been an official language of <a href="/wiki/Aruba" title="Aruba">Aruba</a> since May 2003.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> In the former <a href="/wiki/Netherlands_Antilles" title="Netherlands Antilles">Netherlands Antilles</a>, Papiamento was made an official language on 7 March 2007.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> After the <a href="/wiki/Dissolution_of_the_Netherlands_Antilles" title="Dissolution of the Netherlands Antilles">dissolution of the Netherlands Antilles</a>, Papiamento's official status was confirmed in the newly formed <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Netherlands" title="Caribbean Netherlands">Caribbean Netherlands</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> Also, 150,000 Antillians (mostly from Curaçao) live in the Netherlands and speak their mother language, Papiamento, fluently. Some Papiamento is also spoken on <a href="/wiki/Sint_Maarten" title="Sint Maarten">Sint Maarten</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Paraguan%C3%A1_Peninsula" title="Paraguaná Peninsula">Paraguaná Peninsula</a> of <a href="/wiki/Venezuela" title="Venezuela">Venezuela</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Venezuelan_Spanish" title="Venezuelan Spanish">Venezuelan Spanish</a> and <a href="/wiki/American_English" title="American English">American English</a> are constant influences today. <a href="/wiki/Code-switching" title="Code-switching">Code-switching</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lexical_borrowing" class="mw-redirect" title="Lexical borrowing">lexical borrowing</a> from Spanish, Dutch and English among native speakers is common. This is considered as a threat to the development of the language because of the loss of the authentic and Creole "feel" of Papiamento.
</p><p>Many immigrants from Latin America and the Caribbean choose to learn Papiamento because it is more practical in daily life on the islands. For Spanish-speakers, it is easier to learn than Dutch, because Papiamento uses many Spanish and Portuguese words.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The first <a href="/wiki/Opera" title="Opera">opera</a> in Papiamento, adapted by <a href="/w/index.php?title=Carel_De_Haseth&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Carel De Haseth (page does not exist)">Carel De Haseth</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;">&#160;&#91;<a href="https://nl.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carel_De_Haseth" class="extiw" title="nl:Carel De Haseth">nl</a>&#93;</span> from his novel <i>Katibu di Shon</i>, was performed at the <a href="/wiki/Stadsschouwburg" title="Stadsschouwburg">Stadsschouwburg</a> in Amsterdam on 1 July 2013, commemorating the 150th anniversary of the ending of slavery in the Dutch Caribbean.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Old_Papiamento_texts">Old Papiamento texts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Papiamento&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Old Papiamento texts"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Papiamento language originates from about 1650. The oldest Papiamento texts that have been preserved are written letters. In the following three letters it can be seen that the words changed and the spelling became closer to the Dutch spelling. Although some words are no longer in use, the basis of Papiamento did not change much.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Orthography_and_spelling">Orthography and spelling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Papiamento&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Orthography and spelling"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1033289096">.mw-parser-output .hatnote{font-style:italic}.mw-parser-output div.hatnote{padding-left:1.6em;margin-bottom:0.5em}.mw-parser-output .hatnote i{font-style:normal}.mw-parser-output .hatnote+link+.hatnote{margin-top:-0.5em}</style><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Papiamento_orthography" title="Papiamento orthography">Papiamento orthography</a></div>
<p>Papiamento is written using the <a href="/wiki/Latin_script" title="Latin script">Latin script</a>.
</p><p>Since the 1970s, two different orthographies have been developed and adopted. In 1976, Curaçao and Bonaire officially adopted the Römer-Maduro-Jonis version, a phonetic spelling. In 1977, Aruba approved a more <a href="/wiki/Etymology" title="Etymology">etymology</a>-based spelling, presented by the Comision di Ortografia (Orthography Commission), presided by Jossy Mansur.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_dialects">Distribution and dialects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Papiamento&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Distribution and dialects"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Papiamento has two main dialects, one in <a href="/wiki/Languages_of_Aruba" title="Languages of Aruba">Aruba</a> and one in <a href="/wiki/Languages_of_Cura%C3%A7ao" class="mw-redirect" title="Languages of Curaçao">Curaçao</a> and <a href="/wiki/Languages_of_Bonaire" class="mw-redirect" title="Languages of Bonaire">Bonaire</a> (Papiamentu), with lexical and intonational differences.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> There are also minor differences between Curaçao and Bonaire.
</p><p>The most apparent difference between the two dialects is given away in the name difference. Whereas Bonaire and Curaçao opted for a phonology-based spelling, Aruba uses an etymology-based spelling. Many words in Aruba end with "o" while the same word ends with "u" in Bonaire and Curaçao. And even in Curaçao, the use of the u-ending is still more pronounced among the Sephardic Jewish population. Similarly, the use of "k" in Bonaire and Curaçao replaces "c" in Aruba.
</p><p>For example:
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Stress_and_accent">Stress and accent</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Papiamento&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Stress and accent"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Stress is very important in Papiamento. Many words have a very different meaning when a different stress is used:
</p>
<ul><li>When both syllables are equally stressed, <span title="Papiamento-language text"><i lang="pap">kome</i></span>, it means "to eat".</li>
<li>When the first syllable is stressed, <span title="Papiamento-language text"><i lang="pap"><b>ko</b>me</i></span>, it means "eat!" (imperative).</li>
<li>However, <span title="Papiamento-language text"><i lang="pap">ko<b>m'é</b></i></span> (short for <span title="Papiamento-language text"><i lang="pap">kome é</i></span>) means "eat it!"</li></ul>
<p>There are general rules for the stress and accent but also a great many exceptions.
When a word deviates from the rules, the stressed vowel is indicated by an acute accent ( ´ ), but it is often omitted in casual writing.
</p><p>The main rules are:<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> 
</p>
<ul><li>When a word ends in a vowel (a, e, i, o, u), the stress is placed upon the penultimate (before last) syllable: <span title="Papiamento-language text"><i lang="pap">bu<b>ri</b>ku</i></span> ("donkey").</li>
<li>When a word ends not in a vowel, but with a consonant, the stress is placed upon the last syllable: <span title="Papiamento-language text"><i lang="pap">hospi<b>tal</b></i></span>.</li>
<li>When a verb has two syllables, the syllables are about equally stressed: <span title="Papiamento-language text"><i lang="pap">sòru</i></span> ("to care"), <span title="Papiamento-language text"><i lang="pap">falta</i></span> ("to lack").</li>
<li>When a verb has more than two syllables, the stress is laid upon the last syllable: <span title="Papiamento-language text"><i lang="pap">kontes<b>tá</b></i></span> ("to answer"), <span title="Papiamento-language text"><i lang="pap">primin<b>tí</b></i></span> ("to promise").</li></ul>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vocabulary">Vocabulary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Papiamento&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Vocabulary"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Most of the vocabulary is derived from Portuguese and
derived Portuguese-based creoles and (Early Modern) Spanish. The real origin is usually difficult to tell because the two Iberian languages are very similar, and adaptations were made in Papiamento. A list of 200 basic Papiamento words can be found in the standard <a href="/wiki/Swadesh_list" title="Swadesh list">Swadesh list</a>, with etymological reference to the language of origin.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> There is a remarkable similarity between words in Papiamento, Cape Verdean Creole, and Guinea-Bissau Creole, which all belong to the same language family of the <a href="/wiki/Portuguese-based_creole_languages" title="Portuguese-based creole languages">Upper Guinea Creoles</a>. Most of the words can be connected with their Portuguese origin.
</p><p>Linguistic studies have shown that roughly 80% of the words in Papiamento's present vocabulary are of <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula" title="Iberian Peninsula">Iberian</a> origin, 20% are of Dutch origin, and some of Native American or African origin. A study by Van Buurt and Joubert inventoried the words of Taíno and <a href="/wiki/Caquet%C3%ADo_language" title="Caquetío language">Caquetío</a> Arawak origin, mostly words for plants and animals.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEvan_BuurtJoubert1997_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEvan_BuurtJoubert1997-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> Arawak is an extinct language that was spoken by Indians throughout the Caribbean. The Arawak words were re-introduced in Papiamento by borrowing from the Spanish dialect of Venezuela<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Many words are of Iberian origin, and it is impossible to label them as either Portuguese or Spanish:
</p>
<ul><li><span title="Papiamento-language text"><i lang="pap">por fabor</i></span> ("please") – Portuguese: <span title="Spanish-language text"><i lang="es">por favor</i></span> – Spanish: <span title="Portuguese-language text"><i lang="pt">por favor</i></span></li>
<li><span title="Papiamento-language text"><i lang="pap">señora</i></span> ("madam") – Portuguese: <span title="Spanish-language text"><i lang="es">senhora</i></span> – Spanish: <span title="Portuguese-language text"><i lang="pt">señora</i></span></li>
<li><span title="Papiamento-language text"><i lang="pap">kua</i></span> ("which") – Portuguese: <span title="Spanish-language text"><i lang="es">qual</i></span> – Spanish: <span title="Portuguese-language text"><i lang="pt">cuál</i></span></li>
<li><span title="Papiamento-language text"><i lang="pap">kuantu</i></span> ("how much") – Portuguese: <span title="Spanish-language text"><i lang="es">quanto</i></span> – Spanish: <span title="Portuguese-language text"><i lang="pt">cuánto</i></span></li></ul>
<p>While the presence of word-final <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/u/</span> can easily be traced to Portuguese, the <a href="/wiki/Diphthongisation" class="mw-redirect" title="Diphthongisation">diphthongisation</a> of some vowels is characteristic of Spanish. The use of <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/b/</span>, rather than <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/v/</span>, descends from its pronunciation in the dialects of northern Portugal as well as of <a href="/wiki/Spanish_phonology" title="Spanish phonology">Spanish</a>. Also, a sound shift may have occurred in the direction of Spanish, whose influence on Papiamento came later than that of Portuguese: <span title="Papiamento-language text"><i lang="pap">subrino</i></span> ("nephew"): <span title="Portuguese-language text"><i lang="pt">sobrinho</i></span> in Portuguese, <span title="Spanish-language text"><i lang="es">sobrino</i></span> in Spanish. The pronunciation of <span title="Papiamento-language text"><i lang="pap">o</i></span> as <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/u/</span> is certainly Portuguese, but the use of <span title="Papiamento-language text"><i lang="pap">n</i></span> instead of <span title="Portuguese-language text"><i lang="pt">nh</i></span> (<span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ɲ/</span>) in the ending <span title="Papiamento-language text"><i lang="pap">-no</i></span> is from Spanish.
</p><p>Few Portuguese words come directly from Portuguese, but most come via the Portuguese-based creole; in the examples below, the Cape Verdean Creole equivalents are <span title="Kabuverdianu-language text"><i lang="kea">borboléta</i></span>, <span title="Kabuverdianu-language text"><i lang="kea">katchor</i></span>, <span title="Kabuverdianu-language text"><i lang="kea">prétu</i></span> and <span title="Kabuverdianu-language text"><i lang="kea">fórsa</i></span>.
</p><p>Portuguese-origin words:
</p>
<ul><li><span title="Papiamento-language text"><i lang="pap">barbulètè</i></span> ("butterfly") – Portuguese: <span title="Portuguese-language text"><i lang="pt">borboleta</i></span></li>
<li><span title="Papiamento-language text"><i lang="pap">kachó</i></span> ("dog") – Portuguese: <span title="Portuguese-language text"><i lang="pt">cachorro</i></span></li>

<li><span title="Papiamento-language text"><i lang="pap">forsa</i></span> ("power") – Portuguese: <span title="Portuguese-language text"><i lang="pt">força</i></span></li></ul>
<p>Spanish-origin words:
</p>
<ul><li><span title="Papiamento-language text"><i lang="pap">siudat</i></span> ("city") – Spanish: <span title="Spanish-language text"><i lang="es">ciudad</i></span></li>
<li><span title="Papiamento-language text"><i lang="pap">sombré</i></span> ("hat") – Spanish: <span title="Spanish-language text"><i lang="es">sombrero</i></span></li>

<p>Dutch-origin words:
</p>
<ul><li><span title="Papiamento-language text"><i lang="pap">apel</i></span> ("apple") – Dutch: <span title="Dutch-language text"><i lang="nl">appel</i></span></li>
<li><span title="Papiamento-language text"><i lang="pap">buki</i></span> ("book") – Dutch: <span title="Dutch-language text"><i lang="nl">boek</i></span></li>
<li><span title="Papiamento-language text"><i lang="pap">lesa</i></span> ("to read") – Dutch: <span title="Dutch-language text"><i lang="nl">lezen</i></span></li>
<li><span title="Papiamento-language text"><i lang="pap">mart</i></span> ("March") – Dutch: <span title="Dutch-language text"><i lang="nl">maart</i></span></li></ul>
<p>English-origin words:
</p>
<ul><li><span title="Papiamento-language text"><i lang="pap">bèk</i></span> – English: back</li>
<li><span title="Papiamento-language text"><i lang="pap">bòter</i></span> – English: bottle</li>
<li><span title="Papiamento-language text"><i lang="pap">baiskel</i></span> – English: bicycle</li></ul>
<p>African-origin words:
</p>
<ul><li><span title="Papiamento-language text"><i lang="pap">pinda</i></span> ("peanut") – Kongo: <span title="Kongo-language text"><i lang="kg">mpinda</i></span></li>
<li><span title="Papiamento-language text"><i lang="pap">makamba</i></span> ("white man") – Bantu: <span title="Bantu languages collective text"><i lang="bnt">ma-kamba</i></span></li>

<p>Native American-origin words:

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Literature_and_culture">Literature and culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Papiamento&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Literature and culture"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Aruba and Bonaire's national anthems are in Papiamento, "<a href="/wiki/Aruba_Dushi_Tera" title="Aruba Dushi Tera">Aruba Dushi Tera</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Tera_di_Solo_y_suave_biento" title="Tera di Solo y suave biento">Tera di Solo y suave biento</a>" respectively. The newspaper <i><a href="/wiki/Diario_(Aruba)" title="Diario (Aruba)">Diario</a></i> is also in the language.
</p><p>The 2013 films <i><a href="/wiki/Abo_So" title="Abo So">Abo So</a></i> (Aruba) and <i><a href="/wiki/Sensei_Redenshon" title="Sensei Redenshon">Sensei Redenshon</a></i> (Curaçao) were the first <a href="/wiki/Feature_film" title="Feature film">feature films</a> in Papiamento; the comedy <i><a href="/wiki/Bon_Bini_Holland" title="Bon Bini Holland">Bon Bini Holland</a></i> (Curaçao and Netherlands) also contains some Papiamento.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Expressions">Expressions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Papiamento&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Expressions"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li><span title="Papiamento-language text"><i lang="pap">Hopi skuma, tiki chukulati</i></span> (A lot of foam, little chocolate): Too good to be true.</li>
<li><span title="Papiamento-language text"><i lang="pap">Einan e porko su rabo ta krul</i></span> (That is where the pig's tail curls): That is where the problem lies.</li>
<li><span title="Papiamento-language text"><i lang="pap">Sopi pura ta sali salo</i></span> (Quick soup turns salty): Good things take time.</li>
<li><span title="Papiamento-language text"><i lang="pap">E ke bula ku ala di manteka</i></span> (He wants to fly with wings of butter): He wants to do more than he can handle.</li>
<li><span title="Papiamento-language text"><i lang="pap">Ora dia ta serka di habri, nochi ta mas skur</i></span> (Just before dawn, the night is darkest): When need is greatest, salvation is near.</li></ul>
