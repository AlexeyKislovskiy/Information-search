Core Yuman<ul style="line-height:100%;margin-left:0.45em;padding-left:0;"><li>Pai<ul style="line-height:100%;margin-left:0.45em;padding-left:0;"><li><b>Yavapai</b></li></ul></li></ul></li></ul></div></td></tr><tr><th colspan="2" class="infobox-header" style="color: black; background-color:  #c0dde6;">Language codes</th></tr><tr><th scope="row" class="infobox-label" style="white-space:nowrap;padding-right:0.65em;"><span class="nowrap"><a href="/wiki/ISO_639-3" title="ISO 639-3">ISO 639-3</a></span></th><td class="infobox-data" style="line-height:1.3em;"><code><a href="https://iso639-3.sil.org/code/yuf" class="extiw" title="iso639-3:yuf">yuf</a></code>  Havasupai‑Walapai‑Yavapai</td></tr><tr><th scope="row" class="infobox-label" style="white-space:nowrap;padding-right:0.65em;"><i><a href="/wiki/Glottolog" title="Glottolog">Glottolog</a></i></th><td class="infobox-data" style="line-height:1.3em;"><code><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://glottolog.org/resource/languoid/id/hava1248">hava1248</a></code>&#160; Havasupai‑Walapai‑Yavapai<br /><code><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://glottolog.org/resource/languoid/id/yava1252">yava1252</a></code>&#160; Yavapai</td></tr></tbody></table>
<p><b>Yavapai</b> is an <a href="/wiki/Upland_Yuman_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Upland Yuman language">Upland Yuman language</a>, spoken by <a href="/wiki/Yavapai_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Yavapai people">Yavapai people</a> in central and western <a href="/wiki/Arizona" title="Arizona">Arizona</a>. There are four dialects: Kwevkepaya, Wipukpaya, Tolkepaya, and Yavepe. Linguistic studies of the Kwevkepaya (Southern), Tolkepaya (Western), Wipukepa (Verde Valley), and Yavepe (Prescott) dialects have been published (Mithun 1999:578).
</p><p>Yavapai was once spoken across much of north-central and western Arizona, but is now mostly spoken on the Yavapai reservations at <a href="/wiki/Fort_McDowell_Yavapai_Nation" title="Fort McDowell Yavapai Nation">Fort McDowell</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Yavapai-Apache_Nation" class="mw-redirect" title="Yavapai-Apache Nation">Verde Valley</a> and <a href="/wiki/Yavapai-Prescott_Tribe" title="Yavapai-Prescott Tribe">Prescott</a>.
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geographic_distribution">Geographic distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yavapai_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geographic distribution"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The rate of mutual comprehension between <a href="/wiki/Yavapai_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Yavapai people">Yavapai</a> and <a href="/wiki/Havasupai%E2%80%93Hualapai_language" title="Havasupai–Hualapai language">Havasupai–Hualapai</a> is similar to that between <a href="/wiki/Mohave_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Mohave language">Mohave</a> and <a href="/wiki/Maricopa_language" title="Maricopa language">Maricopa</a> (Biggs 1957).
</p><p>Warren Gazzam, a Tolkapaya speaker, reported that "you know they (Hualapais) speak the same language as we do, some words or accents are a little different".<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Due to extensive cultural interchange, many Yavapai were once bilingual in <a href="/wiki/Southern_Athabaskan_languages" title="Southern Athabaskan languages">Apache</a>, and some Apache were bilingual in Yavapai.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Unlike in Havasupai and Hualapai, postaspirated stops cannot appear in word-initial position (Shaterian 1983:215).
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Syntax">Syntax</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yavapai_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Syntax"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Yavapai is a subject-verb-object language.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id=".2F-k.2F_and_.2F-m.2F_Problem"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="/-k/_and_/-m/_Problem">/-k/ and /-m/ Problem</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yavapai_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: /-k/ and /-m/ Problem"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>According to Martha Kendall, the morphemes /k/ and /m/ are "semantically contrastable," but are pronounced the same. She writes that <a href="/wiki/Homophone" title="Homophone">homophony</a> is present in Yavapai, and /k/ and /m/ are similar in phonological situations, but are syntactically different.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preservation_efforts">Preservation efforts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yavapai_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Preservation efforts"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There have been recordings of Yavapai (as well as other Yuman languages) done in 1974, relating to phonology, syntax, and grammar. This was meant to understand the three topics better and to hear them.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>There is an effort to revitalize the language. There is a Yavapai language program for adults to learn the language and pass on to future generations.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>There have been attempts to save the language in the Yavapai community.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Poetry and stories have been published in Yavapai on several occasions. Yavapai poems are featured in <i>Gigyayk Vo'jka</i>, the anthology of poetry in Yuman languages edited by <a href="/wiki/Hualapai_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Hualapai people">Hualapai</a> linguist <a href="/wiki/Lucille_Watahomigie" title="Lucille Watahomigie">Lucille Watahomigie</a>. Yavapai stories also appear in <i>Spirit Mountain: An Anthology of Yuman Story and Song</i>. Both works are accompanied by English translations, and the poems in Gigyayk Vo'jka also feature a morphological analysis.
</p><p>Alan Shaterian has published a dictionary of Northeastern Yavapai. <a href="/wiki/Pamela_Munro" title="Pamela Munro">Pamela Munro</a> is working<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (October 2019)">when?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> on a dictionary and grammar for Tolkepaya.
</p>
