<p><b>Slavey</b> (<span class="rt-commentedText nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt" lang="en-fonipa"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="&#39;s&#39; in &#39;sigh&#39;">s</span><span title="&#39;l&#39; in &#39;lie&#39;">l</span><span title="/eɪ/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;face&#39;">eɪ</span><span title="&#39;v&#39; in &#39;vie&#39;">v</span><span title="/i/: &#39;y&#39; in &#39;happy&#39;">i</span></span>/</a></span></span>;<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>  also <b>Slave</b>, <b>Slavé</b>) is a group of <a href="/wiki/Athabaskan_languages" title="Athabaskan languages">Athabaskan languages</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Dialect_continuum" title="Dialect continuum">dialect continuum</a> spoken amongst the <a href="/wiki/Dene" title="Dene">Dene</a> peoples of <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Northwest_Territories" title="Northwest Territories">Northwest Territories</a> – or central Denendeh – where it also has official status.<sup id="cite_ref-lang_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lang-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The languages are primarily written using a modified <a href="/wiki/Latin_script" title="Latin script">Latin script</a>, with some using <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Aboriginal_syllabics" title="Canadian Aboriginal syllabics">Canadian Aboriginal syllabics</a>. In their own languages, these languages are referred to as: <b>Sahtúgot’įné Yatı̨́</b> (spoken by the <a href="/wiki/Sahtu" title="Sahtu">Sahtu Dene</a>), <b>K’ashógot’įne Goxedǝ́</b> (the Hare Dene dialect) and <b>Shíhgot’įne Yatı̨́</b> (the Mountain dialect) in the North, and <b>Dené Dháh</b> (primarily by the <a href="/wiki/Dene_Tha%27_First_Nation" title="Dene Tha&#39; First Nation">Dene Tha'</a> in <a href="/wiki/Alberta" title="Alberta">Alberta</a>), <b>Dene Yatıé</b> or <b>Dene Zhatıé</b> in the South.
</p>

Locator.svg">class=notpageimage| </a></div><a href="/wiki/Sahtu_Region" title="Sahtu Region">Sahtu</a> (North Slavey) communities in the Northwest Territories</div></div></div><p><b>North Slavey</b> is spoken by the <a href="/wiki/Sahtu" title="Sahtu">Sahtu (North Slavey)</a> people in the <a href="/wiki/District_of_Mackenzie" title="District of Mackenzie">Mackenzie District</a> along the middle <a href="/wiki/Mackenzie_River" title="Mackenzie River">Mackenzie River</a> from <a href="/wiki/Tulita" title="Tulita">Tulita</a> (Fort Norman) north, around <a href="/wiki/Great_Bear_Lake" title="Great Bear Lake">Great Bear Lake</a>, and in the <a href="/wiki/Mackenzie_Mountains" title="Mackenzie Mountains">Mackenzie Mountains</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_territory" class="mw-redirect" title="Canadian territory">Canadian territory</a> of <a href="/wiki/Northwest_Territories" title="Northwest Territories">Northwest Territories</a>. The dialect has around 800 speakers.<sup id="cite_ref-census_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-census-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Yellowknife_text.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Yellowknife_text.jpg/220px-Yellowknife_text.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Yellowknife_text.jpg/330px-Yellowknife_text.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Yellowknife_text.jpg/440px-Yellowknife_text.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3072" data-file-height="2304" /></a><figcaption>North Slavey text carved into stone in Yellowknife</figcaption></figure>
<p>Northern Slavey is an amalgamation of three separate <i>dialects</i>: 
</p>
<ul><li>K’ashógot’įne (<span title="North Slavey-language text"><span lang="scs-Cans">ᑲᑊᗱᑯᑎᑊᓀ</span></span>) Goxedǝ́: Hare, spoken by the <span title="North Slavey-language text"><span lang="scs" style="font-style: normal;">Gahwié got’iné</span></span> - "Rabbitskin People" or <span title="North Slavey-language text"><span lang="scs" style="font-style: normal;">K’áshogot’ıne</span></span> - "Great Hare People", referring to their dependence on the varying hare for food and clothing, also called <span title="French-language text"><span lang="fr" style="font-style: normal;">Peaux de Lièvre</span></span> or <span title="French-language text"><span lang="fr" style="font-style: normal;">Locheaux</span></span></li>
<li>Sahtúgot’įné (<span title="North Slavey-language text"><span lang="scs-Cans">ᓴᑋᕲᒼᑯᑎᑊᓀ</span></span>) Yatı̨́: Bear Lake, spoken by the <span title="North Slavey-language text"><span lang="scs" style="font-style: normal;">Sahtu Dene</span></span> or <span title="North Slavey-language text"><span lang="scs" style="font-style: normal;">Sahtú got’iné</span></span> - "Bear Lake People", also known as <span title="French-language text"><span lang="fr" style="font-style: normal;">Gens du Lac d'Ours</span></span></li>
<li>Shíhgot’įne (<span title="North Slavey-language text"><span lang="scs-Cans">ᗰᑋᑯᑎᑊᓀ</span></span>) Yatı̨́: Mountain, spoken by the <span title="North Slavey-language text"><span lang="scs" style="font-style: normal;">Shıhgot’ıné, Shuhtaot'iné</span></span> or <span title="North Slavey-language text"><span lang="scs" style="font-style: normal;">Shotah Dene</span></span> - "Mountain People" or <i>Mountain Indians</i>, also called <span title="North Slavey-language text"><span lang="scs" style="font-style: normal;">Nahagot’iné, Nahaa</span></span> or <span title="North Slavey-language text"><span lang="scs" style="font-style: normal;">Nahane Dene</span></span> - "People of the west", so called because they lived in the mountains west of the other Slavey groups, between the Mackenzie Mountains and the Mackenzie River, from the Redstone River to the Mountain River</li></ul>
<p><b>South Slavey</b> (<span title="South Slavey-language text"><span lang="xsl-Cans">ᑌᓀ ᒐ</span></span> <span title="South Slavey-language text"><span lang="xsl" style="font-style: normal;"><a href="/wiki/Dene_Tha%27_First_Nation" title="Dene Tha&#39; First Nation">Dené Dháh</a>, Dene Yatıé</span></span> or <span title="South Slavey-language text"><span lang="xsl" style="font-style: normal;">Dene Zhatıé</span></span>) is spoken by the <a href="/wiki/Slavey" title="Slavey">Slavey</a> people, who were also known as <span title="South Slavey-language text"><span lang="xsl" style="font-style: normal;">Dehghaot'ine, Deh Cho, Etchareottine</span></span> - "People Dwelling in the Shelter", in the region of <a href="/wiki/Great_Slave_Lake" title="Great Slave Lake">Great Slave Lake</a>, upper Mackenzie River (<span title="South Slavey-language text"><span lang="xsl" style="font-style: normal;">Deh Cho</span></span> - "Big River") and its drainage, in the District of Mackenzie, northeast <a href="/wiki/Alberta" title="Alberta">Alberta</a>, and northwest <a href="/wiki/British_Columbia" title="British Columbia">British Columbia</a>.
</p><p>Some communities are bilingual, with the children learning Slavey at home and English when they enter school. Still other communities are monolingual in Slavey <sup id="cite_ref-Rice_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rice-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The dialect has around 1,000 speakers.<sup id="cite_ref-census_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-census-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><i>Alternative names:</i> Slavi, Slave, Dené, Mackenzian
</p><p>The division of Slavey dialects is based largely on the way each one pronounces the old Proto-Athapaskan sounds *dz *ts *ts’ *s and *z.
</p>

<p>The consonant inventories in the dialects of Slavey differ considerably. The table above lists the 30 consonants common to most or all varieties. Hare lacks aspirated affricates (on red background), which have lenited into fricatives, whereas Mountain lacks <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/w/</span> (on blue). In addition, for some speakers of Hare, an <a href="/wiki/Alveolar_flap" class="mw-redirect" title="Alveolar flap">alveolar flap</a> <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ɾ/</span> has developed into a separate phoneme. Prenasalized stops <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ᵐb,<span class="wrap"> </span>ⁿd/</span> may appear in Slavey proper.
</p><p>The most pronounced difference is however the realization of a series of consonants that varies greatly in their place of articulation:<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>

<p>In Slavey proper, these are <a href="/wiki/Dental_consonant" title="Dental consonant">dental</a> affricates and fricatives; comparative Athabaskan work reveals this to be the oldest sound value. Mountain has labials, with the voiceless stop coinciding with pre-existing <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/p/</span>. Bearlake has <a href="/wiki/Labialization" title="Labialization">labialized</a> velars, but has lenited the voiced fricative to coincide with pre-existing <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/w/</span>. The most complicated situation is found in Hare, where the plain stop is a labialized velar, the ejective member is replaced by a <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ʔw/</span> sequence, the aspirated affricate has turned into a fricative <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/f/</span>, and both the voiceless and voiced fricatives have been lenited to <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/w/</span>.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Phonological_processes">Phonological processes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slavey_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Phonological processes"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The following phonological and phonetic statements apply to all four dialects of Slavey.
</p>
<ul><li>Unaspirated obstruents are either voiceless or weakly voiced, e.g.
<ul><li><span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/k/</span> → <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[k]</span> or <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[k̬]</span></li></ul></li>
<li>Aspirated obstruents are strongly aspirated.</li>
<li>Ejectives are strongly ejective.</li>
<li>When occurring between vowels, ejectives are often voiced, e.g.
<ul><li><span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/kʼ/</span> → <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ɡˀ]</span> or <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[kʼ]</span></li></ul></li>
<li><span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/t͡sʰ/</span> is usually strongly velarized, i.e. <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[tˣ]</span>.</li>
<li>Velar obstruents are palatalized before front vowels, e.g.
<ul><li><span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/kɛ/</span> → <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[cɛ]</span></li>
<li><span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/xɛ/</span> → <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[çɛ]</span></li>
<li><span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ɣɛ/</span> → <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ʝɛ]</span></li></ul></li>
<li>Velar fricatives may be labialized before round vowels.
<ul><li>The voiceless fricative is usually labialized, e.g.
<ul><li><span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/xo/</span> → <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[xʷo]</span></li></ul></li>
<li>The voiced fricative is optionally labialized and may additionally be defricated e.g.
<ul><li><span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ɣo/</span> → <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ɣo]</span> or <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ɣʷo]</span> or <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[wo]</span></li></ul></li></ul></li>
<li>Velar stops are also labialized before round vowels. These labialized velars are not as heavily rounded as labial velars (which occur in Bearlake and Hare), e.g.
<ul><li><span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ko/</span> → <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[kʷo]</span></li>
<li><span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/kʷo/</span> → <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[k̹ʷwo]</span></li></ul></li>
<li>Lateral affricates are generally alveolar, but sometimes velar, i.e.
<ul><li><span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/tɬ/</span> → <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[tɬ]</span> or <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[kɬ]</span></li>
<li><span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/tɬʰ/</span> → <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[tɬʰ]</span> or <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[kɬʰ]</span></li>
<li><span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/tɬʼ/</span> → <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[tɬʼ]</span> or <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[kɬʼ]</span></li></ul></li>
<li><span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/x/</span> may be velar or glottal, i.e.
<ul><li><span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/x/</span> → <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[x]</span> or <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[h]</span></li></ul></li></ul>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tone">Tone</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slavey_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Tone"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Slavey has two tones:
</p>
<ul><li>high</li>
<li>low</li></ul>
<p>In Slavey orthography, high tone is marked with an acute accent, and low tone is unmarked.
</p><p>Tones are both lexical and grammatical.
</p><p>Lexical: <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ɡáh/</span> 'along' vs. <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ɡàh/</span> 'rabbit'
</p>

<h2><span id="Person.2C_number_and_gender"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Person,_number_and_gender">Person, number and gender</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slavey_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Person, number and gender"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gender">Gender</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slavey_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Gender"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Slavey marks gender by means of prefixation on the verb theme. There are three different genders, one of which is unmarked; the other two are marked by prefixes <b>[go-]</b> and <b>[de-]</b>. However, only certain verb themes allow gender prefixes.<sup id="cite_ref-Rice_7-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rice-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><b>[go-]</b> is used for nouns which mark location in either time or space. Some examples of these areal nouns are house (ko̜̒e̒), land (de̒h), river (deh), and winder (xay).<sup id="cite_ref-Rice_7-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rice-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The gender pronoun can be a direct object, an oblique object or a possessor.<sup id="cite_ref-Rice_7-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rice-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="interlinear" style="margin-left:4em"><div style="position:absolute;left:1em">ex:</div><div style="float: left; margin-bottom: 0.3em;margin-right: 1em;"><p style="margin: 0px;font-style: italic;">kú̜e̒</p><p style="margin: 0px;">house</p></div><div style="float: left; margin-bottom: 0.3em;margin-right: 1em;"><p style="margin: 0px;font-style: italic;"><b>go</b>detl’e̒h</p><p style="margin: 0px;"><abbr class="gloss-abbr" title="third person, singular" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-variant-numeric: oldstyle-nums; text-transform: lowercase;">3SG</abbr>.paints.area</p></div><p style="display: none;">kú̜e̒ <b>go</b>detl’e̒h</p><p style="display: none;">house 3SG.paints.area</p><p style="clear: left;">'S/he is painting the house.'</p><div style="clear: left; display: block;"></div>
</div>
<div class="interlinear" style="margin-left:4em"><div style="position:absolute;left:1em">ex:</div><div style="float: left; margin-bottom: 0.3em;margin-right: 1em;"><p style="margin: 0px;font-style: italic;">ko̜̒e̒</p><p style="margin: 0px;">house</p></div><div style="float: left; margin-bottom: 0.3em;margin-right: 1em;"><p style="margin: 0px;font-style: italic;"><b>go</b>cha</p><p style="margin: 0px;">area.in</p></div><div style="float: left; margin-bottom: 0.3em;margin-right: 1em;"><p style="margin: 0px;font-style: italic;">&#160;</p><p style="margin: 0px;">shelter</p></div><p style="display: none;">ko̜̒e̒ <b>go</b>cha {}</p><p style="display: none;">house area.in shelter</p><p style="clear: left;">'in the shelter of the house'</p><div style="clear: left; display: block;"></div>
</div>
<div class="interlinear" style="margin-left:4em"><div style="position:absolute;left:1em">ex:</div><div style="float: left; margin-bottom: 0.3em;margin-right: 1em;"><p style="margin: 0px;font-style: italic;">ko̜̒e̒</p><p style="margin: 0px;">house</p></div><div style="float: left; margin-bottom: 0.3em;margin-right: 1em;"><p style="margin: 0px;font-style: italic;"><b>go</b>deshįte̒ee</p><p style="margin: 0px;">area.floor</p></div><p style="display: none;">ko̜̒e̒ <b>go</b>deshįte̒ee</p><p style="display: none;">house area.floor</p><p style="clear: left;">'floor of the house'</p><div style="clear: left; display: block;"></div>
</div>
<p><b>[de-]</b> marks wood, leaves and branches. This gender is optional: some speakers use it and others do not.<sup id="cite_ref-Rice_7-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rice-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="interlinear" style="margin-left:4em"><div style="position:absolute;left:1em">ex:</div><div style="float: left; margin-bottom: 0.3em;margin-right: 1em;"><p style="margin: 0px;font-style: italic;">tse</p><p style="margin: 0px;">wood</p></div><div style="float: left; margin-bottom: 0.3em;margin-right: 1em;"><p style="margin: 0px;font-style: italic;"><b>de̜</b>la</p><p style="margin: 0px;">&#160;</p></div><p style="display: none;">tse <b>de̜</b>la</p><p style="display: none;">wood {}</p><p style="clear: left;">'wood is located'</p><div style="clear: left; display: block;"></div>
</div>
<div class="interlinear" style="margin-left:4em"><div style="position:absolute;left:1em">ex:</div><div style="float: left; margin-bottom: 0.3em;margin-right: 1em;"><p style="margin: 0px;font-style: italic;">ʔo̜̒k’ay</p><p style="margin: 0px;">bird</p></div><div style="float: left; margin-bottom: 0.3em;margin-right: 1em;"><p style="margin: 0px;font-style: italic;">t’oge</p><p style="margin: 0px;">nest</p></div><div style="float: left; margin-bottom: 0.3em;margin-right: 1em;"><p style="margin: 0px;font-style: italic;"><b>de̒</b>ʔo̜</p><p style="margin: 0px;">wooden</p></div><div style="float: left; margin-bottom: 0.3em;margin-right: 1em;"><p style="margin: 0px;font-style: italic;">&#160;</p><p style="margin: 0px;"><abbr class="gloss-abbr" title="patient-like argument (object) of canonical transitive verb">O</abbr></p></div><div style="float: left; margin-bottom: 0.3em;margin-right: 1em;"><p style="margin: 0px;font-style: italic;">&#160;</p><p style="margin: 0px;">is</p></div><div style="float: left; margin-bottom: 0.3em;margin-right: 1em;"><p style="margin: 0px;font-style: italic;">&#160;</p><p style="margin: 0px;">located</p></div><p style="display: none;">ʔo̜̒k’ay t’oge <b>de̒</b>ʔo̜ {} {} {}</p><p style="display: none;">bird nest wooden O is located</p><p style="clear: left;">'A bird’s nest is located'</p><div style="clear: left; display: block;"></div>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Number">Number</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slavey_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Number"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Slavey marks number in the subject prefixes in position 12. The dual is marked by the prefix łe̒h- (Sl)/łe- (Bl)/le- (Hr).
</p>
<dl><dd><i>ni̒łe̒gehtthe</i></dd>
<dd>'They two got stuck in a narrow passage.'</dd></dl>
<p><br />
The plural is marked with the prefix <b>go-</b>.
</p>
<dl><dd><i>Dah<b>go</b>gehthe</i></dd>
<dd>'They dance.'</dd></dl>
<dl><dd><i>ʔeha̒<b>go</b>ni̒dhe</i></dd>
<dd>'We go for meat.'</dd></dl>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Person">Person</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slavey_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Person"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Slavey has first, second, third, and fourth person. When in position 12, acting as a subject, first-person singular is /h-/, second-person singular is /ne-/, first-person dual/plural is /i̒d-/, and second person plural is marked by /ah-/. Third person is not marked in this position. When occurring as a direct or indirect object, the pronoun prefixes change and fourth person becomes relevant.
</p>
<ul><li>First-person singular takes se-.</li>
<li>Second-person singular takes ne-</li>
<li>Third person is marked by be-/me-</li>
<li>Fourth person is marked by ye-<sup id="cite_ref-Rice_7-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rice-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tense_and_aspect">Tense and aspect</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slavey_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Tense and aspect"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tense">Tense</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slavey_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Tense"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Slavey has only one structural tense: future. Other tenses can be indicated periphrastically.<sup id="cite_ref-Rice_7-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rice-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>An immediate future can be formed by the <i>de-</i> inceptive (position 9) plus <i>y-.</i>
</p>
<div class="interlinear" style="margin-left:4em"><div style="position:absolute;left:1em">ex:</div><div style="float: left; margin-bottom: 0.3em;margin-right: 1em;"><p style="margin: 0px;font-style: italic;">dałe</p><p style="margin: 0px;"><abbr class="gloss-abbr" title="third person" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-variant-numeric: oldstyle-nums; text-transform: lowercase;">3</abbr>.<abbr class="gloss-abbr" title="future tense" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-variant-numeric: oldstyle-nums; text-transform: lowercase;">FUT</abbr>.start out</p></div><p style="display: none;">dałe</p><p style="display: none;">{3.FUT.start out}</p><p style="clear: left;">'S/he is just ready to go.'</p><div style="clear: left; display: block;"></div>
</div>
<div class="interlinear" style="margin-left:4em"><div style="position:absolute;left:1em">ex:</div><div style="float: left; margin-bottom: 0.3em;margin-right: 1em;"><p style="margin: 0px;font-style: italic;">nadedajéh</p><p style="margin: 0px;"><abbr class="gloss-abbr" title="third person" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-variant-numeric: oldstyle-nums; text-transform: lowercase;">3</abbr>.<abbr class="gloss-abbr" title="future tense" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-variant-numeric: oldstyle-nums; text-transform: lowercase;">FUT</abbr>.start to heal</p></div><p style="display: none;">nadedajéh</p><p style="display: none;">{3.FUT.start to heal}</p><p style="clear: left;">'It is just starting to heal.'</p><div style="clear: left; display: block;"></div>
</div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Aspect">Aspect</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slavey_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Aspect"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Slavey has two semantic aspects: perfective and imperfective.
</p><p>The perfective is represented in position 11:
</p>
<div class="interlinear" style="margin-left:4em"><div style="position:absolute;left:1em">ex:</div><div style="float: left; margin-bottom: 0.3em;margin-right: 1em;"><p style="margin: 0px;font-style: italic;">déhtla</p><p style="margin: 0px;"><abbr class="gloss-abbr" title="third person, singular" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-variant-numeric: oldstyle-nums; text-transform: lowercase;">3SG</abbr>.<abbr class="gloss-abbr" title="perfective aspect" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-variant-numeric: oldstyle-nums; text-transform: lowercase;">PFV</abbr>.start off</p></div><p style="display: none;">déhtla</p><p style="display: none;">{3SG.PFV.start off}</p><p style="clear: left;">'S/he started off.'</p><div style="clear: left; display: block;"></div>
</div>
<div class="interlinear" style="margin-left:4em"><div style="position:absolute;left:1em">ex:</div><div style="float: left; margin-bottom: 0.3em;margin-right: 1em;"><p style="margin: 0px;font-style: italic;">whá</p><p style="margin: 0px;">long</p></div><div style="float: left; margin-bottom: 0.3em;margin-right: 1em;"><p style="margin: 0px;font-style: italic;">goyįdee</p><p style="margin: 0px;"><abbr class="gloss-abbr" title="third person, singular" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-variant-numeric: oldstyle-nums; text-transform: lowercase;">3SG</abbr>.<abbr class="gloss-abbr" title="perfective aspect" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-variant-numeric: oldstyle-nums; text-transform: lowercase;">PFV</abbr>.talk</p></div><p style="display: none;">whá goyįdee</p><p style="display: none;">long 3SG.PFV.talk</p><p style="clear: left;">'S/he talked for a long time.'</p><div style="clear: left; display: block;"></div>
</div>
<p>The perfective can also be used with a past tense marker to indicate that at the point of reference, which is sometime in the past, the event was completed <sup id="cite_ref-Rice_7-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rice-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="interlinear" style="margin-left:4em"><div style="position:absolute;left:1em">ex:</div><div style="float: left; margin-bottom: 0.3em;margin-right: 1em;"><p style="margin: 0px;font-style: italic;">kǫ́e</p><p style="margin: 0px;">house</p></div><div style="float: left; margin-bottom: 0.3em;margin-right: 1em;"><p style="margin: 0px;font-style: italic;">gohtsį</p><p style="margin: 0px;"><abbr class="gloss-abbr" title="third person, singular" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-variant-numeric: oldstyle-nums; text-transform: lowercase;">3SG</abbr>.<abbr class="gloss-abbr" title="perfective aspect" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-variant-numeric: oldstyle-nums; text-transform: lowercase;">PFV</abbr>.build</p></div><div style="float: left; margin-bottom: 0.3em;margin-right: 1em;"><p style="margin: 0px;font-style: italic;">&#160;</p><p style="margin: 0px;">area</p></div><div style="float: left; margin-bottom: 0.3em;margin-right: 1em;"><p style="margin: 0px;font-style: italic;">&#160;</p><p style="margin: 0px;"><abbr class="gloss-abbr" title="past tense" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-variant-numeric: oldstyle-nums; text-transform: lowercase;">PAST</abbr></p></div><p style="display: none;">kǫ́e gohtsį {} {}</p><p style="display: none;">house 3SG.PFV.build area PAST</p><p style="clear: left;">'He had built a house.'</p><div style="clear: left; display: block;"></div>
</div>
<p>The imperfective indicates that the reference time precedes the end of the event time:
</p>
<div class="interlinear" style="margin-left:4em"><div style="position:absolute;left:1em">ex:</div><div style="float: left; margin-bottom: 0.3em;margin-right: 1em;"><p style="margin: 0px;font-style: italic;">hejį</p><p style="margin: 0px;"><abbr class="gloss-abbr" title="third person" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-variant-numeric: oldstyle-nums; text-transform: lowercase;">3</abbr></p></div><div style="float: left; margin-bottom: 0.3em;margin-right: 1em;"><p style="margin: 0px;font-style: italic;">&#160;</p><p style="margin: 0px;"><abbr class="gloss-abbr" title="imperfective aspect" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-variant-numeric: oldstyle-nums; text-transform: lowercase;">IPFV</abbr>.sing</p></div><p style="display: none;">hejį {}</p><p style="display: none;">3 IPFV.sing</p><p style="clear: left;">'S/he sing, s/he is singing.'</p><div style="clear: left; display: block;"></div>
</div>
<div class="interlinear" style="margin-left:4em"><div style="position:absolute;left:1em">ex:</div><div style="float: left; margin-bottom: 0.3em;margin-right: 1em;"><p style="margin: 0px;font-style: italic;">kǫ́e</p><p style="margin: 0px;">house</p></div><div style="float: left; font-variant-numeric: oldstyle-nums; text-transform: lowercase;">IPFV</abbr>.build</p></div><div style="float: left; margin-bottom: 0.3em;margin-right: 1em;"><p style="margin: 0px;font-style: italic;">&#160;</p><p style="margin: 0px;">area</p></div><div style="float: left; margin-bottom: 0.3em;margin-right: 1em;"><p style="margin: 0px;font-style: italic;">&#160;</p><p style="margin: 0px;"><abbr class="gloss-abbr" title="first person, singular" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-variant-numeric: oldstyle-nums; text-transform: lowercase;">1SG</abbr>.<abbr class="gloss-abbr" title="perfective aspect" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-variant-numeric: oldstyle-nums; text-transform: lowercase;">PFV</abbr>.see</p></div><div style="float: left; margin-bottom: 0.3em;margin-right: 1em;"><p style="margin: 0px;font-style: italic;">&#160;</p><p style="margin: 0px;"><abbr class="gloss-abbr" title="third person" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-variant-numeric: oldstyle-nums; text-transform: lowercase;">3</abbr></p></div><p style="display: none;">kǫ́e gohtsį begháyeyidá {} {} {}</p><p style="display: none;">house 3 IPFV.build area 1SG.PFV.see 3</p><p style="clear: left;">'I saw him building a house.'</p><div style="clear: left; display: block;"></div>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Word_order">Word order</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slavey_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Word order"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Slavey is a verb-final language. The basic word order is SOV.<sup id="cite_ref-Rice_7-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rice-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="interlinear" style="margin-left:4em"><div style="position:absolute;left:1em">ex:</div><div style="float: left; margin-bottom: 0.3em;margin-right: 1em;"><p style="margin: 0px;font-style: italic;">Dene</p><p style="margin: 0px;">man</p></div><div style="float: left; margin-bottom: 0.3em;margin-right: 1em;"><p style="margin: 0px;font-style: italic;">?elá</p><p style="margin: 0px;">boat</p></div><div style="float: left; margin-bottom: 0.3em;margin-right: 1em;"><p style="margin: 0px;font-style: italic;">thehtsį̒</p><p style="margin: 0px;"><abbr class="gloss-abbr" title="third person, singular" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-variant-numeric: oldstyle-nums; text-transform: lowercase;">3SG</abbr>.made</p></div><p style="display: none;">Dene ?elá thehtsį̒</p><p style="display: none;">man boat 3SG.made</p><p style="clear: left;">'The man made the boat.'</p><div style="clear: left; display: block;"></div>
</div>
<div class="interlinear" style="margin-left:4em"><div style="position:absolute;left:1em">ex:</div><div style="float: left; margin-bottom: 0.3em;margin-right: 1em;"><p style="margin: 0px;font-style: italic;">tlį</p><p style="margin: 0px;">dog</p></div><div style="float: left; margin-bottom: 0.3em;margin-right: 1em;"><p style="margin: 0px;font-style: italic;">ts’ǫ̀dani</p><p style="margin: 0px;">child</p></div><div style="float: left; margin-bottom: 0.3em;margin-right: 1em;"><p style="margin: 0px;font-style: italic;">káyį̒ta</p><p style="margin: 0px;"><abbr class="gloss-abbr" title="third person, singular" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-variant-numeric: oldstyle-nums; text-transform: lowercase;">3SG</abbr>.bit</p></div><p style="display: none;">tlį ts’ǫ̀dani káyį̒ta</p><p style="display: none;">dog child 3SG.bit</p><p style="clear: left;">'The dog bit the child.'</p><div style="clear: left; display: block;"></div>
</div>
<p>Oblique objects precede the direct object.<sup id="cite_ref-Rice_7-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rice-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="interlinear" style="margin-left:4em"><div style="position:absolute;left:1em">ex:</div><div style="float: left; margin-bottom: 0.3em;margin-right: 1em;"><p style="margin: 0px;font-style: italic;">T’eere</p><p style="margin: 0px;">girl</p></div><div style="float: left; margin-bottom: 0.3em;margin-right: 1em;"><p style="margin: 0px;font-style: italic;">denǫ</p><p style="margin: 0px;">mother</p></div><div style="float: left; margin-bottom: 0.3em;margin-right: 1em;"><p style="margin: 0px;font-style: italic;">gha</p><p style="margin: 0px;">for</p></div><div style="float: left; margin-bottom: 0.3em;margin-right: 1em;"><p style="margin: 0px;font-style: italic;">?erákee?ee</p><p style="margin: 0px;">parka</p></div><div style="float: left; margin-bottom: 0.3em;margin-right: 1em;"><p style="margin: 0px;font-style: italic;">wihsį</p><p style="margin: 0px;">made</p></div><p style="display: none;">T’eere denǫ gha ?erákee?ee wihsį</p><p style="display: none;">girl mother for parka made</p><p style="clear: left;">'The girl made a parka for her mother.'</p><div style="clear: left; display: block;"></div>
</div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Case">Case</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slavey_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Case"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Slavey has no case markings. To differentiate between subject, direct object, and oblique objects, word order is used. The subject will be the first noun phrase, and the direct object will occur right before the verb. The oblique objects are controlled by postpositions.<sup id="cite_ref-Rice_7-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rice-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Relative_clauses">Relative clauses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slavey_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=38" title="Edit section: Relative clauses"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are three important parts to a relative clause. There is the head, which is the noun that is modified or delimited. The second part is the restricting sentence. The sentence modifies the head noun. The last part is the complementizer.<sup id="cite_ref-Rice_7-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rice-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Status">Status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slavey_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=39" title="Edit section: Status"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>North and South Slavey are recognized as official languages of the Northwest Territories; they may be used in court and in debates and proceedings of the Northwest Territories legislature. However, unlike English and French, the government only publishes laws and documents in North and South Slavey if the legislature requests it, and these documents are not authoritative.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2015, a Slavey woman named Andrea Heron challenged the territorial government over its refusal to permit the <a href="/wiki/Glottal_stop" title="Glottal stop">ʔ</a> character, representing the Slavey glottal stop, in her daughter's name, Sakaeʔah, despite Slavey languages being official in the NWT. The territory argued that territorial and federal identity documents were unable to accommodate the character. Heron had registered the name with a hyphen instead of the ʔ when her daughter was born, but when Sakaeʔah was 6, Ms. Heron joined a challenge by a <a href="/wiki/Chipewyan" title="Chipewyan">Chipewyan</a> woman named Shene Catholique-Valpy regarding the same character in her own daughter's name, Sahaiʔa.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Also in 2015, the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Victoria" title="University of Victoria">University of Victoria</a> launched a language revitalization program in the NWT, pairing learners of indigenous languages including Slavey with fluent speakers. The program requires 100 hours of conversation with the mentor with no English allowed, as well as sessions with instructors in <a href="/wiki/Fort_Providence,_Northwest_Territories" class="mw-redirect" title="Fort Providence, Northwest Territories">Fort Providence</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slavey_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=40" title="Edit section: In popular culture"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Slavey was the native language spoken by the fictional band in the Canadian television series <i><a href="/wiki/North_of_60" title="North of 60">North of 60</a></i>. <a href="/wiki/Nick_Sibbeston" title="Nick Sibbeston">Nick Sibbeston</a>, a former <a href="/wiki/Premier_of_the_Northwest_Territories" title="Premier of the Northwest Territories">Premier of the Northwest Territories</a>, was a Slavey language and culture consultant for the show.
</p>
