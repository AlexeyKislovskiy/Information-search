<p><b>Oʼodham</b> (pronounced <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="azc-Latn-fonipa"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA" title="Help:IPA">&#91;ˈʔɔʔɔðam&#93;</a></span>) or <b>Papago-Pima</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Uto-Aztecan_languages" title="Uto-Aztecan languages">Uto-Aztecan</a> language of southern <a href="/wiki/Arizona" title="Arizona">Arizona</a> and northern <a href="/wiki/Sonora" title="Sonora">Sonora</a>, Mexico,  where the <a href="/wiki/Tohono_O%CA%BCodham_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Tohono Oʼodham people">Tohono Oʼodham</a> (formerly called the Papago) and <a href="/wiki/Pima_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Pima people">Akimel Oʼodham</a> (traditionally called Pima) reside.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> In 2000 there were estimated to be approximately 9,750 speakers in the United States and Mexico combined, although there may be more due to underreporting.
</p><p>It is the 10th most-spoken indigenous language in the United States, the 3rd most-spoken <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_languages_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous languages of the Americas">indigenous language</a> in Arizona after <a href="/wiki/Western_Apache_language" title="Western Apache language">Western Apache</a> and <a href="/wiki/Navajo_language" title="Navajo language">Navajo</a>. It is the third-most spoken language in <a href="/wiki/Pinal_County,_Arizona" title="Pinal County, Arizona">Pinal County, Arizona</a>, and the fourth-most spoken language in <a href="/wiki/Pima_County,_Arizona" title="Pima County, Arizona">Pima County, Arizona</a>.
</p><p>Approximately 8% of Oʼodham speakers in the US speak English "not well" or "not at all", according to results of the 2000 Census. Approximately 13% of Oʼodham speakers in the US were between the ages of 5 and 17, and among the younger Oʼodham speakers, approximately 4% were reported as speaking English "not well" or "not at all".
</p><p>Native names for the language, depending on the dialect and orthography, include <span title="O&#39;odham-language text"><i lang="ood">Oʼodham ha-ñeʼokĭ</i></span>, <span title="O&#39;odham-language text"><i lang="ood">Oʼottham ha-neoki</i></span>, and <span title="O&#39;odham-language text"><i lang="ood">Oʼodham ñiok</i></span>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Morphology">Morphology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=O%CA%BCodham_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Morphology"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Oʼodham is an <a href="/wiki/Agglutinative" class="mw-redirect" title="Agglutinative">agglutinative</a> language, where words use suffix complexes for a variety of purposes with several <a href="/wiki/Morpheme" title="Morpheme">morphemes</a> strung together.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Phonology">Phonology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=O%CA%BCodham_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Phonology"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1033289096">.mw-parser-output .hatnote{font-style:italic}.mw-parser-output div.hatnote{padding-left:1.6em;margin-bottom:0.5em}.mw-parser-output .hatnote i{font-style:normal}.mw-parser-output .hatnote+link+.hatnote{margin-top:-0.5em}</style><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">For clarity, the terms <b>Tohono Oʼodham</b> and <b>Papago</b> refer to the same language; likewise for <b>Akimel Oʼodham</b> and <b>Pima</b>.</div>
<p>Oʼodham phonology has a typical Uto-Aztecan inventory distinguishing 19 consonants and 5 vowels.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Most vowels distinguish two degrees of length: long and short, and some vowels also show <a href="/wiki/Extra-short" class="mw-redirect" title="Extra-short">extra-short</a> duration (voicelessness).

<ul><li><span title="O&#39;odham-language text"><i lang="ood">ṣe꞉l</i></span> <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ʂɨːɭ/</span> "<a href="/wiki/Seri_people" title="Seri people">Seri</a>"</li>
<li><span title="O&#39;odham-language text"><i lang="ood">ṣel</i></span> <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ʂɨɭ/</span> "permission"</li>

<p>Papago <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ɨ/</span> is pronounced <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ʌ]</span> in Pima.
</p><p>Additionally, in common with many northern Uto-Aztecan languages, vowels and nasals at end of words are <a href="/wiki/Devoiced" class="mw-redirect" title="Devoiced">devoiced</a>. Also, a short <a href="/wiki/Schwa" title="Schwa">schwa</a> sound, either voiced or unvoiced depending on position, is often interpolated between consonants and at the ends of words.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Allophony_and_distribution">Allophony and distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=O%CA%BCodham_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Allophony and distribution"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li>Extra short <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;ĭ&#x27e9;</span> is realized as voiceless <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[i̥]</span> and devoices preceding obstruents: <span title="O&#39;odham-language text"><i lang="ood">cuwĭ</i></span> <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/tʃʊwi̥/</span> → <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[tʃʊʍi̥]~[tʃʊʍʲ]</span> "jackrabbit".</li>
<li><span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/w/</span> is a fricative <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[β]</span> before unrounded vowels: <span title="O&#39;odham-language text"><i lang="ood">wisilo</i></span> <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[βisiɭɔ]</span>.</li>
<li><span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ŋ]</span> appears before <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/k/</span> and <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ɡ/</span> in Spanish loanwords, but native words do not have nasal assimilation: <span title="O&#39;odham-language text"><i lang="ood">to꞉nk</i></span> <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[toːnk]</span> "hill", <span title="O&#39;odham-language text"><i lang="ood">namk</i></span> <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[namk]</span> "meet", <span title="O&#39;odham-language text"><i lang="ood">ca꞉ŋgo</i></span> <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[tʃaːŋɡo]</span> "monkey". <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/p/</span>, <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ɭ/</span>, and <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ɖ/</span> rarely occur initially in native words, and <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ɖ/</span> does not occur before <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/i/</span>.</li>
<li><span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ɲ]</span> and <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[n]</span> are largely in complementary distribution, <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ɲ]</span> appearing before high vowels <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/i/<span class="wrap"> </span>/ɨ/<span class="wrap"> </span>/ʊ/</span>, <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[n]</span> appearing before low vowels <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/a/<span class="wrap"> </span>/ɔ/</span>: <span title="O&#39;odham-language text"><i lang="ood">ñeʼe</i></span> "sing". They contrast finally (<span title="O&#39;odham-language text"><i lang="ood">ʼañ</i></span> (1st imperfective auxiliary) vs. <span title="O&#39;odham-language text"><i lang="ood">an</i></span> "next to speaker"), though Saxton analyzes these as <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ani/</span> and <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/an/</span>, respectively, and final <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ɲi]</span> as in <span title="O&#39;odham-language text"><i lang="ood">ʼa꞉ñi</i></span> as <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/niː/</span>. However, there are several Spanish loanwords where <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[nu]</span> occurs: <span title="O&#39;odham-language text"><i lang="ood">nu꞉milo</i></span> "number". Similarly, for the most part <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[t]</span> and <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[d]</span> appear before low vowels while <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[tʃ]</span> and <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[dʒ]</span> before high vowels, but there are exceptions to both, often in Spanish loanwords: <span title="O&#39;odham-language text"><i lang="ood">tiki꞉la ("tequila")</i></span> "wine", TO weco / AO veco ("[de]bajo") "under".</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Orthography">Orthography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=O%CA%BCodham_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Orthography"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are two orthographies commonly used for the Oʼodham language: Alvarez–Hale and Saxton. The Alvarez–Hale orthography is officially used by the <a href="/wiki/Tohono_O%CA%BCodham_Nation" title="Tohono Oʼodham Nation">Tohono Oʼodham Nation</a> and the Salt River Pima–Maricopa Indian Community, and is used in this article, but the Saxton orthography is also common and is official in the Gila River Indian Community. It is relatively easy to convert between the two, the differences between them being largely no more than different graphemes for the same phoneme, but there are distinctions made by Alvarez–Hale not made by Saxton.

<p>The Saxton orthography does not mark word-initial <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ʔ/</span> or extra-short vowels. <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Disputed_spellings">Disputed spellings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=O%CA%BCodham_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Disputed spellings"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There is some disagreement among speakers as to whether the spelling of words should be only phonetic or whether etymological principles should be considered as well.
</p><p>For instance, <span title="O&#39;odham-language text"><i lang="ood"><b>oam</b>ajda</i></span> vs. <span title="O&#39;odham-language text"><i lang="ood"><b>wua</b>majda</i></span> ("frybread"; the spellings <span title="O&#39;odham-language text"><i lang="ood">oamacda</i></span> and <span title="O&#39;odham-language text"><i lang="ood">wuamacda</i></span> are also seen) derives from <span title="O&#39;odham-language text"><i lang="ood">oam</i></span> (a warm color roughly equivalent to yellow or brown). Some believe it should be spelled phonetically as <span title="O&#39;odham-language text"><i lang="ood">wuamajda</i></span>, reflecting the fact that it begins with <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ʊa/</span>, while others think its spelling should reflect the fact that it is derived from <span title="O&#39;odham-language text"><i lang="ood">oam</i></span> (<span title="O&#39;odham-language text"><i lang="ood">oam</i></span> is itself a form of <span title="O&#39;odham-language text"><i lang="ood">s-oam</i></span>, so while it could be spelled <span title="O&#39;odham-language text"><i lang="ood">wuam</i></span>, it is not since it is just a different declension of the same word).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Grammar">Grammar</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=O%CA%BCodham_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Grammar"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Syntax">Syntax</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=O%CA%BCodham_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Syntax"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Oʼodham has relatively free word order within clauses; for example, all of the following sentences mean "the boy brands the pig":<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><span title="O&#39;odham-language text"><i lang="ood">ceoj ʼo g ko꞉jĭ ceposid</i></span></li>
<li><span title="O&#39;odham-language text"><i lang="ood">ko꞉jĭ ʼo g ceoj ceposid</i></span></li>
<li><span title="O&#39;odham-language text"><i lang="ood">ceoj ʼo ceposid g ko꞉jĭ</i></span></li>
<li><span title="O&#39;odham-language text"><i lang="ood">ko꞉jĭ ʼo ceposid g ceoj</i></span></li>
<li><span title="O&#39;odham-language text"><i lang="ood">ceposid ʼo g ceoj g ko꞉jĭ</i></span></li>
<li><span title="O&#39;odham-language text"><i lang="ood">ceposid ʼo g ko꞉jĭ g ceoj</i></span></li></ul>
<p>In principle, these could also mean "the pig brands the boy", but such an interpretation would require an unusual context.
</p><p>Despite the general freedom of sentence word order, Oʼodham is fairly strictly <a href="/wiki/V2_word_order" title="V2 word order">verb-second</a> in its placement of the auxiliary verb (in the above sentences, it is <span title="O&#39;odham-language text"><i lang="ood">ʼo</i></span>):
</p>
<ul><li><span title="O&#39;odham-language text"><i lang="ood">cipkan <b>ʼañ</b></i></span> "I am working"</li>
<li>but <span title="O&#39;odham-language text"><i lang="ood">pi <b>ʼañ</b> cipkan</i></span> "I am not working", not **<i>pi cipkan ʼañ</i></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Verbs">Verbs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=O%CA%BCodham_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Verbs"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Verbs are inflected for <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_aspect" title="Grammatical aspect">aspect</a> (imperfective <span title="O&#39;odham-language text"><i lang="ood">cipkan</i></span>, perfective <span title="O&#39;odham-language text"><i lang="ood">cipk</i></span>), <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_tense" title="Grammatical tense">tense</a> (future imperfective <span title="O&#39;odham-language text"><i lang="ood">cipkanad</i></span>), and <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_number" title="Grammatical number">number</a> (plural <span title="O&#39;odham-language text"><i lang="ood">cicpkan</i></span>). Number agreement displays <a href="/wiki/Ergative%E2%80%93absolutive_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Ergative–absolutive language">absolutive</a> behavior: verbs agree with the number of the subject in intransitive sentences, but with that of the object in transitive sentences:
</p>
<ul><li><span title="O&#39;odham-language text"><i lang="ood">ceoj ʼo cipkan</i></span> "the boy is working"</li>
<li><span title="O&#39;odham-language text"><i lang="ood">cecoj ʼo cicpkan</i></span> "the boys are working"</li>
<li><span title="O&#39;odham-language text"><i lang="ood">ceoj ʼo g ko꞉ji ceposid</i></span> "the boy is branding the pig"</li>
<li><span title="O&#39;odham-language text"><i lang="ood">cecoj ʼo g ko꞉ji ceposid</i></span> "the boys are branding the pig"</li>
<li><span title="O&#39;odham-language text"><i lang="ood">ceoj ʼo g kokji ha-cecposid</i></span> "the boy is branding the pigs"</li></ul>
<p>The main verb agrees with the object for person (<span title="O&#39;odham-language text"><i lang="ood">ha-</i></span> in the above example), but the auxiliary agrees with the subject: <span title="O&#39;odham-language text"><i lang="ood">ʼa꞉ñi ʼañ g kokji ha-cecposid</i></span> "I am branding the pigs".
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nouns">Nouns</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=O%CA%BCodham_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Nouns"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Three numbers are distinguished in nouns: singular, plural, and distributive, though not all nouns have distinct forms for each. Most distinct plurals are formed by reduplication and often vowel loss plus other occasional morphophonemic changes, and distributives are formed from these by gemination of the reduplicated consonant:<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><span title="O&#39;odham-language text"><i lang="ood">gogs</i></span> "dog", <span title="O&#39;odham-language text"><i lang="ood">gogogs</i></span> "dogs", <span title="O&#39;odham-language text"><i lang="ood">goggogs</i></span> "dogs (all over)"</li>
<li><span title="O&#39;odham-language text"><i lang="ood">ma꞉gina</i></span> "car", <span title="O&#39;odham-language text"><i lang="ood">mamgina</i></span> "cars", <span title="O&#39;odham-language text"><i lang="ood">mammagina</i></span> "cars (all over)"</li>
<li><span title="O&#39;odham-language text"><i lang="ood">mi꞉stol</i></span> "cat", <span title="O&#39;odham-language text"><i lang="ood">mimstol</i></span> "cats"</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Adjectives">Adjectives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=O%CA%BCodham_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Adjectives"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Oʼodham adjectives can act both attributively modifying nouns and predicatively as verbs, with no change in form.
</p>
<ul><li><span title="O&#39;odham-language text"><i lang="ood">ʼi꞉da ṣu꞉dagĭ ʼo s-he꞉pid</i></span> "This water is cold"</li>
<li><span title="O&#39;odham-language text"><i lang="ood">ʼs-he꞉pid ṣu꞉dagĭ ʼañ hohoʼid</i></span> "I like cold water"</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sample_text">Sample text</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=O%CA%BCodham_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Sample text"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The following is an excerpt from Oʼodham Piipaash Language Program: <span title="O&#39;odham-language text"><i lang="ood">Taḏai</i></span> ("Roadrunner").<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> It exemplifies the Salt River dialect.
</p>
<dl><dd><span title="O&#39;odham-language text"><i lang="ood">Na꞉nse ʼe꞉da, mo꞉ hek jeweḍ ʼu꞉d si we꞉coc, ma꞉ṣ hek Taḏai siskeg ʼu꞉d ʼuʼuhig. Hek ʼaʼanac c wopo꞉c si wo skegac c ʼep si cecwac. Kuṣ ʼam hebai hai ki g ʼOʼodham ṣam ʼoʼoidam k ʼam ʼupam da꞉da k ʼam ce꞉ ma꞉ṣ he꞉kai cu hek ha na꞉da. ʼI꞉dam ʼOʼodham ṣam ʼeh he꞉mapa k ʼam aʼaga ma꞉ṣ has ma꞉sma vo bei hek na꞉da ʼab ʼamjeḍ hek Tatañki Jioṣ. Ṣa biʼi ʼa ma꞉ṣ mo ka꞉ke hek Taḏai ma꞉ṣ mo me꞉tk ʼamo ta꞉i hek na꞉da ha we꞉hejeḍ ʼi꞉dam ʼOʼodham. Taḏai ṣa꞉ ma so꞉hi ma꞉ṣ mo me꞉ḍk ʼamo ta꞉i g na꞉da hek Tatañki Jioṣ. Tho ṣud me꞉tkam, ʼam "si ʼi nai꞉ṣ hek wo꞉gk" k gau mel ma꞉ṣ ʼam ki g Tatañki Jioṣ.</i></span></dd></dl>
<p>In Saxton orthography:
</p>
<dl><dd><span title="O&#39;odham-language text"><i lang="ood">Nahnse ehtha, moh hek jeved uhth sih vehchoch, mahsh hek Tadai siskeg uhth uʼuhig. Hek aʼanach ch vopohch sih vo skegach ch ep sih chechvach. Kush am hebai hai kih g Oʼottham sham oʼoitham k am upam thahtha k am cheh mahsh hehkai chu hek ha nahtha. Ihtham Oʼothham sham eh hehmapa k am aʼaga mahsh has mahsma vo bei hek nahtha ab amjeth hek Tatanigi Jiosh. Sha biʼih a mahsh mo kahke hek Tadai mahsh mo mehtk amo tahʼih hek nahtha ha vehhejed ihtham Oʼottham. Tadai shah ma sohhih mahsh mo mehdk amo tahʼih g nahtha hek Tatanigi Jiosh. Tho shuth mehtkam, am "sih ih naihsh hek vohgk" k gau mel mahsh am kih g Tatanigi Jiosh.</i></span></dd></dl>
<p><br />
The following is a song from O'odham Hoho'ok A'agida (O'odham Legends and Lore) by Susanne Ignacio Enos, and Dean and Lucille Saxton.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> It exemplifies the "Storyteller dialect". 
</p><p>In Saxton orthography:
</p>
<dl><dd><span title="O&#39;odham-language text"><i lang="ood">Ali s-kohmangi chemamangi wiapo'oge'eli, hemu aichu mahch k e ahnga. Wahsh ng uwi chechenga ch mu'ikko ia melopa, oi wa pi e nako. Wahshana memenada ch gahghai chum a neinahim.</i></span></dd></dl>
<p>English:
</p>
<dl><dd><span title="English-language text"><span lang="en">Little gray horned toad youth, he just now learned something and is telling about himself. Over there he visits a girl repeatedly. And comes many times, yet he can't make it. Over there he keeps running, trying to look across at her.</span></span></dd></dl>
