<p><b>Denaʼina</b> <span class="rt-commentedText nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt" lang="en-fonipa"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="&#39;d&#39; in &#39;dye&#39;">d</span><span title="/ɪ/: &#39;i&#39; in &#39;kit&#39;">ɪ</span><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="&#39;n&#39; in &#39;nigh&#39;">n</span><span title="/aɪ/: &#39;i&#39; in &#39;tide&#39;">aɪ</span><span title="&#39;n&#39; in &#39;nigh&#39;">n</span><span title="/ə/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;about&#39;">ə</span></span>/</a></span></span>, also <b>Tanaina</b>,  is the <a href="/wiki/Athabaskan_languages" title="Athabaskan languages">Athabaskan language</a> of the region surrounding <a href="/wiki/Cook_Inlet" title="Cook Inlet">Cook Inlet</a>. It is geographically unique in Alaska as the only Alaska Athabaskan language to include territory which borders salt water. Four dialects are usually distinguished:
</p>
<ol><li>Upper Inlet, spoken in <a href="/wiki/Eklutna,_Alaska" class="mw-redirect" title="Eklutna, Alaska">Eklutna</a>, <a href="/wiki/Knik-Fairview,_Alaska" title="Knik-Fairview, Alaska">Knik</a>, <a href="/wiki/Susitna,_Alaska" title="Susitna, Alaska">Susitna</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tyonek" class="mw-redirect" title="Tyonek">Tyonek</a></li>
<li>Outer Inlet, spoken in <a href="/wiki/Kenai" class="mw-redirect" title="Kenai">Kenai</a>, Kustatan, <a href="/wiki/Seldovia" class="mw-redirect" title="Seldovia">Seldovia</a></li>
<li>Iliamna, spoken in <a href="/wiki/Pedro_Bay,_Alaska" title="Pedro Bay, Alaska">Pedro Bay</a>, Old Iliamna, <a href="/wiki/Lake_Iliamna" class="mw-redirect" title="Lake Iliamna">Lake Iliamna</a> area</li>
<li>Inland, spoken in <a href="/wiki/Nondalton" class="mw-redirect" title="Nondalton">Nondalton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lime_Village" class="mw-redirect" title="Lime Village">Lime Village</a></li></ol>
<p>Of the total Denaʼina population of about 900 people, only 75–95 members still speak Denaʼina. <a href="/wiki/James_Kari" title="James Kari">James Kari</a> has done extensive work on the language since 1972, including his edition with <a href="/wiki/Alan_Boraas" title="Alan Boraas">Alan Boraas</a> of the collected writings of <a href="/wiki/Peter_Kalifornsky" title="Peter Kalifornsky">Peter Kalifornsky</a> in 1991. <a href="/wiki/Joan_M._Tenenbaum" title="Joan M. Tenenbaum">Joan M. Tenenbaum</a> also conducted extensive field research on the language in the 1970s.
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ethnonym">Ethnonym</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dena%CA%BCina_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Ethnonym"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The word <span title="Tanaina-language text"><i lang="tfn">Denaʼina</i></span> is composed of the <span title="Tanaina-language text"><i lang="tfn">dena</i></span>, meaning 'person' and the human plural suffix <span title="Tanaina-language text"><i lang="tfn">ina</i></span>. While the apostrophe which joins the two parts of this word ordinarily indicates a glottal stop, most speakers pronounce this with a diphthong, so that the second syllable of the word rhymes with English 'nine' (as in the older spelling <b>Tanaina</b>).
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Syllable_structure">Syllable structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dena%CA%BCina_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Syllable structure"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the Inland dialect, syllables at the end of a semantic unit are often longer, lower in pitch, and have longer rhymes. The onset of a syllable has consonant clusters of up to three, such as CCCVC, though these are rare and more commonly, a syllable onset is one or two consonants.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Writing_Systems">Writing Systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dena%CA%BCina_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Writing Systems"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Dena'ina uses a variant of the Latin alphabet, though a Dena'ina artist, Argent Kvasnikoff, created a custom alphabet for the language.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Morphology">Morphology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dena%CA%BCina_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Morphology"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Grammatical_categories">Grammatical categories</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dena%CA%BCina_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Grammatical categories"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Denaʼina indicates classification with obligatory verb prefixes, meaning the root verb appears at the end of the word. The verb will always specify a classification and often person, gender, or object prefixes that indicate aspects of the noun or object for transitive verbs, and aspects of the speaker for intransitive verbs. Person can also be indicated by suffixes on the noun; the singular person suffix on a noun is generally <span title="Tanaina-language text"><i lang="tfn">-en</i></span>, whereas the plural suffix is generally <span title="Tanaina-language text"><i lang="tfn">-na</i></span> or <span title="Tanaina-language text"><i lang="tfn">-ina</i></span>. Plurals for non-persons that are animate are indicated by the noun suffixes <span title="Tanaina-language text"><i lang="tfn">-qa</i></span>, <span title="Tanaina-language text"><i lang="tfn">-ha</i></span>, and <span title="Tanaina-language text"><i lang="tfn">-yi</i></span>. Inanimate plurals are unable to be indicated by a noun suffix, and instead attach to the verb.
</p><p>For examples of person indication on the verb, see the chart under the morphology section above concerning the verb root <span title="Tanaina-language text"><i lang="tfn">-lan</i></span>. Denaʼina specifies between 1st person singular/plural, 2nd person singular/plural, 3rd person singular/plural, and areal.
</p><p>Verbs fall into many categories that are broadly lumped into "active" and "neuter", where an active verb indicates movement, a state of being incomplete, something being made, or in the production of sound, and a neuter verb indicates a general state of being that is complete. Categories of classification that are affixed to a verb also can refer to certain characteristics of the object of that verb. Depending on the gender affix that follows the classificatory affix, the nature of the object can change, as indicated by the following chart:

<p>However, there are other categories of classification or instrumentation that indicate how an action was done or aspects about the outcome of the action. Many instrumental affixes have become causative over time. Causality is expressed by changing a classifier in the verb to "ł". Instrumental affixes that indicate the manner or motion of an action include the following: "-aqʼa", which refers to clubbing an object or leaving a depression in the snow; "-dni", which refers to causing an object to leave, disappear, or die; "-du", which refers to affecting an object with the mouth; "-eł,-eła, and -ł", which all indicate that the object being referred to was used in an instrumental sense; "-iqu (uqu)", which refers to a pointing motion; "-kʼ", which refers to a wiping motion; and "-lu", which refers to the use of a hand.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Space_relations">Space relations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dena%CA%BCina_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Space relations"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Postpositions">Postpositions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dena%CA%BCina_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Postpositions"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Denaʼina shows space relations through the addition of morphemes that are either independent or bound, known as postpositions.
</p>
<ul><li>Independent postpositions follow a noun. For example, "miłni det" (without water) is composed of the noun "miłni" (water) and the post position "det" (without).</li>
<li>Bound postpositions follow a pronoun or a noun, but are said and written as one word, and are often related to directionals.
<ul><li>Sheł ("with me") is composed of the pronoun sh ("me") and the postposition eł ("with").</li>
<li>Object + -ch' (towards the object, in the direction of the object)
<ul><li><span title="Tanaina-language romanization"><i lang="tfn-Latn">Nach' gheyuł</i></span> = "He is walking towards us"</li></ul></li>
<li>Object + -a (object spends time)
<ul><li><span title="Tanaina-language romanization"><i lang="tfn-Latn">Ba shan hghizet</i></span>  = "He spent the whole summer day"</li>
<li>Be = "him/her" (be + a = ba)</li></ul></li></ul></li></ul>
<p>Postpositions can also be incorporated into a verb as a prefix.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Demonstratives">Demonstratives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dena%CA%BCina_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Demonstratives"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Space relations can also be marked by demonstratives pronouns, which indicate proximal/distal distinction.
</p>
<ul><li><span title="Tanaina-language romanization"><span style="font-style: normal" lang="tfn-Latn">ghin</span></span> - that</li>
<li><span title="Tanaina-language romanization"><span style="font-style: normal" lang="tfn-Latn">ghini</span></span> - that/these things, non-human and distant</li>
<li><span title="Tanaina-language romanization"><span style="font-style: normal" lang="tfn-Latn">gini</span></span> - this/these things, non-human and close by</li>
<li><span title="Tanaina-language romanization"><span style="font-style: normal" lang="tfn-Latn">ghu</span></span> - there in the distance</li>
<li><span title="Tanaina-language romanization"><span style="font-style: normal" lang="tfn-Latn">ghunen</span></span> - that person, human and distant</li>
<li><span title="Tanaina-language romanization"><span style="font-style: normal" lang="tfn-Latn">gu</span></span> - here, nearby</li>
<li><span title="Tanaina-language romanization"><span style="font-style: normal" lang="tfn-Latn">gunen</span></span> - this person, human and close by</li>
<li><span title="Tanaina-language romanization"><span style="font-style: normal" lang="tfn-Latn">ghuna</span></span> - those persons, human and distant</li>
<li><span title="Tanaina-language romanization"><span style="font-style: normal" lang="tfn-Latn">guna</span></span> - these persons, human and close by</li></ul>
<p>For example:
</p>
<ul><li><span title="Tanaina-language romanization"><span style="font-style: normal" lang="tfn-Latn">ghini dghili</span></span> = "those mountains"</li>
<li><span title="Tanaina-language romanization"><span style="font-style: normal" lang="tfn-Latn">ghunen uniłni</span></span> = "that person calls"</li>
<li><span title="Tanaina-language romanization"><span style="font-style: normal" lang="tfn-Latn">gunen qisen</span></span> = "this woman"</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Adverbs">Adverbs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dena%CA%BCina_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Adverbs"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Adverbs of location and direction can also convey space relations.
</p>
<ul><li>nes - out from center
<ul><li>nes yanił chet = "he shoved the boat out"</li></ul></li>
<li>en - off or away from
<ul><li>ye'un = "away from it"</li>
<li>ye'un ti'ilgguk = "he went out the door"</li></ul></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Directionals">Directionals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dena%CA%BCina_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Directionals"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The directional system in Denaʼina is based upon river flows, and are used with directional prefixes, roots, and suffixes.
</p>
<ul><li>Prefixes:
<ul><li>ey - unmarked</li>
<li>yu - distant</li>
<li>du - near</li></ul></li>
<li>Roots:
<ul><li>n'e or ni - upstream</li>
<li>du or t'e - downstream</li></ul></li>
<li>Suffixes:
<ul><li>∅ - towards</li>
<li>-ch' - towards, form</li>
<li>-t - at, the place of</li></ul></li></ul>
<p>For example, yunit means "at a place far upstream", and is composed of the prefix "yu", root "ni", and suffix "t".
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Time_and_tense">Time and tense</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dena%CA%BCina_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Time and tense"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Temporal_adverbs">Temporal adverbs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dena%CA%BCina_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Temporal adverbs"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Temporal adverbs convey information about when an action or intent of the verb occurred.
</p>
<ul><li><span title="Tanaina-language romanization"><span style="font-style: normal" lang="tfn-Latn">ch'qeyan</span></span> - always</li>
<li><span title="Tanaina-language romanization"><span style="font-style: normal" lang="tfn-Latn">da'a</span></span> - never</li>
<li><span title="Tanaina-language romanization"><span style="font-style: normal" lang="tfn-Latn">janiq'</span></span> - all day</li>
<li><span title="Tanaina-language romanization"><span style="font-style: normal" lang="tfn-Latn">janq'u</span></span> - still</li></ul>
<p>For example:
</p>
<ul><li><span title="Tanaina-language romanization"><span style="font-style: normal" lang="tfn-Latn">janiq'e ghestnu</span></span> = "I worked all day"</li>
<li><span title="Tanaina-language romanization"><span style="font-style: normal" lang="tfn-Latn">nunigi ch'qeyan</span></span> = "always fog"</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mode">Mode</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dena%CA%BCina_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Mode"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Mode indicates when the action happens. Normal mode is also referred to as tense, and is given by mode/aspect prefix positions in the verbs. The tense modes are the imperfect (present), perfect (past) and the future represented by 4 types of imperfectives—∅, z, n, gh—and 4 types of perfectives—gh, z, n, ∅. There are also 4 modal variations: neuter, inceptive, optative-intentional, and negative.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Tense">Tense</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dena%CA%BCina_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Tense"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<ul><li>Imperfective mode - action was started and is continuing at the present time
<ul><li>∅ imperfective and gh imperfective - generally means the action in moving toward completion</li>
<li>n imperfective - associated w/ motion verbs</li>
<li>z imperfective - generally means the action is static</li></ul></li>
<li>Perfect mode - action was started and completed in the past
<ul><li>gh perfective - most common perfect structure in most aspects</li>
<li>n perfective - often used with motion verbs when action has reached terminal state</li>
<li>z perfective - generally means the actions have achieved a stable state</li>
<li>∅ perfective - used in transitional themes</li></ul></li>
<li>Future mode - action has yet to happen. Used with a future stem, a "ghe" in the mode position, and a "t(e)" in the inceptive position</li></ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Modal_variations">Modal variations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dena%CA%BCina_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Modal variations"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<ul><li>Optative-intentional mode - expresses an intent to act</li>
<li>Inceptive mode - "beginning to" in the imperfect and perfect modes</li>
<li>Imperfect inceptive</li>
<li>Perfective inceptive</li>
<li>Neuter - applicable to neuter verbs, and "to be" neuter</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Aspect">Aspect</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dena%CA%BCina_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Aspect"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Aspect conveys information about how the action happened, and works in conjunction with tense. The most common aspects are conclusive, momentaneous, neuter, onomatopoetic, and semelfactive. The morpheme attached to the root verb can also change depending on aspect.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modality">Modality</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dena%CA%BCina_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Modality"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Modality is most commonly and easily seen in evidentials which usually appear at the end of a sentence.
</p>
<ul><li>Evidentials - words that emphasize certainty
<ul><li><span title="Tanaina-language romanization"><span style="font-style: normal" lang="tfn-Latn">shughu</span></span> - then it is</li>
<li>ł - it is said, it seems, apparently</li>
<li>For example:
<ul><li><span title="Tanaina-language romanization"><span style="font-style: normal" lang="tfn-Latn">shi shughu</span></span> = "I am the one"</li>
<li><span title="Tanaina-language romanization"><span style="font-style: normal" lang="tfn-Latn">Denaʼina łu k'elen hqghe'n ninya egh</span></span> = "The Denaʼina, they say, had some beliefs about the animals"</li></ul></li></ul></li>
<li>Emphatics - words that add emphasis
<ul><li><span title="Tanaina-language romanization"><span style="font-style: normal" lang="tfn-Latn">shida</span></span> - I am</li>
<li>For example:
<ul><li><span title="Tanaina-language romanization"><span style="font-style: normal" lang="tfn-Latn">Albert shi shida</span></span> = "I am Albert, this is who I am"</li></ul></li></ul></li></ul>
<h2><span id="Predicates.2C_arguments.2C_and_case"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Predicates,_arguments,_and_case">Predicates, arguments, and case</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dena%CA%BCina_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Predicates, arguments, and case"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Predicate_(grammar)" title="Predicate (grammar)">Predicate</a> refers to the main verb and auxiliary verbs, while <a href="/wiki/Argument_(linguistics)" title="Argument (linguistics)">arguments</a> usually refer to those words outside of the predicate.
</p><p>Word order in the basic Denaʼina sentence is subject-object-verb (SOV).  Because of this, there is a low danger of referential ambiguity. It is rare to have both the subject and the objects as nouns; instead, one or both usually occur as pronouns. Some sentences differ from the SOV structure. In subject-verb (SV), the object is embedded in the verb as a pronoun, or the sentence doesn't require an object. In object-verb (OV), the subject is a pronoun contained in the verb, and the object is a noun.
</p><p>An example sentence structure: object pronoun (argument) + outer subject pronoun (argument) + (other prefixes) + inner subject pronoun (argument) + ... verb stem (predicate). This example shows multiple arguments attaching to a single predicate.
</p><p>A full clause can be expressed in the verb. In the verb, the verb stem is last, and even when embedded in the verb, the object and subject necessarily come first. In Denaʼina, all verbs require a nominative (subject) and an accusative (object), which indicates a nominative-accusative case. This means the marked morphemes, or those that change to convey more specific meanings, are those that indicate the object. Distinction occurs between the nominative and accusative, and each would have its own core argument.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Obliques">Obliques</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dena%CA%BCina_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Obliques"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Oblique_case" title="Oblique case">Obliques</a> indicate <a href="/wiki/Instrumental_case" title="Instrumental case">instrumentals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Locative_case" title="Locative case">locatives</a>, and other arguments outside of core arguments. Both core and oblique arguments attach to the verb via prefixes which must occur in a certain order. In Denaʼina, obliques are prefixes to the verb which occur between the object prefix and the inner subject pronoun prefix and/or the outer subject pronoun.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Possession">Possession</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dena%CA%BCina_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Possession"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Inherent_possession">Inherent possession</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dena%CA%BCina_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Inherent possession"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Denaʼina has inherently <a href="/wiki/Possessive" title="Possessive">possessed</a> nouns and non-verbs. Inherently possessed words consist of a prefix and a stem which are abound morphemes. For example, "shunkda" means "my mother", where "sh-" is the possessive pronoun meaning "my", and "-unkda" is the possessed root meaning "mother".
</p><p>The pronouns used with inherently possessed bound morphemes:
</p>
<ul><li>sh- = my</li>
<li>qu- = their</li>
<li>n- = your (singular)</li>
<li>k'e- = someone's</li>
<li>be- = his/her</li>
<li>de- = his/her own</li>
<li>na = our</li>
<li>deh- = their own</li>
<li>h- = your (plural)</li>
<li>nił- = each other's</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-inherent_possession">Non-inherent possession</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dena%CA%BCina_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Non-inherent possession"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Non-inherent possession occurs with proper nouns as the possessor, shown by -a or -'a attached as a suffix to the possessed noun. Possessive pronouns are attached as a prefix to the possessed noun and the -'a is added at the end of the word.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Complement_clauses">Complement clauses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dena%CA%BCina_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Complement clauses"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Complement_clause" class="mw-redirect" title="Complement clause">Complement clauses</a> are clauses that act as the direct object of the verb, introduced by a <a href="/wiki/Complementizer" title="Complementizer">complementizer</a> (e.g.: in English, "that" or "which"). Complement clauses exist as subordinate clauses and bare clauses, as with other languages in the Athabaskan language family. Some complement clauses are marked by <a href="/wiki/Enclitic" class="mw-redirect" title="Enclitic">enclitics</a>, and are always embedded as part of the sentence.
</p><p>Relative suffixes are attached to nouns or verbs, and are one of the few suffixes on verbs. Common relative suffixes include:
</p>
<ul><li>-en = "the person that"</li>
<li>-t = "the place that"</li>
<li>-na = "the people that"</li>
<li>-h = "at, to a general area"</li>
<li>-i = "the thing that"</li>
<li>-hdi = "then, next"</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Endangerment">Endangerment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dena%CA%BCina_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Endangerment"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The population of Denaʼina is 900. As of 2007, there are 75-90 speakers, and in 1970 there were only 10 speakers of the Kenai dialect.
</p><p>Linguist <a href="/wiki/Michael_E._Krauss" title="Michael E. Krauss">Michael E. Krauss</a> provides three levels of <a href="/wiki/Language_endangerment" class="mw-redirect" title="Language endangerment">endangerment</a>: safe; endangered, where the language is being learned by children but requires community effort to maintain it; and moribund, where the language is not being learned by children. According to this classification, the Denaʼina is a moribund language.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Revitalization_efforts">Revitalization efforts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dena%CA%BCina_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Revitalization efforts"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are various efforts to revitalize the language.
</p>
<ul><li>The Denaʼina Archiving, Training and Access grant (DATA) aims to archive and provide access to Denaʼina materials. It also attempts to train community members in technology.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Lake_Clark_National_Park_and_Preserve" title="Lake Clark National Park and Preserve">Lake Clark National Park</a> maintains a catalog of audio recordings of the language.</li>
<li>Some books are being published on Denaʼina language and culture, and there is a yearly Denaʼina festival, followed by a three-week intensive course led by elders.</li>
<li>At the <a href="/wiki/Kenai_Peninsula_College" class="mw-redirect" title="Kenai Peninsula College">Kenai Peninsula College</a>, there is a language class on the Cook Inlet dialect. As of October 2014, there are only 15 students, all young adults, in the class, but this indicates momentum.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The class's curriculum is formed from the collected grammars published by linguists.</li></ul>
<p>Contributing factors to the endangerment include the policy of early territorial schools to not let native students speak their own language, especially in regards to the Kenai dialect. This policy was often enforced via corporal punishment; the trauma caused elders, all within one generation, to avoid speaking the language.
</p>
