<p><b>South Estonian</b> is either a <a href="/wiki/Finnic_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Finnic language">Finnic language</a> or an <a href="/wiki/Estonian_language" title="Estonian language">Estonian</a> dialect, spoken in south-eastern <a href="/wiki/Estonia" title="Estonia">Estonia</a>, encompassing the Tartu, Mulgi, <a href="/wiki/V%C3%B5ro_language" title="VÃµro language">VÃµro</a> and <a href="/wiki/Seto_dialect" title="Seto dialect">Seto</a> <a href="/wiki/Dialect#Dialect_or_language" title="Dialect">varieties</a>. There is no academic consensus on its status as a dialect or language.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Sammallahti_1977_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sammallahti_1977-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Diachrony" class="mw-redirect" title="Diachrony">Diachronically</a> speaking, North and South Estonian are separate branches of the Finnic languages.<sup id="cite_ref-Sammallahti_1977_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sammallahti_1977-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Kallio_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kallio-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Modern Standard Estonian has evolved on the basis of the dialects of Northern Estonia. However, from the 17th to the 19th centuries in Southern Estonia, literature was published in a standardized form of Southern Tartu and Northern VÃµro. That usage was called Tartu or literary South Estonian.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The written standard was used in the schools, churches and courts of the VÃµro and Tartu linguistic area but not in the Seto and Mulgi areas.
</p><p>After Estonia gained independence in 1918, the standardized Estonian language policies were implemented further throughout the country. The government officials during the era believed that the Estonian state needed to have one standard language for all of its citizens, which led to the exclusion of South Estonian in education. The ban on the instruction and the use of South Estonian dialects in schools continued during the Soviet occupation (1940â1990).<sup id="cite_ref-CSOSS_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CSOSS-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Since Estonia regained independence in 1991, the Estonian government has become more supportive of the protection and development of South Estonian.<sup id="cite_ref-CSOSS_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CSOSS-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> A modernized literary form founded on the <a href="/wiki/V%C3%B5ro_language" title="VÃµro language">VÃµro</a> dialect of South Estonian has been sanctioned.<sup id="cite_ref-LPOE_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LPOE-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-estnc_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-estnc-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>The present varieties of the South Estonian language area are Mulgi, Tartu, VÃµro and <a href="/wiki/Seto_dialect" title="Seto dialect">Seto</a>. VÃµro and Seto have remained furthest from the standard written Estonian language and are most difficult for speakers of standard Estonian to understand.<sup id="cite_ref-estnc_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-estnc-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-TUL_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TUL-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Three <a href="/wiki/Enclave" class="mw-redirect" title="Enclave">enclave</a> dialects of South Estonian have been attested. The <a href="/wiki/Leivu" class="mw-redirect" title="Leivu">Leivu</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ludza_dialect" title="Ludza dialect">Lutsi</a> enclaves in Latvia became extinct in the 20th century.<sup id="cite_ref-TUL_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TUL-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Kraasna" class="mw-redirect" title="Kraasna">Kraasna</a> enclave in Russia, still aware of their identity, have been assimilated linguistically by Russians.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=South_Estonian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The two different historical Estonian languages, North and South Estonian, are based on the ancestors of modern Estonians' migration into the territory of Estonia in at least two different waves, both groups speaking differing <a href="/wiki/Finnic_languages" title="Finnic languages">Finnic</a> vernaculars.<sup id="cite_ref-LPOE_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LPOE-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template"><span style="white-space: nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Link_rot" title="Wikipedia:Link rot"><span title="&#160;Dead link tagged October 2015">dead link</span></a></i>&#93;</span></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Some of the most ancient<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. <figure class="mw-halign-right" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Speakers-of-South-Estonian-2012.png" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/Speakers-of-South-Estonian-2012.png/300px-Speakers-of-South-Estonian-2012.png" decoding="async" width="300" height="300" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/Speakers-of-South-Estonian-2012.png/450px-Speakers-of-South-Estonian-2012.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/43/Speakers-of-South-Estonian-2012.png 2x" data-file-width="466" data-file-height="466" /></a><figcaption>According to the <a href="/wiki/2011_Estonia_Census" class="mw-redirect" title="2011 Estonia Census">2011 Estonia Census</a> there were 101,857 speakers of South Estonian: 87,048 speakers of VÃµro (including 12,549 Seto speakers), 9,698 Mulgi speakers, 4,109 Tartu language speakers and 1,002 other South Estonian speakers.</figcaption></figure>
<p>The South Estonian language began to undergo a revival in the late 1980s. Today, South Estonian is used in the works of some of Estonia's most well-known playwrights, poets, and authors. Most success has been achieved in promoting the <a href="/wiki/V%C3%B5ro_language" title="VÃµro language">VÃµro language</a> and a new literary standard based on VÃµro. Mulgi and especially Tartu dialects, however, have very few speakers left. The 2011 census in Estonia counted 101,857 self-reported speakers of South Estonian: 87,048 speakers of VÃµro (including 12,549 Seto speakers), 9,698 Mulgi speakers, 4,109 Tartu language speakers and 1,002 other South Estonian speakers who didn't specify their regional language/dialect.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The 2021 census in Estonia counted 159,900 self-reported speakers of South Estonian: 125,780 speakers of VÃµro (including 26,220 Seto speakers), 14,380 Mulgi speakers and 19,740 Tartu language speakers.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
