<p><b>Dzongkha</b> (<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1106655535"><span lang="dz" class="uchen" style="font-size:1.25em; vertical-align:middle; word-wrap:break-word;">རྫོང་ཁ་</span>; <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA" title="Help:IPA">&#91;t͡sòŋkʰɑ́&#93;</a></span>) is a <a href="/wiki/Sino-Tibetan_languages" title="Sino-Tibetan languages">Sino-Tibetan language</a> that is the official and national language of <a href="/wiki/Bhutan" title="Bhutan">Bhutan</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-CoBE_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CoBE-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> It is written using the <a href="/wiki/Tibetan_script" title="Tibetan script">Tibetan script</a>.
</p><p>The word <span title="Dzongkha-language romanization"><i lang="dz-Latn">dzongkha</i></span> means "the language of the fortress", from <i><span title="Dzongkha-language romanization"><i lang="dz-Latn"><a href="/wiki/Districts_of_Bhutan" title="Districts of Bhutan">dzong</a></i></span></i> "fortress" and <i><span title="Dzongkha-language romanization"><i lang="dz-Latn">kha</i></span></i> "language". As of 2013<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Dzongkha&amp;action=edit">&#91;update&#93;</a></sup>, Dzongkha had 171,080 native speakers and about 640,000 total speakers.<sup id="cite_ref-How_many_people_speak_Dzongkha_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-How_many_people_speak_Dzongkha-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Dzongkha is a <a href="/wiki/Tibetic_languages" title="Tibetic languages">South Tibetic language</a>. It is closely related to and partially intelligible with <a href="/wiki/Sikkimese_language" title="Sikkimese language">Sikkimese</a>, and to some other Bhutanese languages such as <a href="/wiki/Chocangaca_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Chocangaca language">Chocha Ngacha</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brokpa_language" title="Brokpa language">Brokpa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brokkat_language" title="Brokkat language">Brokkat</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lakha_language" title="Lakha language">Lakha</a>. It has a more distant relationship to <a href="/wiki/Standard_Tibetan" class="mw-redirect" title="Standard Tibetan">Standard Tibetan</a>. Spoken Dzongkha and Tibetan are around 50 to 80 percent <a href="/wiki/Mutual_intelligibility" title="Mutual intelligibility">mutually intelligible</a>.
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Usage">Usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dzongkha&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Usage"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Dzongkha and its dialects are the native tongue of eight western districts of Bhutan (<i><a href="/wiki/Viz." title="Viz.">viz.</a></i> <a href="/wiki/Wangdue_Phodrang" title="Wangdue Phodrang">Wangdue Phodrang</a>, <span title="Dzongkha-language text"><span lang="dz-Latn" style="font-style: normal;"><a href="/wiki/Punakha" title="Punakha">Punakha</a></span></span>, <a href="/wiki/Thimphu" title="Thimphu">Thimphu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gasa_District" title="Gasa District">Gasa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paro_District" title="Paro District">Paro</a>, <a href="/wiki/Haa_District" title="Haa District">Ha</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dagana_District" title="Dagana District">Dagana</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chukha_District" title="Chukha District">Chukha</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> There are also some native speakers near the Indian town of <a href="/wiki/Kalimpong" title="Kalimpong">Kalimpong</a>, once part of Bhutan but now in <a href="/wiki/North_Bengal" title="North Bengal">North Bengal</a>, and in <a href="/wiki/Sikkim" title="Sikkim">Sikkim</a>.
</p><p>Dzongkha was declared the national language of Bhutan in 1971.<sup id="cite_ref-Guide_to_Official_Dzongkha_Romanization_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Guide_to_Official_Dzongkha_Romanization-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Dzongkha study is mandatory in all schools, and the language is the <i><a href="/wiki/Lingua_franca" title="Lingua franca">lingua franca</a></i> in the districts to the south and east where it is not the mother tongue. The Bhutanese films <i><a href="/wiki/Travellers_and_Magicians" title="Travellers and Magicians">Travellers and Magicians</a></i> (2003) and <i><a href="/wiki/Lunana:_A_Yak_in_the_Classroom" title="Lunana: A Yak in the Classroom">Lunana: A Yak in the Classroom</a></i> (2019) are in Dzongkha.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Writing_system">Writing system</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dzongkha&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Writing system"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Tibetan_script" title="Tibetan script">Tibetan script</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dzongkha_numerals" title="Dzongkha numerals">Dzongkha numerals</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Dzongkha_Braille" title="Dzongkha Braille">Dzongkha Braille</a></div>
<figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Dzongkha_-_in_Bhutanese_script.svg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Dzongkha_-_in_Bhutanese_script.svg/220px-Dzongkha_-_in_Bhutanese_script.svg.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="175" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Dzongkha_-_in_Bhutanese_script.svg/330px-Dzongkha_-_in_Bhutanese_script.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Dzongkha_-_in_Bhutanese_script.svg/440px-Dzongkha_-_in_Bhutanese_script.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="516" data-file-height="410" /></a><figcaption>The word "Dzongkha" in Jôyi, a Bhutanese form of the Uchen script</figcaption></figure>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Tibetan_script" title="Tibetan script">Tibetan script</a> used to write Dzongkha has thirty basic <a href="/wiki/Letter_(alphabet)" title="Letter (alphabet)">letters</a>, sometimes known as "radicals", for <a href="/wiki/Consonant" title="Consonant">consonants</a>. Dzongkha is usually written in Bhutanese forms of the <a href="/wiki/Uchen_script" title="Uchen script">Uchen script</a>, forms of the Tibetan script known as <i>Jôyi</i> "cursive longhand" and <i>Jôtshum</i> "formal longhand". The print form is known simply as <i>Tshûm</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Driem47_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Driem47-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Romanization">Romanization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dzongkha&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Romanization"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are various systems of romanization and transliteration for Dzongkha, but none accurately represents its phonetic sound.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The Bhutanese government adopted a transcription system known as Roman Dzongkha, devised by the linguist <a href="/wiki/George_van_Driem" title="George van Driem">George van Driem</a>, as its standard in 1991.<sup id="cite_ref-Guide_to_Official_Dzongkha_Romanization_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Guide_to_Official_Dzongkha_Romanization-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Phonology">Phonology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dzongkha&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Phonology"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tones">Tones</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dzongkha&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Tones"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Dzongkha is a <a href="/wiki/Tonal_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Tonal language">tonal language</a> and has two register tones: high and low.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEvan_Driem1992_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEvan_Driem1992-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The tone of a <a href="/wiki/Syllable" title="Syllable">syllable</a> determines the <a href="/wiki/Allophone" title="Allophone">allophone</a> of the onset and the <a href="/wiki/Phonation" title="Phonation">phonation</a> type of the nuclear vowel.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDowns2011_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDowns2011-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>All consonants may begin a syllable. In the onsets of low-tone syllables, consonants are <a href="/wiki/Voice_(phonetics)" title="Voice (phonetics)">voiced</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDowns2011_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDowns2011-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Aspirated_consonant" title="Aspirated consonant">Aspirated consonants</a> (indicated by the superscript <i>h</i>), <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ɬ/</span>, and <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/h/</span> are not found in low-tone syllables.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDowns2011_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDowns2011-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The rhotic <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/r/</span> is usually a trill <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">&#91;<a href="/wiki/Voiced_alveolar_trill" class="mw-redirect" title="Voiced alveolar trill">r</a>&#93;</span> or a fricative trill <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">&#91;<a href="/wiki/Voiced_alveolar_fricative_trill" class="mw-redirect" title="Voiced alveolar fricative trill">r̝</a>&#93;</span>,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEvan_Driem1992_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEvan_Driem1992-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> and is voiceless in the onsets of high-tone syllables.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDowns2011_9-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDowns2011-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/t,<span class="wrap"> </span>tʰ,<span class="wrap"> </span>ts,<span class="wrap"> </span>tsʰ,<span class="wrap"> </span>s/</span> are <a href="/wiki/Dental_consonant" title="Dental consonant">dental</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEvan_Driem1992_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEvan_Driem1992-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Descriptions of the <a href="/wiki/Palatal_consonant" title="Palatal consonant">palatal affricates</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fricative" title="Fricative">fricatives</a> vary from <a href="/wiki/Alveolo-palatal_consonant" title="Alveolo-palatal consonant">alveolo-palatal</a> to plain palatal.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEvan_Driem1992_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEvan_Driem1992-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMichailovskyMazaudon1989_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMichailovskyMazaudon1989-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDowns2011_9-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDowns2011-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Only a few consonants are found in syllable-final positions. Most common among them are <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/m,<span class="wrap"> </span>n,<span class="wrap"> </span>p/</span>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDowns2011_9-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDowns2011-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Syllable-final <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ŋ/</span> is often <a href="/wiki/Elision" title="Elision">elided</a> and results in the preceding vowel <a href="/wiki/Nasalized" class="mw-redirect" title="Nasalized">nasalized</a> and prolonged, especially word-finally.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEvan_Driem1994_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEvan_Driem1994-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDowns2011_9-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDowns2011-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Syllable-final <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/k/</span> is most often omitted when word-final as well, unless in formal speech.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDowns2011_9-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDowns2011-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> In literary pronunciation, <a href="/wiki/Liquid_consonant" title="Liquid consonant">liquids</a> <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/r/</span> and <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/l/</span> may also end a syllable.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEvan_Driem1992_8-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEvan_Driem1992-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Though rare, <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ɕ/</span> is also found in syllable-final positions.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEvan_Driem1992_8-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEvan_Driem1992-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDowns2011_9-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDowns2011-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> No other consonants are found in syllable-final positions.
</p>

<ul><li>When in low tone, vowels are produced with <a href="/wiki/Breathy_voice" title="Breathy voice">breathy voice</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEvan_Driem1992_8-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEvan_Driem1992-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEvan_Driem1994_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEvan_Driem1994-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup></li>

<li><span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/yː/</span> varies between <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">&#91;<a href="/wiki/Close_front_rounded_vowel" title="Close front rounded vowel">yː</a>&#93;</span> and <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">&#91;<a href="/wiki/Near-close_near-front_rounded_vowel" title="Near-close near-front rounded vowel">ʏː</a>&#93;</span>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEvan_Driem1992_8-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEvan_Driem1992-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/e/</span> varies between close-mid <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">&#91;<a href="/wiki/Close-mid_front_unrounded_vowel" title="Close-mid front unrounded vowel">e</a>&#93;</span> and open-mid <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">&#91;<a href="/wiki/Open-mid_front_unrounded_vowel" title="Open-mid front unrounded vowel">ɛ</a>&#93;</span>, the latter being common in closed syllables. <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/eː/</span> is close-mid <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">&#91;<a href="/wiki/Close-mid_front_unrounded_vowel" title="Close-mid front unrounded vowel">eː</a>&#93;</span>. <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/eː/</span> may not be longer than <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/e/</span> at all, and differs from <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/e/</span> more often in quality than in length.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEvan_Driem1992_8-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEvan_Driem1992-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Descriptions of <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/øː/</span> vary between close-mid <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">&#91;<a href="/wiki/Close-mid_front_rounded_vowel" title="Close-mid front rounded vowel">øː</a>&#93;</span> and open-mid <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">&#91;<a href="/wiki/Open-mid_front_rounded_vowel" title="Open-mid front rounded vowel">œː</a>&#93;</span>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEvan_Driem1992_8-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEvan_Driem1992-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDowns2011_9-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDowns2011-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/o/</span> is close-mid <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">&#91;<a href="/wiki/Close-mid_back_rounded_vowel" title="Close-mid back rounded vowel">o</a>&#93;</span>, but may approach open-mid <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">&#91;<a href="/wiki/Open-mid_back_rounded_vowel" title="Open-mid back rounded vowel">ɔ</a>&#93;</span> especially in closed syllables. <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/oː/</span> is close-mid <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">&#91;<a href="/wiki/Close-mid_back_rounded_vowel" title="Close-mid back rounded vowel">oː</a>&#93;</span>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEvan_Driem1992_8-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEvan_Driem1992-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ɛː/</span> is slightly lower than open-mid, i.e. <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">&#91;<a href="/wiki/Open-mid_front_unrounded_vowel" title="Open-mid front unrounded vowel">ɛ̞ː</a>&#93;</span>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEvan_Driem1992_8-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEvan_Driem1992-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ɑ/</span> may approach <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">&#91;<a href="/wiki/Near-open_central_vowel" title="Near-open central vowel">ɐ</a>&#93;</span>, especially in closed syllables.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEvan_Driem1992_8-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEvan_Driem1992-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDowns2011_9-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDowns2011-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>When nasalized or followed by <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ŋ]</span>, vowels are always long.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEvan_Driem1994_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEvan_Driem1994-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDowns2011_9-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDowns2011-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Phonotactics">Phonotactics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dzongkha&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Phonotactics"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Many words in Dzongkha are <a href="/wiki/Monosyllable" title="Monosyllable">monosyllabic</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDowns2011_9-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDowns2011-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Syllables usually take the form of CVC, CV, or VC.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDowns2011_9-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDowns2011-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Syllables with complex onsets are also found, but such an onset must be a combination of an unaspirated bilabial stop and a palatal affricate.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDowns2011_9-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDowns2011-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The bilabial stops in complex onsets are often omitted in colloquial speech.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDowns2011_9-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDowns2011-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification_and_related_languages">Classification and related languages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dzongkha&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Classification and related languages"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Dzongkha is considered a <a href="/wiki/Tibetic_languages" title="Tibetic languages">South Tibetic language</a>. It is closely related to and partially intelligible with <a href="/wiki/Sikkimese_language" title="Sikkimese language">Sikkimese</a>, and to some other Bhutanese languages such as <a href="/wiki/Chocangaca_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Chocangaca language">Chocha Ngacha</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brokpa_language" title="Brokpa language">Brokpa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brokkat_language" title="Brokkat language">Brokkat</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lakha_language" title="Lakha language">Lakha</a>.
</p><p>Dzongkha bears a close linguistic relationship to J'umowa, which is spoken in the <a href="/wiki/Chumbi_Valley" title="Chumbi Valley">Chumbi Valley</a> of Southern <a href="/wiki/Tibet" title="Tibet">Tibet</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> It has a much more distant relationship to <a href="/wiki/Standard_Tibetan" class="mw-redirect" title="Standard Tibetan">Standard Tibetan</a>. Spoken Dzongkha and Tibetan are around 50% to 80% mutually intelligible, with the literary forms of both highly influenced by the <a href="/wiki/Liturgy" title="Liturgy">liturgical</a> (clerical) <a href="/wiki/Classical_Tibetan" title="Classical Tibetan">Classical Tibetan</a> language, known in Bhutan as <i>Chöke,</i> which has been used for centuries by <a href="/wiki/Buddhist_monks" class="mw-redirect" title="Buddhist monks">Buddhist monks</a>. Chöke was used as the language of education in Bhutan until the early 1960s when it was replaced by Dzongkha in public schools.<sup id="cite_ref-vandriem1998dzongkha_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vandriem1998dzongkha-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Although descended from Classical Tibetan, Dzongkha shows a great many irregularities in sound changes that make the official spelling and standard pronunciation more distant from each other than is the case with Standard Tibetan. "Traditional orthography and modern phonology are two distinct systems operating by a distinct set of rules."<sup id="cite_ref-Driem110_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Driem110-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
