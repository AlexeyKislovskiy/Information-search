<p>The <b>Laz language</b> or <b>Lazuri</b> (Laz: <span lang="lzz">ლაზური ნენა</span>, <small>romanized:&#160;</small><span title="Laz-language romanization"><i lang="lzz-Latn">lazuri nena</i></span>; Mingrelian: <span lang="xmf">ლაზური ნინა</span>, <small>romanized:&#160;</small><span title="Mingrelian-language romanization"><i lang="xmf-Latn">lazuri nina</i></span>; <a href="/wiki/Georgian_language" title="Georgian language">Georgian</a>&#58; <span title="Georgian-language text"><span lang="ka" style="font-style: normal;">ლაზური ენა/ჭანური ენა</span></span>, <span style="font-size: smaller;"><a href="/wiki/Romanization_of_Georgian" title="Romanization of Georgian">romanized</a>:</span> <span lang="ka-Latn" style="font-style: italic;">lazuri ena/ch'anuri ena</span>; <a href="/wiki/Turkish_language" title="Turkish language">Turkish</a>: <i lang="tr">Lazca</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Kartvelian_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Kartvelian language">Kartvelian language</a> spoken by the <a href="/wiki/Laz_people" title="Laz people">Laz people</a> on the southeastern shore of the <a href="/wiki/Black_Sea" title="Black Sea">Black Sea</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> In 2007, it was estimated that there were around 20,000 native speakers in <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>, in a strip of land extending from <a href="/wiki/Melyat" title="Melyat">Melyat</a> to the Georgian border (officially called <a href="/wiki/Lazistan" title="Lazistan">Lazistan</a> until 1925), and around 1,000 native speakers around <a href="/wiki/Adjara" title="Adjara">Adjara</a> in <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(country)" title="Georgia (country)">Georgia</a>. There are also around 1,000 native speakers of Laz in <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-e25_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-e25-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Laz is not historically a <a href="/wiki/Written_language" title="Written language">written language</a> or <a href="/wiki/Literary_language" title="Literary language">literary language</a>. As of 1989, Benninghaus could write that the Laz themselves had no interest in writing in Laz.<sup id="cite_ref-:5_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:5-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">Classification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Laz_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Classification"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Laz is one of the four <a href="/wiki/Kartvelian_languages" title="Kartvelian languages">Kartvelian languages</a> also known as South Caucasian languages. Along with <a href="/wiki/Mingrelian_language" title="Mingrelian language">Mingrelian</a>, it forms the <a href="/wiki/Zan_languages" title="Zan languages">Zan branch</a> of this <a href="/wiki/Kartvelian_languages" title="Kartvelian languages">Kartvelian language family</a>. The two languages are very closely related, to the extent that some linguists refer to Mingrelian and Laz as dialects or regional variants of a single <i><a href="/wiki/Zan_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Zan language">Zan language</a></i>, a view held officially in the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet</a> era and still so in Georgia today. In general, however, Mingrelian and Laz are considered as separate languages, due both to the long-standing separation of their communities of speakers (500 years) and to a lack of mutual intelligibility.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Laz_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although the Laz people are recorded in written sources repeatedly from antiquity onwards, the earliest written evidence of their language is from 1787. There is a poem in <a href="/wiki/Evliya_%C3%87elebi" title="Evliya Çelebi">Evliya Çelebi</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Seyahatn%C3%A2me" title="Seyahatnâme">Seyahatnâme</a></i> (17th century) that has been interpreted as Laz, but it is more likely to represent <a href="/wiki/Pontic_Greek" title="Pontic Greek">Pontic Greek</a>. The first definite record of Laz in 1787 was produced by the Spanish Jesuit linguist <a href="/wiki/Lorenzo_Herv%C3%A1s" title="Lorenzo Hervás">Lorenzo Hervás</a>. It was largely ignored because Hervás conflated the name of the language with that of the <a href="/wiki/Lezgian_language" title="Lezgian language">Lezgian language</a>, calling it <i>lingua Lasga, detta ancora Laza, e Lassa</i>. In 1823, <a href="/wiki/Julius_Heinrich_von_Klaproth" class="mw-redirect" title="Julius Heinrich von Klaproth">Julius Heinrich von Klaproth</a> published a list of 67 Laz words with German translations in his <i>Asia Polyglotta</i>. He identified three dialects. In 1844, <a href="/wiki/Georg_Rosen_(1821%E2%80%931891)" title="Georg Rosen (1821–1891)">Georg Rosen</a> published in German the first monograph on Laz, <i>Über die Sprache der Lazen</i>. In 1887, the British diplomat <a href="/w/index.php?title=Demetrius_Rudolph_Peacock&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Demetrius Rudolph Peacock (page does not exist)">Demetrius Rudolph Peacock</a> included Laz among five languages of the western Caucasus in a paper designed for the use of English-speaking diplomats.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geographical_distribution">Geographical distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Laz_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Geographical distribution"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<figure class="mw-default-size mw-halign-left" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Mother_language_in_1965_Turkey_census_-_Laz.png" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Mother_language_in_1965_Turkey_census_-_Laz.png/350px-Mother_language_in_1965_Turkey_census_-_Laz.png" decoding="async" width="350" height="171" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Mother_language_in_1965_Turkey_census_-_Laz.png/525px-Mother_language_in_1965_Turkey_census_-_Laz.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Mother_language_in_1965_Turkey_census_-_Laz.png/700px-Mother_language_in_1965_Turkey_census_-_Laz.png 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="500" /></a><figcaption>Laz-speaking population in Turkey according to the 1965 census</figcaption></figure>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Georgian_language" title="Georgian language">Georgian language</a>, along with its relatives Mingrelian, Laz, and <a href="/wiki/Svan_language" title="Svan language">Svan</a>, comprise the Kartvelian language family. The initial breakup of <a href="/wiki/Proto-Kartvelian_language" title="Proto-Kartvelian language">Proto-Kartvelian</a> is estimated to have been around 2500–2000 B.C., with the divergence of Svan from Proto-Kartvelian (Nichols, 1998). Assyrian, Urartian, Greek, and Roman documents reveal that in early historical times (2nd–1st millennia B.C.), the numerous Kartvelian tribes were in the process of migrating into the Caucasus from the southwest. The northern coast and coastal mountains of <a href="/wiki/Anatolia" title="Anatolia">Asia Minor</a> were dominated by Kartvelian peoples at least as far west as <a href="/wiki/Samsun" title="Samsun">Samsun</a>. Their eastward migration may have been set in motion by the <a href="/wiki/Trojan_War" title="Trojan War">fall of Troy</a> (dated by Eratosthenes to 1183 B.C.). It thus appears that the Kartvelians represent an intrusion into the Georgian plain from northeastern Anatolia, displacing their predecessors, the unrelated Northwest Caucasian and <a href="/wiki/Vainakh" class="mw-redirect" title="Vainakh">Vainakh peoples</a>, into the Caucasian highlands (Tuite, 1996; Nichols, 2004).<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The oldest known settlement of the Lazoi is the town of Lazos or "old Lazik" which <a href="/wiki/Arrian" title="Arrian">Arrian</a> puts 680 stadia (about 80 miles) south of the Sacred Port (<a href="/wiki/Novorossiysk" title="Novorossiysk">Novorossiisk</a>) and 1,020 stadia (100 miles) north of <a href="/wiki/Pitsunda" title="Pitsunda">Pityus</a>, i.e.somewhere in the neighborhood of <a href="/wiki/Tuapse" title="Tuapse">Tuapse</a>. Kiessling sees in the Lazoi a section of the Kerketai, who in the first centuries of the Christian era had to migrate southwards under pressure from the Zygoi. The same author regards the Kerketai as a "Georgian" tribe. The fact is that at the time of Arrian (2nd century A.D.), the Lazoi were already living to the south of Um. The order of the peoples living along the coast to the east of Trebizond was as follows: Colchi (and Sanni); Machelones; Heniochi; Zydritae; Lazai, subjects of King Malassus, who owned the suzerainty of Rome; <a href="/wiki/Apsilae" title="Apsilae">Apsilae</a>; <a href="/wiki/Abasgoi" title="Abasgoi">Abacsi</a>; Sanigae near Sebastopolis.<sup id="cite_ref-colchianstudies.files.wordpress.com_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-colchianstudies.files.wordpress.com-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Social_and_cultural_status">Social and cultural status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Laz_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Social and cultural status"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Okitxuseni_Supara.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Okitxuseni_Supara.jpg/250px-Okitxuseni_Supara.jpg" decoding="async" width="250" height="188" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Okitxuseni_Supara.jpg/375px-Okitxuseni_Supara.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Okitxuseni_Supara.jpg/500px-Okitxuseni_Supara.jpg 2x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="602" /></a><figcaption>A Laz book "<a href="/wiki/Mothertongue" class="mw-redirect" title="Mothertongue">Mothertongue</a>"</figcaption></figure>
<figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Mcita_murutsxi.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Mcita_murutsxi.jpg/250px-Mcita_murutsxi.jpg" decoding="async" width="250" height="353" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Mcita_murutsxi.jpg/375px-Mcita_murutsxi.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Mcita_murutsxi.jpg/500px-Mcita_murutsxi.jpg 2x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="1131" /></a><figcaption>A Laz newspaper in 1928</figcaption></figure>
<p>Laz has no official status in either Turkey or Georgia, and no written standard. It is presently used only for familiar and casual interaction; for literary, business, and other purposes, Laz speakers use their country's official language (<a href="/wiki/Turkish_language" title="Turkish language">Turkish</a> or Georgian).
</p><p>Laz is unique among the Kartvelian languages in that most of its speakers live in <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a> rather than Georgia. While the differences between the various dialects are minor, their speakers feel that their level of mutual intelligibility is low. Given that there is no common standard form of Laz, speakers of its different dialects use <a href="/wiki/Turkish_language" title="Turkish language">Turkish</a> to communicate with each other.
</p><p>Between 1930 and 1938, Zan (Laz and Mingrelian) enjoyed cultural autonomy in Georgia and was used as a literary language, but an official standard form of the language was never established. Since then, all attempts to create a written tradition in Zan have failed, despite the fact that most intellectuals use it as a literary language.
</p><p>In Turkey, Laz has been a written language since 1984, when an alphabet based on the <a href="/wiki/Turkish_alphabet" title="Turkish alphabet">Turkish alphabet</a> was created. Since then, this system has been used in most of the handful of publications that have appeared in Laz. Developed specifically for the Kartvelian languages, the <a href="/wiki/Georgian_alphabet" class="mw-redirect" title="Georgian alphabet">Georgian alphabet</a> is better suited to the sounds of Laz, but the fact that most of the language's speakers live in Turkey, where the Latin alphabet is used, has rendered the adoption of the former impossible. Nonetheless, 1991 saw the publication of a textbook called Nana-nena ('Mother tongue'), which was aimed at all Laz speakers and used both the Latin and Georgian alphabets. The first Laz–Turkish dictionary was published in 1999.
</p><p>Speaking Laz was forbidden in Turkey between 1980 and 1991 because this was seen as a political threat to unity of the country. During this era, some  of the academicians regret the existence of the Laz ethnic group. Because speaking Laz was banned in public areas, many children lost their mother tongue as a result of not communicating with their parents. Most Laz people have a heavy Turkish accent because they can not practice their mother tongue.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Writing_system">Writing system</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Laz_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Writing system"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><span class="anchor" id="Alphabet"></span>
Laz is written in <a href="/wiki/Mkhedruli_script" class="mw-redirect" title="Mkhedruli script">Mkhedruli script</a> and in an extension of the <a href="/wiki/Turkish_alphabet" title="Turkish alphabet">Turkish alphabet</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-kutscher-tl12_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kutscher-tl12-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> For the Laz letters written in the Latin script, the first is a letter from the writing system introduced in Turkey in 1984 that was developed by <a href="/w/index.php?title=Fahri_Lazo%C4%9Flu&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Fahri Lazoğlu (page does not exist)">Fahri Lazoğlu</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Wolfgang_Feurstein&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Wolfgang Feurstein (page does not exist)">Wolfgang Feurstein</a> and the second is the transcription system used by Caucasianists.<sup id="cite_ref-kutscher-tl12_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kutscher-tl12-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Linguistic_features">Linguistic features</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Laz_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Linguistic features"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Like many <a href="/wiki/Languages_of_the_Caucasus" title="Languages of the Caucasus">languages of the Caucasus</a>, Laz has a rich consonantal system but only five vowels (a, e, i, o, u). The <a href="/wiki/Noun" title="Noun">nouns</a> are inflected with <a href="/wiki/Agglutinative" class="mw-redirect" title="Agglutinative">agglutinative</a> suffixes to indicate <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_function" class="mw-redirect" title="Grammatical function">grammatical function</a> (four to seven <a href="/wiki/Case_(linguistics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Case (linguistics)">cases</a>, depending on the dialect) and <a href="/wiki/Number_(linguistics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Number (linguistics)">number</a> (singular or plural), but not by gender.
The Laz verb is inflected with suffixes according to person and number, and also for <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_tense" title="Grammatical tense">grammatical tense</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_aspect" title="Grammatical aspect">aspect</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_mood" title="Grammatical mood">mood</a>, and (in some dialects) <a href="/wiki/Evidentiality" title="Evidentiality">evidentiality</a>. Up to 50 verbal prefixes are used to indicate spatial orientation/direction. Person and number suffixes provided for the subject as well as for one or two objects involved in the action, e.g. <i>gimpulam</i> = "I hide it from you".
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Grammar">Grammar</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Laz_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Grammar"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1033289096">.mw-parser-output .hatnote{font-style:italic}.mw-parser-output div.hatnote{padding-left:1.6em;margin-bottom:0.5em}.mw-parser-output .hatnote i{font-style:normal}.mw-parser-output .hatnote+link+.hatnote{margin-top:-0.5em}</style><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Laz_grammar" title="Laz grammar">Laz grammar</a></div>
<p>Some distinctive features of Laz among its family are:
</p>
<ul><li>All nouns end with a vowel.</li>
<li>More extensive verb inflection, using directional prefixes.</li>
<li>Some lexical borrowings from Greek and <a href="/wiki/Turkic_languages" title="Turkic languages">Turkic languages</a>.</li></ul>
