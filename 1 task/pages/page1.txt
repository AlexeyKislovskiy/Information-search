<p><b>North Frisian</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Minority_language" title="Minority language">minority language</a> of <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, spoken by about 10,000 people in <a href="/wiki/North_Frisia" title="North Frisia">North Frisia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-sh_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sh-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The language is part of the larger group of the <a href="/wiki/West_Germanic_languages" title="West Germanic languages">West Germanic</a> <a href="/wiki/Frisian_languages" title="Frisian languages">Frisian languages</a>. The language comprises 10 dialects which are themselves divided into an insular and a mainland group.
</p><p>North Frisian is closely related to the <a href="/wiki/Saterland_Frisian_language" title="Saterland Frisian language">Saterland Frisian language</a> of Northwest Germany and <a href="/wiki/West_Frisian_language" title="West Frisian language">West Frisian</a> which is spoken in the Netherlands. All of these are also closely related to the <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English language</a> forming the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Frisian_languages" title="Anglo-Frisian languages">Anglo-Frisian</a> group.
</p><p>The phonological system of the North Frisian dialects is strongly being influenced by Standard German and is slowly adapting to that of the German language. With a number of native speakers probably even less than 10,000 and decreasing use in mainland North Frisia, the North Frisian language is endangered. It is protected as a minority language and has become an official language in the <a href="/wiki/Nordfriesland" class="mw-redirect" title="Nordfriesland">Nordfriesland</a> district and on <a href="/wiki/Heligoland" title="Heligoland">Heligoland</a> island.
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">Classification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=North_Frisian_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Classification"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The closest relatives of North Frisian are the two other Frisian languages, the <a href="/wiki/Saterland_Frisian_language" title="Saterland Frisian language">Saterland Frisian</a> of north-western <a href="/wiki/Lower_Saxony" title="Lower Saxony">Lower Saxony</a>, Germany, and the <a href="/wiki/West_Frisian_language" title="West Frisian language">West Frisian language</a> spoken in the northern <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>. Together, the three sub-groups form the group of Frisian languages.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a> is also closely related to Frisian. The two languages are classified in a common <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Frisian_languages" title="Anglo-Frisian languages">Anglo-Frisian</a> group, which is grouped among the <a href="/wiki/Ingvaeonic_languages" class="mw-redirect" title="Ingvaeonic languages">Ingvaeonic languages</a>, together with <a href="/wiki/Low_German" title="Low German">Low German</a>. The related Low German has developed differently since <a href="/wiki/Old_Saxon" title="Old Saxon">Old Saxon</a> times and has lost many Ingvaeonic characteristics.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dialects">Dialects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=North_Frisian_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Dialects"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=North_Frisian_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Overview"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The North Frisian dialects can be grouped into two main dialectal divisions: mainland and insular dialects. Altogether, both groups have 10 dialects.<sup id="cite_ref-arhammar_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-arhammar-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Since the beginning of Frisian linguistic studies in the 19th century, the following ten dialects have typically been noted as distinct:
</p>
<dl><dt>Insular North Frisian</dt></dl>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Sylt_Frisian" class="mw-redirect" title="Sylt Frisian">Sylt Frisian</a> (Söl'ring)</li>
<li>Föhr-Amrum Frisian (<a href="/wiki/F%C3%B6hr_Frisian" class="mw-redirect" title="Föhr Frisian">Fering</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amrum_Frisian" class="mw-redirect" title="Amrum Frisian">Öömrang</a>)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Heligolandic_Frisian" class="mw-redirect" title="Heligolandic Frisian">Heligolandic Frisian</a> (Halunder)</li></ul>
<dl><dt>Mainland North Frisian</dt></dl>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Wiedingharde_Frisian" title="Wiedingharde Frisian">Wiedingharde Frisian</a> (Wiringhiirder)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/B%C3%B6kingharde_Frisian" class="mw-redirect" title="Bökingharde Frisian">Bökingharde Frisian</a> (Mooringer)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Karrharde_Frisian" title="Karrharde Frisian">Karrharde Frisian</a> (Karrharder)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Goesharde_Frisian" title="Goesharde Frisian">Goesharde Frisian</a> (Gooshirder)
<ul><li>Northern Goesharde Frisian (incl. Hoorninger Fräisch &amp; Hoolmer Freesch)</li>
<li>Central Goesharde Frisian</li>
<li>Southern Goesharde Frisian (extinct since early 1980s)</li></ul></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Halligen_Frisian" title="Halligen Frisian">Halligen Frisian</a> (Halifreesk)</li></ul>
<p>The mainland and insular dialects clearly differ from each other because they were shaped by Frisian immigrants in different centuries. The islands of <a href="/wiki/Sylt" title="Sylt">Sylt</a>, <a href="/wiki/F%C3%B6hr" title="Föhr">Föhr</a> and <a href="/wiki/Amrum" title="Amrum">Amrum</a> were colonised in around AD 800, and the mainland was settled by Frisians in AD 1100.
</p><p>There are also various influences of neighbouring languages on the dialects. On Sylt, Föhr and Amrum and in parts of the northern mainland such as Wiedingharde, there is a strong <a href="/wiki/Danish_language" title="Danish language">Danish</a> (<a href="/wiki/South_Jutlandic" title="South Jutlandic">South Jutlandic</a>) influence, but on <a href="/wiki/Heligoland" title="Heligoland">Heligoland</a> and the rest of mainland North Frisia, the Low German influence is predominant. Moreover, there has historically been little exchange between the dialects and so hardly any <a href="/wiki/Lingua_franca" title="Lingua franca">lingua franca</a> could develop and there was no cultural centre in North Frisia for which the dialect could have had a leading role.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Samples">Samples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=North_Frisian_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Samples"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The sentence displayed below in many variants reads, <span style="padding-right:.15em;">"</span>'Shine, old moon, shine!', cried Häwelmann, but the moon was nowhere to be seen and the stars neither; they had all already gone to bed" (based on <a href="/wiki/Theodor_Storm" title="Theodor Storm">Theodor Storm</a>'s <i>Der kleine Häwelmann</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dt>Insular</dt></dl>
<p><a href="/wiki/S%C3%B6l%27ring" class="mw-redirect" title="Söl&#39;ring">Söl'ring</a> (dialect of <a href="/wiki/Sylt" title="Sylt">Sylt</a>)
</p>
<dl><dd><span title="North Frisian-language text"><i lang="frr">"Ljucht, ual Muun, ljucht!" skriilt Häwelmann, man di Muun wiar narigen tö sen en uk di Stiaren ek; ja wiar al altermaal tö Bēr gingen.</i></span></dd></dl>
<p><a href="/wiki/Fering" class="mw-redirect" title="Fering">Fering</a>-<a href="/wiki/%C3%96%C3%B6mrang" class="mw-redirect" title="Öömrang">Öömrang</a> (dialect of <a href="/wiki/F%C3%B6hr" title="Föhr">Föhr</a> and <a href="/wiki/Amrum" title="Amrum">Amrum</a>)
</p>
<dl><dd><span title="North Frisian-language text"><i lang="frr">"Locht, ual muun, locht!" rep Heewelmaan, man a muun wiar nochhuaren tu sen an a stäären uk ei; jo wiar al altermaal tu baad gingen.</i></span></dd></dl>
<p><a href="/wiki/Heligolandic" class="mw-redirect" title="Heligolandic">Heligolandic</a> (dialect of <a href="/wiki/Helgoland" class="mw-redirect" title="Helgoland">Helgoland</a>)
</p>
<dl><dd><span title="North Frisian-language text"><i lang="frr">"Lochte, ool Muun, lochte!" rüp Heäwelman, oawers de Muun wear naarni tu sin'n en uk de Steern ni; dja wear al allemoal tu Baad gingen.</i></span></dd></dl>
<dl><dt>Mainland</dt></dl>
<p><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_Goesharde_Frisian&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Northern Goesharde Frisian (page does not exist)">Northern Goesharde Frisian</a>, Hoorninger Fräisch variety of <a href="/wiki/Langenhorn_(Nordfriesland)" title="Langenhorn (Nordfriesland)">Langenhorn</a>
</p>
<dl><dd><span title="North Frisian-language text"><i lang="frr">"Jocht, uule moune, jocht!" biilked Hääwelmoon, ors e moune waas närngs to schüns än da steere ok ai; ja weern al aal to beede gingen.</i></span></dd></dl>
<p><a href="/wiki/Wiedingharde_Frisian_dialect" class="mw-redirect" title="Wiedingharde Frisian dialect">Wiedingharde Frisian</a>
</p>
<dl><dd><span title="North Frisian-language text"><i lang="frr">"Ljocht, uuile moone, ljocht!" biilked Hääwelmuon, män e moone was näärgen to schüns än uk e steere ai; jä würn al altomoale to beerd gingen.</i></span></dd></dl>
<p><a href="/wiki/Halligen_Frisian" title="Halligen Frisian">Halligen Frisian</a> (although it is spoken on the <a href="/wiki/Halligen" title="Halligen">Halligen</a> islands, it is linguistically grouped with the mainland dialects)
</p>
<dl><dd><span title="North Frisian-language text"><i lang="frr">"Jaacht, uale mööne, jaacht!" bölked Hääwelmoon, man de mööne woas näärngs to siinen än de steere uk ee; jä weern al altomaole to beed giangen.</i></span></dd></dl>
<p><a href="/wiki/Mooring_dialect_(North_Frisian)" class="mw-redirect" title="Mooring dialect (North Frisian)">Mooring</a> (dialect of <a href="/wiki/B%C3%B6kingharde" title="Bökingharde">Bökingharde</a>)
</p>
<dl><dd><span title="North Frisian-language text"><i lang="frr">"Jucht, üülje moune, jucht!" biiljked Hääwelmoon, ouers e moune wus nargne tu schüns än e stääre uk ai; ja wjarn ål åltumååle tu beed lim.</i></span></dd></dl>
<p>Despite the differences between the dialects, the Fering and Öömrang are highly similar; in this example nearly identical.
</p><p>Another source that highlights the differences between all of the main dialects of North Frisian (even extinct ones) are <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://archive.org/details/lords-prayer-northfrisian">translations of the Lord's Prayer.</a>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Extinct_dialects">Extinct dialects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=North_Frisian_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Extinct dialects"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Eiderstedt_Frisian" title="Eiderstedt Frisian">Eiderstedt Frisian</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Eiderstedt" title="Eiderstedt">Eiderstedt</a> peninsula were abandoned in favour of Low German during the 17th and 18th centuries. In contrast to the northern <a href="/wiki/Hundred_(county_subdivision)" class="mw-redirect" title="Hundred (county subdivision)">hundreds</a>, Eiderstedt was economically strong and wealthy and was oriented towards the southern, Low German parts of Schleswig-Holstein. Moreover, there was a strong Dutch immigration during the 16th century.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A similar situation was to be found on the island of <a href="/wiki/Strand_(island)" title="Strand (island)">Strand</a>, which was destroyed during the <a href="/wiki/Burchardi_flood" title="Burchardi flood">Burchardi flood</a>. The population of the eastern, remaining part of Strand, the modern <a href="/wiki/Nordstrand,_Germany" title="Nordstrand, Germany">Nordstrand</a>, did not succeed in rebuilding the dikes on their own. Therefore, many Frisian speaking people left their homeland on Strand or were otherwise not able to maintain their native language against mostly Dutch-speaking immigrants. On <a href="/wiki/Pellworm" title="Pellworm">Pellworm</a>, the western remainder of Strand, the repair of the dikes was quickly accomplished and so the Frisian language was still spoken in the 18th century, until it also vanished due to changes in population structure. The old <a href="/wiki/Strand_Frisian" title="Strand Frisian">Strand Frisian</a> was presumably closest to Halligen Frisian.
</p><p>Likewise close to Halligen Frisian was the Wyk Frisian that used to be spoken in <a href="/wiki/Wyk_auf_F%C3%B6hr" title="Wyk auf Föhr">Wyk auf Föhr</a> until the town completely shifted to Low German. The Wyk dialect is thought to have developed from the dialects of immigrants from the Halligen and Strand island.
</p><p>The dialect that most recently died out is Southern Goesharde Frisian which became extinct with the death of its last speaker in the early 1980s. Other mainland dialects are also facing extinction.
</p><p>North of the German-Danish border North Frisian was spoken only in some marsh-farms, located directly at the border.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Self-designation">Self-designation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=North_Frisian_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Self-designation"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Due to the large number of dialects there is no original native name for the North Frisian language as such. E.g. the Wiedingharde and Halligen Frisians call their language <span title="North Frisian-language text"><i lang="frr">freesk</i></span>, in the Bökingharde it is called <span title="North Frisian-language text"><i lang="frr">frasch</i></span>, and in the Goesharde likewise <span title="North Frisian-language text"><i lang="frr">fräisch</i></span> or <span title="North Frisian-language text"><i lang="frr">freesch</i></span>. While these names all translate to "Frisian" the native names of the insular dialects refer to the particular islands as in <i>Fering</i>, <i>Öömrang</i>, <i>Söl'ring</i> or <i>Halunder</i>. E.g. "Frisian" would mean <i>"fresk"</i> in the Föhr dialect.
</p><p>The North Frisians eventually agreed upon the inter-dialectal name <i>"friisk"</i> which corresponds to the West Frisian native name <i>"frysk"</i>. This designation is today mostly used when the North Frisian collectivity is addressed or in the names of official institutions such as Nordfriisk Instituut, Friisk Foriining or Friisk Gesäts. The northern section of the Interfrisian Council has however kept its name "Frasche Rädj" in the Mooring dialect.
</p>

<li>/<span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">aˑi</span>/, /<span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">œi</span>/, /<span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">ɔˑi</span>/, and /<span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">ou</span>/ only occur in the Bökingharde dialect.<sup id="cite_ref-Walker_and_Wilts_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Walker_and_Wilts-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>Despite the strong differences among the North Frisian dialects, there are still some traits of phonology that are more or less common to all dialects. Among them is the lowering from <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">&#91;<a href="/wiki/Near-close_near-front_unrounded_vowel" title="Near-close near-front unrounded vowel">ɪ</a>&#93;</span> to <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">&#91;<a href="/wiki/Open_front_unrounded_vowel" title="Open front unrounded vowel">a</a>&#93;</span>, which is mostly complete in the central dialects but is only at the stage <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">&#91;<a href="/wiki/Open-mid_front_unrounded_vowel" title="Open-mid front unrounded vowel">ɛ</a>&#93;</span> or <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">&#91;<a href="/wiki/Close-mid_front_unrounded_vowel" title="Close-mid front unrounded vowel">eː</a>&#93;</span> in the periphery. For example, the word "fish" translates to Mooring <span title="North Frisian-language text"><i lang="frr">fasch</i></span> and Fering-Öömrang <span title="North Frisian-language text"><i lang="frr">fask</i></span> but Söl'ring <span title="North Frisian-language text"><i lang="frr">fesk</i></span> (cf. Low German: <span title="Low German-language text"><i lang="nds">Fisch/Fisk</i></span>, <a href="/wiki/Danish_language" title="Danish language">Danish</a>: <i lang="da">fisk</i>, German: <i lang="de">Fisch</i>, <a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">Dutch</a>: <i lang="nl">vis</i>).
</p><p>The distribution of the <a href="/wiki/Lenition" title="Lenition">lenition</a> of the unvoiced <a href="/wiki/Plosive" title="Plosive">plosives</a> <i>p</i>, <i>t</i> and <i>k</i> is similar as they have become voiced plosives and partially even developed to <a href="/wiki/Fricatives" class="mw-redirect" title="Fricatives">fricatives</a> in the central dialects. That can be demonstrated from the <a href="/wiki/Verb" title="Verb">verb</a> "to know": Mooring <span title="North Frisian-language text"><i lang="frr">waase</i></span>, Fering-Öömrang <span title="North Frisian-language text"><i lang="frr">wed</i></span>, Sölring <span title="North Frisian-language text"><i lang="frr">weet</i></span>, Halunder <span title="North Frisian-language text"><i lang="frr">wet</i></span> (cf. West Frisian <span title="West Frisian-language text"><i lang="fy">witte</i></span>, Low German <span title="Low German-language text"><i lang="nds">weten</i></span>, German <span title="German-language text"><i lang="de">wissen</i></span>).
</p><p>The North Frisian dialects differ from modern <a href="/wiki/Standard_German" title="Standard German">Standard German</a> by a more diverse system of <a href="/wiki/Diphthong" title="Diphthong">diphthongs</a> and consonants. All of the dialects have an additional line of <a href="/wiki/Palatalization_(sound_change)" title="Palatalization (sound change)">palatalizations</a>, which is uncommon for a Germanic language. Until recently, an additional number of dental consonants contrasted phonemically with their alveolar counterparts in the <a href="/wiki/Fering" class="mw-redirect" title="Fering">dialect of Föhr</a>. In general, the insular dialects feature a relatively complicated consonantal system, but the mainland dialects have more diverse vowels.
</p><p>Recently, the phonological system of the North Frisian dialects has been strongly influenced by Standard German and is slowly adapting to its system.<sup id="cite_ref-Walker_and_Wilts_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Walker_and_Wilts-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="Statement needs to be more specific about the content to which it refers. (June 2020)">specify</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_situation">Current situation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=North_Frisian_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Current situation"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Officially, the number of North Frisian speakers ranges from 8,000 to 10,000<sup id="cite_ref-sh_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sh-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> but linguists propose significantly lower numbers. In 2007, Århammar estimated a total of 5,000 speakers inside and 1,500 to 2,000 speakers outside North Frisia proper.<sup id="cite_ref-arhammar_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-arhammar-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Exact surveys do not exist.
</p><p>North Frisian is an endangered language, as in most places, children no longer learn it. In UNESCO's <i><a href="/wiki/Atlas_of_the_World%E2%80%99s_Languages_in_Danger" class="mw-redirect" title="Atlas of the World’s Languages in Danger">Atlas of the World’s Languages in Danger</a></i>, North Frisian is classified as "severely endangered".<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Exceptions are a few villages on the islands of <a href="/wiki/F%C3%B6hr" title="Föhr">Föhr</a> and <a href="/wiki/Amrum" title="Amrum">Amrum</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Risum-Lindholm" title="Risum-Lindholm">Risum-Lindholm</a> area. Especially in the western parts of Föhr, the language community is still relatively common.<sup id="cite_ref-arhammar_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-arhammar-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The number of speakers on Föhr and Amrum alone is estimated to around 3,500. The other dialects are, in fact, seriously endangered, like Karrharde Frisian, Central Goesharde and Halligen Frisian.
</p><p>The elementary and grammar school on Amrum is called <i>Öömrang Skuul</i> and, among other subjects, focuses on teaching the local dialect. Fering is also taught in schools on Föhr and the <i>Risum Skole/Risem Schölj</i> in <a href="/wiki/Risum-Lindholm" title="Risum-Lindholm">Risum-Lindholm</a> on the mainland is a combined Danish-North Frisian elementary school.
</p><p>All speakers of North Frisian are at least bilingual (North Frisian and <a href="/wiki/Standard_German" title="Standard German">German</a>). Many are trilingual (North Frisian, Standard German and <a href="/wiki/Low_German" title="Low German">Low German</a>) and, especially along the Danish border, quadrilingualism used to be widespread (North Frisian, Standard German, Low German and <a href="/wiki/South_Jutlandic" title="South Jutlandic">South Jutlandic</a>).
</p><p>In Schleswig-Holstein, North Frisian is protected by the <a href="/wiki/European_Charter_for_Regional_or_Minority_Languages" title="European Charter for Regional or Minority Languages">European Charter for Regional or Minority Languages</a> as a minority language. On 24 December 2004 a state law became effective in Schleswig-Holstein that recognises the North Frisian language for official use in the <a href="/wiki/Nordfriesland" class="mw-redirect" title="Nordfriesland">Nordfriesland</a> district and on <a href="/wiki/Heligoland" title="Heligoland">Heligoland</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
