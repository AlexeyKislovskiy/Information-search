<p><b>Luwati</b> (<b>Al-Lawatia</b>, <a href="/wiki/Arabic_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>: <span lang="ar" dir="rtl">اللواتية</span>, <small><a href="/wiki/Romanization_of_Arabic" title="Romanization of Arabic">romanized</a>:&#160;</small><span title="Arabic-language romanization"><i lang="ar-Latn">al-lawātiyya</i></span>; also known as <b>Khoja</b>, <b>Khojki</b>, <b>Lawatiyya</b>, <b>Lawatiya</b>, or <b>Hyderabadi</b>) is an <a href="/wiki/Indo-Aryan_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Indo-Aryan language">Indo-Aryan language</a> spoken by 8,940 people known as the <a href="/wiki/Al-Lawatia" title="Al-Lawatia">Lawatiya</a> (also called the Khojas or Hyderabadis) in the country of <a href="/wiki/Oman" title="Oman">Oman</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-e26_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-e26-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> In total it has been estimated there are 20,000 to 30,000 Lawatiya people.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Despite the various names, the Lawatiya refer to the language as Khojki.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> It is considered an endangered language because a portion of the Lawatiya do not speak Luwati, and it is not continuously passed down to younger generations.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geographic_distribution_and_status">Geographic distribution and status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Luwati_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geographic distribution and status"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Luwati language is superficially similar to <a href="/wiki/Kutchi_language" title="Kutchi language">Kutchi</a>, but retains sounds found in other <a href="/wiki/Sindhi_languages" title="Sindhi languages">Sindhi languages</a> and <a href="/wiki/Saraiki_dialect" class="mw-redirect" title="Saraiki dialect">Saraiki</a> but that have been lost from Kutchi.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Luwati also bears similarities to other languages such as <a href="/wiki/Sindhi_language" title="Sindhi language">Sindhi</a>, Kachichi, <a href="/wiki/Gujarati_language" title="Gujarati language">Gujarati</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hindustani_language" title="Hindustani language">Hindustani</a> and <a href="/wiki/Persian_language" title="Persian language">Persian</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> As with other languages located in Oman, Luwati is influenced by the Omani dialect of Arabic.
</p><p>Originating from the Pakistani province of <a href="/wiki/Sindh" title="Sindh">Sindh</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-:1_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> the Luwati language has had a presence in <a href="/wiki/Oman" title="Oman">Oman</a> for nearly four centuries.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The language and people were first mentioned historically by the Omani historian Ibn Ruzayq. The Lawatiya appeared to have settled in Oman in waves of immigration from Sindh between 1780 and 1880 bringing the language with them.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> A number of historians assign an <a href="/wiki/Arabs" title="Arabs">Arab</a> pedigree to the Luwatis. The Luwati speakers inhabited the <a href="/wiki/Arabian_Peninsula" title="Arabian Peninsula">Arabian Peninsula</a> until their displacement during the 8th century C.E. Others assert that the Luwati speakers originate in <a href="/wiki/Hyderabad" title="Hyderabad">Hydrabad</a>. The Luwatis entered <a href="/wiki/Muscat" title="Muscat">Muscat</a> as prosperous merchants and insularized themselves, for the most part, in Sūr al-Luwātiyya in <a href="/wiki/Muttrah" title="Muttrah">Muttrah</a>, preserving their language.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Luwati is a minority language found in Oman specifically in the capital of <a href="/wiki/Muscat,_Oman" class="mw-redirect" title="Muscat, Oman">Muscat</a> as well as in the coastal towns of <a href="/wiki/Saham" title="Saham">Saham</a>, Barka, Khabura, and Musana. It is spoken by 5,000 to 10,000 people.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Phonology">Phonology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Luwati_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Phonology"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Luwati phonology is simpler than that of Sindhi, having lost the breathy-voiced consonants and simplified the vowel system. All of the implosives, however, are retained.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Writing_system">Writing system</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Luwati_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Writing system"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Luwati no longer has a writing system and is only a spoken language.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Its script was used by <a href="/wiki/Nizari_Isma%27ilism" title="Nizari Isma&#39;ilism">Nizari Ismailis</a> in <a href="/wiki/Punjab" title="Punjab">Punjab</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sindh" title="Sindh">Sindh</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Gujarat" title="Gujarat">Gujarat</a> to produce a religious corpus.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
