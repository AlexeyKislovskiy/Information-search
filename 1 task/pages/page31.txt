<p><b>Nawat</b> (academically <b>Pipil</b>, also known as <b>Nahuat</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Nahuan_languages" title="Nahuan languages">Nahuan</a> language native to <a href="/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America">Central America</a>. It is the southernmost extant member of the <a href="/wiki/Uto-Aztecan_languages" title="Uto-Aztecan languages">Uto-Aztecan</a> family.<sup id="cite_ref-Campbell1985_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Campbell1985-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> It was spoken in several parts of present-day Central America before <a href="/wiki/Spanish_colonization_of_the_Americas" title="Spanish colonization of the Americas">Spanish colonization</a>, but now is mostly confined to western <a href="/wiki/El_Salvador" title="El Salvador">El Salvador</a>. It has been on the verge of extinction in El Salvador, and has already gone extinct elsewhere in Central America. In 2012, a large number of new Nawat speakers started to appear. As of today, the language is currently going through a revitalization.
</p><p>In El Salvador, Nawat (Nahuat) was the language of several groups: <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nonualcos&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Nonualcos (page does not exist)">Nonualcos</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cuscatlecos&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Cuscatlecos (page does not exist)">Cuscatlecos</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Izalcos&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Izalcos (page does not exist)">Izalcos</a> and is known to be the Nahua variety of migrating <a href="/wiki/Toltec" title="Toltec">Toltec</a>. The name <i>Pipil</i> for this language is mostly used by the international scholarly community to differentiate it more clearly from <a href="/wiki/Nahuatl" title="Nahuatl">Nahuatl</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Nicaragua" title="Nicaragua">Nicaragua</a> it was spoken by the <a href="/wiki/Nicarao_people" title="Nicarao people">Nicarao people</a> who split from the Pipil around 1200 CE when they migrated south. Nawat became the <a href="/wiki/Lingua_franca" title="Lingua franca">lingua franca</a> there during the 16th century.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> A hybrid form of Nahuat-Spanish was spoken by many Nicaraguans up until the 19th century.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nawat_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Most authors refer to this language by the names <i>Nawat</i>, <i>Nahuat</i>, <i>Pipil</i>, or <i>Nicarao</i>. However, <i>Nawat</i> (along with the synonymous <i>Eastern Nahuatl</i>) has also been used to refer to Nahuatl <a href="/wiki/Variety_(linguistics)" title="Variety (linguistics)">language varieties</a> in southern <a href="/wiki/Veracruz" title="Veracruz">Veracruz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tabasco" title="Tabasco">Tabasco</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Chiapas" title="Chiapas">Chiapas</a>, states in the south of Mexico, that like Pipil have reduced the earlier /t͡ɬ/ consonant (a lateral <a href="/wiki/Affricate" title="Affricate">affricate</a>) to a /t/.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Those Mexican <a href="/wiki/Lect" class="mw-redirect" title="Lect">lects</a> share more similarities with Nawat than do the other Nahuatl varieties.
</p><p>Nawat specialists (<a href="/wiki/Lyle_Campbell" title="Lyle Campbell">Campbell</a>, Fidias Jiménez, <a href="/wiki/Pedro_Geoffroy_Rivas" title="Pedro Geoffroy Rivas">Geoffroy Rivas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alan_R._King" title="Alan R. King">King</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jorge_Ernesto_Lemus&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Jorge Ernesto Lemus (page does not exist)">Lemus</a>, and Schultze, <i>inter alia</i>) generally treat Pipil/Nawat as a separate language, at least in practice. <a href="/wiki/Yolanda_Lastra" title="Yolanda Lastra">Lastra de Suárez</a> (1986) and <a href="/wiki/Una_Canger" title="Una Canger">Canger</a> (1988) classify Pipil among "Eastern Periphery" dialects of Nahuatl.
</p>

<p>Uto-Aztecan is uncontroversially divided into eight branches, including Nahuan. Research continues into verifying higher level groupings. However, the grouping adopted by Campbell of the four southernmost branches is not yet universally accepted.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Status">Status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nawat_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Status"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As of 2012, extensive <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://tushik.org/english/what-is-nawat">online resources for learning Nawat</a> are available at the website of linguist Alan R. King, including video lessons and a Facebook group.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> A video documentation project is also underway, in collaboration with the <a href="/wiki/Living_Tongues_Institute" class="mw-redirect" title="Living Tongues Institute">Living Tongues Institute</a>, focusing on "Pipil culture, such as natural medicines, traditions, traditional games, agricultural practices, and childhood songs," which is intended for language learners.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The varieties of Nawat in <a href="/wiki/Guatemala" title="Guatemala">Guatemala</a>, <a href="/wiki/Honduras" title="Honduras">Honduras</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nicaragua" title="Nicaragua">Nicaragua</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Panama" title="Panama">Panama</a> are now <a href="/wiki/Extinct_language" title="Extinct language">extinct</a>. In El Salvador, Nawat is endangered: it is spoken mostly by a few elderly speakers in the Salvadoran departments of <a href="/wiki/Sonsonate_Department" title="Sonsonate Department">Sonsonate</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=San_Salvador_Departament&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="San Salvador Departament (page does not exist)">San Salvador</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ahuachap%C3%A1n_Department" title="Ahuachapán Department">Ahuachapán</a>. The towns of <a href="/wiki/Cuisnahuat" title="Cuisnahuat">Cuisnahuat</a> and <a href="/wiki/Santo_Domingo,_San_Vicente" class="mw-redirect" title="Santo Domingo, San Vicente">Santo Domingo de Guzmán</a> have the highest concentration of speakers. Campbell's 1985 estimate (based on fieldwork conducted 1970–1976) was 200 speakers. Gordon (2005) reports only 20 speakers were left in 1987. Official Mexican reports have recorded as many as 2000 speakers.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>The exact number of speakers has been difficult to determine because persecution of Nawat speakers throughout the 20th century (massacres after suppression of the <a href="/wiki/1932_Salvadoran_peasant_uprising" class="mw-redirect" title="1932 Salvadoran peasant uprising">1932 Salvadoran peasant uprising</a>, laws that made speaking Nawat illegal) made them conceal their use of the language.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> (About 30,000 people were killed during the uprising over the course of a few weeks, and those who spoke Nawat outside their homes against the new rules "provoked shame and fear." A young Nawat language activist, Carlos Cortez, explained in 2010 that this fear is worse for older speakers.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>)
</p><p><br />
A few small-scale projects to revitalize Nawat in El Salvador have been attempted since 1990. The Asociación Coordinadora de Comunidades Indígenas de El Salvador (<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.oekohaus-rostock.de/accies/">ACCIES</a> <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://web.archive.org/web/20070302090700/http://www.oekohaus-rostock.de/accies/">Archived</a> 2 March 2007 at the <a href="/wiki/Wayback_Machine" title="Wayback Machine">Wayback Machine</a>) and <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.udb.edu.sv/">Universidad Don Bosco</a> of San Salvador have both produced some teaching materials. Monica Ward has developed an on-line language course.<sup id="cite_ref-Ward_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ward-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> The Nawat Language Recovery Initiative<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> is a grassroots association currently engaged in several activities including an ongoing <a href="/wiki/Language_documentation" title="Language documentation">language documentation</a> project, and has also produced a range of printed materials. Thus, as the number of native speakers continues to dwindle, there is growing interest in some quarters in keeping the language alive, but as of 2002, the national government had not joined these efforts (cf. Various, 2002).<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>As of 2010, the town of <a href="/wiki/Santo_Domingo,_Sonsonate" class="mw-redirect" title="Santo Domingo, Sonsonate">Santo Domingo de Guzmán</a> had a <a href="/wiki/Language_nest" title="Language nest">language nest</a>, “Xuchikisa nawat” ("the house where Nawat blooms"), where children three to five years of age learned Nawat, run in cooperation with <a href="/w/index.php?title=Don_Bosco_University&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Don Bosco University (page does not exist)">Don Bosco University</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2010, Salvadoran President <a href="/wiki/Mauricio_Funes" title="Mauricio Funes">Mauricio Funes</a> awarded the National Culture Prize (Premio Nacional de Cultura 2010) to linguist Dr. Jorge Ernesto Lemus of Don Bosco University for his work with Nawat.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>According to a 2009 report in <i><a href="/wiki/El_Diario_de_Hoy" title="El Diario de Hoy">El Diario de Hoy</a>,</i> Nawat had started to make a comeback as a result of the preservation and revitalization efforts of various non-profit organizations in conjunction with several universities, combined with a post-civil war resurgence of Pipil identity in El Salvador. In the 1980s, Nawat had about 200 speakers. By 2009, 3,000 people were participating in Nawat language learning programs, the vast majority being young people, giving rise to hopes that the language might be pulled back from the brink of extinction.<sup id="cite_ref-elsalvador.com_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-elsalvador.com-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Gordon (2009) lists <a href="/wiki/Dolores,_Caba%C3%B1as" class="mw-redirect" title="Dolores, Cabañas">Dolores</a> as a Pipil-speaking area.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>Nahuat was also formerly spoken in Guatemala, Honduras, Nicaragua and Costa Rica, though it is now extinct in all of these countries.
</p><p>Kaufman (1970:66) lists <a href="/wiki/Escuintla" title="Escuintla">Escuintla</a> and <a href="/wiki/Comapa,_Jutiapa" title="Comapa, Jutiapa">Comapa</a> as former Pipil-speaking areas of <a href="/wiki/Guatemala" title="Guatemala">Guatemala</a>, and <a href="/wiki/San_Agust%C3%ADn_Acasaguastl%C3%A1n" title="San Agustín Acasaguastlán">San Agustín Acasaguastlán</a> as a former "Mejicano"-speaking town.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> The genetic position of San Agustín Acasaguastlán Mejicano is still uncertain (<i>see <a href="/wiki/Alag%C3%BCilac_language" title="Alagüilac language">Alagüilac language</a></i>). 
</p><p>In Honduras, ethnic Nahua populations are present in small numbers in the <a href="/wiki/Olancho_Department" title="Olancho Department">Olancho Department</a>, in the municipalities of <a href="/wiki/Catacamas" title="Catacamas">Catacamas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gualaco" title="Gualaco">Gualaco</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guata" title="Guata">Guata</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jano" title="Jano">Jano</a> and <a href="/wiki/Esquipulas_del_Norte" title="Esquipulas del Norte">Esquipulas del Norte</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> The conquest-era Papayeca population, who lived in the environs of the present-day city of <a href="/wiki/Trujillo,_Honduras" title="Trujillo, Honduras">Trujillo</a>, have also been speculated to have been Nahuat speakers.
</p><p>In Nicaragua, the Nicaraos are present in the <a href="/wiki/Rivas_Department" title="Rivas Department">Rivas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jinotega_Department" title="Jinotega Department">Jinotega</a> departments, and in <a href="/wiki/S%C3%A9baco" title="Sébaco">Sébaco</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Bagaces" title="Bagaces">Bagaces</a>, Costa Rica was home to a Nahua population during the 16th century.<sup id="cite_ref-ferrerop75_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ferrerop75-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>An extinct variation of Nahuatl spoken on the Pacific coast of the Mexican state of Chiapas is speculated to have been closely related to Nahuat.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nawat_and_Nahuatl_compared">Nawat and Nahuatl compared</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nawat_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Nawat and Nahuatl compared"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Nawat_grammar" title="Nawat grammar">Nawat grammar</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Phonology">Phonology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nawat_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Phonology"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Two salient features of Nawat are found in several Mexican dialects: the change of [t͡ɬ] to [t] and [u] rather than [o] as the predominant <a href="/wiki/Allophone" title="Allophone">allophone</a> of a single basic rounded vowel phoneme.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2022)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> These features are thus characteristic but not diagnostic.
</p><p>However, Nawat <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/t/</span> corresponds to not only the two Classical Nahuatl sounds <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/t/</span> and <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/t͡ɬ/</span> but also a word final <i>saltillo</i> or <a href="/wiki/Glottal_stop" title="Glottal stop">glottal stop</a> <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ʔ/</span> in nominal plural suffixes (e.g. Nawat <i>-met</i>&#160;: Classical <i>-meh</i>) and verbal plural endings (Nawat <i>-t</i> present plural, <i>-ket</i> past plural, etc.). This fact has been claimed by Campbell to be diagnostic for the position of Nawat in a genetic classification, on the assumption that this /t/ is more archaic than the Classical Nahuatl reflex, where the direction change has been <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/t/</span> &gt; <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ʔ/</span> <i>saltillo</i>.
</p><p>One other characteristic phonological feature is the merger in Nawat of original geminate <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ll/</span> with single <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/l/</span>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Grammar">Grammar</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nawat_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Grammar"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Nawat_grammar" title="Nawat grammar">Nawat grammar</a></div>
<p>Nawat lacks some grammatical features present in Classical Nahuatl, such as the past prefix <i>o-</i> in verbs. It distributes others differently: for example, 'subtractive' past formation, which is very common in the classical language, exists in Nawat but is much rarer. On the other hand, <a href="/wiki/Reduplication" title="Reduplication">reduplication</a> to form plural nouns, of more limited distribution in the language of the Aztecs, is greatly generalised in Nawat. Still other grammatical features that were productive in Classical Nahuatl have left only fossilised traces in Nawat: for example, synchronically Nawat has no <a href="/wiki/Postposition" class="mw-redirect" title="Postposition">postpositions</a>, although a few lexical forms derive etymologically from older postpositional forms, e.g. <i>apan</i> 'river' &lt; *'in/on the water', <i>kujtan</i> 'uncultivated land, forest' &lt; *'under the trees'; these are synchronically unanalyzable in modern Nawat.
</p>

<p>Nawat has developed two widely used <a href="/wiki/Article_(grammar)" title="Article (grammar)">articles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Definite_article" class="mw-redirect" title="Definite article">definite</a> <i>ne</i> and <a href="/wiki/Indefinite_article" class="mw-redirect" title="Indefinite article">indefinite</a> <i>se</i>. The <a href="/wiki/Demonstrative" title="Demonstrative">demonstrative</a> pronouns/determiners <i>ini</i> 'this, these' and <i>uni</i> 'that, those' are also distinctively Nawat in form. The obligatory marking of <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_number" title="Grammatical number">number</a> extends in Nawat to almost all <a href="/wiki/Plural" title="Plural">plural</a> <a href="/wiki/Noun_phrase" title="Noun phrase">noun phrases</a> (regardless of <a href="/wiki/Animacy" title="Animacy">animacy</a>), which will contain at least one plural form, most commonly marked by <a href="/wiki/Reduplication" title="Reduplication">reduplication</a>.
</p><p>Many nouns are invariable for <a href="/wiki/Construct_state" title="Construct state">state</a>, since <i>-ti</i> (cf. Classical <i>-tli</i>, the absolute suffix after consonants) is rarely added to polysyllabic noun stems, while the Classical postconsonantal construct suffix, <i>-wi</i>, is altogether unknown in Nawat: thus <i>sin-ti</i> 'maize'&#160;: <i>nu-sin</i> 'my maize', <i>uj-ti</i> 'way'&#160;: <i>nu-uj</i> 'my way', <i>mistun</i> 'cat'&#160;: <i>nu-mistun</i> 'my cat'.
</p><p>An important number of nouns lack absolute forms and occur only <a href="/wiki/Possession_(linguistics)" title="Possession (linguistics)">inalienably</a> possessed, e.g. <i>nu-mey</i> 'my hand' (but not *<i>mey</i> or *<i>mey-ti</i>), <i>nu-nan</i> 'my mother' (but not *<i>nan</i> or *<i>nan-ti</i>), thus further reducing the number of absolute-construct oppositions and the incidence of absolute <i>-ti</i> in comparison to Classical Nahuatl.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Postposition" class="mw-redirect" title="Postposition">Postpositions</a> have been eliminated from the Pipil grammatical system, and some monosyllabic <a href="/wiki/Preposition" class="mw-redirect" title="Preposition">prepositions</a> originating from <a href="/wiki/Relational_noun" title="Relational noun">relationals</a> have become <a href="/wiki/Grammaticalization" title="Grammaticalization">grammaticalized</a>.
</p>

<p>To form the <a href="/wiki/Past_tense" title="Past tense">past tense</a>, most Nawat verbs add <i>-k</i> (after vowels) or <i>-ki</i> (after consonants, following loss of the final vowel of the present stem), e.g. <i>ki-neki</i> 'he wants it'&#160;: <i>ki-neki-k</i> 'he wanted it', <i>ki-mati</i> 'he knows it'&#160;: <i>ki-mat-ki</i> 'he knew it'. The mechanism of simply removing the present stem vowel to form past stems, so common in Classical Nahuatl, is limited in Nawat to polysyllabic verb stems such as <i>ki-talia</i> 'he puts it' → <i>ki-tali(j)</i> 'he put it', <i>mu-talua</i> 'he runs' → <i>mu-talu(j)</i> 'he ran', and a handful of other verbs, e.g. <i>ki-tajtani</i> 'he asks him' → <i>ki-tajtan</i> 'he asked him'.
</p><p>Nawat has a <a href="/wiki/Perfect_(grammar)" title="Perfect (grammar)">perfect</a> in <i>-tuk</i> (synchronically unanalyzable), plural <i>-tiwit</i>. Another tense suffix, <i>-tuya</i>, functions both as a <a href="/wiki/Pluperfect" title="Pluperfect">pluperfect</a> (<i>k-itz-tuya ne takat</i> 'he had seen the man') and as an <a href="/wiki/Imperfect" title="Imperfect">imperfect</a> of <a href="/wiki/Stative" class="mw-redirect" title="Stative">stative</a> verbs (<i>inte weli-tuya</i> 'he couldn't'), in the latter case having supplanted the <i>-ya</i> imperfect found in Mexican dialects.
</p><p>Nawat has two <a href="/wiki/Conditional_mood" title="Conditional mood">conditional</a> tenses, one in <i>-skia</i> expressing possible conditions and possible results, and one in <i>-tuskia</i> for impossible ones, although the distinction is sometimes blurred in practice. A <a href="/wiki/Future_tense" title="Future tense">future tense</a> in <i>-s</i> (plural <i>-sket</i>) is attested but rarely used, a <a href="/wiki/Periphrastic" class="mw-redirect" title="Periphrastic">periphrastic</a> future being preferred, e.g. <i>yawi witz</i> (or <i>yu-witz</i>) 'he will come'.
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Serial_verb" class="mw-redirect" title="Serial verb">serial</a> constructions, the <a href="/wiki/Present_tense" title="Present tense">present tense</a> (really the <a href="/wiki/Markedness" title="Markedness">unmarked</a> tense) is generally found except in the first verb, regardless of the tense of the latter, e.g. <i>kineki / kinekik / kinekiskia kikwa</i> 'he wants / wanted / would like to eat it'.
</p><p>There are also some differences regarding how <a href="/wiki/Prefix" title="Prefix">prefixes</a> are attached to verb-initial stems; principally, that in Nawat the prefixes <i>ni-</i>, <i>ti-</i>, <i>shi-</i> and <i>ki-</i> when word-initial retain their <i>i</i> in most cases, e.g. <i>ni-ajsi</i> 'I arrive', <i>ki-elkawa</i> 'he forgets it'.
</p>
