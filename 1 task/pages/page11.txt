<p>The <b>Yidgha</b> language (<span title="Yidgha-language text"><span lang="ydg"><span class="Nastaliq" dir="rtl" title="Nastaliq" style="font-family: &#39;Jameel Noori Nastaleeq&#39;, &#39;Urdu Typesetting&#39;, &#39;Noto Nastaliq Urdu&#39;, &#39;Noto Nastaliq Urdu Draft&#39;, &#39;Hussaini Nastaleeq&#39;, &#39;AlQalam Taj Nastaleeq&#39;, IranNastaliq, &#39;Awami Nastaliq&#39;, &#39;Awami Nastaliq Beta3&#39;, &#39;Awami Nastaliq Beta2&#39;, &#39;Awami Nastaliq Beta1&#39;, &#39;Nafees Nastaleeq&#39;, &#39;Nafees Nastaleeq v1.01&#39;, &#39;Pak Nastaleeq&#39;, &#39;PDMS_Jauhar&#39;, &#39;Alvi Lahori Nastaleeq&#39;; font-size: 110%; font-style: normal;">یدغا زڤون</span></span></span>) is an <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Iranian_languages" title="Eastern Iranian languages">Eastern Iranian</a> language of the <a href="/wiki/Pamir_languages" title="Pamir languages">Pamir group</a> spoken in the upper <a href="/wiki/Lotkoh" class="mw-redirect" title="Lotkoh">Lotkoh</a> Valley (Tehsil Lotkoh) of <a href="/wiki/Chitral_District" title="Chitral District">Chitral</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Khyber_Pakhtunkhwa" title="Khyber Pakhtunkhwa">Khyber Pakhtunkhwa</a> province of <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a>. Yidgha is similar to the <a href="/wiki/Munji_language" title="Munji language">Munji language</a> spoken on the Afghan side of the border.
</p><p>The Garam Chashma area became important during the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_invasion_of_Afghanistan" class="mw-redirect" title="Soviet invasion of Afghanistan">Soviet invasion of Afghanistan</a> because the Soviets were unable to stop the flow of arms and men back and forth across the <a href="/wiki/Dorah_Pass" title="Dorah Pass">Dorah Pass</a> that separates Chitral from <a href="/wiki/Badakshan" class="mw-redirect" title="Badakshan">Badakshan</a> in Afghanistan. Almost the entire Munji-speaking population of Afghanistan fled across the border to Chitral during the War in Afghanistan.
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Name">Name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yidgha_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Name"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to <a href="/wiki/Georg_Morgenstierne" title="Georg Morgenstierne">Georg Morgenstierne</a> (1931), the name <i>Yidgha</i> probably derives from <i>*(h)ind(a,i)-ka-</i>, likely referring to the part of the Munji tribe that settled on the "Indian" or "Indo-Aryan" side near the Lotkoh Valley. Ľubomír Novák (2013) revises the reconstruction as <i>*hindū̆-ka-ka-</i>, with the same assumption.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Study">Study</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yidgha_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Study"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Yidgha language has not been given serious study by linguists, except that it is mentioned by <a href="/wiki/Georg_Morgenstierne" title="Georg Morgenstierne">Georg Morgenstierne</a> (1926), <a href="/w/index.php?title=Kendall_Decker&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Kendall Decker (page does not exist)">Kendall Decker</a> (1992) and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Badshah_Munir_Bukhari&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Badshah Munir Bukhari (page does not exist)">Badshah Munir Bukhari</a> (2005). A 280-page joint description of Yidgha and <a href="/wiki/Munji_language" title="Munji language">Munji</a> (descriptive and historical phonetics and grammar, glossary with etymologies where possible) is given by <a href="/wiki/Georg_Morgenstierne" title="Georg Morgenstierne">Morgenstierne</a> (1938).
</p><p>Norwegian linguist Georg Morgenstierne wrote that Chitral is the area of the greatest linguistic diversity in the world.<a rel="nofollow" class="external autonumber" href="http://www.nb.no/baser/morgenstierne/nirmali/nirmali/Imra/Text/sacrifice/sacrifice29.html">[1]</a> Although <a href="/wiki/Khowar_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Khowar language">Khowar</a> is the predominant language of Chitral, more than ten other languages are spoken here. These include <a href="/wiki/Kalasha-mun_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Kalasha-mun language">Kalasha-mun</a>, <a href="/wiki/Palula_language" title="Palula language">Palula</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dameli_language" title="Dameli language">Dameli</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gawar-Bati_language" title="Gawar-Bati language">Gawar-Bati</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nuristani_languages" title="Nuristani languages">Nuristani</a>, Yidgha, <a href="/wiki/Burushaski_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Burushaski language">Burushaski</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wakhi_language" title="Wakhi language">Wakhi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kyrgyz_language" title="Kyrgyz language">Kyrgyz</a>, the Madaglashti dialect of <a href="/wiki/Persian_language" title="Persian language">Persian</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Pashto_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Pashto language">Pashto</a>. Since many of these languages have no written form, letters are usually written in <a href="/wiki/Urdu" title="Urdu">Urdu</a>.
</p>
