<p>The <b>Tibeto-Burman languages</b> are the non-<a href="/wiki/Sinitic_languages" title="Sinitic languages">Sinitic</a> members of the <a href="/wiki/Sino-Tibetan_languages" title="Sino-Tibetan languages">Sino-Tibetan language family</a>, over 400 of which are spoken throughout the <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asian_Massif" title="Southeast Asian Massif">Southeast Asian Massif</a> ("Zomia") as well as parts of <a href="/wiki/East_Asia" title="East Asia">East Asia</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_Asia" title="South Asia">South Asia</a>. Around 60 million people speak Tibeto-Burman languages.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The name derives from the most widely spoken of these languages, <a href="/wiki/Burmese_language" title="Burmese language">Burmese</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Tibetic_languages" title="Tibetic languages">Tibetic languages</a>, which also have extensive literary traditions, dating from the 12th and 7th centuries respectively. Most of the other languages are spoken by much smaller communities, and many of them have not been described in detail.
</p><p>Though the division of Sino-Tibetan into Sinitic and Tibeto-Burman branches (e.g. Benedict, Matisoff) is widely used, some <a href="/wiki/Historical_linguist" class="mw-redirect" title="Historical linguist">historical linguists</a> criticize this classification, as the non-Sinitic Sino-Tibetan languages lack any <a href="/wiki/Shared_innovation" class="mw-redirect" title="Shared innovation">shared innovations</a> in <a href="/wiki/Phonology" title="Phonology">phonology</a> or <a href="/wiki/Morphology_(linguistics)" title="Morphology (linguistics)">morphology</a><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> to show that they comprise a <a href="/wiki/Clade" title="Clade">clade</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Phylogenetic_tree" title="Phylogenetic tree">phylogenetic tree</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHandel2008431_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHandel2008431-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tibeto-Burman_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<figure class="mw-halign-right" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:The_origin_and_spread_of_the_Sino-Tibetan_language_family.png" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/The_origin_and_spread_of_the_Sino-Tibetan_language_family.png/400px-The_origin_and_spread_of_the_Sino-Tibetan_language_family.png" decoding="async" width="400" height="268" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/The_origin_and_spread_of_the_Sino-Tibetan_language_family.png/600px-The_origin_and_spread_of_the_Sino-Tibetan_language_family.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/The_origin_and_spread_of_the_Sino-Tibetan_language_family.png/800px-The_origin_and_spread_of_the_Sino-Tibetan_language_family.png 2x" data-file-width="2851" data-file-height="1910" /></a><figcaption>A model of dispersal of the Sino-Tibetan languages.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESagartJacquesLaiRyder201910319–10320_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESagartJacquesLaiRyder201910319–10320-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup></figcaption></figure>
<p>During the 18th century, several scholars noticed parallels between Tibetan and Burmese, both languages with extensive literary traditions.
In the following century, <a href="/wiki/Brian_Houghton_Hodgson" title="Brian Houghton Hodgson">Brian Houghton Hodgson</a> collected a wealth of data on the non-literary languages of the Himalayas and northeast India, noting that many of these were related to Tibetan and Burmese.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHodgson1853_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHodgson1853-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
Others identified related languages in the highlands of Southeast Asia and south-west China.
The name "Tibeto-Burman" was first applied to this group in 1856 by <a href="/wiki/James_Logan_(lawyer)" class="mw-redirect" title="James Logan (lawyer)">James Logan</a>, who added <a href="/wiki/Karen_languages" class="mw-redirect" title="Karen languages">Karen</a> in 1858.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELogan1856_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELogan1856-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELogan1858_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELogan1858-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
Charles Forbes viewed the family as uniting the Gangetic and Lohitic branches of <a href="/wiki/Max_M%C3%BCller" title="Max Müller">Max Müller</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Turanian_languages" title="Turanian languages">Turanian</a>, a huge family consisting of all the Eurasian languages except the <a href="/wiki/Semitic_languages" title="Semitic languages">Semitic</a>, "Aryan" (<a href="/wiki/Indo-European_languages" title="Indo-European languages">Indo-European</a>) and Chinese languages.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEForbes1878_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEForbes1878-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
The third volume of the <i><a href="/wiki/Linguistic_Survey_of_India" title="Linguistic Survey of India">Linguistic Survey of India</a></i> was devoted to the Tibeto-Burman languages of <a href="/wiki/British_India" class="mw-redirect" title="British India">British India</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Julius_Klaproth" title="Julius Klaproth">Julius Klaproth</a> had noted in 1823 that Burmese, Tibetan and Chinese all shared common basic <a href="/wiki/Vocabulary" title="Vocabulary">vocabulary</a>, but that <a href="/wiki/Thai_language" title="Thai language">Thai</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mon_language" title="Mon language">Mon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vietnamese_language" title="Vietnamese language">Vietnamese</a> were quite different.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEvan_Driem2001334_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEvan_Driem2001334-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
Several authors, including Ernst Kuhn in 1883 and <a href="/wiki/August_Conrady" title="August Conrady">August Conrady</a> in 1896, described an "Indo-Chinese" family consisting of two branches, Tibeto-Burman and Chinese-Siamese.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEvan_Driem2001341–342_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEvan_Driem2001341–342-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
The <a href="/wiki/Tai_languages" title="Tai languages">Tai languages</a> were included on the basis of vocabulary and typological features shared with Chinese.
Jean Przyluski introduced the term <i>sino-tibétain</i> (Sino-Tibetan) as the title of his chapter on the group in <a href="/wiki/Antoine_Meillet" title="Antoine Meillet">Antoine Meillet</a> and <a href="/wiki/Marcel_Cohen" title="Marcel Cohen">Marcel Cohen</a>'s <i>Les Langues du Monde</i> in 1924.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESapir1925_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESapir1925-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Tai languages have not been included in most Western accounts of Sino-Tibetan since the Second World War, though many Chinese linguists still include them.
The link between Tibeto-Burman and Chinese is now accepted by most linguists, with a few exceptions such as <a href="/wiki/Roy_Andrew_Miller" title="Roy Andrew Miller">Roy Andrew Miller</a> and <a href="/wiki/Christopher_I._Beckwith" title="Christopher I. Beckwith">Christopher Beckwith</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMiller1974_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMiller1974-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBeckwith1996_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBeckwith1996-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBeckwith2002_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBeckwith2002-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
More recent controversy has centred on the proposed primary branching of Sino-Tibetan into Chinese and Tibeto-Burman subgroups.
In spite of the popularity of this classification, first proposed by Kuhn and Conrady, and also promoted by <a href="/wiki/Paul_K._Benedict" title="Paul K. Benedict">Paul Benedict</a> (1972) and later <a href="/wiki/James_Matisoff" title="James Matisoff">James Matisoff</a>, Tibeto-Burman has not been demonstrated to be a valid subgroup in its own right.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHandel2008431_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHandel2008431-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tibeto-Burman_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Overview"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Most of the Tibeto-Burman languages are spoken in remote mountain areas, which has hampered their study. Many lack a written standard.
It is generally easier to identify a language as Tibeto-Burman than to determine its precise relationship with other languages of the group.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHandel2008424–432_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHandel2008424–432-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
The subgroupings that have been established with certainty number several dozen, ranging from well-studied groups of dozens of languages with millions of speakers to several <a href="/wiki/Language_isolate" title="Language isolate">isolates</a>, some only discovered in the 21st century but in danger of extinction.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEvan_Driem2011a_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEvan_Driem2011a-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
These subgroups are here surveyed on a geographical basis.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Southeast_Asia_and_southwest_China">Southeast Asia and southwest China</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tibeto-Burman_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Southeast Asia and southwest China"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<figure class="mw-default-size mw-halign-right" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Ethnolinguistic_map_of_Burma_1972_en.svg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Ethnolinguistic_map_of_Burma_1972_en.svg/170px-Ethnolinguistic_map_of_Burma_1972_en.svg.png" decoding="async" width="170" height="326" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Ethnolinguistic_map_of_Burma_1972_en.svg/255px-Ethnolinguistic_map_of_Burma_1972_en.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Ethnolinguistic_map_of_Burma_1972_en.svg/340px-Ethnolinguistic_map_of_Burma_1972_en.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="1063" data-file-height="2037" /></a><figcaption>Language families of <a href="/wiki/Myanmar" title="Myanmar">Myanmar</a></figcaption></figure>
<p>The southernmost group is the <a href="/wiki/Karen_languages" class="mw-redirect" title="Karen languages">Karen languages</a>, spoken by three million people on both sides of the Burma–Thailand border. They differ from all other Tibeto-Burman languages (except Bai) in having a <a href="/wiki/Subject%E2%80%93verb%E2%80%93object" class="mw-redirect" title="Subject–verb–object">subject–verb–object</a> word order, attributed to contact with <a href="/wiki/Tai%E2%80%93Kadai_languages" class="mw-redirect" title="Tai–Kadai languages">Tai–Kadai</a> and <a href="/wiki/Austroasiatic_languages" title="Austroasiatic languages">Austroasiatic languages</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThurgood200318_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThurgood200318-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The most widely spoken Tibeto-Burman language is <a href="/wiki/Burmese_language" title="Burmese language">Burmese</a>, the national language of Myanmar, with over 32 million speakers and a literary tradition dating from the early 12th century. It is one of the <a href="/wiki/Lolo-Burmese_languages" title="Lolo-Burmese languages">Lolo-Burmese languages</a>, an intensively studied and well-defined group comprising approximately 100 languages spoken in Myanmar and the highlands of Thailand, Laos, Vietnam, and <a href="/wiki/Southwest_China" class="mw-redirect" title="Southwest China">southwest China</a>. Major languages include the <a href="/wiki/Loloish_languages" title="Loloish languages">Loloish languages</a>, with two million speakers in western <a href="/wiki/Sichuan" title="Sichuan">Sichuan</a> and northern <a href="/wiki/Yunnan" title="Yunnan">Yunnan</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Akha_language" title="Akha language">Akha language</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hani_language" title="Hani language">Hani languages</a>, with two million speakers in southern Yunnan, eastern Myanmar, Laos and Vietnam, and <a href="/wiki/Lisu_language" title="Lisu language">Lisu</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lahu_language" title="Lahu language">Lahu</a> in Yunnan, northern Myanmar and northern Thailand. All languages of the Loloish subgroup show significant Austroasiatic influence.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThurgood20038–9_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThurgood20038–9-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
The <a href="/wiki/Pai-lang" class="mw-redirect" title="Pai-lang">Pai-lang</a> songs, transcribed in Chinese characters in the 1st century, appear to record words from a Lolo-Burmese language, but arranged in Chinese order.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECoblin1979_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECoblin1979-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<figure class="mw-default-size mw-halign-right" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Ethnolinguistic_map_of_China_1983.png" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Ethnolinguistic_map_of_China_1983.png/220px-Ethnolinguistic_map_of_China_1983.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="206" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Ethnolinguistic_map_of_China_1983.png/330px-Ethnolinguistic_map_of_China_1983.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Ethnolinguistic_map_of_China_1983.png/440px-Ethnolinguistic_map_of_China_1983.png 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="960" /></a><figcaption>Language families of China, with Tibeto-Burman in orange<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;a&#93;</a></sup></figcaption></figure>
<p>The Tibeto-Burman languages of south-west China have been heavily influenced by Chinese over a long period, leaving their affiliations difficult to determine. The grouping of the <a href="/wiki/Bai_language" title="Bai language">Bai language</a>, with one million speakers in Yunnan, is particularly controversial, with some workers suggesting that it is a sister language to Chinese. The <a href="/wiki/Naxi_language" title="Naxi language">Naxi language</a> of northern Yunnan is usually included in Lolo-Burmese, though other scholars prefer to leave it unclassified.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThurgood200320_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThurgood200320-23">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> The hills of northwestern Sichuan are home to the small <a href="/wiki/Qiangic_languages" title="Qiangic languages">Qiangic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rgyalrongic_languages" class="mw-redirect" title="Rgyalrongic languages">Rgyalrongic</a> groups of languages, which preserve many archaic features. The most easterly Tibeto-Burman language is <a href="/wiki/Tujia_language" title="Tujia language">Tujia</a>, spoken in the <a href="/wiki/Wuling_Mountains" title="Wuling Mountains">Wuling Mountains</a> on the borders of Hunan, Hubei, Guizhou and Chongqing.
</p><p>Two historical languages are believed to be Tibeto-Burman, but their precise affiliation is uncertain. The <a href="/wiki/Pyu_language_(Burma)" class="mw-redirect" title="Pyu language (Burma)">Pyu language</a> of central Myanmar in the first centuries is known from inscriptions using a variant of the <a href="/wiki/Gupta_script" title="Gupta script">Gupta script</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Tangut_language" title="Tangut language">Tangut language</a> of the 12th century <a href="/wiki/Western_Xia" title="Western Xia">Western Xia</a> of northern China is preserved in numerous texts written in the Chinese-inspired <a href="/wiki/Tangut_script" title="Tangut script">Tangut script</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThurgood200317,_19–20_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThurgood200317,_19–20-24">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tibet_and_South_Asia">Tibet and South Asia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tibeto-Burman_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Tibet and South Asia"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<figure class="mw-default-size mw-halign-right" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:South_Asian_Language_Families.png" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/South_Asian_Language_Families.png/220px-South_Asian_Language_Families.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="264" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/South_Asian_Language_Families.png/330px-South_Asian_Language_Families.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/South_Asian_Language_Families.png/440px-South_Asian_Language_Families.png 2x" data-file-width="1000" data-file-height="1201" /></a><figcaption>Language families of South Asia, with Tibeto-Burman in orange</figcaption></figure>
<p>Over eight million people in the <a href="/wiki/Tibetan_Plateau" title="Tibetan Plateau">Tibetan Plateau</a> and neighbouring areas in <a href="/wiki/Baltistan" title="Baltistan">Baltistan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ladakh" title="Ladakh">Ladakh</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nepal" title="Nepal">Nepal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sikkim" title="Sikkim">Sikkim</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bhutan" title="Bhutan">Bhutan</a> speak one of several related <a href="/wiki/Tibetic_languages" title="Tibetic languages">Tibetic languages</a>. There is an extensive literature in <a href="/wiki/Classical_Tibetan" title="Classical Tibetan">Classical Tibetan</a> dating from the 8th century. The Tibetic languages are usually grouped with the smaller <a href="/wiki/East_Bodish_languages" title="East Bodish languages">East Bodish languages</a> of Bhutan and <a href="/wiki/Arunachal_Pradesh" title="Arunachal Pradesh">Arunachal Pradesh</a> as the <a href="/wiki/Bodish_languages" title="Bodish languages">Bodish</a> group.
</p><p>Many diverse Tibeto-Burman languages are spoken on the southern slopes of the Himalayas.
Sizable groups that have been identified are the <a href="/wiki/West_Himalayish_languages" title="West Himalayish languages">West Himalayish languages</a> of <a href="/wiki/Himachal_Pradesh" title="Himachal Pradesh">Himachal Pradesh</a> and western Nepal, the <a href="/wiki/Tamangic_languages" title="Tamangic languages">Tamangic languages</a> of western Nepal, including <a href="/wiki/Tamang_language" title="Tamang language">Tamang</a> with one million speakers, and the <a href="/wiki/Kiranti_languages" title="Kiranti languages">Kiranti languages</a> of eastern Nepal.
The remaining groups are small, with several isolates.
The <a href="/wiki/Newar_language" title="Newar language">Newar language</a> (Nepal Bhasa) of central Nepal has a million speakers and literature dating from the 12th century, and nearly a million people speak <a href="/wiki/Magaric_languages" title="Magaric languages">Magaric languages</a>, but the rest have small speech communities.
Other isolates and small groups in Nepal are <a href="/wiki/Dura_language" title="Dura language">Dura</a>, <a href="/wiki/Raji%E2%80%93Raute_languages" title="Raji–Raute languages">Raji–Raute</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chepangic_languages" title="Chepangic languages">Chepangic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dhimal_languages" class="mw-redirect" title="Dhimal languages">Dhimalish</a>.
<a href="/wiki/Lepcha_language" title="Lepcha language">Lepcha</a> is spoken in an area from eastern Nepal to western Bhutan.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEvan_Driem2007296_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEvan_Driem2007296-25">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
Most of the languages of Bhutan are Bodish, but it also has three small isolates, <a href="/wiki/%27Ole_language" class="mw-redirect" title="&#39;Ole language">'Ole</a> ("Black Mountain Monpa"), <a href="/wiki/Lhokpu_language" title="Lhokpu language">Lhokpu</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gongduk_language" title="Gongduk language">Gongduk</a> and a larger community of speakers of <a href="/wiki/Tshangla_language" title="Tshangla language">Tshangla</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEvan_Driem2011a_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEvan_Driem2011a-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Tani_languages" title="Tani languages">Tani languages</a> include most of the Tibeto-Burman languages of Arunachal Pradesh and adjacent areas of Tibet.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBurling2003178,_180–181_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBurling2003178,_180–181-26">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
The remaining languages of Arunachal Pradesh are much more diverse, belonging to the small <a href="/wiki/Siangic_languages" title="Siangic languages">Siangic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kho-Bwa_languages" title="Kho-Bwa languages">Kho-Bwa</a> (or Kamengic), <a href="/wiki/Hruso_languages" class="mw-redirect" title="Hruso languages">Hruso</a>, <a href="/wiki/Miju_languages" title="Miju languages">Miju</a> and <a href="/wiki/Digaro_languages" title="Digaro languages">Digaro languages</a> (or Mishmic) groups.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBurling2003178–182_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBurling2003178–182-27">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
These groups have relatively little Tibeto-Burman vocabulary, and Bench and Post dispute their inclusion in Sino-Tibetan.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBlenchPost2011_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBlenchPost2011-28">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The greatest variety of languages and subgroups is found in the highlands stretching from northern Myanmar to northeast India.
</p><p>Northern Myanmar is home to the small <a href="/wiki/Nungish_languages" title="Nungish languages">Nungish</a> group, as well as the <a href="/wiki/Jingpho%E2%80%93Luish_languages" title="Jingpho–Luish languages">Jingpho–Luish languages</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Jingpho_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Jingpho language">Jingpho</a> with nearly a million speakers.
The Brahmaputran or <a href="/wiki/Sal_languages" title="Sal languages">Sal languages</a> include at least the <a href="/wiki/Boro%E2%80%93Garo_languages" title="Boro–Garo languages">Boro–Garo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Konyak_languages" title="Konyak languages">Konyak languages</a>, spoken in an area stretching from northern Myanmar through the Indian states of <a href="/wiki/Nagaland" title="Nagaland">Nagaland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Meghalaya" title="Meghalaya">Meghalaya</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tripura" title="Tripura">Tripura</a>, and are often considered to include the Jingpho–Luish group.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThurgood200311–12_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThurgood200311–12-29">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBurling2003174–178_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBurling2003174–178-30">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The border highlands of <a href="/wiki/Nagaland" title="Nagaland">Nagaland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Manipur" title="Manipur">Manipur</a> and western Myanmar are home to the small <a href="/wiki/Ao_languages" title="Ao languages">Ao</a>, <a href="/wiki/Angami%E2%80%93Pochuri_languages" title="Angami–Pochuri languages">Angami–Pochuri</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tangkhulic_languages" title="Tangkhulic languages">Tangkhulic</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Zeme_languages" class="mw-redirect" title="Zeme languages">Zeme</a> groups of languages, as well as the <a href="/wiki/Karbi_language" title="Karbi language">Karbi language</a>.
<a href="/wiki/Meithei_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Meithei language">Meithei</a>, the main language of Manipur with 1.4 million speakers, is sometimes linked with the 50 or so <a href="/wiki/Kuki-Chin_languages" title="Kuki-Chin languages">Kuki-Chin languages</a> are spoken in <a href="/wiki/Mizoram" title="Mizoram">Mizoram</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Chin_State" title="Chin State">Chin State</a> of Myanmar.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Mru_language" title="Mru language">Mru language</a> is spoken by a small group in the <a href="/wiki/Chittagong_Hill_Tracts" title="Chittagong Hill Tracts">Chittagong Hill Tracts</a> between Bangladesh and Myanmar.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThurgood200312–14_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThurgood200312–14-31">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBurling2003182–189_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBurling2003182–189-32">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">Classification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tibeto-Burman_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Classification"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There have been two milestones in the classification of Sino-Tibetan and Tibeto-Burman languages, <a href="#CITEREFShafer1955">Shafer (1955)</a> and <a href="#CITEREFBenedict1972">Benedict (1972)</a>, which were actually produced in the 1930s and 1940s respectively.
</p>
<h3><span id="Shafer_.281955.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Shafer_(1955)">Shafer (1955)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tibeto-Burman_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Shafer (1955)"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Shafer's tentative classification took an agnostic position and did not recognize Tibeto-Burman, but placed Chinese (Sinitic) on the same level as the other branches of a Sino-Tibetan family.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEShafer1955_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEShafer1955-33">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> He retained Tai–Kadai (Daic) within the family, allegedly at the insistence of colleagues, despite his personal belief that they were not related.

<h3><span id="Benedict_.281972.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Benedict_(1972)">Benedict (1972)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tibeto-Burman_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Benedict (1972)"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A very influential, although also tentative, classification is that of <a href="#CITEREFBenedict1972">Benedict (1972)</a>, which was actually written around 1941.  Like Shafer's work, this drew on the data assembled by the Sino-Tibetan Philology Project, which was directed by Shafer and Benedict in turn. Benedict envisaged Chinese as the first family to branch off, followed by Karen.
</p>

<h3><span id="Matisoff_.281978.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Matisoff_(1978)">Matisoff (1978)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tibeto-Burman_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Matisoff (1978)"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/James_Matisoff" title="James Matisoff">James Matisoff</a> proposes a modification of Benedict that demoted Karen but kept the divergent position of Sinitic.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENamkung1996455_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENamkung1996455-34">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> Of the 7 branches within Tibeto-Burman, 2 branches (Baic and Karenic) have <a href="/wiki/Subject%E2%80%93verb%E2%80%93object" class="mw-redirect" title="Subject–verb–object">SVO</a>-order languages, whereas all the other 5 branches have <a href="/wiki/Subject%E2%80%93object%E2%80%93verb" class="mw-redirect" title="Subject–object–verb">SOV</a>-order languages.
</p>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1116488514"><div class="treeview">
<ul><li>Sino-Tibetan
<ul><li>Chinese</li>
<li><b>Tibeto-Burman</b></li></ul></li></ul>
</div>
<p>Tibeto-Burman is then divided into several branches, some of them geographic conveniences rather than linguistic proposals:
</p>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1116488514"><div class="treeview">
<ul><li><b>Tibeto-Burman</b>
<ul><li>Kamarupan (geographic)
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Kuki-Chin%E2%80%93Naga_languages" title="Kuki-Chin–Naga languages">Kuki-Chin–Naga</a> (geographic)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Tani_languages" title="Tani languages">Abor–Miri–Dafla</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Boro%E2%80%93Garo_languages" title="Boro–Garo languages">Boro–Garo</a></li></ul></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Himalayish_languages" class="mw-redirect" title="Himalayish languages">Himalayish</a> (geographic)

<p>Matisoff makes no claim that the families in the Kamarupan or Himalayish branches have a special relationship to one another other than a geographic one. They are intended rather as categories of convenience pending more detailed comparative work.
</p><p>Matisoff also notes that Jingpho–Nungish–Luish is central to the family in that it contains features of many of the other branches, and is also located around the center of the Tibeto-Burman-speaking area.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_languages">Other languages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tibeto-Burman_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Other languages"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The classification of <a href="/wiki/Tujia_language" title="Tujia language">Tujia</a> is difficult due to extensive borrowing. Other unclassified Tibeto-Burman languages include <a href="/wiki/Basum_language" title="Basum language">Basum</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Songlin_language" title="Songlin language">Songlin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chamdo_languages" title="Chamdo languages">Chamdo languages</a>, both of which were only described in the 2010s. New Tibeto-Burman languages continue to be recognized, some not closely related to other languages. Distinct languages only recognized in the 2010s include <a href="/wiki/Koki_Naga_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Koki Naga language">Koki Naga</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Randy_LaPolla" title="Randy LaPolla">Randy LaPolla</a> (2003) proposed a <a href="/wiki/Rung_languages" title="Rung languages">Rung branch</a> of Tibeto-Burman, based on morphological evidence, but this is not widely accepted.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Scott_DeLancey" title="Scott DeLancey">Scott DeLancey</a> (2015)<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> proposed a <a href="/wiki/Central_Tibeto-Burman_languages" title="Central Tibeto-Burman languages">Central branch</a> of Tibeto-Burman based on morphological evidence.
</p><p>Roger Blench and Mark Post (2011) list a number of divergent languages of <a href="/wiki/Arunachal_Pradesh" title="Arunachal Pradesh">Arunachal Pradesh</a>, in northeastern India, that might have non-Tibeto-Burman substrates, or could even be non-Tibeto-Burman <a href="/wiki/Language_isolate" title="Language isolate">language isolates</a>:<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBlenchPost2011_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBlenchPost2011-28">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
