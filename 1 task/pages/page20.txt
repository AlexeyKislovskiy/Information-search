<p>The <b>Tuareg</b> (<span class="rt-commentedText nowrap"><small>English:  </small><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt" lang="en-fonipa"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="&#39;t&#39; in &#39;tie&#39;">t</span><span title="&#39;w&#39; in &#39;wind&#39;">w</span><span title="/ɑːr/: &#39;ar&#39; in &#39;far&#39;">ɑːr</span><span title="/ɛ/: &#39;e&#39; in &#39;dress&#39;">ɛ</span><span title="/ɡ/: &#39;g&#39; in &#39;guy&#39;">ɡ</span></span>/</a></span></span>) languages constitute a group of closely related <a href="/wiki/Berber_languages" title="Berber languages">Berber languages</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dialect" title="Dialect">dialects</a>. They are spoken by the <a href="/wiki/Tuareg_people" title="Tuareg people">Tuareg</a> <a href="/wiki/Berbers" title="Berbers">Berbers</a> in large parts of <a href="/wiki/Mali" title="Mali">Mali</a>, <a href="/wiki/Niger" title="Niger">Niger</a>, <a href="/wiki/Algeria" title="Algeria">Algeria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Libya" title="Libya">Libya</a> and <a href="/wiki/Burkina_Faso" title="Burkina Faso">Burkina Faso</a>, with a few speakers, the <i>Kinnin</i>, in <a href="/wiki/Chad" title="Chad">Chad</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tuareg_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Tuareg dialects belong to the South Berber group and are sometimes regarded as a single language (as for instance by <a href="/wiki/Karl-Gottfried_Prasse" title="Karl-Gottfried Prasse">Karl-Gottfried Prasse</a>). They are distinguished mainly by a few sound shifts (notably affecting the pronunciation of original <i>z</i> and <i>h</i>). The Tuareg varieties are unusually <a href="/wiki/Conservative_(language)" class="mw-redirect" title="Conservative (language)">conservative</a> in some respects; they retain two short vowels where Northern-Berber languages have one or none, and have a much lower proportion of <a href="/wiki/Arabic_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a> <a href="/wiki/Loanword" title="Loanword">loanwords</a> than most Berber languages.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2021)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>The Tuareg languages are traditionally written in the indigenous <a href="/wiki/Tifinagh" title="Tifinagh">Tifinagh</a> alphabet. However, the <a href="/wiki/Arabic_script" title="Arabic script">Arabic script</a> is commonly used in some areas (and has been since medieval times), while the <a href="/wiki/Latin_script" title="Latin script">Latin script</a> is official in <a href="/wiki/Mali" title="Mali">Mali</a> and <a href="/wiki/Niger" title="Niger">Niger</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2021)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Subclassification">Subclassification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tuareg_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Subclassification"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li>Northern
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Tamahaq_language" title="Tamahaq language">Tamahaq</a> – language of the <a href="/wiki/Kel_Ahaggar" title="Kel Ahaggar">Kel Ahaggar</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Kel_Ajjer" title="Kel Ajjer">Kel Ajjer</a> spoken in Algeria, western <a href="/wiki/Libya" title="Libya">Libya</a> and in the north of Niger by around 77,000 people. Also known as Tahaggart.</li></ul></li>
<li>Southern
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Tamasheq_language" title="Tamasheq language">Tamasheq</a> – language of the Kel Adrar (also known as <a href="/wiki/Adrar_des_Ifoghas" title="Adrar des Ifoghas">Adrar des Ifoghas</a>), spoken in Mali by approximately 500,000 people.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Air_Tamajeq_language" title="Air Tamajeq language">Air Tamajaq</a> – language of the <a href="/wiki/Kel_Ayr" title="Kel Ayr">Kel Ayer</a> (sometimes spelled Aïr), spoken in Niger by approximately 250,000 people.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Tawellemmet_language" title="Tawellemmet language">Tawellemet</a> – language of the Iwellemmeden, spoken in Mali and Niger by approximately 800,000 people. The term <a href="/wiki/Iwellemmedan_people" title="Iwellemmedan people">Iwellemmeden</a> (the name of the people) is sometimes used to denote the language.</li>
<li>Tamashaq language of Kal Asakan.</li></ul></li></ul>
<p><a href="/wiki/Roger_Blench" title="Roger Blench">Blench</a> (ms, 2006) lists the following as separate languages, with dialects in parentheses:<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Tawellemmet_language" title="Tawellemmet language">Tawellemet</a> (Abalagh/East, West)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Air_Tamajeq_language" title="Air Tamajeq language">Tayiṛt</a> (Ingal, Gofat)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Air_Tamajeq_language" title="Air Tamajeq language">Tamesgrest</a> (Azerori)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Tafaghist_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Tafaghist language">Tafaghist</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Tamahaq_language" title="Tamahaq language">Tahaggart/Ahaggar</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Tamahaq_language" title="Tamahaq language">Ghat</a></li></ul>
<p>Speakers of <a href="/wiki/Tetserret_language" title="Tetserret language">Tin Sert</a> (Tetserret) identify as Tuareg, but the language is <a href="/wiki/Western_Berber_languages" title="Western Berber languages">Western Berber</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Orthography">Orthography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tuareg_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Orthography"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Tuareg languages may be written using the ancient <a href="/wiki/Tifinagh" title="Tifinagh">Tifinagh</a> (Libyco-Berber) script, the <a href="/wiki/Latin_script" title="Latin script">Latin script</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Arabic_script" title="Arabic script">Arabic script</a>. The Malian national literacy program <a href="/wiki/DNAFLA" class="mw-redirect" title="DNAFLA">DNAFLA</a> has established a standard for the Latin alphabet, which is used with modifications in Prasse's <i>Lexique</i> and the government literacy program in Burkina, while in Niger a different system was used. There is also some variation in Tifinagh and in the Arabic script.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Early uses of the Tifinagh script have been found on <a href="/wiki/Rock_art" title="Rock art">rock art</a> and in various <a href="/wiki/Sepulchre" class="mw-redirect" title="Sepulchre">sepulchres</a>. Among these are the <a href="/wiki/Tin_Hinan_Tomb" title="Tin Hinan Tomb">1,500 year old monumental tomb</a> of the Tuareg matriarch <a href="/wiki/Tin_Hinan" title="Tin Hinan">Tin Hinan</a>, where vestiges of a Tifinagh <a href="/wiki/Inscription" class="mw-redirect" title="Inscription">inscription</a> have been found on one of its walls.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Tifinagh usage is now restricted mainly to writing magical formulae, writing on palms when silence is required, and in letter-writing.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The Arabic script is mostly in use by tribes more involved in Islamic learning, and little is known about its conventions.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Phonology">Phonology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tuareg_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Phonology"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vowels">Vowels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tuareg_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Vowels"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The vowel system includes five long vowels, <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/a,<span class="wrap"> </span>e,<span class="wrap"> </span>i,<span class="wrap"> </span>o,<span class="wrap"> </span>u/</span> and two short vowels, <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ə,<span class="wrap"> </span>ă/</span> (on this page, <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ă/</span> is used to represent <a href="/wiki/International_Phonetic_Alphabet" title="International Phonetic Alphabet">IPA</a> <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[æ]</span>). Some of the vowels have more open "emphatic" allophones that occur immediately before emphatic consonants, subject to dialectal variation. These allophones include <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ɛ]</span> for <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/e/</span> and <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/i/</span> (although <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/i/</span> may be less open), <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ɔ]</span> for <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/o/</span> and <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/u/</span> (although <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/u/</span> may be less open), and <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ă]</span> for <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ə/</span>.<sup id="cite_ref-auto_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Karl Prasse argued that /e/ goes back to <a href="/wiki/Proto-Berber_language" title="Proto-Berber language">Proto-Berber</a>, while /o/ is derived from /u/.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Comparative evidence shows that /ə/ derives from a merger of Proto-Berber */ĭ/ and */ŭ/.
</p><p>Sudlow classes the "semivowels" /w, j/ with the vowels, and notes the following possible diphthongs: /əw/ (&gt; [u]), /ăw/, /aw/, /ew/, /iw/, /ow/, /uw/, /əj/ (&gt; [i]), /ăj/, /aj/, /ej/, /ij/, /oj/, /uj/.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>The consonant inventory largely resembles Arabic: differentiated voicing; uvulars, pharyngeals (traditionally referred to as emphatics) <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/tˤ/,<span class="wrap"> </span>/lˤ/,<span class="wrap"> </span>/sˤ/,<span class="wrap"> </span>/dˤ/,<span class="wrap"> </span>/zˤ/</span>; requiring the pharynx muscles to contract and influencing the pronunciation of the following vowel (although <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/lˤ,<span class="wrap"> </span>sˤ/</span> only occur in Arabic loans and <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/<a href="/wiki/Pharyngealization" title="Pharyngealization">lˤ</a>/</span> only in the name of <a href="/wiki/Allah" title="Allah">Allah</a>), and no <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_bilabial_plosive" title="Voiceless bilabial plosive">voiceless bilabial plosive</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ŋ/</span> is rare, <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ʒ/</span> is rare in Tadɣaq, and <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ħ,<span class="wrap"> </span>ʕ/</span> are only used in Arabic words in the Tanəsləmt dialect (most Tamasheq replace them with <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/x,<span class="wrap"> </span>ɣ/</span> respectively).<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Glottal_stop" title="Glottal stop">glottal stop</a> is non-phonemic. It occurs at the beginning of vowel-initial words to fill the place of the initial consonant in the syllable structure (see below), although if the words is preceded by a word ending in a consonant, it makes a <a href="/wiki/Sandhi" title="Sandhi">liaison</a> instead. Phrase-final /a/ is also followed by a phonetic <a href="/wiki/Glottal_stop" title="Glottal stop">glottal stop</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Harvcoltxt&#124;Sudlow&#124;2001&#124;p=27_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harvcoltxt|Sudlow|2001|p=27-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Gemination is contrastive.<sup id="cite_ref-Harvcoltxt&#124;Sudlow&#124;2001&#124;p=28_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harvcoltxt|Sudlow|2001|p=28-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Normally <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ɣɣ/</span> becomes <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[qː]</span>, <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ww/</span> becomes <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ɡː]</span>, and <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/dˤdˤ/</span> becomes <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[tˤː]</span>.<sup id="cite_ref-Harvcoltxt&#124;Sudlow&#124;2001&#124;p=28_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harvcoltxt|Sudlow|2001|p=28-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/q/</span> and <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/tˤ/</span> are predominantly geminate. In addition, in Tadɣaq <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ɡ/</span> is usually geminate, but in Tudalt singleton <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ɡ/</span> may occur.<sup id="cite_ref-Harvcoltxt&#124;Sudlow&#124;2001&#124;p=28_26-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harvcoltxt|Sudlow|2001|p=28-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Voicing assimilation occurs, with the first consonant taking the voicing of the second (e.g. <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/edˤkăr/</span> &gt; <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[etˤkăr]</span>).<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Cluster_reduction" title="Cluster reduction">Cluster reduction</a> turns word/morpheme-final <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/-ɣt,<span class="wrap"> </span>-ɣk/</span> into <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[-qː]</span> and <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/-kt,<span class="wrap"> </span>-ɟt,<span class="wrap"> </span>-ɡt/</span> into <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[-kː]</span> (e.g. <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/tămaʃăɣt/</span> &gt; <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[tămaʃăq]</span> 'Tamasheq'<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>).<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Phonotactics">Phonotactics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tuareg_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Phonotactics"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Syllable structure is CV(C)(C), including <a href="/wiki/Glottal_stop" title="Glottal stop">glottal stops</a> (see above).<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Suprasegmentals">Suprasegmentals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tuareg_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Suprasegmentals"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Contrastive stress may occur in the stative aspect of verbs.<sup id="cite_ref-auto_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dialectal_differences">Dialectal differences</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tuareg_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Dialectal differences"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Different dialects have slightly different consonant inventories. Some of these differences can be <a href="/wiki/Diachronic_linguistics" class="mw-redirect" title="Diachronic linguistics">diachronically</a> accounted for. For example, Proto-Berber <i>*h</i> is mostly lost in Ayer Tuareg, while it is maintained in almost every position in Mali Tuareg. The Iwellemmeden and Ahaggar Tuareg dialects are midway between these positions.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> The Proto-Berber consonant <i>*z</i> comes out differently in different dialects, a development that is to some degree reflected in the dialect names. It is realized as <i>h</i> in Tamahaq (Tahaggart), as <i>š</i> in Tamasheq and as simple <i>z</i> in the Tamajaq dialects Tawallammat and Tayart. In the latter two, <i>*z</i> is realised as <i>ž</i> before palatal vowels, explaining the form <i>Tamajaq</i>. In Tawallammat and especially Tayart, this kind of palatalization actually does not confine itself to <i>z</i>. In these dialects, dentals in general are palatalized before <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/i/</span> and <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/j/</span>. For example, <i>tidət</i> is pronounced <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[tidʲət]</span> in Tayart.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Other differences can easily be traced back to borrowing. For example, the Arabic pharyngeals <i>ħ</i> and <i>ʻ</i> have been borrowed along with Arabic loanwords by dialects specialized in Islamic (<a href="/wiki/Maraboutic" class="mw-redirect" title="Maraboutic">Maraboutic</a>) learning. Other dialects substitute <i>ħ</i> and <i>ʻ</i> respectively with <i>x</i> and <i>ɣ</i>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Grammar">Grammar</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tuareg_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Grammar"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The basic word order in Tuareg is <a href="/wiki/Verb%E2%80%93subject%E2%80%93object" class="mw-redirect" title="Verb–subject–object">verb–subject–object</a>. Verbs can be grouped into 19 morphological classes; some of these classes can be defined semantically. Verbs carry information on the subject of the sentence in the form of pronominal marking. No simple adjectives exist in the Tuareg languages; adjectival concepts are expressed using a relative verb form traditionally called 'participle'. The Tuareg languages have very heavily influenced Northern <a href="/wiki/Songhay_languages" title="Songhay languages">Songhay languages</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Sawaq_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Sawaq language">Sawaq</a>, whose speakers are culturally Tuareg but speak Songhay; this influence includes points of phonology and sometimes grammar as well as extensive loanwords.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Syntax">Syntax</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tuareg_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Syntax"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Tamasheq prefers VSO order; however it contains <a href="/wiki/Topic%E2%80%93comment" class="mw-redirect" title="Topic–comment">topic–comment</a> structure (like in American Sign Language, Modern Hebrew, Japanese and Russian), allowing the emphasized concept to be placed first, be it the subject or object, the latter giving an effect somewhat like the English passive.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> Sudlow uses the following examples, all expressing the concept "Men don't cook porridge" (e denotes Sudlow's schwa):
</p>

<p>The verbal correspondence with the use of aspect; Tamasheq uses four, as delineated by Sudlow:
</p>
<ol><li>Perfective: complete actions</li>
<li>Stative: "lasting states as the ongoing results of a completed action."</li>
<li>Imperfective: future or possible actions, "often used following a verb expressing emotion, decision or thought," it can be marked with "'ad'" (shortened to "'a-'" with prepositions).</li>
<li>Cursive: ongoing actions, often habitual ones.</li></ol>
