<p><b>Western Armenian</b> (<a href="/wiki/Western_Armenian_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Western Armenian language">Western Armenian</a>: <span lang="hyw">Արեւմտահայերէն</span>, <small>romanized:&#160;</small><span title="Western Armenian-language romanization"><i lang="hyw-Latn">Arevmdahayeren</i></span> <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="hyw-Latn-fonipa"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA" title="Help:IPA">&#91;ɑɾɛvmədɑhɑjɛˈɾɛn&#93;</a></span>)<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;a&#93;</a></sup> is one of the two <a href="/wiki/Standard_language" title="Standard language">standardized</a><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> forms of <a href="/wiki/Armenian_language" title="Armenian language">Modern Armenian</a>, the other being <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Armenian" title="Eastern Armenian">Eastern Armenian</a>. It is based mainly on the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Istanbul_dialect_(Armenian)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Istanbul dialect (Armenian) (page does not exist)">Istanbul Armenian dialect</a>, as opposed to Eastern Armenian, which is mainly based on the <a href="/wiki/Yerevan_dialect" title="Yerevan dialect">Yerevan Armenian dialect</a>.
</p><p>Until the early 20th century, various Western Armenian dialects were also spoken in the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman Empire</a>, especially in the eastern regions historically populated by Armenians known as <a href="/wiki/Western_Armenia" title="Western Armenia">Western Armenia</a>. The spoken or dialectal varieties of Western Armenian currently in use include <a href="/wiki/Homshetsi_dialect" title="Homshetsi dialect">Homshetsi</a>, spoken by the <a href="/wiki/Hemshin_peoples" class="mw-redirect" title="Hemshin peoples">Hemshin peoples</a>;<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> the dialects of Armenians of <a href="/wiki/Kessab" title="Kessab">Kessab</a>, <a href="/wiki/Latakia" title="Latakia">Latakia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jisr_al-Shughur" class="mw-redirect" title="Jisr al-Shughur">Jisr al-Shughur</a> of Syria, <a href="/wiki/Anjar,_Lebanon" title="Anjar, Lebanon">Anjar</a> of Lebanon, and <a href="/wiki/Armenians_in_Istanbul" title="Armenians in Istanbul">Istanbul</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vak%C4%B1fl%C4%B1,_Samanda%C4%9F" title="Vakıflı, Samandağ">Vakıflı</a>, of Turkey (part of the "Sueidia" dialect). Sasun and Mush dialect is also spoken in modern-day Armenia villages such as Bazmaberd and Sasnashen. The <a href="/wiki/Cilicia" title="Cilicia">Cilician</a> dialect is also spoken in <a href="/wiki/Cyprus" title="Cyprus">Cyprus</a>, where it is taught in Armenian schools (Nareg), and is the first language of about 3,000 people of Armenian descent.
</p><p>Forms of the <a href="/wiki/Karin_dialect" title="Karin dialect">Karin dialect</a> of Western Armenian are spoken by several hundred thousand people in Northern Armenia, mostly in <a href="/wiki/Gyumri" title="Gyumri">Gyumri</a>, <a href="/wiki/Artik" title="Artik">Artik</a>, <a href="/wiki/Akhuryan" title="Akhuryan">Akhuryan</a>, and around 130 villages in the <a href="/wiki/Shirak_Province" title="Shirak Province">Shirak province</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> and by <a href="/wiki/Armenians_in_Samtskhe%E2%80%93Javakheti" title="Armenians in Samtskhe–Javakheti">Armenians in Samtskhe–Javakheti</a> province of Georgia (<a href="/wiki/Akhalkalaki" title="Akhalkalaki">Akhalkalaki</a>, <a href="/wiki/Akhaltsikhe" title="Akhaltsikhe">Akhaltsikhe</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-Hovannisian_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hovannisian-7">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A mostly <a href="/wiki/Diaspora" title="Diaspora">diasporic</a> language and one that is not an official language of any state, Western Armenian faces extinction as its native speakers lose fluency in Western Armenian amid pressures to assimilate into their host countries. According to Ethnologue, there are 1.58 million native speakers of Western Armenian, primarily in Turkey, Armenia, Georgia, Lebanon, and Iraq. The language is classified as 6b (i.e., threatened, with interruptions in intergenerational transmission).<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">Classification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Western_Armenian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Classification"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Western Armenian is an <a href="/wiki/Indo-European_languages" title="Indo-European languages">Indo-European language</a> and belongs to the <a href="/wiki/Armenian_language" title="Armenian language">Armenic</a> branch of the family, along with <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Armenian" title="Eastern Armenian">Eastern</a> and <a href="/wiki/Classical_Armenian" title="Classical Armenian">Classical Armenian</a>. According to <i><a href="/wiki/Glottolog" title="Glottolog">Glottolog</a></i> <a href="/wiki/Antioch" title="Antioch">Antioch</a>, Artial, Asia Minor, <a href="/wiki/Bolu" title="Bolu">Bolu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Homshetsi_dialect" title="Homshetsi dialect">Hamshenic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cilicia" title="Cilicia">Kilikien</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mu%C5%9F" title="Muş">Mush</a>-Tigranakert, Stanoz, <a href="/wiki/Van,_Turkey" title="Van, Turkey">Vanic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Yozgat" title="Yozgat">Yozgat</a> are the main dialects of Western Armenian.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Eastern Armenian and Western Armenian are, for the most part, mutually intelligible for educated or literate users of the other, while illiterate or semiliterate users of lower registers of each one may have difficulty understanding the other variant. One phonological difference is that <a href="/wiki/Voiced" class="mw-redirect" title="Voiced">voiced</a> <a href="/wiki/Plosive" title="Plosive">stops</a> in Eastern Armenian are <a href="/wiki/Voiceless" class="mw-redirect" title="Voiceless">voiceless</a> in Western Armenian.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Speakers">Speakers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Western_Armenian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Speakers"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Western Armenian is spoken by Armenians of most of the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a> except for <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Rostov-on-Don" title="Rostov-on-Don">Rostov-on-Don</a> in <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>. It is spoken by only a small percentage of <a href="/wiki/Armenians_in_Turkey" title="Armenians in Turkey">Armenians in Turkey</a> as a first language, with 18 percent among the community in general and 8 percent among younger people.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Western Armenian used to be the dominant Armenian variety, but as a result of the <a href="/wiki/Armenian_genocide" title="Armenian genocide">Armenian genocide</a>, the speakers of Western Armenian were mostly murdered or exiled. Those who fled to Eastern Armenia now speak either Eastern Armenian or have a diglossic situation between Western Armenian dialects in informal usage and an Eastern Armenian standard. The only Western Armenian dialect still spoken in Western Armenia is the <a href="/wiki/Homshetsi_dialect" title="Homshetsi dialect">Homshetsi dialect</a>, since the <a href="/wiki/Hemshin_peoples" class="mw-redirect" title="Hemshin peoples">Hemshin peoples</a>, who were Muslim converts, did not fall victim to the Armenian genocide.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2022)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>On 21 February 2009, <a href="/wiki/International_Mother_Language_Day" title="International Mother Language Day">International Mother Language Day</a>, a new edition of the <i>Atlas of the World's Languages in Danger</i> was released by <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a> in which the Western Armenian language in Turkey was defined as a <a href="/wiki/Endangered_language" title="Endangered language">definitely endangered language</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-unesco.org_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-unesco.org-12">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Endangerment_and_controversy">Endangerment and controversy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Western_Armenian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Endangerment and controversy"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>With Western Armenian being declared an endangered language, there has been recent pushback on reviving the language in <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> which is home to the largest concentration of Western Armenians.
</p><p><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shushan_Karapetian&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Shushan Karapetian (page does not exist)">Shushan Karapetian</a>, in her evaluation of both the Eastern and Western dialects of Armenian, concludes that heritage languages, in the face of an English dominant society, rapidly die out within no more than 2 generations, calling America a "linguistic graveyard."<sup id="cite_ref-:0_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-15">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> In US census data, the number of people who speak Western Armenian at home has rapidly declined, down from 25% in 1980 to 16% in 2000.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-15">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Diphthongs">Diphthongs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Western_Armenian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Diphthongs"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Western Armenian has ten environments in which two vowels in the orthography appear next to each other, called diphthongs. By definition, they appear in the same syllable. For those unfamiliar with IPA symbols, <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/<b>j</b>/</span> represents the English "y" sound. The Armenian letter "ե" is often used in combinations such as <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/<b><span title="Western Armenian-language romanization"><span style="font-style: normal" lang="hyw-Latn">ja</span></span></b>/</span> (ya) and <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/<b><span title="Western Armenian-language romanization"><span style="font-style: normal" lang="hyw-Latn">jo</span></span></b>/</span> (yo).  If used at the beginning of a word, "ե" alone is sufficient to represent <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/<b><span title="Western Armenian-language romanization"><span style="font-style: normal" lang="hyw-Latn">jɛ</span></span></b>/</span> (as in yes). The Armenian letter "յ" is used for the glide after vowels. The IPA <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/<b><span title="Western Armenian-language romanization"><span style="font-style: normal" lang="hyw-Latn">ɑj</span></span></b>/</span> (like English long i) and <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/<b><span title="Western Armenian-language romanization"><span style="font-style: normal" lang="hyw-Latn">uj</span></span></b>/</span> diphthongs are common, while <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/<b><span title="Western Armenian-language romanization"><span style="font-style: normal" lang="hyw-Latn">ej</span></span></b>/</span> (English long a), <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/<b><span title="Western Armenian-language romanization"><span style="font-style: normal" lang="hyw-Latn">ij,<span class="wrap"> </span>iə</span></span></b>/</span> (a stretched-out long e), and <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/<b><span title="Western Armenian-language romanization"><span style="font-style: normal" lang="hyw-Latn">oj</span></span></b>/</span> (oy) are rare. The following examples are sometimes across syllable and morpheme boundaries, and gliding is then expected:

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Differences_from_Classical_Armenian">Differences from Classical Armenian</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Western_Armenian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Differences from Classical Armenian"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Differences in phonology between Western Armenian and <a href="/wiki/Classical_Armenian" title="Classical Armenian">Classical Armenian</a> include the distinction of <a href="/wiki/Stop_consonant" class="mw-redirect" title="Stop consonant">stops</a> and <a href="/wiki/Affricate_consonant" class="mw-redirect" title="Affricate consonant">affricates</a>.
</p><p>Firstly, while <a href="/wiki/Classical_Armenian" title="Classical Armenian">Classical Armenian</a> has a three-way distinction of stops and affricates (one voiced and two voiceless: one plain and one <a href="/wiki/Aspirated_consonant" title="Aspirated consonant">aspirated</a>), Western Armenian has kept only a two-way distinction (one voiced and one aspirated). For example, Classical Armenian has three bilabial stops (<span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/b/</span> <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;բ&#x27e9;</span>, <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/p/</span> <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;պ&#x27e9;</span>, and <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/pʰ/</span> <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;փ&#x27e9;</span>), but Western Armenian has only two bilabial stops (<span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/b/</span> <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;պ&#x27e9;</span> and <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/pʰ/</span> <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;բ&#x27e9;</span>/<span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;փ&#x27e9;</span>).
</p><p>Secondly, Western Armenian has both changed the Classical Armenian voiced stops and voiced affricates to aspirated stops and <i>aspirated</i> affricates and replaced the plain stops and affricates with voiced consonants.
</p><p>Specifically, here are the shifts from Classical Armenian to Western Armenian:
</p>
<ol><li>Bilabial stops:
<ol><li>merging of Classical Armenian <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/b/</span> <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;բ&#x27e9;</span> and <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/pʰ/</span> <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;փ&#x27e9;</span> as <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/pʰ/</span></li>
<li>voicing of Classical <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/p/</span> <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;պ&#x27e9;</span> to <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/b/</span></li></ol></li>
<li>Alveolar stops:
<ol><li>merging of Classical Armenian <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/d/</span> <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;դ&#x27e9;</span> and <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/tʰ/</span> <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;թ&#x27e9;</span> as <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/tʰ/</span></li>
<li>voicing of Classical <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/t/</span> <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;տ&#x27e9;</span> to <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/d/</span></li></ol></li>
<li>Velar stops:
<ol><li>merging of Classical Armenian <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ɡ/</span> <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;գ&#x27e9;</span> and <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/kʰ/</span> <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;ք&#x27e9;</span> as <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/kʰ/</span></li>
<li>voicing of Classical <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/k/</span> <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;կ&#x27e9;</span> to <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ɡ/</span></li></ol></li>
<li>Alveolar affricates:
<ol><li>merging of Classical Armenian <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/dz/</span> <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;ձ&#x27e9;</span> and <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/tsʰ/</span> <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;ց&#x27e9;</span> as <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/tsʰ/</span></li>
<li>voicing of Classical <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ts/</span> <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;ծ&#x27e9;</span> to <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/dz/</span></li></ol></li>
<li>Post-alveolar affricates:
<ol><li>merging of Classical Armenian <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/dʒ/</span> <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;ջ&#x27e9;</span> and <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/tʃʰ/</span> <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;չ&#x27e9;</span> as <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/tʃʰ/</span></li>
<li>voicing of Classical <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/tʃ/</span> <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;ճ&#x27e9;</span> to <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/dʒ/</span></li></ol></li></ol>
<p>As a result, a word like <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[dʒuɹ]</span> 'water' (spelled <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;ջուր&#x27e9;</span> in Classical Armenian) is cognate with Western Armenian <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[tʃʰuɹ]</span> (also spelled <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;ջուր&#x27e9;</span>). However, <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[tʰoɹ]</span> 'grandson' and <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[kʰaɹ]</span> 'stone' are pronounced similarly in both Classical and Western Armenian.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Orthography">Orthography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Western_Armenian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Orthography"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Classical_Armenian_orthography" title="Classical Armenian orthography">Classical Armenian orthography</a></div>
<p>Western Armenian uses <a href="/wiki/Classical_Armenian_orthography" title="Classical Armenian orthography">Classical Armenian orthography</a>, also known as traditional <i>Mashtotsian</i> orthography. The <a href="/wiki/Armenian_orthography_reform" title="Armenian orthography reform">Armenian orthography reform</a>, commonly known as the <i>Abeghian</i> orthography, was introduced in the <a href="/wiki/Armenian_Soviet_Socialist_Republic" title="Armenian Soviet Socialist Republic">Armenian Soviet Socialist Republic</a> and is still used by most Eastern Armenian speakers from modern Armenia. However, it has not been adopted by Eastern Armenian speakers of Iran and their diaspora or by speakers of Western Armenian, with the exception of periodical publications published in Romania and Bulgaria while under Communist regimes.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Morphology">Morphology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Western_Armenian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Morphology"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nouns">Nouns</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Western_Armenian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Nouns"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Western Armenian nouns have four <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_case" title="Grammatical case">grammatical cases</a>: <a href="/wiki/Nominative_case" title="Nominative case">nominative</a>-<a href="/wiki/Accusative_case" title="Accusative case">accusative</a> (subject / direct object), <a href="/wiki/Genitive_case" title="Genitive case">genitive</a>-<a href="/wiki/Dative_case" title="Dative case">dative</a> (possession / indirect object), <a href="/wiki/Ablative_case" title="Ablative case">ablative</a> (origin) and <a href="/wiki/Instrumental_case" title="Instrumental case">instrumental</a> (means). Of the six cases, the nominative and accusative are the same, except for personal pronouns, and the genitive and dative are the same, meaning that nouns have four distinct forms for case. Nouns in Armenian also decline for number (singular and plural).
</p><p>Declension in Armenian is based on how the genitive is formed. There are several <a href="/wiki/Declension" title="Declension">declensions</a>, but one is dominant (the genitive in <i>i</i>) while a half-dozen other forms are in gradual decline and are being replaced by the <i>i</i>-form, which has virtually attained the status of a regular form:

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Articles">Articles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Western_Armenian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Articles"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Like English and some other languages, Armenian has definite and indefinite articles. The indefinite article in Western Armenian is <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/mə/</span>, which follows the noun:
</p><p><i>ator mə</i> ('a chair', Nom.sg), <i>atori mə</i> ('of a chair', Gen.sg)
</p><p>The definite article is a suffix attached to the noun, and is one of two forms, either <b>-n</b> (when the final sound is a vowel) or <b>-ə</b> (when the final sound is a consonant). When the word is followed by <b>al</b> (ալ = also, too), the conjunction <b>u</b> (ու), or the present or imperfect conjugated forms of the verb <b>em</b> (to be); however, it will always take <b>-n</b>:
</p>
<dl><dd><i>kirkə</i> ('the book', Nom.sg)</dd>
<dd><i>karin</i> ('the barley' Nom.sg)</dd></dl>
<p>but:
</p>
<dl><dd><i>As kirkn e</i> ('This is the book')</dd>
<dd><i>Parin u charə</i> ('The good and the bad')</dd>
<dd><i>Inkn al</i> ('S/he too')</dd></dl>
<p>The indefinite article becomes <b>mən</b> when it is followed by <b>al</b> (ալ = also, too) or the Present or imperfect conjugated forms of the verb <b>em</b> (to be):
</p>
<dl><dd><i>kirk mə</i> ('a book', Nom.sg)</dd></dl>
<p>but:
</p>
<dl><dd><i>As kirk mən e</i> ('This is a book')</dd>
<dd><i>Kirk mən al</i> ('A book as well')</dd></dl>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Adjectives">Adjectives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Western_Armenian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Adjectives"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Adjectives in Armenian do not decline for case or number, and precede the noun:
</p>
<dl><dd><i>agheg martə</i> ('the good man', Nom.sg)</dd>
<dd><i>agheg martun</i> ('to the good man', Gen.sg)</dd></dl>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Verbs">Verbs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Western_Armenian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Verbs"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Armenian_verbs" title="Armenian verbs">Armenian verbs</a></div>
<p>Verbs in Armenian are based on two basic series of forms, a "present" form and an "imperfect" form. From this, all other tenses and moods are formed with various particles and constructions. There is a third form, the preterite, which in Armenian is a tense in its own right, and takes no other particles or constructions.
</p><p>The "present" tense in Western Armenian is based on three <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_conjugation" title="Grammatical conjugation">conjugations</a> (<b>a, e, i</b>):

<p>The present tense (as we know it in English) is made by adding the particle <b>gə</b> before the "present" form, except the defective verbs <i>em</i> (I am), <i>gam</i> (I exist, I'm there), <i>unim</i> (I have), <i>kidem</i> (I know) and <i>gərnam</i> (I can), while the future is made by adding <b>bidi</b>:
</p>
<dl><dd><i>Yes kirk′ə gə gartam</i> (I am reading the book or I read the book, Pres)</dd>
<dd><i>Yes kirk′ə bidi gartam </i>(I will read the book, Fut).</dd></dl>
<p>For the exceptions: <span title="Western Armenian-language romanization"><span style="font-style: normal" lang="hyw-Latn">bidi əllam, unenam, kidnam, garenam</span></span> (I shall be, have, know, be able).
In vernacular language, the particle "gor" is added after the verb to indicate present progressive tense. The distinction is not made in literary Armenian.
</p>
<dl><dd><span title="Western Armenian-language romanization"><i lang="hyw-Latn">Yes kirk′ə gə gartam gor</i></span> (I am reading the book)<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup></dd></dl>

<p><b>Western Armenian Online Dictionaries</b>
</p>
<ul><li><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.nayiri.com/">Nayiri.com</a> (Library of Armenian dictionaries):
<ul><li><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.nayiri.com/search?l=en&amp;dt=HY_HY">Բառգիրք հայերէն լեզուի</a> by Rev. Antranig Granian (about 18,000 terms; published in 1998 in Beirut). Great dictionary for students.</li>
<li><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.nayiri.com/imagedDictionaryBrowser.jsp?dictionaryId=8">ՀԱՅՈՑ ԼԵԶՈՒԻ ՆՈՐ ԲԱՌԱՐԱՆ</a> published in two volumes in Beirut in 1992 (about 56,000 headwords). Arguably the best Western Armenian dictionary currently available.</li>
<li><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.nayiri.com/imagedDictionaryBrowser.jsp?dictionaryId=6">ՀԱՅԵՐԷՆ ԲԱՑԱՏՐԱԿԱՆ ԲԱՌԱՐԱՆ</a> by <a href="/wiki/Stepan_Malkhasiants" class="mw-redirect" title="Stepan Malkhasiants">Stepan Malkhasiants</a> (about 130,000 entries). One of the definitive Armenian dictionaries. (Definitions are in Eastern Armenian, but include Western Armenian meanings of headwords.)</li>
<li><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.nayiri.com/imagedDictionaryBrowser.jsp?dictionaryId=7">ՀԱՅԵՐԷՆ ԱՐՄԱՏԱԿԱՆ ԲԱՌԱՐԱՆ</a> by <a href="/wiki/Hrachia_Acharian" title="Hrachia Acharian">Hrachia Acharian</a> (5,062 word roots). The definitive study of the history and origins of word roots in Armenian. Also includes explanations of each word root as it is used today. (Explanations are in Eastern Armenian, but root words span the entire Armenian language, including Western Armenian.)</li>
<li><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.nayiri.com/search?l=en&amp;dt=HY_EN">Armenian-English dictionary</a> (about 70,000 entries).</li>
<li><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.nayiri.com/search?l=en&amp;dt=EN_HY">English-Armenian dictionary</a> (about 96,000 entries).</li>
<li><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.nayiri.com/search?l=en&amp;dt=HY_FR">Armenian-French dictionary</a> (about 18,000 entries).</li>
<li><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.nayiri.com/search?l=en&amp;dt=FR_HY">French-Armenian dictionary</a> (about 20,000 entries).</li></ul></li></ul>
