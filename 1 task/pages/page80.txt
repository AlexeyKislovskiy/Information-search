</p><p>Filipino, like other Austronesian languages, commonly uses <a href="/wiki/Verb%E2%80%93subject%E2%80%93object" class="mw-redirect" title="Verb–subject–object">verb-subject-object</a> order, but can also use <a href="/wiki/Subject-verb-object" class="mw-redirect" title="Subject-verb-object">subject-verb-object</a> order. Filipino follows the <a href="/wiki/Symmetrical_voice" title="Symmetrical voice">trigger system</a> of <a href="/wiki/Morphosyntactic_alignment" title="Morphosyntactic alignment">morphosyntactic alignment</a> that is also common among Austronesian languages. It has <a href="/wiki/Head-directionality_parameter" title="Head-directionality parameter">head-initial</a> directionality. It is an <a href="/wiki/Agglutinative_language" title="Agglutinative language">agglutinative language</a> but can also display <a href="/wiki/Inflection" title="Inflection">inflection</a>. It is not a <a href="/wiki/Tone_(linguistics)" title="Tone (linguistics)">tonal language</a> and can be considered a <a href="/wiki/Pitch-accent_language" title="Pitch-accent language">pitch-accent language</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Isochrony" title="Isochrony">syllable-timed language.</a> It has nine basic <a href="/wiki/Part_of_speech" title="Part of speech">parts of speech</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Filipino_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Philippines is a multilingual state with 175 living languages originating and spoken by various ethno-linguistic groups. Many of these languages descend from a common <a href="/wiki/Malayo-Polynesian_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Malayo-Polynesian language">Malayo-Polynesian language</a> due to the Austronesian migration from Taiwan. The common Malayo-Polynesian language split into different languages, and usually through the Malay language, the lingua franca of maritime Southeast Asia, these were able to adopt terms that ultimately originate from other languages such as <a href="/wiki/Japanese_language" title="Japanese language">Japanese</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hokkien" title="Hokkien">Hokkien</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">Sanskrit</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tamil_language" title="Tamil language">Tamil</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Arabic" title="Arabic">Arabic</a>. The Malay language was generally used by the ruling classes and the merchants from the states and various cultures in the Philippine archipelago for international communication as part of maritime Southeast Asia. In fact, Filipinos first interacted with the Spaniards using the Malay language. In addition to this, 16th-century chroniclers of the time noted that the kings and lords in the islands usually spoke around five languages.
</p><p>Spanish intrusion into the Philippine islands started in 1565 with the fall of Cebu. The eventual capital established by Spain for its settlement in the Philippines was <a href="/wiki/Manila" title="Manila">Manila</a>, situated in a Tagalog-speaking region, after the capture of Manila from the Muslim Kingdom of Luzon ruled by <a href="/wiki/Raja_Matanda" class="mw-redirect" title="Raja Matanda">Raja Matanda</a> with the heir apparent <a href="/wiki/Raja_Sulayman" class="mw-redirect" title="Raja Sulayman">Raja Sulayman</a> and the Hindu-Buddhist <a href="/wiki/Tondo_(historical_polity)" title="Tondo (historical polity)">Kingdom of Tondo</a> ruled by <a href="/wiki/Lakandula" title="Lakandula">Lakan Dula</a>. After its fall to the Spaniards, Manila was made the capital of the Spanish settlement in Asia due to the city's commercial wealth and influence, its strategic location, and Spanish fears of raids from the Portuguese and the Dutch.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The first dictionary of Tagalog, published as the <span title="Filipino-language text"><i lang="fil">Vocabulario de la lengua tagala</i></span>, was written by the <a href="/wiki/Franciscan" class="mw-redirect" title="Franciscan">Franciscan</a> Pedro de San Buenaventura,<sup id="cite_ref-vocabularioAmbeth_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vocabularioAmbeth-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> and published in 1613 by the "Father of Filipino Printing" <a href="/wiki/Tomas_Pinpin" class="mw-redirect" title="Tomas Pinpin">Tomás Pinpin</a> in <a href="/wiki/Pila,_Laguna" title="Pila, Laguna">Pila</a>, <a href="/wiki/Laguna_(province)" title="Laguna (province)">Laguna</a>. A latter book of the same name was written by <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Bohemia" title="Kingdom of Bohemia">Czech</a> <a href="/wiki/Jesuit" class="mw-redirect" title="Jesuit">Jesuit</a> missionary <a href="/wiki/Pablo_Clain" title="Pablo Clain">Paul Klein</a> (known locally as Pablo Clain) at the beginning of the 18th century. Klein spoke Tagalog and used it actively in several of his books. He wrote a dictionary, which he later passed to Francisco Jansens and José Hernández.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Further compilation of his substantial work was prepared by Juan de Noceda and Pedro de Sanlúcar and published as <i><span title="Filipino-language text"><i lang="fil"><a href="/wiki/Vocabulario_de_la_lengua_tagala" title="Vocabulario de la lengua tagala">Vocabulario de la lengua tagala</a></i></span></i> in Manila in 1754 and then repeatedly<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> re-edited, with the latest edition being published in 2013 in Manila.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a> served in an official capacity as language of the government during the Spanish colonial period. During the American colonial period, <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a> became an additional official language of the Philippines alongside Spanish; however, the number of speakers of Spanish steadily decreased.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Designation_as_the_national_language">Designation as the national language</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Filipino_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Designation as the national language"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>While Spanish and English were considered "official languages" during the American colonial period, there existed no "national language" initially. Article XIII, section 3 of the <a href="/wiki/1935_Constitution_of_the_Philippines" class="mw-redirect" title="1935 Constitution of the Philippines">1935 constitution</a> establishing the <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_the_Philippines" title="Commonwealth of the Philippines">Commonwealth of the Philippines</a> provided that:
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r996844942">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>The National Assembly shall take steps toward the development and adoption of a common national language based on one of the existing native languages. Until otherwise provided by law, English and Spanish shall continue as official languages.</p></blockquote>
<p>On November 13, 1936, the first <a href="/wiki/National_Assembly_of_the_Philippines" title="National Assembly of the Philippines">National Assembly of the Philippine Commonwealth</a> approved Commonwealth Act No. 184; creating the Institute of National Language (later the <a href="/wiki/Surian_ng_Wikang_Pambansa" class="mw-redirect" title="Surian ng Wikang Pambansa">Surián ng Wikang Pambansâ</a> or SWP) and tasking it with making a study and survey of each existing native language, hoping to choose which was to be the base for a standardized national language.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Later, President <a href="/wiki/Manuel_L._Quezon" title="Manuel L. Quezon">Manuel L. Quezon</a> later appointed representatives for each major regional language to form the NLI. Led by <a href="/wiki/Jaime_C._de_Veyra" title="Jaime C. de Veyra">Jaime C. De Veyra</a>, who sat as the chair of the Institute and as the representative of <a href="/wiki/Waray_language" title="Waray language">Samar-Leyte-Visayans</a>, the Institute's members were composed of Santiago A. Fonacier (representing the <a href="/wiki/Ilocano_language" title="Ilocano language">Ilokano-speaking regions</a>), <a href="/wiki/Filemon_Sotto" title="Filemon Sotto">Filemon Sotto</a> (the <a href="/wiki/Cebuano_language" title="Cebuano language">Cebu-Visayans</a>), Casimiro Perfecto (the <a href="/wiki/Bikol_languages" title="Bikol languages">Bikolanos</a>), Felix S. Sales Rodriguez (the <a href="/wiki/Hiligaynon_language" title="Hiligaynon language">Panay-Visayans</a>), Hadji Butu (the languages of <a href="/wiki/Moro_people" title="Moro people">Muslim Filipinos</a>), and Cecilio Lopez (the <a href="/wiki/Tagalog_language" title="Tagalog language">Tagalogs</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-aspillera_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aspillera-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Institute of National Language adopted a resolution on November 9, 1937 recommending Tagalog to be basis of the national language. On December 30, President Quezon issued Executive Order No.&#160;134, s. 1937, approving the adoption of Tagalog as the language of the Philippines, and proclaimed the national language of the Philippines so based on the Tagalog language. Quezon himself was born and raised in <a href="/wiki/Baler,_Aurora" title="Baler, Aurora">Baler, Aurora</a>, which is a native Tagalog-speaking area. The order stated that it would take effect two years from its promulgation.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> On December 31 of the same year, Quezon proclaimed Tagalog as the basis of the <i><a href="/wiki/National_language" title="National language">Wikang Pambansâ</a></i> (National Language) giving the following factors:<sup id="cite_ref-aspillera_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aspillera-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ol><li>Tagalog is widely spoken and is the most understood language in all the <a href="/wiki/Regions_of_the_Philippines" title="Regions of the Philippines">Philippine Regions</a>.</li>
<li>It is not divided into smaller daughter languages, as <a href="/wiki/Visayan_languages" class="mw-redirect" title="Visayan languages">Visayan</a> or <a href="/wiki/Bikol_languages" title="Bikol languages">Bikol</a> are.</li>
<li>Its literary tradition is the richest of all Philippine languages, the most developed and extensive (mirroring that of the <a href="/wiki/Tuscan_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Tuscan language">Tuscan language</a> <i>vis-à-vis</i> <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">Italian</a>). From at least before 1935, more books were written in Tagalog than in any other Philippine language.</li>
<li>Tagalog has always been the language of <a href="/wiki/Manila" title="Manila">Manila</a>, the political centre of the Philippines in much of its history as a multiethnic country and a considerable <a href="/wiki/Concentration_of_wealth" class="mw-redirect" title="Concentration of wealth">economic centre</a> of the Philippine islands since time immemorial.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Katipunan" title="Katipunan">Katipunan</a> generally used the Tagalog language for its operations, and the <a href="/wiki/Philippine_Revolution" title="Philippine Revolution">Philippine Revolution</a> and the <a href="/wiki/First_Philippine_Republic" title="First Philippine Republic">First Philippine Republic</a> operationally used Spanish afterwards, but many of the leaders of the revolution spoke Tagalog, more so among ethnic groups from central to southern Luzon including some adjacent islands. Tagalog also became a choice for some non-Tagalog Filipino revolutionary leaders and nationalists in some of their publications, especially if they were to publish in Manila. The Katipunan extended the meaning of the term Tagalog to all people native to the Philippine islands, including Cebuanos, Ilocanos, Kapampangans, etc, and extended the term <i>Katagalugan</i> to the whole Philippine islands not just native Tagalog-speaking areas, building a <a href="/wiki/Tagalog_Republic" title="Tagalog Republic">Tagalog Republic</a>, the reason being a unified opposition against Spanish hegemony.</li></ol>
<p>On June 7, 1940, the Philippine National Assembly passed Commonwealth Act No. 570 declaring that the Filipino national language would be considered an official language effective July 4, 1946<sup id="cite_ref-Presidential_Proclamations_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Presidential_Proclamations-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> (coinciding with the country's expected date of independence from the United States). That same year, the <i>Balarílà ng Wikang Pambansâ</i> (English: <span lang="en">Grammar of the National Language</span>) of grammarian <a href="/wiki/Lope_K._Santos" title="Lope K. Santos">Lope K. Santos</a> introduced the 20-letter <a href="/wiki/Abakada_alphabet" title="Abakada alphabet">Abakada alphabet</a> which became the standard of the national language.<sup id="cite_ref-ebolusyon_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ebolusyon-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> The alphabet was officially adopted by the Institute for the Tagalog-Based National Language.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Further_history">Further history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Filipino_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Further history"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1959, the language became known as <i>Pilipino</i> in an effort to disassociate it from the <a href="/wiki/Tagalog_people" title="Tagalog people">Tagalog ethnic group</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Gonzalez98_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gonzalez98-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> The changing of the name did not, however, result in universal acceptance among non-<a href="/wiki/Tagalog_people" title="Tagalog people">Tagalogs</a>, especially <a href="/wiki/Cebuano_people" title="Cebuano people">Cebuanos</a> who had previously not accepted the 1937 selection.<sup id="cite_ref-Gonzalez_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gonzalez-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The 1960s saw the rise of the purist movement where new words were being coined to replace loanwords. This era of "purism" by the SWP sparked criticisms by a number of persons. Two counter-movements emerged during this period of "purism": one campaigning against Tagalog and the other campaigning for more inclusiveness in the national language. In 1963, <a href="/wiki/Negros_Occidental" title="Negros Occidental">Negros Occidental</a> congressman Innocencio V. Ferrer took a case reaching the Supreme Court questioning the constitutionality of the choice of Tagalog as the basis of the national language (a case ruled in favor of the national language in 1970). Accusing the national language as simply being Tagalog and lacking any substantial input from other Philippine languages, Congressman Geruncio Lacuesta eventually led a "Modernizing the Language Approach Movement" (MOLAM). Lacuesta hosted a number of "anti-purist" conferences and promoted a "Manila Lingua Franca" which would be more inclusive of loanwords of both foreign and local languages. Lacuesta managed to get nine congressmen to propose a bill aiming to abolish the SWP with an <i>Akademia ng Wikang Filipino</i>, to replace the <i>balarila</i> with a <i>Gramatica ng Wikang Filipino</i>, to replace the 20-letter Abakada with a 32-letter alphabet, and to prohibit the creation of neologisms and the respelling of loanwords. This movement quietened down following the death of Lacuesta.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Gonzalez_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gonzalez-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The national language issue was revived once more during the <a href="/wiki/1970_Philippine_Constitutional_Convention_election" title="1970 Philippine Constitutional Convention election">1971 Constitutional Convention</a>. While there was a sizable number of delegates in favor of retaining the Tagalog-based national language, majority of the delegates who were non-Tagalogs were even in favor of scrapping the idea of a "national language" altogether.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> A compromise was reached and the wording on the 1973 constitution made no mention of dropping the national language Pilipino or made any mention of Tagalog. Instead, the <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_the_Philippines#1973_constitution" title="Constitution of the Philippines">1973 Constitution</a>, in both its original form and as amended in 1976, designated English and Pilipino as official languages and provided for development and formal adoption of a common national language, termed <i>Filipino</i>, to replace Pilipino. Neither the original nor the amended version specified either Tagalog or Pilipino as the basis for Filipino; Instead, tasking the <a href="/wiki/National_Assembly_of_the_Philippines" title="National Assembly of the Philippines">National Assembly</a> to:<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r996844942"><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>take steps toward the development and formal adoption of a common national language to be known as Filipino.</p></blockquote>
<p>In 1987, a <a href="/wiki/1987_Constitution_of_the_Philippines" class="mw-redirect" title="1987 Constitution of the Philippines">new constitution</a> designated Filipino as the national language and, along with English, as an official language.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> That constitution included several provisions related to the Filipino language.<sup id="cite_ref-1987_constitution_art_14_sec_6-7_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1987_constitution_art_14_sec_6-7-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Article XIV, Section 6, omits any mention of Tagalog as the basis for Filipino, and states that:<sup id="cite_ref-1987_constitution_art_14_sec_6-7_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1987_constitution_art_14_sec_6-7-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r996844942"><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>as Filipino evolves, it shall be further developed and enriched on the basis of existing Philippine and other languages.</p></blockquote>
<p>And also states in the article:
</p>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r996844942"><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Subject to provisions of law and as the Congress may deem appropriate, the Government shall take steps to initiate and sustain the use of Filipino as a medium of official communication and as language of instruction in the educational system.</p></blockquote>
<p>and:
</p>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r996844942"><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>The regional languages are the auxiliary official languages in the regions and shall serve as auxiliary media of instruction therein.</p></blockquote>
<p>Section 17(d) of Executive Order 117 of January 30, 1987 renamed the <i>Institute of National Language</i> as <i>Institute of Philippine Languages</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>  Republic Act No. 7104, approved on August 14, 1991, created the <i>Komisyon sa Wikang Filipino</i> (Commission on the Filipino Language, or KWF), superseding the Institute of Philippine Languages. The KWF reports directly to the President and was tasked to undertake, coordinate and promote researches for the development, propagation and preservation of Filipino and other Philippine languages.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> On May 13, 1992, the commission issued Resolution 92-1, specifying that Filipino is the
</p>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r996844942"><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>indigenous written and spoken language of <a href="/wiki/Metro_Manila" title="Metro Manila">Metro Manila</a> and <a href="/wiki/List_of_cities_in_the_Philippines" title="List of cities in the Philippines">other urban centers</a> in the Philippines used as the <a href="/wiki/Lingua_franca" title="Lingua franca">language of communication</a> of <a href="/wiki/Ethnic_groups_in_the_Philippines" title="Ethnic groups in the Philippines">ethnic groups</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Res921_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Res921-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<p>However, as with the 1973 and 1987 Constitutions, 92-1 went neither so far as to categorically identify, nor so far as to dis-identify this language as Tagalog. Definite, absolute, and unambiguous interpretation of 92–1 is the prerogative of the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_the_Philippines" title="Supreme Court of the Philippines">Supreme Court</a> in the absence of directives from the KWF, otherwise the sole legal arbiter of the Filipino language.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:No_original_research" title="Wikipedia:No original research"><span title="The material near this tag possibly contains original research. (February 2017)">original research?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Filipino was presented and registered with the <a href="/wiki/International_Organization_for_Standardization" title="International Organization for Standardization">International Organization for Standardization</a> (ISO), by Ateneo de Manila University student Martin Gomez, and was added to the ISO registry of languages on September 21, 2004, with it receiving the ISO <a href="/wiki/List_of_ISO_639-2_codes" title="List of ISO 639-2 codes">639-2 code</a> <i>fil</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-ISO_639-2_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ISO_639-2-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On August 22, 2007, it was reported that three <a href="/wiki/Malolos_City" class="mw-redirect" title="Malolos City">Malolos City</a> regional trial courts in <a href="/wiki/Bulacan" title="Bulacan">Bulacan</a> decided to use Filipino, instead of <a href="/wiki/Philippine_English" title="Philippine English">English</a>, in order to promote the national language. Twelve <a href="/wiki/Stenographers" class="mw-redirect" title="Stenographers">stenographers</a> from Branches 6, 80 and 81, as model courts, had undergone training at Marcelo H. del Pilar College of Law of <a href="/wiki/Bulacan_State_University" title="Bulacan State University">Bulacan State University</a> following a directive from the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_the_Philippines" title="Supreme Court of the Philippines">Supreme Court of the Philippines</a>. De la Rama said it was the dream of Chief Justice <a href="/wiki/Reynato_Puno" title="Reynato Puno">Reynato Puno</a> to implement the program in other areas such as <a href="/wiki/Laguna_(province)" title="Laguna (province)">Laguna</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cavite" title="Cavite">Cavite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quezon" title="Quezon">Quezon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aurora_(province)" title="Aurora (province)">Aurora</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nueva_Ecija" title="Nueva Ecija">Nueva Ecija</a>, <a href="/wiki/Batangas" title="Batangas">Batangas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rizal_(province)" title="Rizal (province)">Rizal</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Metro_Manila" title="Metro Manila">Metro Manila</a>, all of which mentioned are natively Tagalog-speaking.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Commemoration">Commemoration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Filipino_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Commemoration"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Since 1997, a month-long celebration of the national language occurs during August, known in Filipino as <i><a href="/wiki/Buwan_ng_Wika" title="Buwan ng Wika">Buwan ng Wika</a></i> (Language Month). Previously, this lasted only a week and was known as <i>Linggo ng Wika</i> (Language Week). The celebration coincides with the month of birth of President Manuel L. Quezon, regarded as the "Ama ng Wikang Pambansa" (Father of the national language).
</p><p>In 1946, Proclamation No. 35 of March 26 provided for a week-long celebration of the national language.<sup id="cite_ref-Presidential_Proclamations_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Presidential_Proclamations-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> this celebration would last from March 27 until April 2 each year, the last day coinciding with birthday of the Filipino writer <a href="/wiki/Francisco_Baltazar" class="mw-redirect" title="Francisco Baltazar">Francisco Baltazar</a>, author of the Tagalog epic <i><a href="/wiki/Florante_at_Laura" title="Florante at Laura">Florante at Laura</a></i>.
</p><p>In 1954, Proclamation No. 12 of March 26 provided that the week of celebration would be from March 29 to April 4 every year.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> This proclamation was amended the following year by President <a href="/wiki/Ramon_Magsaysay" title="Ramon Magsaysay">Ramon Magsaysay</a> by Proclamation No. 186 of September 23, moving the dates of celebration to August 13–19, every year.<sup id="cite_ref-temp1_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-temp1-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> Now coinciding with the birthday of President Manuel L. Quezon. The reason for the move being given that the original celebration was a period "outside of the school year, thereby precluding the participation of schools in its celebration".<sup id="cite_ref-temp1_31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-temp1-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1988, President <a href="/wiki/Corazon_Aquino" title="Corazon Aquino">Corazon Aquino</a> signed Proclamation No. 19, reaffirming the celebration every August 13 to 19. In 1997, the celebration was extended from a week to a month by  Proclamation 1041 of July 15 signed by President <a href="/wiki/Fidel_V._Ramos" title="Fidel V. Ramos">Fidel V. Ramos</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Comparison_of_Filipino_and_Tagalog">Comparison of Filipino and Tagalog</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Filipino_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Comparison of Filipino and Tagalog"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>While the official view (shared by the government, the <span title="Filipino-language text"><span lang="fil" style="font-style: normal;"><a href="/wiki/Komisyon_sa_Wikang_Filipino" class="mw-redirect" title="Komisyon sa Wikang Filipino">Komisyon sa Wikang Filipino</a></span></span>, and a number of educators) is that Filipino and <a href="/wiki/Tagalog_language" title="Tagalog language">Tagalog</a> are considered separate languages, in practical terms, <i>Filipino</i> may be considered the official name of Tagalog, or even a synonym of it.<sup id="cite_ref-ELL_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ELL-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> Today's Filipino language is best described as "Tagalog-based".<sup id="cite_ref-morrow_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-morrow-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> The language is usually called <i>Tagalog</i> within the Philippines and among Filipinos to differentiate it from other Philippine languages, but it has also come to be known as <i>Filipino</i> to differentiate it from the languages of other countries; the former implies a regional origin, the latter national. This is similar to the comparison between <a href="/wiki/Castilian_Spanish" title="Castilian Spanish">Castilian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Mandarin_Chinese" title="Mandarin Chinese">Mandarin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>.
</p><p>Political designations aside, Tagalog and Filipino are linguistically the same; sharing, among other things, the same grammatical structure. On May 23, 2007, Ricardo Maria Nolasco, KWF chair and a linguistics expert, acknowledged in a keynote speech during the NAKEM Conference at the <a href="/wiki/Mariano_Marcos_State_University" title="Mariano Marcos State University">Mariano Marcos State University</a> in Batac, Ilocos Norte, that Filipino was simply Tagalog in syntax and grammar, with as yet no grammatical element or lexicon coming from <a href="/wiki/Ilokano_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Ilokano language">Ilokano</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cebuano_language" title="Cebuano language">Cebuano</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hiligaynon_language" title="Hiligaynon language">Hiligaynon</a>, or any of the other <a href="/wiki/Philippine_languages" title="Philippine languages">Philippine languages</a>. He said further that this is contrary to the intention of Republic Act No. 7104, which requires that the <a href="/wiki/National_language" title="National language">national language</a> be developed and enriched by the lexicon of the country's other languages, something toward which the commission was working.<sup id="cite_ref-Nolasco07_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nolasco07-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> On August 24, 2007, Nolasco elaborated further on the relationship between Tagalog and Filipino in a separate article, as follows:
</p>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r996844942"><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Are "Tagalog," "Pilipino" and "Filipino" different languages? No, they are mutually intelligible varieties, and therefore belong to one language. According to the KWF, Filipino is that speech variety spoken in Metro Manila and other urban centers where different ethnic groups meet. It is the most prestigious variety of Tagalog and the language used by the national mass media.
The other yardstick for distinguishing a <a href="/wiki/Language" title="Language">language</a> from a <a href="/wiki/Dialect" title="Dialect">dialect</a> is: different grammar, different language. "Filipino", "Pilipino" and "Tagalog" share identical grammar. They have the same determiners (ang, ng and sa); the same personal pronouns (siya, ako, niya, kanila, etc.); the same demonstrative pronouns (ito, iyan, doon, etc.); the same linkers (na, at and ay); the same particles (na and pa); and the same verbal affixes -in, -an, i- and -um-. In short, same grammar, same language.<sup id="cite_ref-Filipino-Tagalog-Not-So-Simple_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Filipino-Tagalog-Not-So-Simple-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<p>In connection with the use of Filipino, or specifically the promotion of the national language, the related term <i>Tagalista</i> is frequently used. While the word <i>Tagalista</i> literally means "one who specializes in Tagalog language or culture" or a "Tagalog specialist", in the context of the debates on the national language and "<a href="/wiki/Imperial_Manila" title="Imperial Manila">Imperial Manila</a>", the word <i>Tagalista</i> is used as a reference to "people who promote or would promote the primacy of Tagalog at the expense of [the] other [Philippine] indigenous tongues".<sup id="cite_ref-Tagalista_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tagalista-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>This is a translation of Article 1 of the <a href="/wiki/Universal_Declaration_of_Human_Rights" title="Universal Declaration of Human Rights">Universal Declaration of Human Rights</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> Usually, the diacritics are not written, and the syntax and grammar are based on that of <a href="/wiki/Tagalog_language" title="Tagalog language">Tagalog</a>.

</td>
<td><b>Pangkalahatáng Pagpapahayág ng Karapatáng Pantao</b>

<td><i>Now, therefore,</i>
<p><i><b>the General Assembly proclaims</b></i>
</p><p><i>this <b>UNIVERSAL DECLARATION OF HUMAN RIGHTS</b> as a common standard of achievement for all peoples and all nations, to the end that every individual and every organ of society, keeping this Declaration constantly in mind, shall strive by teaching and education to promote respect for these rights and freedoms and by progressive measures, national and international, to secure their universal and effective recognition and observance, both among the peoples of Member States themselves and among the peoples of territories under their jurisdiction.</i>
</p>

</p>

</td>
<td><b>Unang Artikulo</b>
