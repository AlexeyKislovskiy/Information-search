<p>The <b>Kru languages</b> are spoken by the <a href="/wiki/Kru_people" title="Kru people">Kru people</a> from the southeast of <a href="/wiki/Liberia" title="Liberia">Liberia</a> to the west of <a href="/wiki/Ivory_Coast" title="Ivory Coast">Ivory Coast</a>.
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">Classification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kru_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Classification"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to Güldemann (2018), Kru lacks sufficient lexical resemblances and noun class resemblances to conclude a relationship with Niger-Congo. <a href="/wiki/Glottolog" title="Glottolog">Glottolog</a> considers Kru an independent language family.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kru_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Etymology"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The term "Kru" is of unknown origin. According to Westermann (1952) it was used by Europeans to denote a number of tribes speaking related dialects. Marchese (1989) notes the fact that many of these peoples were recruited as "crew" by European seafarers; "the homonymy with crew is obvious, and is at least one source of the confusion among Europeans that there was a Kru/crew tribe".<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> 
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kru_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Andrew Dalby noted the historical importance of the Kru languages for their position at the crossroads of African-European interaction. He wrote that "Kru and associated languages were among the first to be encountered by European voyagers on what was then known as the <a href="/wiki/Pepper_Coast" title="Pepper Coast">Pepper Coast</a>, a centre of the production and export of <a href="/wiki/Guinea_pepper" title="Guinea pepper">Guinea</a> and <a href="/wiki/Melegueta_pepper" class="mw-redirect" title="Melegueta pepper">melegueta pepper</a>; a once staple African seaborne trade".<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The Kru languages are known for some of the most complex <a href="/wiki/Tone_(linguistics)" title="Tone (linguistics)">tone</a> systems in Africa, rivaled perhaps only by the <a href="/wiki/Omotic_languages" title="Omotic languages">Omotic languages</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_status">Current status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kru_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Current status"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Recent documentation has noted "Kru societies can now be found along the coast of <a href="/wiki/Monrovia" title="Monrovia">Monrovia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Liberia" title="Liberia">Liberia</a> to <a href="/wiki/Bandama_River" title="Bandama River">Bandama River</a> in <a href="/wiki/C%C3%B4te_d%27Ivoire" class="mw-redirect" title="Côte d&#39;Ivoire">Côte d'Ivoire</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-EMW_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EMW-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> "Villages maintain their ties based on presumed common descent, reinforced by ceremonial exchanges and gifts".<sup id="cite_ref-EMW_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EMW-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>  The Kru people and their languages, although now many speak English (in Liberia) or French (in Côte d'Ivoire) as a second language, are said to be "dominant in the southwest region where the forest zone reaches the coastal lagoons".<sup id="cite_ref-EMW_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EMW-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>  The Kru people rely on the forest for farming, supplemented by hunting for their livelihood.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Subgroups_and_associated_languages">Subgroups and associated languages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kru_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Subgroups and associated languages"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Kru languages include many subgroups such as <a href="/wiki/Kuwaa_language" title="Kuwaa language">Kuwaa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grebo_language" title="Grebo language">Grebo</a>, Bassa, Belle, Belleh, Kwaa and many others. According to Breitbonde, categorization of communities based on cultural distinctiveness, historical or ethnic identity, and socio-political autonomy "may have brought about the large number of distinct Kru dialects; "Although the natives were in many respects similar in type and tribe, every village was an independent state; there was also very little intercommunication".<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Breitbonde notes the Kru people were categorized based on their cultural distinctiveness, separate historical or ethnic identities, and social and political autonomy. This is the possible reason for so many subgroups of the Kru language. As noted by Fisiak, there is very little documentation on the Kru and associated languages.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Marchese's (1989) classification of Kru languages is as follows.<sup id="cite_ref-Marchese1989_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marchese1989-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Many of these languages are <a href="/wiki/Dialect_cluster" class="mw-redirect" title="Dialect cluster">dialect clusters</a> and are sometimes considered more than a single language.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Grammar">Grammar</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kru_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Grammar"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reconstruction">Reconstruction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kru_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Reconstruction"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1066479718"><table class="infobox" style="width:22em;"><tbody><tr><th colspan="2" class="infobox-above" style="background-color:  #ffddaa;">Proto-Kru</th></tr><tr><th scope="row" class="infobox-label">Reconstruction&#160;of</th><td class="infobox-data">Kru languages</td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>According to Marchese Zogbo (2012), Proto-Kru had:<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>phonemic nasalized vowels</li>
<li>four level tones</li>
<li>*CVCV-(C)V and probably *CVV syllable structure. *CCV syllables, and possibly also *CVV syllables, are derived from *CVCV roots.</li>
<li>SVO word order, but with much OV typology</li>
<li>suffixing morphology</li>
<li>perfective and imperfective aspects</li></ul>
