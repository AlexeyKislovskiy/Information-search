<p><b>Laal</b> is an endangered <a href="/wiki/Language_isolate" title="Language isolate">language isolate</a> spoken by 749 people (as of 2000<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Laal_language&amp;action=edit">&#91;update&#93;</a></sup>) in three villages in the <a href="/wiki/Moyen-Chari_Prefecture" class="mw-redirect" title="Moyen-Chari Prefecture">Moyen-Chari</a> prefecture of <a href="/wiki/Chad" title="Chad">Chad</a> on opposite banks of the <a href="/wiki/Chari_River" title="Chari River">Chari River</a>, 
called <a href="/wiki/Gori,_Chad" title="Gori, Chad">Gori</a> (<i>lá</i>), Damtar (<i>ɓual</i>), and Mailao. It represents an isolated survival of an earlier language group of <a href="/wiki/Central_Africa" title="Central Africa">Central Africa</a>. It is unwritten except in <a href="/wiki/Transcription_(linguistics)" title="Transcription (linguistics)">transcription</a> by linguists. According to former <a href="/wiki/Summer_Institute_of_Linguistics" class="mw-redirect" title="Summer Institute of Linguistics">Summer Institute of Linguistics</a>-Chad member David Faris, it is in danger of extinction, with most people under 25 shifting to the locally more widespread <a href="/wiki/Bagirmi_language" title="Bagirmi language">Bagirmi</a>.
</p><p>This language first came to the attention of academic linguists in 1977 through <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pascal_Boyeldieu&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Pascal Boyeldieu (page does not exist)">Pascal Boyeldieu</a>'s fieldwork<sup id="cite_ref-:0_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> in 1975 and 1978. His fieldwork was based, for the most part, on a single speaker, Djouam Kadi of Damtar.
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Speakers_and_status">Speakers and status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Laal_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Speakers and status"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The language's speakers are mainly <a href="/wiki/River" title="River">river</a> <a href="/wiki/Fishing" title="Fishing">fishermen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">farmers</a>, who also sell <a href="/wiki/Salt" title="Salt">salt</a> extracted from the ashes of <a href="/wiki/Doum_palm" class="mw-redirect" title="Doum palm">doum palms</a> and <i><a href="/wiki/Vossia_cuspidata" title="Vossia cuspidata">Vossia cuspidata</a></i>. Like their neighbours, the <a href="/wiki/Niellim_language" title="Niellim language">Niellim</a>, they were formerly cattle herders but lost their herds around the turn of the 19th century. They are mainly <a href="/wiki/Muslim" class="mw-redirect" title="Muslim">Muslims</a>, but until the latter half of the 20th century, they followed the traditional <i><a href="/wiki/Yondo" class="mw-redirect" title="Yondo">Yondo</a></i> religion of the <a href="/wiki/Niellim" class="mw-redirect" title="Niellim">Niellim</a>.  The area is fairly undeveloped; while there are <a href="/wiki/Qur%27anic_school" class="mw-redirect" title="Qur&#39;anic school">Qur'anic schools</a> in <a href="/wiki/Gori,_Chad" title="Gori, Chad">Gori</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Damtar&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Damtar (page does not exist)">Damtar</a>, the nearest government school is 7&#160;km away, and there is no medical dispensary in the region (as of 1995<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Laal_language&amp;action=edit">&#91;update&#93;</a></sup>).
</p><p>The village of <a href="/w/index.php?title=Damtar&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Damtar (page does not exist)">Damtar</a> formerly had a distinct dialect, called Laabe (<i>la:bé</i>), with two or three speakers remaining in 1977; it was replaced by the dialect of Gori after two Gori families fled there at the end of the 19th century to escape a <a href="/wiki/War" title="War">war</a>. No other dialects of Laal are known.
</p><p>Under Chadian law, Laal, like all languages of Chad other than <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a> and <a href="/wiki/Arabic_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>, is regarded as a <a href="/wiki/National_language" title="National language">national language</a>. Although the 1996 Constitution stipulates that "the law shall fix the conditions of promotion and development of national languages", national languages are not used for education, for official purposes, or usually for written media, but some of the larger ones (but not Laal) are used on the radio.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">Classification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Laal_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Classification"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Laal remains <a href="/wiki/Unclassified_language" title="Unclassified language">unclassified</a>, although extensive <a href="/wiki/Adamawa_languages" title="Adamawa languages">Adamawa</a> (specifically <a href="/wiki/Bua_languages" title="Bua languages">Bua</a>) and to a lesser extent <a href="/wiki/Chadic_languages" title="Chadic languages">Chadic</a> influence is found. It is sometimes grouped with one of those two <a href="/wiki/Language_family" title="Language family">language families</a>, and sometimes seen as a <a href="/wiki/Language_isolate" title="Language isolate">language isolate</a>.  Boyeldieu (1982) summarizes his view as "Its classification remains problematic; while it shows certain lexical, and no doubt morphological, traits with the Bua languages (Adamawa-13, <a href="/wiki/Niger%E2%80%93Congo_languages" title="Niger–Congo languages">Niger–Congo family</a> of <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Greenberg" title="Joseph Greenberg">Joseph H. Greenberg</a>), it differs from them radically in many ways of which some, <i>a priori</i>, make one think of geographically nearby Chadic languages."  <a href="/wiki/Roger_Blench" title="Roger Blench">Roger Blench</a> (2003), similarly, considers that "its vocabulary and morphology seem to be partly drawn from Chadic (i.e. <a href="/wiki/Afroasiatic_languages" title="Afroasiatic languages">Afro-Asiatic</a>), partly from <a href="/wiki/Adamawa_languages" title="Adamawa languages">Adamawa</a> (i.e. Niger–Congo) and partly from an unknown source, perhaps its original phylum, a now-vanished grouping from <a href="/wiki/Central_Africa" title="Central Africa">Central Africa</a>."  It is the last possibility which attracts particular interest; if this proves true, Laal may be the only remaining window on the linguistic state of Central Africa before the expansion of the main <a href="/wiki/African_language" class="mw-redirect" title="African language">African language</a> families—Afro-Asiatic, <a href="/wiki/Nilo-Saharan_languages" title="Nilo-Saharan languages">Nilo-Saharan</a>, and Niger–Congo—into it.
</p><p>Their immediate neighbors speak <a href="/wiki/Bua_language" title="Bua language">Bua</a>, <a href="/wiki/Niellim_language" title="Niellim language">Niellim</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ndam_language" title="Ndam language">Ndam</a>. Laal contains a number of <a href="/wiki/Loanword" title="Loanword">loanwords</a> from Baguirmi, which for several centuries was the lingua franca of the region under the <a href="/wiki/Baguirmi_Empire" class="mw-redirect" title="Baguirmi Empire">Baguirmi Empire</a>, and perhaps a dozen Chadic roots, which are not similar to the Chadic languages that currently neighbor Laal. In addition, almost all Laal speak Niellim as a second language, and 20%–30% of their vocabulary is cognate with Niellim, especially agricultural vocabulary (Boyeldieu 1977, Lionnet 2010). Like the <a href="/wiki/Baguirmi_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Baguirmi language">Baguirmi</a>, the Laal are <a href="/wiki/Muslim" class="mw-redirect" title="Muslim">Muslims</a>; partly because of this, some <a href="/wiki/Arabic_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a> loanwords are also found. However some 60% of the vocabulary, including most core vocabulary, cannot be identified with any known language family (Lionnet 2010). Indeed, some of the words cognate with Niellim, including some basic vocabulary, is not cognate with closely related Bua, suggesting that these are not Adamawa roots but loans in Niellim from the Laal substrate (Lionnet 2010). Pozdniakov (2010) believes Laal is a distinct branch of Niger–Congo with part of its pronominal system borrowed from a Chadic language like Kera.
</p><p>Alternatively, it may be a language descending from a language of a group of Neolithic Near Eastern farmers who immigrated to Chad, since recent genetic studies have found that the Laal people have significant Eurasian admixture similar to <a href="/wiki/Natufian" class="mw-redirect" title="Natufian">Natufians</a> and Neolithic Levantines.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag needs to be fact-checked with the cited source(s). (June 2020)">verification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Laal is grouped with the <a href="/wiki/Chadic_languages" title="Chadic languages">Chadic languages</a> in an automated computational analysis (<a href="/wiki/Automated_Similarity_Judgment_Program" title="Automated Similarity Judgment Program">ASJP</a> 4) by Müller et al. (2013), suggesting early contact with Chadic.<sup id="cite_ref-ASJP-4_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ASJP-4-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Implosives and prenasalised stops as well as h are found only word-initially. Voiceless stops as well as s cannot occur at the end of a syllable. <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ŋ/</span> occurs only intervocalically and word-finally. <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/s/</span> appears exclusively in <a href="/wiki/Loanword" title="Loanword">loanwords</a> and certain numbers.  Prenasalized stops as well as the implosive <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ʄ/</span> are extremely rare.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vowels">Vowels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Laal_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Vowels"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The vowel system for non-initial syllables is <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/i/,<span class="wrap"> </span>/ɨ/,<span class="wrap"> </span>/u/,<span class="wrap"> </span>/e/,<span class="wrap"> </span>/ə/,<span class="wrap"> </span>/o/,<span class="wrap"> </span>/a/</span> and the <a href="/wiki/Diphthong" title="Diphthong">diphthong</a> <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ua/</span>, with no length distinction.  For initial syllables, however, it is much more complicated, allowing length distinctions and distinguishing the following additional diphthongs:  <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ia/,<span class="wrap"> </span>/yo/,<span class="wrap"> </span>/ya/</span> (but the latter two appear only as morphologically conditioned forms of <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/e/</span> and <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ia/</span> and so are perhaps better seen as allophonic).
</p><p>In addition, <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/y/</span> may occur very occasionally; Boyeldieu quotes the example of <i>mỳlùg</i> "red (pl.)".
</p><p>There are three level tones: high (á), middle (a), low (à). Combinations may occur on a single vowel, resulting in phonetic rising and falling tone, and which are phonemically sequences of level tones. Such cases are transcribed here by repeating the vowel (e.g. àá); long vowels are indicated only by a colon (e.g. a:).
</p><p>Suffixes may force any of four kinds of <a href="/wiki/Ablaut" class="mw-redirect" title="Ablaut">ablaut</a> on the vowels of preceding words: raising (takes <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ia/,<span class="wrap"> </span>/a/,<span class="wrap"> </span>/ua/</span> to <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[e],<span class="wrap"> </span>[ə],<span class="wrap"> </span>[o]</span>), lowering (takes <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/e/,<span class="wrap"> </span>/ə/,<span class="wrap"> </span>/o/</span> to <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ia],<span class="wrap"> </span>[a],<span class="wrap"> </span>[ua]</span>), low rounding (takes <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/i/</span> and <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ɨ/</span> to <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[u]</span>; <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/e/</span> and <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ia/</span> to <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[yo]</span>; <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ə/,<span class="wrap"> </span>/a/</span>, and <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ua/</span> to <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[o]</span>), and high rounding (takes <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/i/</span> and <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ɨ/</span> to <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[u]</span>; <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/e/</span> and <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ia/</span> to <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ya]</span>; <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ə/,<span class="wrap"> </span>/a/</span>, and <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/o/</span> to <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ua]</span>). They are transcribed in the suffix section as ↑, ↓, ↗, ↘ respectively. In some verbs, a/ə is "raised" to <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[e]</span> rather than, as expected, to <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ə]</span>.
</p><p>In suffixes, ə and o undergo <a href="/wiki/Vowel_harmony" title="Vowel harmony">vowel harmony</a>: they become <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">ɨ</span> and u respectively if the preceding vowel is one of {i, <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">ɨ</span>, u}. Likewise, r undergoes consonant harmony, becoming l after words containing l.  Suffixes with a neutral tone copy the final tone of the word to which they are suffixed.<a rel="nofollow" class="external autonumber" href="https://s3.amazonaws.com/academia.edu.documents/42030562/African_linguistic_isolates.pdf?AWSAccessKeyId=AKIAIWOWYYGZ2Y53UL3A&amp;Expires=1526872805&amp;Signature=QykLYKQ%2BZ0SaMXcREtVAyBJD0rI%3D&amp;response-content-disposition=inline%3B%20filename%3DAfrican_language_isolates.pdf">[1]</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template"><span style="white-space: nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Link_rot" title="Wikipedia:Link rot"><span title="&#160;Dead link tagged May 2021">dead link</span></a></i>&#93;</span></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Morphology">Morphology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Laal_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Morphology"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Syntax">Syntax</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Laal_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Syntax"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The typical <a href="/wiki/Word_order" title="Word order">word order</a> can be summarised as <a href="/wiki/Subject_(grammar)" title="Subject (grammar)">subject</a>–(verbal <a href="/wiki/Particle_(grammar)" class="mw-redirect" title="Particle (grammar)">particle</a>)–<a href="/wiki/Verb" title="Verb">verb</a>–<a href="/wiki/Object_(grammar)" title="Object (grammar)">object</a>–<a href="/wiki/Adverb" title="Adverb">adverb</a>; <a href="/wiki/Preposition" class="mw-redirect" title="Preposition">preposition</a>–<a href="/wiki/Noun" title="Noun">noun</a>; <a href="/wiki/Possession_(linguistics)" title="Possession (linguistics)">possessed–possessor</a>; <a href="/wiki/Noun" title="Noun">noun</a>–<a href="/wiki/Adjective" title="Adjective">adjective</a>.  Nouns can be fronted when <a href="/wiki/Topic_(linguistics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Topic (linguistics)">topicalized</a>.  See the <a href="#Sample_sentences">sample sentences</a> below for examples and the <a href="#Conjunctions">conjunctions</a> for clause syntax.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nouns">Nouns</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Laal_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Nouns"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Nouns have plural and singular forms (the latter are perhaps better viewed as <a href="/wiki/Singulative" class="mw-redirect" title="Singulative">singulative</a> in some cases), with plural formation hard to predict: <i>kò:g</i> "bone" &gt; <i>kuagmi</i> "bones", <i>tuà:r</i> "chicken" &gt; <i>tò:rò</i> "chickens", <i>ɲaw</i> "hunger" &gt; <i>ɲə̀wə́r</i> "hungers". Nouns do not have arbitrary gender, but three natural genders (male, female, non-human) are distinguished by pronouns.
</p><p>The possessive is expressed in two ways:
</p>
<ul><li>"<a href="/wiki/Inalienable_possession" title="Inalienable possession">inalienable</a>", or direct, possession: by following the possessed with the possessor (and modifying the tone or ending of the possessed in some cases): <i>piá:r no</i> "person's leg" ("leg person");</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Alienable_possession" class="mw-redirect" title="Alienable possession">alienable possession</a>: by putting a connecting word, conjugated according to number and gender, between the possessed and the possessor:</li></ul>
<div class="interlinear" style="margin-left:3em"><div style="float: left; margin-bottom: 0.3em;margin-right: 1em;"><p style="margin: 0px;font-style: italic;">làgɨˋm</p><p style="margin: 0px;">horse</p></div><div style="float: left; margin-bottom: 0.3em;margin-right: 1em;"><p style="margin: 0px;font-style: italic;">má</p><p style="margin: 0px;"><abbr class="gloss-abbr" title="connective" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-variant-numeric: oldstyle-nums; text-transform: lowercase;">CONN</abbr></p></div><div style="float: left; margin-bottom: 0.3em;margin-right: 1em;"><p style="margin: 0px;font-style: italic;">màr-dɨb</p><p style="margin: 0px;">man+of-forge</p></div><p style="display: none;">làgɨˋm má màr-dɨb</p><p style="display: none;">horse CONN man+of-forge</p><p style="clear: left;">"blacksmith's horse"</p><div style="clear: left; display: block;"></div>
</div>
<dl><dd>The connecting word is sometimes abbreviated to a simple high tone.</dd></dl>
<p>However, if the possessor is a pronoun, it is suffixed with extensive vowel <a href="/wiki/Ablaut" class="mw-redirect" title="Ablaut">ablaut</a> (in the first case) or prepositional forms with "at" and optionally the connector as well, are used (in the second case): <i>na:ra ɟá ɗe:</i> "my man" ("man CONN. at-me"), <i>mùlù</i> "her eye" ("eye-her", from <i>mɨla</i> "eye").  Some nouns (<i>páw-</i> "friend") occur only with bound pronouns and have no independent form. That is called <a href="/wiki/Obligatory_possession" title="Obligatory possession">obligatory possession</a> and is found in many other language, usually for words referring to personal relationships.  See <a href="#Pronouns">pronouns</a> for the relevant suffixes.
</p><p>A noun indicating someone who does, is, or has something can be formed with the prefix <i>màr</i>, meaning roughly "he/she/it who/of": <i>màr jùgòr</i> "landowner", <i>màr ce</i> "farmer" (<i>ce</i> = cultivate), <i>màr pál</i> "fisherman" (<i>pál</i> = to fish), <i>màr pàlà ta:</i> "a fisher of fish".
</p><p>Laal does show traces of an old Adamawa-type noun-class system, but apart from loans, the forms do not appear to be cognate with the Adamawa system (Lionnet 2010).

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pronouns">Pronouns</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Laal_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Pronouns"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal">Personal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Laal_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Personal"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In the following tables, note the distinction between <a href="/wiki/Clusivity" title="Clusivity">inclusive and exclusive we</a>, found in many other languages but not <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a>, and the gender differentiation of "I" in certain forms.  The inanimate plural has been dropped by most popular younger speakers in favour of the animate plural, but both are given below. The object paradigm for verbs is quite complex; only two of its several sets of <a href="/wiki/Allomorph" title="Allomorph">allomorphs</a> are given in the table below. "He" and "she" are used only for human referents; other nouns take the neuter pronoun. That is quite distinct from the languages with which Laal shares vocabulary, but Laal has traces of an old Adamawa-type noun-class system (Lionnet 2010). The first- and second-person plural forms are quite similar to Chadic languages (specifically, <a href="/wiki/Kera_language" title="Kera language">Kera</a>) which are currently quite distant from the Laal-speaking region, but they have no similarities to Adamawa.

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Prepositions">Prepositions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Laal_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Prepositions"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Preposition" class="mw-redirect" title="Preposition">Prepositions</a> precede their objects: <i>gɨ̀ pə:l</i> "in(to) the village", <i>kɨ́ jà:ná</i> "to his body" (="to near him").
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Verbs">Verbs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Laal_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Verbs"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The verb does not vary according to the person or gender of the subject, but some verbs (about a quarter of the verbs attested) vary according to its number: <i>no kaw</i> "the person eats", <i>mùáŋ kɨw</i> "the people eat". The plural form of the verb is hard to predict, but is often formed by ablaut (typically raising the vowel height) with or without a suffix <i>-i(ɲ)</i> or <i>-ɨɲ</i> and tonal change.  The verb, however, changes according to the <a href="/wiki/Direct_object" class="mw-redirect" title="Direct object">direct object</a>. It takes personal suffixes to indicate a pronominal <a href="/wiki/Direct_object" class="mw-redirect" title="Direct object">direct object</a>, and it commonly changes when a non-pronominal direct object is added to a <a href="/wiki/Transitive_verb" title="Transitive verb">transitive</a> form with final low tone (formed similarly to the "centripetal", for which see below): <i>ʔà ná ká</i> "he will do"; <i>ʔà ná kàrà mɨ́ná</i> "he will do something"; <i>ʔà kú na:ra</i> "he sees the man"; <i>ʔà kúù:rùúŋ</i> "he sees you (pl.)".
</p><p>The verb has three basic forms: simple, "centripetal", and "participative" to <a href="/wiki/Calque" title="Calque">calque</a> Boyeldieu's terminology. The simple form is used in the simple <a href="/wiki/Present_tense" title="Present tense">present tense</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Imperative_mood" title="Imperative mood">imperative</a>: <i>ʔà duàg jə́w gə̀m</i> "he goes down the riverbank" (lit. "he descend mouth riverbank"). The "centripetal" indicates action "hither", either spatially, motion towards the speaker, or temporally, action up to the present moment; it is formed mainly by suffixing a vowel (often, but not always, identical to the last vowel in the word): <i>ʔà duàgà jə́w gə̀m</i> "he comes down the riverbank (towards me)". The "participative", generally formed like the centripetal but with final high tone, generally indicates an omitted object or instrument:
</p>
<div class="interlinear" style="margin-left:2em"><div style="float: left; margin-bottom: 0.3em;margin-right: 1em;"><p style="margin: 0px;font-style: italic;">ʔà</p><p style="margin: 0px;">he</p></div><div style="float: left; margin-bottom: 0.3em;margin-right: 1em;"><p style="margin: 0px;font-style: italic;">sá</p><p style="margin: 0px;">take</p></div><div style="float: left; margin-bottom: 0.3em;margin-right: 1em;"><p style="margin: 0px;font-style: italic;">ɗa:g</p><p style="margin: 0px;">calabash</p></div><div style="float: left; margin-bottom: 0.3em;margin-right: 1em;"><p style="margin: 0px;font-style: italic;">ʔà</p><p style="margin: 0px;">he</p></div><div style="float: left; margin-bottom: 0.3em;margin-right: 1em;"><p style="margin: 0px;font-style: italic;">sɨ̀rɨ́</p><p style="margin: 0px;">drink-<abbr class="gloss-abbr" title="participative" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-variant-numeric: oldstyle-nums; text-transform: lowercase;">PARTICIP</abbr></p></div><div style="float: left; margin-bottom: 0.3em;margin-right: 1em;"><p style="margin: 0px;font-style: italic;">su</p><p style="margin: 0px;">water</p></div><p style="display: none;">ʔà sá ɗa:g ʔà sɨ̀rɨ́ su</p><p style="display: none;">he take calabash he drink-<abbr class="gloss-abbr" title="participative" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-variant-numeric: oldstyle-nums; text-transform: lowercase;">PARTICIP</abbr> water</p><p style="clear: left;">"he takes a <a href="/wiki/Calabash" title="Calabash">calabash</a> and drinks water with it"</p><div style="clear: left; display: block;"></div>
</div>
<p>Immediately before the verb, a particle may be placed to indicate forms other than a simple present tense; such particles include <i>ná</i> (pl. <i>ní</i>) marking future tense, <i>taá:/teé:</i> (pl. <i>tií:</i>) marking continuous action, <i>wáa:</i> (pl. <i>wíi:</i>) marking motion, <i>náa:</i> (pl. <i>níi:</i>) being apparently a combination of <i>ná</i> and <i>wáa:</i>, <i>mà</i> (pl. <i>mì</i>) meaning "must", <i>mɨ́</i> marking reported speech (apparently an <a href="/wiki/Evidential" class="mw-redirect" title="Evidential">evidential</a>), <i>mɨ́nà</i> (pl. <i>mínì</i>) expressing intention, <i>kò</i> marking habitual action, <i>ɓə́l</i> or <i>ga</i> (pl. <i>gi</i>) marking incomplete action, and <i>wó</i> (always accompanied by <i>ʔàle</i> after the verb) meaning "maybe".
</p><p>Mediopassives (see <a href="/wiki/Passive_voice" title="Passive voice">passive voice</a>, <a href="/wiki/Middle_voice" class="mw-redirect" title="Middle voice">middle voice</a>) can be formed from <a href="/wiki/Transitive_verbs" class="mw-redirect" title="Transitive verbs">transitive verbs</a> by adding a suffix <i>-↑ɨ́ɲ</i>: <i>no siár sà:b</i> "someone ripped the cloth" &gt; <i>sà:b sérɨ́ɲ</i> "the cloth ripped".  For the inverse, forming transitive verbs from <a href="/wiki/Intransitive" class="mw-redirect" title="Intransitive">intransitives</a>, changes in tone or to the plural sometimes occur.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Verbal_noun" title="Verbal noun">Verbal nouns</a> can sometimes be formed, mainly from intransitives, by the addition of a suffix -(vowel)<i>l</i>, sometimes with <a href="/wiki/Ablaut" class="mw-redirect" title="Ablaut">ablaut</a> and tone change: <i>wal</i> "fall" &gt; <i>wàlál</i> "a fall", <i>sùbá</i> "lie" &gt; <i>sɨ́blál</i> (pl. <i>súbɨ̀r</i>) "a lie".  The <i>l</i> becomes <i>n</i> near a nasal and <i>r</i> near <i>r</i>: <i>man</i> "taste good", <i>manan</i> "a good taste".
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Adjectives">Adjectives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Laal_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Adjectives"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Adjectives do not seem to constitute an independent category in Laal; to all intents and purposes, they behave just like verbs: <i>gò: ʔì:r</i> "the goat is black".  Attributively, they are typically linked as a <a href="/wiki/Relative_clause" title="Relative clause">relative clause</a>:

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modals">Modals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Laal_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Modals"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Here are the most important modals:
</p>
<ul><li>Before the verb: <i>mɨ́</i> "(say) that", <i>gàná</i> "then"</li>
<li>After the verb: <i>wó</i> "not", <i>(ʔà)le</i> "maybe", <i>ɓə́l</i> "again", <i>ʔá</i> or <i>gà</i> "already", <i>à</i> <a href="/wiki/Interrogative" title="Interrogative">interrogative</a>, <i>wá</i> <a href="/wiki/Phrasal_exclamation" class="mw-redirect" title="Phrasal exclamation">exclamatory</a>, <i>ta</i> "now", <i>cám</i> "again, anew".</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Conjunctions">Conjunctions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Laal_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Conjunctions"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Conjunctions can be divided into five types:
</p>
<ul><li>only (<a href="/wiki/Main_clause" class="mw-redirect" title="Main clause">main clause</a> - <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_conjunction" class="mw-redirect" title="Grammatical conjunction">conjunction</a> - <a href="/wiki/Subordinate_clause" class="mw-redirect" title="Subordinate clause">subordinate clause</a>}: <i>mɨ́</i> "(say) that", <i>ɓə</i> "because"</li>
<li>either {main clause - conjunction - subordinate clause} or {conjunction - subordinate clause - main clause}: <i>ɟò</i> "if", <i>dànngà</i> (possibly from Baguirmian) "when"</li>
<li>circumposed: either {conjunction - main clause - conjunction - subordinate clause} or {conjunction - subordinate clause - conjunction - main clause}: <i>ɟò... gàná</i> "if"</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Coordinate_clause" class="mw-redirect" title="Coordinate clause">coordinate clause</a> - conjunction - coordinate clause: <i>ní</i> "then afterwards", <i>ku</i> "then", <i>kó</i> "nonetheless", <i>á</i> or <i>ná</i> "and", <i>ɓe:</i> "or", <i>ʔàmá</i> (from Arabic or Baguirmian) "but".</li>
<li>circumposed: conjunction - coordinate clause - conjunction - coordinate clause: <i>ku... ku</i> "then", <i>jàn... jàn</i> "both... and".</li></ul>
