<p><b>Na-Dene</b> (<span class="rt-commentedText nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt" lang="en-fonipa"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˌ/: secondary stress follows">ˌ</span><span title="&#39;n&#39; in &#39;nigh&#39;">n</span><span title="/ɑː/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;father&#39;">ɑː</span><span title="&#39;d&#39; in &#39;dye&#39;">d</span><span title="/ɪ/: &#39;i&#39; in &#39;kit&#39;">ɪ</span><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="&#39;n&#39; in &#39;nigh&#39;">n</span><span title="/eɪ/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;face&#39;">eɪ</span></span>/</a></span></span> <a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key"><i title="English pronunciation respelling"><span style="font-size:90%">NAH</span>-dih-<span style="font-size:90%">NAY</span></i></a>; also <b>Nadene</b>, <b>Na-Dené</b>, <b>Athabaskan–Eyak–Tlingit</b>, <b>Tlina–Dene</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Language_family" title="Language family">family</a> of <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_languages_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous languages of the Americas">Native American languages</a> that includes at least the <a href="/wiki/Athabaskan_languages" title="Athabaskan languages">Athabaskan languages</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eyak_language" title="Eyak language">Eyak</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tlingit_language" title="Tlingit language">Tlingit</a> languages. <a href="/wiki/Haida_language" title="Haida language">Haida</a> was formerly included, but is now considered doubtful. By far the most widely spoken Na-Dene language today is <a href="/wiki/Navajo_language" title="Navajo language">Navajo</a>.
</p><p>In February 2008, a proposal connecting Na-Dene (excluding Haida) to the <a href="/wiki/Yeniseian_languages" title="Yeniseian languages">Yeniseian languages</a> of central <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a> into a <a href="/wiki/Den%C3%A9%E2%80%93Yeniseian_languages" title="Dené–Yeniseian languages">Dené–Yeniseian</a> family was published and well-received by a number of linguists.<sup id="cite_ref-DYS_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DYS-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> It was proposed in a 2014 paper that the Na-Dene languages of North America and the Yeniseian languages of Siberia had a common origin in a language spoken in <a href="/wiki/Beringia" title="Beringia">Beringia</a>, between the two continents.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Na-Dene_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Edward_Sapir" title="Edward Sapir">Edward Sapir</a> originally constructed the term <i>Na-Dene</i> to refer to a combined family of Athabaskan, Tlingit, and Haida (the existence of the <a href="/wiki/Eyak_language" title="Eyak language">Eyak language</a> was not known to him at the time). In his “The Na-Dene languages: A preliminary report”, he describes how he arrived at the term (Sapir 1915, p.&#160;558):
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r996844942">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>The name that I have chosen for the stock, <i>Na-dene</i>, may be justified by reference to no. 51 of the comparative vocabulary. <i>Dene</i>, in various dialectic forms, is a wide-spread Athabaskan term for “person, people”; the element <i>*-ne</i> (<i>*-n</i>, <i>*-η</i>) which forms part of it is an old stem for “person, people” which, as suffix or prefix, is frequently used in Athabaskan in that sense. It is cognate with H. [= Haida] <i>na</i> "to dwell; house" and Tl. [= Tlingit] <i>na</i> “people”. The compound term <i>Na-dene</i> thus designates by means of native stems the speakers of the three languages concerned, besides continuing the use of the old term <i>Dene</i> for the Athabaskan branch of the stock.</p></blockquote>

<p>In its uncontroversial core, Na-Dene consists of two branches, Tlingit and Athabaskan–Eyak:

<p>For linguists who follow <a href="/wiki/Edward_Sapir" title="Edward Sapir">Edward Sapir</a> in connecting Haida to the above languages, Haida represents an additional branch, with Athabaskan–Eyak–Tlingit together forming the other. <a href="/wiki/Dene" title="Dene">Dene</a> or Dine (the Athabaskan languages) is a widely distributed group of Native languages spoken by associated peoples in <a href="/wiki/Alberta" title="Alberta">Alberta</a>, <a href="/wiki/British_Columbia" title="British Columbia">British Columbia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Manitoba" title="Manitoba">Manitoba</a>, <a href="/wiki/Northwest_Territories" title="Northwest Territories">Northwest Territories</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nunavut" title="Nunavut">Nunavut</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saskatchewan" title="Saskatchewan">Saskatchewan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yukon" title="Yukon">Yukon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a>, parts of <a href="/wiki/Oregon" title="Oregon">Oregon</a>, northern <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/American_Southwest" class="mw-redirect" title="American Southwest">American Southwest</a> as far as northern <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>.
</p><p>The southwestern division of Athabaskan is also called Southern Athabaskan or <a href="/wiki/Apachean" class="mw-redirect" title="Apachean">Apachean</a>, and includes <a href="/wiki/Navajo_language" title="Navajo language">Navajo</a> and all the <a href="/wiki/Apache" title="Apache">Apache</a> languages. <a href="/wiki/Eyak" title="Eyak">Eyak</a> was spoken in south-central Alaska; the last first language speaker died in 2008. <a href="/wiki/Navajo_language" title="Navajo language">Navajo</a> is by far the most widely spoken language of the Na-Dene family, spoken in <a href="/wiki/Arizona" title="Arizona">Arizona</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Mexico" title="New Mexico">New Mexico</a>, and other regions of the American Southwest.
</p>
<h2><span id="Typological_profile_of_Athabaskan.E2.80.93Eyak.E2.80.93Tlingit"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Typological_profile_of_Athabaskan–Eyak–Tlingit">Typological profile of Athabaskan–Eyak–Tlingit</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Na-Dene_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Typological profile of Athabaskan–Eyak–Tlingit"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1097763485"><table class="box-Unreferenced_section plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Unreferenced" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div class="mbox-image-div"><span typeof="mw:File"><a href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg" class="mw-file-description"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" decoding="async" width="50" height="39" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="512" data-file-height="399" /></a></span></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>does not <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">cite</a> any <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">sources</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Na-Dene_languages&amp;action=edit">improve this section</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Referencing_for_beginners" title="Help:Referencing for beginners">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. Unsourced material may be challenged and <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability#Burden_of_evidence" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">removed</a>.</span>  <span class="date-container"><i>(<span class="date">June 2020</span>)</i></span><span class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<small><a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a></small>)</i></span></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>All of these languages share a highly complex prefixing verb structure in which tense and mood markers are interdigitated between subject and object agreement markers. The morphological hallmark of the family is a series of prefixes found directly before the verb root that raise or lower the transitivity of the verb word. These prefixes, traditionally known as "classifiers", derive historically from a combination of three distinct classes of morphemes and are not found in any other Native American language family.
</p><p>The phoneme system contains a large number of dorsal (velar or uvular) consonants (fronting in many modern Athabaskan languages to palatals and velars, correspondingly) as well as a general absence of labial <a href="/wiki/Obstruent" title="Obstruent">obstruents</a> (except where /b/ has arisen from *w). In the historical phonology there is a widespread tendency, observable across many Athabaskan languages, for phonemic tonal distinctions to arise from glottal features originally found at the end of the syllable. The glottal features in question are often evident in Eyak or Tlingit. These languages are typologically unusual in containing extensive prefixation yet being SOV and postpositional, features normally associated with suffixing languages.
</p>
<h2><span id="Proposals_of_deeper_genealogical_relations_involving_Athabaskan.E2.80.93Eyak.E2.80.93Tlingit"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Proposals_of_deeper_genealogical_relations_involving_Athabaskan–Eyak–Tlingit">Proposals of deeper genealogical relations involving Athabaskan–Eyak–Tlingit</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Na-Dene_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Proposals of deeper genealogical relations involving Athabaskan–Eyak–Tlingit"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A genealogical connection between the Tlingit, Eyak and Athabaskan languages was suggested early in the 19th century, but not universally accepted until much later. <a href="/wiki/Haida_language" title="Haida language">Haida</a>, with 15 fluent speakers (M. Krauss, 1995), was originally linked to Tlingit by <a href="/wiki/Franz_Boas" title="Franz Boas">Franz Boas</a> in 1894. Both Haida and Tlingit were then connected to Athabaskan by Edward Sapir in 1915. Linguists such as <a href="/wiki/Lyle_Campbell" title="Lyle Campbell">Lyle Campbell</a> (1997) today consider the evidence inconclusive. They have classified Haida as a <a href="/wiki/Language_isolate" title="Language isolate">language isolate</a>. In order to emphasise the exclusion of Haida, Campbell refers to the language family as <i>Athabaskan–Eyak–Tlingit</i> rather than <i>Na-Dene</i>. In 2010 Jeff Leer published extensive primary materials on what he calls <i>PAET</i> (Proto-Athabaskan–Eyak–Tlingit).
</p>
<h3><span id="Den.C3.A9.E2.80.93Yeniseian"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Dené–Yeniseian">Dené–Yeniseian</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Na-Dene_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Dené–Yeniseian"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Den%C3%A9%E2%80%93Yeniseian_languages" title="Dené–Yeniseian languages">Dené–Yeniseian languages</a></div>
<p>In 2008, <a href="/wiki/Edward_Vajda" title="Edward Vajda">Edward Vajda</a> of <a href="/wiki/Western_Washington_University" title="Western Washington University">Western Washington University</a> presented evidence suggesting that the Na-Dene languages (Athabaskan–Eyak–Tlingit) might be related to the <a href="/wiki/Yeniseian_languages" title="Yeniseian languages">Yeniseian</a> (or Yeniseic) languages of <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-vajd08b_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vajd08b-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> the only living representative of which is the <a href="/wiki/Ket_language" title="Ket language">Ket language</a>.
</p><p>Key evidence by current comparative methodologies includes homologies in verb prefixes and also a systematic correspondence between the distribution of Ket tones and consonant articulations found in Athabaskan–Eyak–Tlingit. Vajda's paper has been favorably reviewed by several experts on Na-Dene and Yeniseic languages, including <a href="/wiki/Michael_E._Krauss" title="Michael E. Krauss">Michael Krauss</a>, Jeff Leer, <a href="/wiki/James_Kari" title="James Kari">James Kari</a>, and Heinrich Werner, as well as a number of other well-known linguists, including <a href="/wiki/Bernard_Comrie" title="Bernard Comrie">Bernard Comrie</a>, <a href="/wiki/Johanna_Nichols" title="Johanna Nichols">Johanna Nichols</a>, Victor Golla, <a href="/wiki/Michael_Fortescue" title="Michael Fortescue">Michael Fortescue</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Eric_P._Hamp" title="Eric P. Hamp">Eric Hamp</a>. The conclusion of this seminar was that the comparison with Yeniseic data shows that Haida cannot be classified in a genealogical unit with Athabaskan–Eyak–Tlingit.<sup id="cite_ref-DYS_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DYS-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sino-Tibetan">Sino-Tibetan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Na-Dene_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Sino-Tibetan"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Sino-Tibetan_languages" title="Sino-Tibetan languages">Sino-Tibetan languages</a></div>
<p>A link between the Na–Dené languages and Sino-Tibetan languages, known as <b>Sino–Dené</b> was proposed by <a href="/wiki/Edward_Sapir" title="Edward Sapir">Edward Sapir</a>. Around 1920 Sapir became convinced that Na-Dené was more closely related to Sino-Tibetan than to other American families.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> He wrote a series of letters to <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Kroeber" title="Alfred Kroeber">Alfred Kroeber</a> where he enthusiastically spoke of a connection between Na-Dene and "Indo-Chinese". In 1925, a supporting article summarizing his thoughts, albeit not written by him, entitled "The Similarities of Chinese and Indian Languages", was published in Science Supplements.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>Edward Vadja's Dené–Yeniseian proposal renewed interest among linguists such as <a href="/w/index.php?title=Geoffrey_Caveney&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Geoffrey Caveney (page does not exist)">Geoffrey Caveney</a> (2014) to look into support for the Sino–Dené hypothesis. Caveney considered a link between Sino-Tibetan, Na-Dené, and Yeniseian to be plausible but did not support the hypothesis that Sino-Tibetan and Na-Dené were related to the Caucasian languages (Sino–Caucasian and Dené–Caucasian).<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A 2023 analysis by <a href="/wiki/David_Bradley_(linguist)" title="David Bradley (linguist)">David Bradley</a> using the standard techniques of comparative linguistics supports a distant genetic link between the Sino-Tibetan, Na-Dené, and Yeniseian language families. Bradley argues that any similarities Sino-Tibetan shares with other language families of the East Asia area such as Hmong-Mien, <a href="/wiki/Altaic_languages" title="Altaic languages">Altaic</a> (which is actually a <a href="/wiki/Sprachbund" title="Sprachbund">sprachbund</a>), Austroasiatic, Kra-Dai, Austronesian came through contact; but as there has been no recent contact between Sino-Tibetan, Na-Dené, and Yeniseian language families then any similarities these groups share must be residual.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_proposals">Other proposals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Na-Dene_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Other proposals"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>According to <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Greenberg" title="Joseph Greenberg">Joseph Greenberg</a>'s controversial classification of the languages of Native North America, Na-Dené (including Haida) is one of the three main groups of Native languages spoken in the Americas. Contemporary supporters of Greenberg's theory, such as <a href="/wiki/Merritt_Ruhlen" title="Merritt Ruhlen">Merritt Ruhlen</a>, have suggested that the Na-Dené language family represents a distinct migration of people from <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a> into the New World that occurred six to eight thousand years ago, placing it around four thousand years later than the previous migration into the Americas by <a href="/wiki/Amerind_languages" title="Amerind languages">Amerind</a> speakers; this remains an unproven hypothesis.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Ruhlen speculates that the Na-Dené speakers may have arrived in boats, initially settling near the <a href="/wiki/Haida_Gwaii" title="Haida Gwaii">Haida Gwaii</a>, now in <a href="/wiki/British_Columbia" title="British Columbia">British Columbia</a>, Canada.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A fringe hypothesis by <a href="/wiki/Sergei_Starostin" title="Sergei Starostin">Sergei Starostin</a> suggested that Na-Dené (including Haida) may belong to the much broader <a href="/wiki/Den%C3%A9%E2%80%93Caucasian_languages" title="Dené–Caucasian languages">Dené–Caucasian</a> superfamily, which also contains the <a href="/wiki/North_Caucasian_languages" title="North Caucasian languages">North Caucasian languages</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sino-Tibetan_languages" title="Sino-Tibetan languages">Sino-Tibetan languages</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Yeniseian_languages" title="Yeniseian languages">Yeniseian languages</a>. This proposal is rejected by nearly all current linguists.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
