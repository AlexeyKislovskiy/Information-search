<p><b>Kalasha</b> (<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1177148991">.mw-parser-output .IPA-label-small{font-size:85%}.mw-parser-output .references .IPA-label-small,.mw-parser-output .infobox .IPA-label-small,.mw-parser-output .navbox .IPA-label-small{font-size:100%}</style><span class="IPA-label IPA-label-small">IPA:</span> <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="kls-Latn-fonipa"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA" title="Help:IPA">&#91;kaɭaʂaː&#93;</a></span>, <small>locally:</small> <span title="Kalasha-language text"><i lang="kls">Kal'as'amondr</i></span>) is an <a href="/wiki/Indo-Aryan_languages" title="Indo-Aryan languages">Indo-Aryan language</a> spoken by the <a href="/wiki/Kalash_people" title="Kalash people">Kalash people</a>, in the <a href="/wiki/Chitral_District" title="Chitral District">Chitral District</a> of <a href="/wiki/Khyber_Pakhtunkhwa" title="Khyber Pakhtunkhwa">Khyber Pakhtunkhwa</a> province of <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a>. There are an estimated 4,100 speakers of Kalasha.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> It is an <a href="/wiki/Endangered_language" title="Endangered language">endangered language</a> and there is an ongoing <a href="/wiki/Language_shift" title="Language shift">language shift</a> to <a href="/wiki/Khowar_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Khowar language">Khowar</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Kalasha should not be confused with the nearby <a href="/wiki/Nuristani_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Nuristani language">Nuristani language</a> <a href="/wiki/Waigali_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Waigali language">Waigali</a> (Kalasha-ala). According to Badshah Munir Bukhari, a researcher on the Kalash, "Kalasha" is also the ethnic name for the <a href="/wiki/Nuristanis" title="Nuristanis">Nuristani</a> inhabitants of a region southwest of the Kalasha Valleys, in the <a href="/wiki/Waygal_District" title="Waygal District">Waygal</a> and middle <a href="/wiki/Pech_Valley" title="Pech Valley">Pech Valleys</a> of Afghanistan's <a href="/wiki/Nuristan_Province" title="Nuristan Province">Nuristan Province</a>. The name "Kalasha" seems to have been adopted for the Kalash people by the Kalasha speakers of Chitral from the Nuristanis of Waygal, who for a time expanded up to southern Chitral several centuries ago.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> However, there is no close connection between the Indo-Aryan language Kalasha-mun (Kalasha) and the Nuristani language Kalasha-ala (Waigali), which descend from different branches of the <a href="/wiki/Indo-Iranian_languages" title="Indo-Iranian languages">Indo-Iranian languages</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kalasha_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Early scholars to have done work on Kalasha include the 19th-century orientalist <a href="/wiki/Gottlieb_Wilhelm_Leitner" title="Gottlieb Wilhelm Leitner">Gottlieb Wilhelm Leitner</a> and the 20th-century linguist <a href="/wiki/Georg_Morgenstierne" title="Georg Morgenstierne">Georg Morgenstierne</a>. More recently, studies have been undertaken by <a href="/wiki/Elena_Bashir" title="Elena Bashir">Elena Bashir</a> and several others. The development of practical literacy materials has been associated with the Kalasha linguist <a href="/wiki/Taj_Khan_Kalash" class="mw-redirect" title="Taj Khan Kalash">Taj Khan Kalash</a>.  The Southern Kalash or Urtsun Kalash shifted to a Khowar-influenced dialect of Kalasha-mun in the 20th century called <a href="/wiki/Urtsuniwar" title="Urtsuniwar">Urtsuniwar</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">Classification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kalasha_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Classification"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Of all the languages in <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a>, Kalasha is likely the most conservative, along with the nearby language <a href="/wiki/Khowar_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Khowar language">Khowar</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> In a few cases, Kalasha is even more conservative than Khowar, e.g. in retaining voiced aspirate consonants, which have disappeared from most other Dardic languages.

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conservative_traits">Conservative traits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kalasha_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Conservative traits"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Examples of conservative features in Kalasha and Khowar are (note, NIA = <a href="/wiki/New_Indo-Aryan" class="mw-redirect" title="New Indo-Aryan">New Indo-Aryan</a>, MIA = <a href="/wiki/Middle_Indo-Aryan" class="mw-redirect" title="Middle Indo-Aryan">Middle Indo-Aryan</a>, OIA = <a href="/wiki/Old_Indo-Aryan" class="mw-redirect" title="Old Indo-Aryan">Old Indo-Aryan</a>):<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>Preservation of intervocalic /m/ (reduced to a nasalized /w/ or /v/ in late MIA elsewhere), e.g. Kal. <i>grom</i>, Kho. <i>gram</i> "village" &lt; OIA <i>grāma</i></li>
<li>Non-deletion of intervocalic /t/, preserved as /l/ or /w/ in Kalasha, /r/ in Khowar (deleted in middle MIA elsewhere), e.g. Kho. <i>brār</i> "brother" &lt; OIA <i>bhrātṛ</i>; Kal. <i>ʃau</i> &lt; <i>*ʃal</i>, Kho. <i>ʃor</i> "hundred" &lt; OIA <i>śata</i></li>
<li>Preservation of the distinction between all three OIA sibilants (dental /s/, palatal /ś/, retroflex /ṣ/); in most of the subcontinent, these three had already merged before 200 BC (early MIA)</li>
<li>Preservation of sibilant + consonant, stop + /r/ clusters (lost by early MIA in most other places):
<ul><li>Kal. <i>aṣṭ</i>, Kho. <i>oṣṭ</i> "eight" &lt; OIA <i>aṣṭā</i>; Kal. <i>hast</i>, Kho. <i>host</i> "hand" &lt; OIA <i>hasta</i>; Kal. <i>istam</i> "bunch" &lt; OIA <i>stamba</i>; Kho. <i>istōr</i> "pack horse" &lt; OIA <i>sthōra</i>; Kho. <i>isnār</i> "bathed" &lt; OIA <i>snāta</i>; Kal. Kho. <i>iskow</i> "peg" &lt; OIA <i>*<span title="Indic languages collective text"><i lang="inc">skabha</i></span></i> (&lt; <i>skambha</i>); Kho. <i>iśper</i> "white" &lt; OIA <i>śvēta</i>; Kal. <i>isprɛs</i>, Kho. <i>iśpreṣi</i> "mother-in-law" &lt; OIA <i>śvaśru</i>; Kal. <i>piṣṭ</i> "back" &lt; OIA <i>pṛṣṭha</i>; Kho. <i>aśrū</i> "tear" &lt; OIA <i>aśru</i>.</li>
<li>Kho. <i>kren-</i> "buy" &lt; OIA <i>krīṇ-</i>; Kal. <i>grom</i>, Kho. <i>grom</i> "village" &lt; OIA <i>grāma</i>; Kal. <i>gŕä</i> "neck" &lt; OIA <i>grīva</i>; Kho. <i>griṣp</i> "summer" &lt; OIA <i>grīṣma</i></li></ul></li>
<li>Preservation of /ts/ in Kalasha (reinterpreted as a single phoneme)</li>
<li>Direct preservation of many OIA case endings as so-called "layer 1" case endings (as opposed to newer "layer 2" case endings, typically tacked onto a layer-1 oblique case):
<ul><li>Nominative</li>
<li>Oblique (Animate): Pl. Kal. <i>-an</i>, Kho. <i>-an</i> &lt; OIA <i>-ān</i></li>
<li>Genitive: Kal. <i>-as</i> (sg.), <i>-an</i> (pl.); Kho. <i>-o</i> (sg.), <i>-an, -ān</i> (pl.) &lt; OIA <i>-asya</i> (sg.), <i>āṇām</i> (pl.)</li>
<li>Dative: Kal. <i>-a</i>, Kho. <i>-a</i> &lt; OIA dative <i>-āya</i>, elsewhere lost already in late OIA</li>
<li>Instrumental: Kal. <i>-an</i>, Kho. <i>-en</i> &lt; OIA <i>-ēna</i></li>
<li>Ablative: Kal. <i>-au</i>, Kho. <i>-ār</i> &lt; OIA <i>-āt</i></li>
<li>Locative: Kal. <i>-ai</i>, Kho. <i>-i</i> &lt; OIA <i>-ai</i></li></ul></li>
<li>Preservation of more than one verbal conjugation (e.g. Kho. <i>mār-īm</i> "I kill" vs. <i>bri-um</i> "I die")</li>
<li>Preservation of OIA distinction between "primary" (non-past) and "secondary" (past) endings and of a past-tense "augment" in a-, both lost entirely elsewhere: Kal. <i>pim</i> "I drink", <i>apis</i> "I drank"; <i>kārim</i> "I do", <i>akāris</i> "I did"</li>
<li>Preservation of a verbal preterite tense (see examples above), with normal nominative/accusative marking and normal verbal agreement, as opposed to the <a href="/wiki/Ergative_verb" class="mw-redirect" title="Ergative verb">ergative</a>-type past tenses with nominal-type agreement elsewhere in NIA (originally based on a participial passive construction)</li></ul>
