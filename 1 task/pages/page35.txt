<p><b>Nogai</b> (<span class="rt-commentedText nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt" lang="en-fonipa"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="&#39;n&#39; in &#39;nigh&#39;">n</span><span title="/oʊ/: &#39;o&#39; in &#39;code&#39;">oʊ</span><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="/ɡ/: &#39;g&#39; in &#39;guy&#39;">ɡ</span><span title="/aɪ/: &#39;i&#39; in &#39;tide&#39;">aɪ</span></span>/</a></span></span> <a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key"><i title="English pronunciation respelling">no-<span style="font-size:90%">GUY</span></i></a>; <span title="Nogai-language text"><span lang="nog-Cyrl">Ногай тили</span></span>, <span title="Nogai-language text"><i lang="nog-Latn">Nogay tili</i></span>, <span title="Nogai-language text"><span lang="nog-Cyrl">Ногайша</span></span>, <span title="Nogai-language text"><i lang="nog-Latn">Nogayşa</i></span>) also known as <b>Noğay</b>, <b>Noghay</b>, <b>Nogay</b>, or <b>Nogai Tatar</b>, is a <a href="/wiki/Turkic_languages" title="Turkic languages">Turkic language</a> spoken in Southeastern <a href="/wiki/European_Russia" title="European Russia">European Russia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kazakhstan" title="Kazakhstan">Kazakhstan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Uzbekistan" title="Uzbekistan">Uzbekistan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">Ukraine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bulgaria" title="Bulgaria">Bulgaria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Romania" title="Romania">Romania</a> and <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>. It is the ancestral language of the <a href="/wiki/Nogais" title="Nogais">Nogais</a>. As a member of the <a href="/wiki/Kipchak_languages" title="Kipchak languages">Kipchak branch</a>, it is closely related to <a href="/wiki/Kazakh_language" title="Kazakh language">Kazakh</a>, <a href="/wiki/Karakalpak_language" title="Karakalpak language">Karakalpak</a> and <a href="/wiki/Crimean_Tatar_language" title="Crimean Tatar language">Crimean Tatar</a>. In 2014 the first Nogai novel (<i>Akşa Nenem</i>) was published, written in the Latin alphabet.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">Classification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nogai_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Classification"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Nogai is generally classified into the Kipchak–Nogai branch of <a href="/wiki/Kipchak_languages" title="Kipchak languages">Kipchak</a> <a href="/wiki/Turkic_languages" title="Turkic languages">Turkic</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The latter also includes <a href="/wiki/Siberian_Tatar_language" title="Siberian Tatar language">Siberian Tatar</a> in <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kazakh_language" title="Kazakh language">Kazakh</a> in <a href="/wiki/Kazakhstan" title="Kazakhstan">Kazakhstan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Karakalpak_language" title="Karakalpak language">Karakalpak</a> in <a href="/wiki/Uzbekistan" title="Uzbekistan">Uzbekistan</a>.
</p><p>Three distinct dialects are recognized:<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> 
</p>
<ul><li><b>Karanogay</b> or <b>Qara-Nogai</b> (literally "Black Nogai"; "Northern Nogai"), spoken in <a href="/wiki/Dagestan" title="Dagestan">Dagestan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chechnya" title="Chechnya">Chechnya</a>.</li>
<li><b>Central Nogai</b> or <b>Nogai Proper</b>, in <a href="/wiki/Stavropol" title="Stavropol">Stavropol</a>.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Ak_Nogai" title="Ak Nogai">Aqnogai</a></b> (White or Western Nogai), by the <a href="/wiki/Kuban_River" class="mw-redirect" title="Kuban River">Kuban River</a>, its tributaries in <a href="/wiki/Karachay%E2%80%93Cherkessia" class="mw-redirect" title="Karachay–Cherkessia">Karachay–Cherkessia</a> and in the Mineralnye Vody District. Qara-Nogai and Nogai Proper are very close linguistically, whereas Aqnogai is more different. However, all three are mutually intelligible.</li></ul>
<p>Outside of the southern Caucasus, other varieties exist that are either considered dialects, or distinct languages:
</p>
<ul><li>Nogais in <a href="/wiki/Bulgaria" title="Bulgaria">Bulgaria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Romania" title="Romania">Romania</a> and <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a> has <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Turkish" title="Ottoman Turkish">Ottoman Turkish</a> influences.</li>
<li>Astrakhan Nogai, with the following three varieties:<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
<ul><li>Karagash (also known as Kundrov Tatar)</li>
<li>Yurt Tatar or Yurt Nogai</li>
<li>Alabugat Tatar or Alabugat Nogai (also known as Utar)</li></ul></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nogai_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Nogais" title="Nogais">Nogais</a>, descended from the peoples of the <a href="/wiki/Golden_Horde" title="Golden Horde">Golden Horde</a>, take their name and that of their language from the grandson of <a href="/wiki/Genghis_Khan" title="Genghis Khan">Genghis Khan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nogai_Khan" title="Nogai Khan">Nogai Khan</a>, who ruled the nomadic people west of the <a href="/wiki/Danube" title="Danube">Danube</a> toward the end of the 13th century. They then settled along the <a href="/wiki/Black_Sea" title="Black Sea">Black Sea</a> coast of present-day <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">Ukraine</a>.
</p><p>Historically, Nogai was a spoken language. When speakers wanted to write, they utilized the <a href="/wiki/Cuman_language" title="Cuman language">Kypchak</a> or <a href="/wiki/Chagatai_language" title="Chagatai language">Chagatai</a> languages, which were similar to Nogai and were written in the <a href="/wiki/Perso-Arabic" class="mw-redirect" title="Perso-Arabic">Perso-Arabic</a> script. In 1928, a <a href="/wiki/Latin_script" title="Latin script">Latin alphabet</a> was introduced. It was devised by the Nogai academic <a href="/w/index.php?title=Abdul-Khamid_Shershenbievich_Dzhanibekov&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Abdul-Khamid Shershenbievich Dzhanibekov (page does not exist)">Abdul-Khamid Shershenbievich Dzhanibekov</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;">&#160;&#91;<a href="https://ru.wikipedia.org/wiki/%D0%94%D0%B6%D0%B0%D0%BD%D0%B8%D0%B1%D0%B5%D0%BA%D0%BE%D0%B2,_%D0%90%D0%B1%D0%B4%D1%83%D0%BB-%D0%A5%D0%B0%D0%BC%D0%B8%D0%B4_%D0%A8%D0%B5%D1%80%D1%88%D0%B5%D0%BD%D0%B1%D0%B8%D0%B5%D0%B2%D0%B8%D1%87" class="extiw" title="ru:Джанибеков, Абдул-Хамид Шершенбиевич">ru</a>&#93;</span> (Djanibek), following principles adopted for all <a href="/wiki/Turkic_languages" title="Turkic languages">Turkic languages</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1938, a transition to the <a href="/wiki/Cyrillic_alphabet" class="mw-redirect" title="Cyrillic alphabet">Cyrillic alphabet</a> began. The <a href="/wiki/Orthography" title="Orthography">orthography</a> based on the Latin alphabet was alleged to be an impediment to learning Russian.
</p><p>The expulsion of the Nogais from Ukraine in the nineteenth century separated Nogai speakers into several geographically isolated groups. Some went to <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a> and <a href="/wiki/Romania" title="Romania">Romania</a>, while others stayed within the <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Russia" class="mw-redirect" title="Imperial Russia">Russian Empire</a>, settling in northern Dagestan and neighbouring areas of <a href="/wiki/Chechnya" title="Chechnya">Chechnya</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stavropol_Kray" class="mw-redirect" title="Stavropol Kray">Stavropol Kray</a>.
</p><p>The Nogai language has disappeared very rapidly in Turkey. Today, it is mostly spoken by the older generation; however, there are still younger speakers, as there are some villages in Turkey where it is a common mode of communication. In the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> the language of instruction in schools was Russian, and the number of speakers declined there also. Recent estimates place the total number of Nogai speakers at about 80,000.
</p><p>In 1973, two small Nogai-language newspapers were being published, one in Karachay–Cherkessia and another in the Dagestan Autonomous SSR (<i>Ленин йолы</i>), but most speakers never heard of these publications, and the papers did not reach Nogai villages.
</p><p>Nogai is now part of the school curriculum from the 1st to the 10th year in the Nogai District of Dagestan. It is also taught at the Karachayevo-Cherkess Pedagogical School and the national branch of the Pedagogical Institute.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Alphabet">Alphabet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nogai_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Alphabet"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1033289096">.mw-parser-output .hatnote{font-style:italic}.mw-parser-output div.hatnote{padding-left:1.6em;margin-bottom:0.5em}.mw-parser-output .hatnote i{font-style:normal}.mw-parser-output .hatnote+link+.hatnote{margin-top:-0.5em}</style><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Nogai_alphabets" title="Nogai alphabets">Nogai alphabets</a></div>
<p>There are 3 stages in the history of Nogai writing:
</p>
<ul><li>before 1928 — unwritten; <a href="/wiki/Cuman_language" title="Cuman language">Kypchak</a> and/or <a href="/wiki/Chagatai_language" title="Chagatai language">Chagatai</a> was used instead with the <a href="/wiki/Perso-Arabic" class="mw-redirect" title="Perso-Arabic">Perso-Arabic</a> script</li>
<li>1928—1938 — writing based on the Latin alphabet</li>
<li>from 1938 — writing based on the Cyrillic alphabet</li></ul>
<p>The Nogai alphabet based on <a href="/wiki/Cyrillic" class="mw-redirect" title="Cyrillic">Cyrillic</a> was created in 1938. It included all of the <a href="/wiki/Russian_alphabet" title="Russian alphabet">Russian alphabet</a> letters except <b>Ё ё</b>, and also the <a href="/wiki/Digraph_(orthography)" title="Digraph (orthography)">digraphs</a> <b>Гъ гъ, Къ къ, Нъ нъ</b>. The digraphs <b>Оь оь, Уь уь</b> were added in the same year. In 1944 the digraphs <b>Гъ гъ, Къ къ</b> were excluded from the alphabet.
</p><p>The last reform of the Nogai writing took place in 1960, when, as a result of discussions at the <a href="/wiki/Karachay-Cherkessia" title="Karachay-Cherkessia">Karachay-Cherkessia</a> Research Institute, Language and Literature, the letters <b>Аь аь</b> and <b>Ё ё</b> were added to it. After that, the Nogai alphabet took its present form.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
