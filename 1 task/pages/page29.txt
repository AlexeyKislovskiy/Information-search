<p><b>Norfuk</b> (<a href="/wiki/Pitcairn-Norfolk_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Pitcairn-Norfolk language">Pitcairn-Norfolk</a>: <i lang="pih">Norfuk</i>) (increasingly spelt <b>Norfolk</b>) or <b>Norf'k</b><sup id="cite_ref-Norfolk_Island_Language_(Norf’k)_Act_2004_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Norfolk_Island_Language_(Norf’k)_Act_2004-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> is the language spoken on <a href="/wiki/Norfolk_Island" title="Norfolk Island">Norfolk Island</a> (in the Pacific Ocean) by the local residents. It is a blend of 18th-century English and <a href="/wiki/Tahitian_language" title="Tahitian language">Tahitian</a>, originally introduced by <a href="/wiki/Pitkern_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Pitkern language">Pitkern</a>-speaking settlers from the <a href="/wiki/Pitcairn_Islands" title="Pitcairn Islands">Pitcairn Islands</a>.  Along with English, it is the <a href="/wiki/Norfolk_Island#Language" title="Norfolk Island">co-official language</a> of Norfolk Island.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Norfuk has always been a linguistic <a href="/wiki/Cant_(language)" title="Cant (language)">cant</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Laycock_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Laycock-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> As travel to and from Norfolk Island becomes more common, Norfuk is falling into disuse.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Efforts are being made to restore the language to more common usage, such as the education of children, the publication of English–Norfuk dictionaries, the use of the language in signage, and the renaming of some tourist attractions – most notably the <a href="/wiki/Rainforest" title="Rainforest">rainforest</a> walk "<span title="Pitcairn-Norfolk-language text"><i lang="pih-NF">A Trip Ina Stik</i></span>" – to their Norfuk equivalents.  In 2007, the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> added Norfuk to its list of <a href="/wiki/Endangered_language" title="Endangered language">endangered languages</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norfuk_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the 1970s, the Norfolk community and specialists from mainland Australia noted that the Norfuk language was falling into decline, prompting discussions about how to implement Norfolk into the school system.<sup id="cite_ref-Mühlhäusler_2007_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mühlhäusler_2007-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> At this point in time, Norfuk did not have a standardized writing system, as it was mostly an oral language. The Society of the Descendants of Pitcairn Islanders, founded in 1977, was a driving force behind the campaign to include Norfuk language as a teachable subject in schools.<sup id="cite_ref-Mühlhäusler_2007_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mühlhäusler_2007-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/w/index.php?title=Faye_Bataille&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Faye Bataille (page does not exist)">Faye Bataille</a> was one of the first to teach Norfolk classes in public schools, in the 1980s.
</p><p>The first Norfolk dictionary was compiled in 1986 by Beryl Nobbs-Palmer.<sup id="cite_ref-Mühlhäusler_2007_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mühlhäusler_2007-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> It was titled <i>A Dictionary of Norfolk words and usages</i> and contained examples of words in the Norfuk language and how to use them.
</p><p>The book <i>Speak Norfuk Today</i> was written by <a href="/wiki/Alice_Buffett" title="Alice Buffett">Alice Buffett</a> and Dr <a href="/wiki/Donald_Laycock" title="Donald Laycock">Donald Laycock</a>. It is an encyclopedia incorporating a large majority of the information about the Norfuk language and was one of the first instances in which the orthography of Norfuk was documented.<sup id="cite_ref-Mühlhäusler_2007_10-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mühlhäusler_2007-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Norfuk became a language of Norfolk Island in 2004 by virtue of the Norfolk Island Language (Norf'k) Act 2004 passed by the island's legislative assembly.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Norfolk_Island_Language_(Norf’k)_Act_2004_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Norfolk_Island_Language_(Norf’k)_Act_2004-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2018, <a href="/wiki/Eve_Semple" title="Eve Semple">Eve Semple</a> and colleagues received a grant from the Australian Research Council, in order to promote and facilitate revival.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relationship_to_Pitkern">Relationship to Pitkern</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norfuk_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Relationship to Pitkern"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Norfuk is descended predominantly from the <a href="/wiki/Pitkern_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Pitkern language">Pitkern</a> (Pitcairnese or Pi'kern) spoken by settlers from the Pitcairn Islands. The relative ease of travel from English-speaking countries such as Australia and New Zealand to Norfolk Island, particularly when compared with that of travel to the Pitcairn Islands, has meant that Norfuk has been exposed to much greater contact with English relative to Pitkern. The difficulties in accessing the Pitcairn population have meant that a serious comparison of the two languages for mutual intelligibility has proven difficult.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">Classification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norfuk_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Classification"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Norfuk has been classified as an <a href="/wiki/English-based_creole_language" class="mw-redirect" title="English-based creole language">Atlantic Creole language</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> despite the island's location in the Pacific Ocean, because of the heavy influence of <a href="/wiki/Ned_Young" class="mw-redirect" title="Ned Young">Ned Young</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Saint_Kitts_Creole" title="Saint Kitts Creole">Saint Kitts Creole</a>-speaker, and his role as a "<a href="/wiki/Linguistic_anthropology#Socialization" title="Linguistic anthropology">linguistic socializer</a>" among the first generation of children born on Pitcairn.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The language is closely related to Pitkern but has no other close relatives other than its parent tongues of English and Tahitian.  It is generally considered that English has had more of an influence upon the language than Tahitian, with words of Tahitian extraction being confined largely to <a href="/wiki/Taboo" title="Taboo">taboo</a> subjects, negative characterisations, and adjectives indicating that something is undesirable.<sup id="cite_ref-emsah.uq.edu.au_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-emsah.uq.edu.au-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Many expressions which are not commonly used in modern English that are spoken in most areas of the world carry on in Pitkern. These expressions include words from British maritime culture in the age of sailing ships. The influence of <a href="/wiki/Seventh-day_Adventist_Church" title="Seventh-day Adventist Church">Seventh-day Adventist Church</a> missionaries and the <a href="/wiki/King_James_Version_of_the_Bible" class="mw-redirect" title="King James Version of the Bible">King James Version of the Bible</a> are also notable.
</p><p>In the mid-19th century, the people of Pitcairn resettled on Norfolk Island; later, some moved back. Most speakers of Pitkern today are the descendants of those who stayed. Pitkern and <a href="/wiki/Norfuk_dialect" class="mw-redirect" title="Norfuk dialect">Norfuk dialects</a> are mutually intelligible, but differ significantly in vocabulary and grammar.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Norfolk language is ordered in subject verb object.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Orthography">Orthography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norfuk_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Orthography"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Norfolk/Pitcairnese_alphabet" title="Norfolk/Pitcairnese alphabet">Norfolk/Pitcairnese alphabet</a></div>
<p>The language is largely a spoken rather than written language,<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> and there is a lack of standardisation.<sup id="cite_ref-emsah.uq.edu.au_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-emsah.uq.edu.au-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> However, a number of attempts have been made at developing an <a href="/wiki/Orthography" title="Orthography">orthography</a> for the language. Early attempts either attempted to enforce English spelling onto the Norfuk words,<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> or used <a href="/wiki/Diacritic" title="Diacritic">diacritical marks</a> to represent sounds distinct to the language.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Alice_Buffett" title="Alice Buffett">Alice Buffett</a>, a Norfolk Island <a href="/wiki/Norfolk_Legislative_Assembly" class="mw-redirect" title="Norfolk Legislative Assembly">parliamentarian</a> and Australian-trained linguist, developed a codified grammar and <a href="/wiki/Orthography" title="Orthography">orthography</a> for the language in the 1980s, assisted by Dr <a href="/wiki/Donald_Laycock" title="Donald Laycock">Donald Laycock</a>, an <a href="/wiki/Australian_National_University" title="Australian National University">Australian National University</a> academic. Their book, <i>Speak Norfuk Today,</i> was published in 1988. This orthography has won the endorsement of the Norfolk Island government, and its use is becoming prevalent.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Vocabulary">Vocabulary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norfuk_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Vocabulary"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The language itself does not have words to express some concepts, particularly those having to do with science and technology. Some Islanders believe that the only solution is to create a committee charged with creating <a href="/wiki/Neologism" title="Neologism">new words</a> in Norfuk rather than simply adopting English words for new technological advances. For example, Norfuk recently adopted the word <span title="Pitcairn-Norfolk-language text"><i lang="pih-NF">kompyuuta</i></span>, a Norfuk-ised version of <i>computer</i>. Processes similar to this exist in relation to other languages around the world, such as the <a href="/wiki/M%C4%81ori_language" title="Māori language">Māori language</a> in New Zealand and the <a href="/wiki/Faroese_language" title="Faroese language">Faroese</a> and <a href="/wiki/Icelandic_language" title="Icelandic language">Icelandic</a> languages. Some languages already have official bodies, such as New Zealand's <a href="/wiki/M%C4%81ori_Language_Commission" title="Māori Language Commission">Māori Language Commission</a> or France's <a href="/wiki/Acad%C3%A9mie_fran%C3%A7aise" class="mw-redirect" title="Académie française">Académie française</a>, for creating new words.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Norfuk vocabulary has been heavily influenced by the history of Norfolk Island. Many words were created for specific animals or plants on the island and the way in which these things are named is unique to the Island of Norfolk.<sup id="cite_ref-Nash_&amp;_Mühlhäusler_2014_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nash_&amp;_Mühlhäusler_2014-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> For example, many fish that are indigenous to the island were named either by the people who caught them or by whoever received them after dividing the catch.<sup id="cite_ref-Nash_&amp;_Mühlhäusler_2014_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nash_&amp;_Mühlhäusler_2014-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> One such instance is the naming of the fish <span title="Pitcairn-Norfolk-language text"><i lang="pih-NF">Sandford</i></span> which received its name by a man named Sandford Warren after receiving the fish as his share.<sup id="cite_ref-Nash_&amp;_Mühlhäusler_2014_23-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nash_&amp;_Mühlhäusler_2014-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
