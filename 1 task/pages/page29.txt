<p><b>Finnish Sign Language</b> (<a href="/wiki/Finnish_language" title="Finnish language">Finnish</a>: <i lang="fi">suomalainen viittomakieli</i>) is the <a href="/wiki/Sign_language" title="Sign language">sign language</a> most commonly used in <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a>. There are 3,000 <i>(2012 estimate)</i> Finnish <a href="/wiki/Deaf" class="mw-redirect" title="Deaf">deaf</a> who have Finnish Sign Language as a first language. As the Finnish system records users by their <a href="/wiki/Written_language" title="Written language">written language</a>, not their <a href="/wiki/Spoken_language" title="Spoken language">spoken</a> alone, nearly all deaf people who sign are assigned this way and may be subsumed into the overall Finnish language figures. Historically the aim was <a href="/wiki/Oralism" title="Oralism">oralism</a>, whereby deaf people were taught to speak oral Finnish, even if they could not hear it; thus older people are recorded under these figures. In 2014, only 500 people registered Finnish Sign Language as their first language. There are several sign languages that come under this label; FSL for those that can see; Signed Finnish, which does not follow the same <a href="/wiki/Grammar" title="Grammar">grammatical rules</a>, and a version for those who are <a href="/wiki/Visual_impairment" title="Visual impairment">blind</a> and deaf. Thus, there are around 8,000 people that use a Finnish Sign Language linguistically. Many estimates say 5,000, but these are exaggerations derived from the 14,000 deaf people in Finland (many of whom do not speak Finnish Sign Language). Finnish Sign Language is derived from <a href="/wiki/Swedish_Sign_Language" title="Swedish Sign Language">Swedish Sign Language</a>, which is a different language from Finnish Swedish Sign Language (which is <a href="/wiki/Finland_Swedish" title="Finland Swedish">Swedish Finnish language</a> derived from Finnish Sign Language, of which there are an estimated 90 speakers in Finland), from which it began to separate as an independent language in the middle of the 19th century.
</p><p>Finnish legislation <a href="/wiki/Recognition_of_sign_languages#Finland" class="mw-redirect" title="Recognition of sign languages">recognized Finnish Sign Language</a> as one of Finland's domestic languages in 1995 when it was included in the renewed <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_Finland" title="Constitution of Finland">constitution</a>. Finland then became the third country in the world to recognize a sign language as a <a href="/wiki/Natural_language" title="Natural language">natural language</a> and the right to use it as a <a href="/wiki/Mother_tongue" class="mw-redirect" title="Mother tongue">mother tongue</a>.
</p><p>Courses in "sign language" have been taught in Finland since the 1960s. At that time, instruction taught signs but followed Finnish <a href="/wiki/Word_order" title="Word order">word order</a> (see <a href="/wiki/Manually_Coded_Language" class="mw-redirect" title="Manually Coded Language">Manually Coded Language</a>). Later, as research on sign languages in general and Finnish Sign Language in particular determined that sign languages tend to have a very different grammar from oral languages, the teaching of Finnish Sign Language and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Signed_Finnish&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Signed Finnish (page does not exist)">Signed Finnish</a> diverged.

Natural sign languages are not related to the spoken language used in the same region. For example, French Sign Language originated in France, but is not related to French. Conversely, <a href="/wiki/American_Sign_Language" title="American Sign Language">ASL</a> and <a href="/wiki/British_Sign_Language" title="British Sign Language">BSL</a> both originated in English-speaking countries but are not related to each other; ASL however is related to <a href="/wiki/French_Sign_Language" title="French Sign Language">French Sign Language</a>.
