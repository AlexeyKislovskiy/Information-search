<p><b>Tol</b> (<i>Tolpan</i>), also known as <b>Eastern Jicaque</b>, <b>Tolupan</b>, and <b>Torupan</b>, is spoken by approximately 500 <a href="/wiki/Tolupan_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Tolupan people">Tolupan people</a> in <a href="/w/index.php?title=La_Monta%C3%B1a_de_la_Flor&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="La Montaña de la Flor (page does not exist)">La Montaña de la Flor</a> reservation in <a href="/wiki/Francisco_Moraz%C3%A1n_Department" title="Francisco Morazán Department">Francisco Morazán Department</a>, <a href="/wiki/Honduras" title="Honduras">Honduras</a>.
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Name">Name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tol_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Name"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Tol speakers refer to themselves as the Tolpán, but are called Jicaques or Turrupanes by <a href="/wiki/Ladino_people" title="Ladino people">ladinos</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Former_extent">Former extent</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tol_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Former extent"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Tol was also spoken in much of <a href="/wiki/Yoro_Department" title="Yoro Department">Yoro Department</a>, but only a few speakers were reported in the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Yoro_Valley&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Yoro Valley (page does not exist)">Yoro Valley</a> in 1974. 
</p><p>Tol used to be spoken from the <a href="/wiki/R%C3%ADo_Ul%C3%BAa" class="mw-redirect" title="Río Ulúa">Río Ulúa</a> in the west, to modern-day <a href="/wiki/Trujillo,_Honduras" title="Trujillo, Honduras">Trujillo</a> in the east, and to the <a href="/wiki/R%C3%ADo_Sulaco" class="mw-redirect" title="Río Sulaco">Río Sulaco</a> in the inland south. This area included the areas around modern-day <a href="/wiki/El_Progreso" title="El Progreso">El Progreso</a>, <a href="/wiki/La_Ceiba" title="La Ceiba">La Ceiba</a>, and possibly also <a href="/wiki/San_Pedro_Sula" title="San Pedro Sula">San Pedro Sula</a>. Most Tolupan had fled the Spanish from coastal regions by the early 1800s. The Tol speakers at La Montaña de la Flor fled the Yoro Valley in 1865 to avoid being conscripted into forced labor by the local governor (Campbell &amp; Oltrogge 1980:206, Hagen 1943, Chapman 1978).
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Grammar">Grammar</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tol_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Grammar"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The following overview is based on Haurholm-Larsen (2014).<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Constituent_order">Constituent order</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tol_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Constituent order"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The basic constituent order of Tol is SOV and the language displays a consistently head final order of constituents, i.e. verbs follow the subject and the object, there are <a href="/wiki/Postposition" class="mw-redirect" title="Postposition">postpositions</a> instead of prepositions, and subordinating conjunctions appear at the end of subordinate clauses.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Inflection">Inflection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tol_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Inflection"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Verbs and nouns are inflected for person, number and, in the case of verbs, tense, using a number of different morpho-syntactic means which often conflate various meanings (polyexponentiality). These means include, prefixing, suffixing and infixing, ablaut and stress shift and the use of independent pronouns. Tense is also expressed by the use of particles. Number is only marked in noun phrases with animate referents. Some examples are given below.
</p>
<dl><dd><i>m-wayúm</i> 'my husband'<br /><i>w-y-ayúm</i> 'your husband'<br /><i>woyúm</i> 'her husband'<br /><i>kʰis wayúm</i> 'our husband'<br /><i>his wayúm</i> 'your husband'<br /><i>his wayúm</i> 'their husband'</dd></dl>
<dl><dd><i>napʰ üsü müˀüs</i> 'I am drinking water'<br /><i>hipʰ üsü müs</i> 'you are drinking water'<br /><i>hupʰ üsü mü</i> 'he is drinking water'<br /><i>kupʰ üsü miskʰékʰ</i> 'we are drinking water'<br /><i>nun üsü müskʰé</i> 'you are drinking water'<br /><i>yupʰ üsü miˀün</i> 'they are drinking water'</dd></dl>
<p>Most nouns take one of three suffixes: <i>-(sV)s</i>, <i>-(V)N</i>, <i>-(V)kʰ</i>.
</p><p>Examples:
</p>
<dl><dd><i>wo-sís</i> 'house' (root: <i>wa</i>)<br /><i>sitʰ-ím</i> 'avocado' (root: <i>sitʰ</i>)<br /><i>kʰon-íkʰ</i> 'bed' (root: <i>kʰan</i>)</dd></dl>
<p>Nouns that never take suffixes refer to body parts and kinship terms.
</p>
