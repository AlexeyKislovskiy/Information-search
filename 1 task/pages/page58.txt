<p><b>Kota</b> is a language of the <a href="/wiki/Dravidian_languages" title="Dravidian languages">Dravidian languages</a> with about 900 native speakers in the <a href="/wiki/Nilgiri_hills" class="mw-redirect" title="Nilgiri hills">Nilgiri hills</a> of <a href="/wiki/Tamil_Nadu" title="Tamil Nadu">Tamil Nadu</a> state, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>. It is spoken mainly by the tribal <a href="/wiki/Kota_people_(India)" title="Kota people (India)">Kota people (India)</a>. In the late 1800s, the native speaking population was about 1,100.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> In 1990, the population was only 930, out of an ethnic population of perhaps 1,400, despite the great increase in the population of the area.<sup id="cite_ref-e18_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-e18-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The language is 'critically endangered' due to the greater social status of neighbouring languages.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The Kota language may have originated from <a href="/wiki/Tamil-Kannada" class="mw-redirect" title="Tamil-Kannada">Tamil-Kannada</a> and is closely related to <a href="/wiki/Toda_language" title="Toda language">Toda language</a>. The Kota population is about 2500. The origin of the name Kota is derived from the Dravidian root word 'Ko' meaning Mountain.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
