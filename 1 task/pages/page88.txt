<p><b>Gagauz</b> (<span title="Gagauz-language text"><i lang="gag">gagauz dili</i></span> or <span title="Gagauz-language text"><i lang="gag">gagauzça</i></span>) is a <a href="/wiki/Turkic_languages" title="Turkic languages">Turkic language</a> spoken by the <a href="/wiki/Gagauz_people" title="Gagauz people">Gagauz people</a> of <a href="/wiki/Moldova" title="Moldova">Moldova</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">Ukraine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a> and it is an official language of the <a href="/wiki/Gagauzia" title="Gagauzia">Autonomous Region of Gagauzia</a> in Moldova. Gagauz belongs to the <a href="/wiki/Oghuz_languages" title="Oghuz languages">Oghuz</a> branch of Turkic languages, alongside <a href="/wiki/Azerbaijani_language" title="Azerbaijani language">Azerbaijani</a>, <a href="/wiki/Turkmen_language" title="Turkmen language">Turkmen</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Turkish_language" title="Turkish language">Turkish</a>. Gagauz is a distinct language from <a href="/wiki/Balkan_Gagauz_Turkish" class="mw-redirect" title="Balkan Gagauz Turkish">Balkan Gagauz Turkish</a> to some degree.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Though it was established as a written language in 1957, Gagauz was not used in schools until 1959.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Gagauz is a language derived from Balkan Gagauz Turkish; Balkan linguistics was the first to view the consequences of language contact as normal rather than corrupt.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The term "Gagauz language" and the identification of one's language as "Gagauz" were established concurrently with or even after the creation of national self-awareness.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> About 150,000 Gagauz resided in Moldova in 1986, where they lived in settlements within the <a href="/wiki/Comrat" title="Comrat">Comrat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cead%C3%AEr-Lunga" title="Ceadîr-Lunga">Ceadîr-Lunga</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vulc%C4%83ne%C8%99ti" title="Vulcănești">Vulcănești</a> Rayons.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Along with the majority of the Gagauz living in Moldova, there are four other cities in Bulgaria in which the Gagauz reside.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gagauz_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Between 1750<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2021)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> and 1846, ancestors of the Gagauz today emigrated from the current-day Bulgarian Black Sea coast north of Varna to Russia and settled in the region that is now the current-day Republic of Moldova, allowed to do so on the condition that they converted to Orthodox Christianity by Empress Catherine.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> In the aftermath of the dissolution of the Soviet Union, the 1994 law on Special Legal Status of Gagauzia was passed in Moldova, which was put into effect in 1995, granting the Gagauz territorial autonomy.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Orthography">Orthography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gagauz_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Orthography"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Gagauz_alphabet" title="Gagauz alphabet">Gagauz alphabet</a></div>
<p>It appears that the first alphabet to be used for the language was the <a href="/wiki/Greek_alphabet" title="Greek alphabet">Greek alphabet</a><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> in the late 19th century. For example, orientalist <a href="/w/index.php?title=Otto_Blau&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Otto Blau (page does not exist)">Otto Blau</a> claims that plays of <a href="/wiki/Euripides" title="Euripides">Euripides</a> had been translated into the Gagauz language and had been written with Greek letters.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Beginning in 1957, <a href="/wiki/Cyrillic_script" title="Cyrillic script">Cyrillic</a> was used until 1993. On  May 13, 1993, the parliament of the Republic of Moldova passed a decision providing for the official adoption of the <a href="/wiki/Latin_script" title="Latin script">Latin-based alphabet</a> for the Gagauz language.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> This was subsequently amended in 1996.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> The Gagauz alphabet adopted is modelled on the modern <a href="/wiki/Turkish_alphabet" title="Turkish alphabet">Turkish alphabet</a>, with the addition of three letters: <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;ä&#x27e9;</span> to represent the sound of <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[æ]</span> (as <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;ə&#x27e9;</span> in <a href="/wiki/Azerbaijani_alphabet" title="Azerbaijani alphabet">Azeri</a>), <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;ê&#x27e9;</span> to represent the <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ə]</span> (schwa) sound, which does not exist in Turkish, and <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;ţ&#x27e9;</span> to represent the sound <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ts]</span> from the Romanian alphabet. On the other hand, unlike Crimean Tatar, Turkish, and some other Turkic languages, Gagauz does not have the letter <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;ğ&#x27e9;</span>, which had become completely silent in the Gagauz language.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/I-dot" class="mw-redirect" title="I-dot">Dotted</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dotless_I" title="Dotless I">dotless I</a> are separate letters, each with its own uppercase and lowercase form. <i>I</i> is the capital form of <i>ı</i>, and <i>İ</i> is the capital form of <i>i</i>. The Gagauz alphabet has no q, w or x. Instead, those characters are transliterated into Gagauz as k, v and ks.

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_situation">Current situation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gagauz_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Current situation"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A study in 2012 was conducted on the Gagauz community to assess the current situation and sociocultural context. The findings show that within Gagauzia, official documents, printed publications, and official web sites are only in Russian. The National Passport System in Moldova does not allow the spelling of names in Gagauz. Signposts in Gagauzia are mostly in Romanian, and the names of squares and streets have not changed since the time of the Soviet Union.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gagauz_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Education"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Despite various laws that support the rights of citizens to education in their native language, almost all instruction in Gagauzian schools is in Russian. Gagauz, while the native language of all students, is only taught as a "native language" class for a few hours per week.<sup id="cite_ref-Dağdeviren_Kırmızı_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dağdeviren_Kırmızı-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Research has also shown that there are not serious desires or attempts to institute Gagauz as a language of instruction. In a study, 80.6% of respondents preferred Russian as the medium of instruction at schools.<sup id="cite_ref-Dağdeviren_Kırmızı_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dağdeviren_Kırmızı-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> There are, however, some notable efforts to increase Gagauz language education.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Todur_Zanet" title="Todur Zanet">Todur Zanet</a>, editor-in-chief of the <i><a href="/wiki/Ana_S%C3%B6z%C3%BC" title="Ana Sözü">Ana Sözü</a></i> local newspaper, has played an active role in encouraging readers and local authorities to promote instruction in their mother tongue. Zanet has also contributed significantly to efforts to standardize the language and increase its accessibility through print and other mediums.
</p>

<p><i>Ana Sözü</i> is the largest local newspaper in Gagauzia. It is also the only local newspaper still written entirely in Gagauz, and was the first newspaper of any kind published in the Gagauz language. Apart from <i>Ana Sözü</i>, there are various newspapers published in the Autonomous Territorial Unit of Gagauzia, including <i>Açık Göz</i>, <i>Gagauz Yeri</i>, <i>Gagauz Sesi</i>, <i>Halk Birlii</i>, <i>Novıy Vzgliad</i>, <i>Vesti Gagauzii</i>, and <i>Znamea</i>.
</p><p>In addition to printed materials, the company Gagauz Radio Televisionu (GRT) produces radio and television broadcasts in Gagauz.
