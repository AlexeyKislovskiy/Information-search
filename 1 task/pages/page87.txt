in &#39;dye&#39;">d</span><span title="/aɪ/: &#39;i&#39; in &#39;tide&#39;">aɪ</span></span>/</a></span></span> <a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key"><i title="English pronunciation respelling"><span style="font-size:90%">KRAH</span>-dy</i></a>, also known as <b>Tai–Kadai</b> <span class="rt-commentedText nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt" lang="en-fonipa"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="&#39;t&#39; All languages in the family are <a href="/wiki/Tonal_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Tonal language">tonal</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Thai_language" title="Thai language">Thai</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lao_language" title="Lao language">Lao</a>, the national languages of <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Thailand</a> and <a href="/wiki/Laos" title="Laos">Laos</a>, respectively.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Around 93 million people speak Kra–Dai languages; 60% of those speak Thai.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Ethnologue" title="Ethnologue">Ethnologue</a></i> lists 95 languages in the family, with 62 of these being in the <a href="/wiki/Tai_languages" title="Tai languages">Tai branch</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Names">Names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kra%E2%80%93Dai_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Names"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The name "Kra–Dai" was proposed by Weera Ostapirat (2000), as Kra and Dai are the reconstructed <a href="/wiki/Exonym_and_endonym" class="mw-redirect" title="Exonym and endonym">autonyms</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Kra_languages" title="Kra languages">Kra</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tai_languages" title="Tai languages">Tai</a> branches, respectively.<sup id="cite_ref-Ostapirat2000_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ostapirat2000-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> "Kra–Dai" has since been used by the majority of specialists working on Southeast Asian linguistics, including Norquest (2007),<sup id="cite_ref-norquest_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-norquest-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Pittayaporn (2009),<sup id="cite_ref-pittayaporn_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pittayaporn-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-jenks_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jenks-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/William_H._Baxter" title="William H. Baxter">Baxter</a> &amp; <a href="/wiki/Laurent_Sagart" title="Laurent Sagart">Sagart</a> (2014),<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> and Enfield &amp; <a href="/wiki/Bernard_Comrie" title="Bernard Comrie">Comrie</a> (2015).<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The name "Tai–Kadai" is used in many references, as well as <i><a href="/wiki/Ethnologue" title="Ethnologue">Ethnologue</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Glottolog" title="Glottolog">Glottolog</a></i>, but Ostapirat (2000) and others suggest that it is problematic and confusing, preferring the name "Kra–Dai" instead.<sup id="cite_ref-Ostapirat2000_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ostapirat2000-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> "Tai–Kadai" comes from an obsolete bifurcation of the family into two branches, Tai and Kadai, which had first been proposed by <a href="/wiki/Paul_K._Benedict" title="Paul K. Benedict">Paul K. Benedict</a> (1942).<sup id="cite_ref-Benedict1942_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Benedict1942-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> In 1942, Benedict placed three Kra languages (<a href="/wiki/Gelao_languages" title="Gelao languages">Gelao</a>, Laqua (<a href="/wiki/Qabiao_language" title="Qabiao language">Qabiao</a>), and <a href="/wiki/Lachi_language" title="Lachi language">Lachi</a>) together with <a href="/wiki/Hlai_languages" title="Hlai languages">Hlai</a> in a group that he called "Kadai", from <i>ka</i>, meaning "person" in Gelao and Laqua and <i>dai</i>, a form of a Hlai autonym.<sup id="cite_ref-Benedict1942_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Benedict1942-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Benedict's (1942) "Kadai" group was based on his observation that Kra and Hlai languages have <a href="/wiki/Austronesian_languages" title="Austronesian languages">Austronesian</a>-like numerals. However, this classification is now universally rejected as obsolete after Ostapirat (2000) demonstrated the coherence of the Kra branch, which does not subgroup with the Hlai branch as Benedict (1942) had proposed. "Kadai" is sometimes used to refer to the entire Kra–Dai family, including by Solnit (1988).<sup id="cite_ref-Solnit1988_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Solnit1988-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-edmondson1988_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-edmondson1988-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Adding to the confusion, some other references<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Avoid_weasel_words" class="mw-redirect" title="Wikipedia:Avoid weasel words"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too vague attribution or weasel words. (February 2024)">which?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> restrict the usage of "Kadai" to only the Kra branch of the family.
</p><p>The name "Daic" is used by <a href="/wiki/Roger_Blench" title="Roger Blench">Roger Blench</a> (2008).<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin">Origin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kra%E2%80%93Dai_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Origin"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<figure typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Gerner_Tai-Kadai_migration_route.png" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Gerner_Tai-Kadai_migration_route.png/300px-Gerner_Tai-Kadai_migration_route.png" decoding="async" width="300" height="238" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Gerner_Tai-Kadai_migration_route.png/450px-Gerner_Tai-Kadai_migration_route.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Gerner_Tai-Kadai_migration_route.png/600px-Gerner_Tai-Kadai_migration_route.png 2x" data-file-width="1000" data-file-height="792" /></a><figcaption>Tai–Kadai migration route, according to Matthias Gerner's <i>Northeast to Southwest Hypothesis</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup></figcaption></figure>
<p>James R. Chamberlain (2016) proposes that the Tai–Kadai (Kra–Dai) language family was formed as early as the 12th century BCE in the middle of the <a href="/wiki/Yangtze_basin" class="mw-redirect" title="Yangtze basin">Yangtze basin</a>, coinciding roughly with the establishment of the <a href="/wiki/Chu_(state)" title="Chu (state)">Chu fiefdom</a> and the beginning of the <a href="/wiki/Zhou_dynasty" title="Zhou dynasty">Zhou dynasty</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Chamberlain-Kra-Dai_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chamberlain-Kra-Dai-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> The high diversity of Kra–Dai languages in southern China, especially in <a href="/wiki/Guizhou" title="Guizhou">Guizhou</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hainan" title="Hainan">Hainan</a>, points to that being an origin of the Kra–Dai language family, founding the nations that later became Thailand and Laos in what had been <a href="/wiki/Austroasiatic_languages" title="Austroasiatic languages">Austroasiatic</a> territory. Genetic and linguistic analyses show great homogeneity among Kra–Dai-speaking people in Thailand.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Although the position of Kra–Dai in relation to Austronesian is still contested, some propose that Kra–Dai and Austronesian are genetically connected. Weera Ostapirat (2005) sets out a series of regular sound correspondences between them, assuming a model of a primary split between the two; they would then be co-ordinate branches.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBlench201711_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBlench201711-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Ostapirat (2013) continues to maintain that Kra–Dai and Austronesian are sister language families, based on certain phonological correspondences.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEOstapirat20131–10_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEOstapirat20131–10-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> On the other hand, Laurent Sagart (2008) proposes that Kra–Dai is a later form of "FATK",<sup id="cite_ref-FATK_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FATK-19">&#91;a&#93;</a></sup> a branch of Austronesian belonging to the subgroup "Puluqic", developed in Taiwan, whose speakers migrated back to the mainland, to Guangdong, Hainan, and north Vietnam, around the second half of the 3rd millennium BCE.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESagart2008146–152_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESagart2008146–152-20">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Upon their arrival in this region, they underwent linguistic contact with an unknown population, resulting in a partial relexification of FATK vocabulary.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESagart2008151_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESagart2008151-21">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> If Sagart's hypothesis that Kra–Dai is a subgroup of Austronesian migrated out of Taiwan and back to the coastal regions of Guangdong, Guangxi, Hainan, and (possibly) Vietnam is right, they would simply not have had a development resembling anything like the fate of other Austronesian languages that migrated out of Taiwan to the Philippines and other islands in Southeast Asia.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBrindley201551_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBrindley201551-22">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Besides various concrete pieces of evidence for a Kra–Dai existence in present-day Guangdong, remnants of Kra–Dai languages spoken further north can be found in unearthed <a href="/wiki/Chinese_bronze_inscriptions" title="Chinese bronze inscriptions">inscriptional materials</a> and non-Han <a href="/wiki/Stratum_(linguistics)#Substratum" title="Stratum (linguistics)">substrata</a> in <a href="/wiki/Min_Chinese" title="Min Chinese">Min</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wu_Chinese" title="Wu Chinese">Wu Chinese</a>.
</p><p>Wolfgang Behr (2002, 2006, 2009, 2017)<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBehr2002_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBehr2002-23">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBehr2006_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBehr2006-24">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBehr2009_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBehr2009-25">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> points out that most non-Sinitic words found in Chu inscriptional materials are of Kra–Dai origin. For example, the Chu graph for 'one, once' written as <span typeof="mw:File"><a href="/wiki/File:Neng2.svg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Neng2.svg/20px-Neng2.svg.png" decoding="async" width="20" height="16" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Neng2.svg/30px-Neng2.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Neng2.svg/40px-Neng2.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="999" data-file-height="775" /></a></span> (? &lt; <a href="/wiki/Old_Chinese" title="Old Chinese">OC</a> <b>*nnəŋ</b>) in the <i>E jun qijie</i> 鄂君啟筯 bronze tally and in Warring States bamboo inscriptions, which represents a Kra–Dai areal word; compare Proto-Tai <b>*hnïŋ</b> <i>=</i> <b>*hnɯŋ</b> (Siamese <sup>22</sup>nɯŋ, Dai <sup>33</sup>nɯŋ, Longzhou nəəŋ<sup>A</sup> etc.) 'one, once'.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBehr201712_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBehr201712-26">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the early 1980s, Wei Qingwen (韦庆稳), a <a href="/wiki/Zhuang_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Zhuang language">Zhuang</a> linguist, proposed that the <a href="/wiki/Old_Yue_language" title="Old Yue language">Old Yue language</a> recorded in the <a href="/wiki/Song_of_the_Yue_Boatman" title="Song of the Yue Boatman">Song of the Yue Boatman</a> is in fact a language ancestral to Zhuang.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHolm2013785_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHolm2013785-27">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Wei used reconstructed <a href="/wiki/Old_Chinese" title="Old Chinese">Old Chinese</a> for the characters and discovered that the resulting vocabulary showed strong resemblance to modern Zhuang.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEdmondson200716_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEdmondson200716-28">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> Later, <a href="/wiki/Zhengzhang_Shangfang" title="Zhengzhang Shangfang">Zhengzhang Shangfang</a> (1991) followed Wei's proposal but used Thai script for comparison, since this orthography dates from the 13th century and preserves archaisms not found in modern pronunciation.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEdmondson200716_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEdmondson200716-28">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEZhengzhang1991159–168_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEZhengzhang1991159–168-29">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> Zhengzhang notes that 'evening, night, dark' bears the C tone in Wuming Zhuang <i>xam<sup>C2</sup></i> and <i>ɣam<sup>C2</sup></i> 'night'. The item <i>raa</i> normally means 'we (inclusive)' but in some places, e.g., Tai Lue and White Tai, it means 'I'.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEdmondson200717_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEdmondson200717-30">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> However, <a href="/wiki/Laurent_Sagart" title="Laurent Sagart">Laurent Sagart</a> criticizes Zhengzhang's interpretation as anachronistic, because however archaic the Thai script is, the Thai language was only written 2,000 years after the song had been recorded; even if <a href="/wiki/Kam%E2%80%93Tai_languages" title="Kam–Tai languages">Proto-Kam–Tai</a> had emerged by the 6th century BCE, its pronunciation would have been substantially different from Thai.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESagart2008143_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESagart2008143-31">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<figure class="mw-halign-right" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Chinese_plain_5c._BC_with_Yue-en.png" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/Chinese_plain_5c._BC_with_Yue-en.png/350px-Chinese_plain_5c._BC_with_Yue-en.png" decoding="async" width="350" height="237" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/Chinese_plain_5c._BC_with_Yue-en.png/525px-Chinese_plain_5c._BC_with_Yue-en.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/Chinese_plain_5c._BC_with_Yue-en.png/700px-Chinese_plain_5c._BC_with_Yue-en.png 2x" data-file-width="1280" data-file-height="865" /></a><figcaption>Map of the Chinese plain at the start of the <a href="/wiki/Warring_States_Period" class="mw-redirect" title="Warring States Period">Warring States Period</a>, in the 5th century BC.</figcaption></figure>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Internal_classification">Internal classification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kra%E2%80%93Dai_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Internal classification"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<figure typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:%22tooth%22_in_Kra-Dai_languages.svg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/%22tooth%22_in_Kra-Dai_languages.svg/300px-%22tooth%22_in_Kra-Dai_languages.svg.png" decoding="async" width="300" height="261" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/%22tooth%22_in_Kra-Dai_languages.svg/450px-%22tooth%22_in_Kra-Dai_languages.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/%22tooth%22_in_Kra-Dai_languages.svg/600px-%22tooth%22_in_Kra-Dai_languages.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="1866" data-file-height="1621" /></a><figcaption>Example of the divergence among the Kra-Dai languages, using the word for "tooth".</figcaption></figure>
<p>Kra–Dai consists of at least five well-established branches, namely Kra, Kam–Sui, Tai, Be, and Hlai (Ostapirat 2005:109).
</p>

<p>Chinese linguists have also proposed a <a href="/wiki/Kam%E2%80%93Tai_languages" title="Kam–Tai languages">Kam–Tai</a> group that includes <a href="/wiki/Kam%E2%80%93Sui_languages" title="Kam–Sui languages">Kam–Sui</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tai_languages" title="Tai languages">Tai</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Be_languages" title="Be languages">Be</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-32">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Kra–Dai languages that are not securely classified and may constitute independent Kra–Dai branches include the following:
</p>
<ul><li><b><a href="/wiki/Lakkia_language" title="Lakkia language">Lakkia</a></b> and <b><a href="/wiki/Biao_language" title="Biao language">Biao</a></b>, which may or may not subgroup with each other, are difficult to classify due to aberrant vocabulary but are sometimes classified as <a href="/wiki/Sister_group" title="Sister group">sisters</a> of <a href="/wiki/Kam%E2%80%93Sui_languages" title="Kam–Sui languages">Kam–Sui</a> (Solnit 1988).<sup id="cite_ref-Solnit1988_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Solnit1988-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Jiamao_language" title="Jiamao language">Jiamao</a></b> of southern Hainan, China, is an aberrant Kra–Dai language traditionally classified as a <a href="/wiki/Hlai_languages" title="Hlai languages">Hlai language</a>, although Jiamao contains many words of non-Hlai origin.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Jizhao_language" title="Jizhao language">Jizhao</a></b> of <a href="/wiki/Guangdong" title="Guangdong">Guangdong</a>, China, is currently unclassified within Kra–Dai but appears to be most closely related to <a href="/wiki/Be_languages" title="Be languages">Be</a> (Ostapirat 1998).<sup id="cite_ref-Ostapirat1998_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ostapirat1998-34">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>Kra–Dai languages of mixed origins are:
</p>
<ul><li><i><a href="/wiki/Hezhang_Buyi_language" title="Hezhang Buyi language">Hezhang Buyi</a></i>: <a href="/wiki/Northern_Tai_languages" title="Northern Tai languages">Northern Tai</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kra_languages" title="Kra languages">Kra</a></li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/E_language" title="E language">E</a></i>: <a href="/wiki/Northern_Tai_languages" title="Northern Tai languages">Northern Tai</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pinghua" title="Pinghua">Pinghua</a> Chinese</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Caolan_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Caolan language">Caolan</a></i>: <a href="/wiki/Northern_Tai_languages" title="Northern Tai languages">Northern Tai</a> and <a href="/wiki/Central_Tai_languages" title="Central Tai languages">Central Tai</a></li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Sanqiao_language" title="Sanqiao language">Sanqiao</a></i>: <a href="/wiki/Kam%E2%80%93Sui_languages" title="Kam–Sui languages">Kam–Sui</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hmongic_languages" title="Hmongic languages">Hmongic</a>, and Chinese</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Jiamao_language" title="Jiamao language">Jiamao</a></i>: <a href="/wiki/Hlai_languages" title="Hlai languages">Hlai</a> and other unknown elements (<a href="/wiki/Austroasiatic_languages" title="Austroasiatic languages">Austroasiatic</a>?)</li></ul>

<h3><span id="Ostapirat_.282005.29.3B_Norquest_.282007.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Ostapirat_(2005);_Norquest_(2007)">Ostapirat (2005); Norquest (2007)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kra%E2%80%93Dai_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Ostapirat (2005); Norquest (2007)"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Weera Ostapirat (2005:128) suggests the possibility of Kra and Kam–Sui being grouped together as Northern Kra–Dai and Hlai with Tai as Southern Kra–Dai.<sup id="cite_ref-Ostapirat2005_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ostapirat2005-38">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Peter_K._Norquest" title="Peter K. Norquest">Norquest</a> (2007) has further updated this classification to include <a href="/wiki/Lakkia_language" title="Lakkia language">Lakkia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Be_languages" title="Be languages">Be</a>. Norquest notes that Lakkia shares some similarities with Kam–Sui, while Be shares some similarities with Tai. Norquest (2007:15) notes that Be shares various similarities with <a href="/wiki/Northern_Tai_languages" title="Northern Tai languages">Northern Tai languages</a> in particular.<sup id="cite_ref-norquest_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-norquest-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Following Ostapirat, Norquest adopts the name Kra–Dai for the family as a whole. <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hypotheses_regarding_external_relationships">Hypotheses regarding external relationships</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kra%E2%80%93Dai_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Hypotheses regarding external relationships"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Austro-Tai">Austro-Tai</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kra%E2%80%93Dai_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Austro-Tai"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1033289096">.mw-parser-output .hatnote{font-style:italic}.mw-parser-output div.hatnote{padding-left:1.6em;margin-bottom:0.5em}.mw-parser-output .hatnote i{font-style:normal}.mw-parser-output .hatnote+link+.hatnote{margin-top:-0.5em}</style><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Austro-Tai_languages" title="Austro-Tai languages">Austro-Tai languages</a></div>
<figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Genesis_of_Daic_languages_and_their_relation_with_Austronesians.png" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Genesis_of_Daic_languages_and_their_relation_with_Austronesians.png/220px-Genesis_of_Daic_languages_and_their_relation_with_Austronesians.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="119" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Genesis_of_Daic_languages_and_their_relation_with_Austronesians.png/330px-Genesis_of_Daic_languages_and_their_relation_with_Austronesians.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Genesis_of_Daic_languages_and_their_relation_with_Austronesians.png/440px-Genesis_of_Daic_languages_and_their_relation_with_Austronesians.png 2x" data-file-width="1519" data-file-height="824" /></a><figcaption>Proposed genesis of Daic languages and their relation to <a href="/wiki/Austronesian_languages" title="Austronesian languages">Austronesian languages</a> (<a href="/wiki/Roger_Blench" title="Roger Blench">Blench</a>, 2018)<sup id="cite_ref-Blench2018_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Blench2018-42">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup></figcaption></figure>
<p>Several scholars have presented evidence that Kra–Dai may be related to, or even a branch of, the <a href="/wiki/Austronesian_languages" title="Austronesian languages">Austronesian language family</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> There are a number of possible cognates in the core vocabulary, displaying regular sound correspondences. Among proponents, there is yet no agreement as to whether they are a sister group to Austronesian in a family called <a href="/wiki/Austro-Tai_languages" title="Austro-Tai languages">Austro-Tai</a>, a back-migration from <a href="/wiki/Formosan_languages" title="Formosan languages">Taiwan</a> to the mainland or a later migration from the Philippines to Hainan during the Austronesian expansion.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEOstapirat20131–10_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEOstapirat20131–10-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The inclusion of <a href="/wiki/Japonic_languages" title="Japonic languages">Japanese</a> in the Austro-Tai family, as proposed by <a href="/wiki/Paul_K._Benedict" title="Paul K. Benedict">Paul K. Benedict</a> in the late 20th century,<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> is not supported by the current proponents of the Austro-Tai hypothesis.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sino-Tai">Sino-Tai</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kra%E2%80%93Dai_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Sino-Tai"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Kra–Dai languages were formerly considered to be part of the <a href="/wiki/Sino-Tibetan_languages" title="Sino-Tibetan languages">Sino-Tibetan family</a>, partly because they contain large numbers of words that are similar to Sino-Tibetan languages. However, Western scholars generally consider them to be Sinitic loanwords and note that basic vocabulary words in Kra–Dai languages often have cognates with Austronesian instead.<sup id="cite_ref-Ostapirat2005_38-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ostapirat2005-38">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> Outside China, the Kra–Dai languages are now classified as an independent family. In China, they are called <i>Dong–Tai</i> (侗台) or <i>Zhuang–Dong</i> (壮侗) languages and are generally included, along with the <a href="/wiki/Hmong%E2%80%93Mien_languages" title="Hmong–Mien languages">Hmong–Mien languages</a>, in the Sino-Tibetan family.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Hmong.E2.80.93Mien"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Hmong–Mien">Hmong–Mien</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kra%E2%80%93Dai_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Hmong–Mien"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Kosaka (2002) has argued specifically for a <a href="/wiki/Proto-Hmong%E2%80%93Mien#Kra-Dai" class="mw-redirect" title="Proto-Hmong–Mien">Miao–Dai family</a>. Based on proposed lexical cognates, he proposes a genetic relation between Hmong–Mien and Kra–Dai languages. He further suggests that similarities between Kra–Dai and Austronesian are due to later areal contact in the coastal areas of eastern and southeastern China or an older ancestral relation (Proto-<a href="/wiki/East_Asian_languages" title="East Asian languages">East Asian</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-Kosaka_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kosaka-46">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Japonic">Japonic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kra%E2%80%93Dai_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Japonic"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Alexander_Vovin" title="Alexander Vovin">Vovin</a> (2014) has proposed that the location of the <a href="/wiki/Japonic_languages" title="Japonic languages">Japonic</a> <a href="/wiki/Urheimat" class="mw-redirect" title="Urheimat">Urheimat</a> (linguistic homeland) is in southern China. He argues for typological evidence that Proto-Japanese may have been a monosyllabic, SVO syntax and isolating language, which is also characteristic of Kra–Dai languages. According to him, these common features are however not due to a genetic relationship but rather the result of intense contact.<sup id="cite_ref-Vovin_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vovin-47">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
