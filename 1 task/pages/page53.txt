<p><b>Comanche</b> (<span class="rt-commentedText nowrap"><small>English: </small><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt" lang="en-fonipa"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="&#39;k&#39; in &#39;kind&#39;">k</span><span title="/ə/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;about&#39;">ə</span><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="&#39;m&#39; in &#39;my&#39;">m</span><span title="/æ/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;bad&#39;">æ</span><span title="&#39;n&#39; in &#39;nigh&#39;">n</span><span title="/tʃ/: &#39;ch&#39; in &#39;China&#39;">tʃ</span><span title="/i/: &#39;y&#39; in &#39;happy&#39;">i</span></span>/</a></span></span>, <small><a href="/wiki/Endonym" class="mw-redirect" title="Endonym">endonym</a></small> <span title="Comanche-language text"><i lang="com">Nʉmʉ Tekwapʉ̲</i></span>) is a <a href="/wiki/Uto-Aztecan_languages" title="Uto-Aztecan languages">Uto-Aztecan language</a> spoken by the <a href="/wiki/Comanche_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Comanche people">Comanche people</a>, who split from the <a href="/wiki/Shoshone_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Shoshone people">Shoshone people</a> soon after the Comanche had acquired <a href="/wiki/Horses" class="mw-redirect" title="Horses">horses</a> around 1705. The Comanche language and the <a href="/wiki/Shoshoni_language" title="Shoshoni language">Shoshoni language</a> are therefore quite similar, but certain consonant changes in Comanche have inhibited <a href="/wiki/Mutual_intelligibility" title="Mutual intelligibility">mutual intelligibility</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The name <i>Comanche</i> comes from the <a href="/wiki/Ute_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Ute language">Ute</a> word <span title="Ute-Southern Paiute-language text"><i lang="ute">kɨmantsi</i></span> 'enemy, stranger'.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Their own name for the language is <span title="Comanche-language text"><i lang="com">nʉmʉ tekwap<u>ʉ</u></i></span> which means 'language of the people'.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_and_revitalization_efforts">Use and revitalization efforts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Comanche_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Use and revitalization efforts"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although efforts are now being made to ensure its survival, most speakers of the language are elderly. In the late 19th century, Comanche children were placed in boarding schools where they were discouraged from speaking their native language, and even severely punished for doing so. The second generation then grew up speaking English, because of the belief that it was better for them not to know Comanche.
</p><p>The Comanche language was briefly prominent during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>. A group of seventeen young men referred to as the Comanche <a href="/wiki/Code_talker" title="Code talker">Code Talkers</a> were trained, and used by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army" title="United States Army">U.S. Army</a> to send messages conveying sensitive information in the Comanche language so that it could not be deciphered by the enemy.
</p><p>As of July 2013, there were roughly 25-30 native speakers of the language, according to <i><a href="/wiki/The_Boston_Globe" title="The Boston Globe">The Boston Globe</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-mcdaniels_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mcdaniels-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The Comanche Language and Cultural Preservation Committee  offers dictionaries and language learning materials.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Comanche language courses were available at the now-closed <a href="/wiki/Comanche_Nation_College" title="Comanche Nation College">Comanche Nation College</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The college previously conducted a language recording project, as the language is "mostly oral", and emphasizing instruction for tribal members.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>As of 2022, there were fewer than nine fluent native speakers of Comanche, many of the old speakers having succumbed to old age, health problems or the <a href="/wiki/COVID-19_pandemic" title="COVID-19 pandemic">COVID-19 pandemic</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Duncan_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Duncan-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Phonology">Phonology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Comanche_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Phonology"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vowels">Vowels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Comanche_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Vowels"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Comanche has a typical <a href="/wiki/Numic" class="mw-redirect" title="Numic">Numic</a> <a href="/wiki/Vowel" title="Vowel">vowel</a> inventory of six vowels. In addition, there is the common <a href="/wiki/Diphthong" title="Diphthong">diphthong</a> <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ai/</span>.  Historically, there was a certain amount of free variation between <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ai]</span> and <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[e]</span> (as shown by comparison with Shoshoni cognates), but the variation is no longer so common and most morphemes have become fixed on either <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ai/</span> or <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/e/</span>.<sup id="cite_ref-Charney1993_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Charney1993-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> In the following chart, the basic symbols given are in the <a href="/wiki/International_Phonetic_Alphabet" title="International Phonetic Alphabet">IPA</a>, whereas the equivalent symbols in the conventional orthography are given to the right of them.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. </p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Vowel_length_and_voicing">Vowel length and voicing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Comanche_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Vowel length and voicing"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Comanche distinguishes vowels by length. Vowels can be either long or short. Long vowels are never devoiced and in the orthography they are represented as (aa, ee, ii, oo, uu, ʉʉ). An example of a long vowel is the (ee) in [wakaréʔeː] 'turtle'.<sup id="cite_ref-Charney1993_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Charney1993-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Short vowels can be lengthened when they are stressed.
</p><p>Short vowels can be either voiced or voiceless.  Unstressed short vowels are usually devoiced when /s/ or /h/ follows and optionally when word-final.<sup id="cite_ref-Charney1993_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Charney1993-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Voiceless" class="mw-redirect" title="Voiceless">voiceless</a> vowels are non-phonemic and therefore not represented in this chart. In the conventional orthography, these vowels are marked with an underline: <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;a̱, e̱, i̱, o̱, u̱, ʉ̱&#x27e9;</span>.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Stress">Stress</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Comanche_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Stress"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Comanche stress most commonly falls on the first syllable. Exceptions to this rule, such as in the words <i>Waʔsáasiʔ</i> '<a href="/wiki/Osage_Nation" title="Osage Nation">Osage people</a>', and <i>aná</i> 'ouch!', are marked with an acute accent.
</p><p>For the purpose of stress placement, the <a href="/wiki/Diphthong" title="Diphthong">diphthongs</a> /ai/, /oi/, and /ui/ act as one vowel with one <a href="/wiki/Mora_(linguistics)" title="Mora (linguistics)">mora</a>. Additionally, possessive pronouns, which serve as <a href="/wiki/Clitic" title="Clitic">proclitics</a>, do not affect the stress of a word (so that <i>nʉ + námi</i> 'my sister' retains its stress on the /a/ in <i>námi</i>).
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Secondary_stress" title="Secondary stress">Secondary stress</a> is placed on the second syllable of a two-syllable word, the third syllable of a word with three, four, or five syllables, and the fourth syllable of a word with six syllables.<sup id="cite_ref-Robinson_92_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Robinson_92-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><b>Primary stress</b>: Primary stress is "marked when it is non-initial stress".<sup id="cite_ref-Charney1993_11-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Charney1993-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> In addition, "when a pronoun is suffixed by, for instance a postposition, the pronoun does take primary – and initial – stress."<sup id="cite_ref-Charney1993_11-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Charney1993-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> An example is [nɨpía] nʉ-pia 'my mother' (my-mother).<sup id="cite_ref-Charney1993_11-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Charney1993-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> In the following data where primary stress appears it will be shown as an "acute accent".<sup id="cite_ref-Robinson_92_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Robinson_92-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Primary stress is found in words or compounds of three, six and five syllables. However, when primary stress is marked in a third syllable it can also be consider as a secondary stress according to <a href="/wiki/Elliott_D._Canonge" title="Elliott D. Canonge">Canonge</a>'s but an "exception to this case is when both a proclitic and prefix are used."<sup id="cite_ref-Robinson_92_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Robinson_92-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> An example of a third syllable stress is [há.bi+hu.píː.tu] 'stopped and lay down'.<sup id="cite_ref-Robinson_92_12-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Robinson_92-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Words with "five syllables have primary stress on the first syllable."  An example is [ká.wo+nò.ka.tu] 'stress'.<sup id="cite_ref-Robinson_92_12-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Robinson_92-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Also, words with six syllables have primary stress on the first syllable.  An example is [kú.ʔi.na.kù.ʔe.tu] 'roasts for'.<sup id="cite_ref-Robinson_92_12-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Robinson_92-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<ul><li><b>Non-initial stress</b>: Non-initial stress can be found in any syllable of a word that is not in the initial position and it can also fall on long vowel. Also, the "initial syllable never weakens to the point of voicelessness."<sup id="cite_ref-Charney1993_11-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Charney1993-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> However, some exceptions to the non-initial stress are animal and plant names because some of them end with a stress long vowel plus which is represented by "ʔ".<sup id="cite_ref-Charney1993_11-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Charney1993-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> "Loans are common sources of words with nonitinal stress" an example is [pirísiː] <i>pitísii</i> 'policeman'.<sup id="cite_ref-Charney1993_11-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Charney1993-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> A word with two stresses is [ánikúra] <i>ánikúta</i> 'ant' (analysis unknown).<sup id="cite_ref-Charney1993_11-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Charney1993-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<ul><li><b>Alternating stress</b>: Alternating stress occurs when there are words with three four, five and six syllables. In addition alternating stress is given "when nouns of compound are coequal, a root or stem has one-syllable suffix."<sup id="cite_ref-Robinson_92_12-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Robinson_92-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Also, prefixes or not stem-changes do not receive an indicial stress because the alternating stress "begins on the second syllable as in the following word of six syllables, following the pattern of five-syllable words."<sup id="cite_ref-Robinson_92_12-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Robinson_92-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> e.g. [wu.hká.ʔa.mí.ʔanu] 'went to cut down'.<sup id="cite_ref-Robinson_92_12-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Robinson_92-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Examples of alternating stress are:</li></ul>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Phonological_processes">Phonological processes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Comanche_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Phonological processes"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li><b><a href="/wiki/Free_variation" title="Free variation">Free Variation</a></b>: although not often reflected in the orthography, certain sounds occur in free variation. For instance, <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/j/</span> can be pronounced as a <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[dʒ]</span> (for example: <i>ma yaa</i> <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ma<span class="wrap"> </span>dʒaː]</span>), and a labialized <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/k/</span> can be voiced (as in <i>nʉ gwʉhʉ</i>: it is written <b>gw</b>, even though it is more accurately <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ɡʷ/</span>, just as the labialized <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/k/</span> is written in Comanche as <b>kw</b> rather than <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/kʷ/</span>). In contemporary times, preaspiration and preglottalization may occur in free variation with a long vowel: <i>aakaaʔ</i> / <i>ahkaaʔ</i> ('devil's horn').</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Lenition" title="Lenition">Spirantization</a></b>: spirantization can occur in the phonemes <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/p/</span> and <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/t/</span> when they are preceded by vowels. <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/p/</span> becomes the voiced bilabial fricative, <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[β]</span>, usually written as a <b>b</b>, and <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/t/</span> becomes the voiced alveolar tap, <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ɾ]</span>, written as an <b>r</b>. An intervening <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/h/</span> or <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ʔ/</span> does not block this spirantization process, as seen in <i>tuaʔbaʔa</i> 'on the son' (the sound <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[β]</span> is written here as <b>b</b>, and is <a href="/wiki/Allophone" title="Allophone">allophonic</a> with the Comanche <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/p/</span>). In the past, there was a process of nasalization in Comanche which has since been lost and which blocked spirantization; certain words that would otherwise exhibit spirantization in modern Comanche do not, therefore, as a result of the historical presence of what would have been a preceding nasal (<i>ʉ papi</i> 'your head' would historically have been <i>ʉn papi</i>).</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Metathesis_(linguistics)" title="Metathesis (linguistics)">Metathesis</a></b>: A fairly regular process of metathesis occurs, sporadically with voiceless consonants and regularly with voiced consonants. It is accompanied by the deletion of a vowel: <i>otʉnhʉh</i> &gt; <i>orʉhʉ</i> &gt; <i>ohrʉ</i> ('they', dual). In modern Comanche, voiceless, unaspirated stops followed by a long <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ɨ/</span> (written <b>ʉ</b>) and an <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/h/</span> may be realized as aspirated equivalents at the expense of the subsequent long vowel and <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[h]</span>: <i>pitsipʉ&#x331;ha</i> &gt; <i>pitsipʰa</i> ('milk').</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Preaspiration" title="Preaspiration">Preaspiration</a> and <a href="/wiki/Glottalization#Glottal_reinforcement" title="Glottalization">Preglottalization</a></b>: Certain consonants undergo preaspiration word medially, namely, <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/n/</span> and the voiceless, unaspirated stops <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/p/</span>, <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/t/</span>, and <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/k/</span> (rendered in the IPA as <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ʰn]</span>, <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ʰp]</span>, <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ʰt]</span>, <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ʰk]</span>, respectively). It is usually written with an <b>h</b> before the consonant, as in <i>aworahna</i> 'cupboard' or <i>ekasahpanaʔ</i> 'soldier'. Similarly, many of the same consonants can also undergo preglottalization, which is written with <b>ʔ</b> before the consonant (resulting in the digraphs <b>ʔn</b>, <b>ʔb</b>, <b>ʔw</b>, and <b>ʔr</b>), as in <i>hunuʔbiʔ</i> 'creek' or <i>taʔwoʔiʔ</i> 'gun'.</li>
<li><b>Organic and Inorganic <a href="/wiki/Voiceless" class="mw-redirect" title="Voiceless">Devoicing</a></b>: each Comanche vowel has an allophonic <a href="/wiki/Voiceless" class="mw-redirect" title="Voiceless">voiceless</a> (or "whispered") equivalent. The devoicing process in Comanche follows a predictable pattern, and can be broken down into two categories - organic (compulsory) and inorganic (optional).
<ul><li>A vowel which precedes an /s/ or an /h/ undergoes induced <b>organic devoicing</b>, provided that the vowel is unstressed, <a href="/wiki/Vowel_length" title="Vowel length">short</a>, and not part of a cluster (contrast <i>sit<s><u>u</u></s>suʔa</i>, 'this one also', which undergoes organic devoicing, with the similar word <i>sit<s>u</s><s>u</s>suʔa</i>, 'these ones also', which does not because the vowel is not short). Two adjacent syllables cannot both have organic voiceless vowels. In such a situation, the second vowel does not devoice.</li>
<li>The second type of devoicing that can occur in Comanche is <b>inorganic devoicing</b>. Short vowels that are not part of a cluster may be optionally devoiced at the end of a breath group, and this may apply even if the preceding vowel has undergone organic devoicing. Additionally, an inorganic, voiceless vowel conditions optional lengthening of a voiced penultimate vowel if there is no intervening <a href="/wiki/Preaspiration" title="Preaspiration">preaspirated</a> consonant (for example, <i>kaasa̲</i> 'wing' and <i>oomo̲</i> 'leg').<sup id="cite_ref-Robinson_92_12-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Robinson_92-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup></li></ul></li></ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Morphology">Morphology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Comanche_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Morphology"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Like many languages of the Americas, Comanche can be classified as a <a href="/wiki/Polysynthetic_language" title="Polysynthetic language">polysynthetic language</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nouns">Nouns</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Comanche_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Nouns"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Comanche nouns are inflected for case and number, and the language possesses a <a href="/wiki/Dual_(grammatical_number)" title="Dual (grammatical number)">dual</a> number. Like many Uto-Aztecan languages, nouns may take an absolutive suffix. Many cases are also marked using <a href="/wiki/Preposition_and_postposition" title="Preposition and postposition">postpositions</a>.
</p><p>Personal pronouns exist for three <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_number" title="Grammatical number">numbers</a> (singular, dual, and plural) and three <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_person" title="Grammatical person">persons</a>. They have different forms depending on whether or not they are the subject or object of a verb, possessive (including <a href="/wiki/Reflexive_pronoun" title="Reflexive pronoun">reflexive</a> possessive forms), or the object of a postposition. Like many <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_languages_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous languages of the Americas">languages of the Americas</a>, Comanche first-person plural pronouns have both <a href="/wiki/Clusivity" title="Clusivity">inclusive and exclusive forms</a>.

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Verbs">Verbs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Comanche_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Verbs"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Many of the verb stems regularly are <a href="/wiki/Suppletion" title="Suppletion">suppletive</a>: <a href="/wiki/Intransitive_verb" title="Intransitive verb">intransitive verbs</a> are suppletive for <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_number#Singular_versus_plural" title="Grammatical number">singular versus plural</a> <a href="/wiki/Subject_(grammar)" title="Subject (grammar)">subject</a> and <a href="/wiki/Transitive_verbs" class="mw-redirect" title="Transitive verbs">transitive verbs</a> are suppletive for singular versus plural <a href="/wiki/Object_(grammar)" title="Object (grammar)">object</a>.  Verbs can take various affixes, including <a href="/wiki/Incorporation_(linguistics)" title="Incorporation (linguistics)">incorporated</a> nouns before the stem.  Most verb affixes are suffixes, except for voicing-changing prefixes and instrumental prefixes.<sup id="cite_ref-Charney1993_11-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Charney1993-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

</p><p>In addition to verbal affixes, Comanche verbs can also be augmented by other verbs. Although in principle Comanche verbs may be freely combined with other verbs, in actuality only a handful of verbs, termed <a href="/wiki/Auxiliary_verb" title="Auxiliary verb">auxiliary verbs</a>, are frequently combined with others. These forms take the full range of aspectual suffixes. Common auxiliary verbs in Comanche include <i>hani</i> 'to do, make', <i>naha</i> 'to be, become', <i>miʔa</i> 'to go', and <i>katʉ / yʉkwi</i> 'to sit'. An example of how the verbs combine:

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Instrumental_prefixes">Instrumental prefixes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Comanche_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Instrumental prefixes"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>As mentioned above, Comanche has a rich repertoire of <a href="/w/index.php?title=Instrumental_affix&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Instrumental affix (page does not exist)">instrumental prefixes</a>, and certain verbs (termed instrumental verbs) cannot occur without an instrumental prefix. These prefixes can affect the transitivity of a verb. The Comanche instrumental prefixes are listed below:
</p>
<ul><li><i>kʉh-</i> = 'with the teeth, chin, mouth'</li>
<li><i>kuh-</i> = 'with heat, fire'</li>
<li><i>ma-</i> = 'with the hand' and as a generalized instrumental</li>
<li><i>mu-</i> / <i>muh</i> = 'with the nose, lips, front'</li>
<li><i>nih-</i> = 'verbally'</li>
<li><i>pih-</i> = 'with the buttocks, rear (e.g., of a car)'</li>
<li><i>sʉ-</i> = 'with cold'; <a href="/wiki/Fortis_and_lenis" title="Fortis and lenis">fortis</a> is applied at the end of the prefix</li>
<li><i>sʉh-</i> = 'with the foot, in a violent motion'</li>
<li><i>su-</i> = 'with the mind, mental activity'; fortis is applied at the end of the prefix</li>
<li><i>tah-</i> = 'with the foot'</li>
<li><i>toh-</i> = 'with the hand, violent or completed action'</li>
<li><i>tsah-</i> = 'with the hand (extended to hand tools)'</li>
<li><i>tsih-</i> = 'with a sharp point, with the finger'</li>
<li><i>tsoh-</i> = 'with the head'</li>
<li><i>wʉh-</i> = an all-purpose instrumental</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Syntax">Syntax</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Comanche_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Syntax"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Comanche parts of speech include nouns, verbs, adjectives, adverbs, pronouns, and <a href="/wiki/Interjection" title="Interjection">interjections</a> (such as <i>haa</i> 'yes' and <i>kee</i> 'no'), as well as <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_particle" title="Grammatical particle">particles</a>.
</p><p>The standard <a href="/wiki/Word_order" title="Word order">word order</a> is <a href="/wiki/Subject%E2%80%93object%E2%80%93verb" class="mw-redirect" title="Subject–object–verb">subject–object–verb</a>, but it can shift in two specific circumstances. The <a href="/wiki/Topic%E2%80%93comment" class="mw-redirect" title="Topic–comment">topic</a> of a sentence, though marked with one of two particles, is often placed at the beginning of the sentence, defying the standard word order. Furthermore, the subject of a sentence is often placed second in a sentence. When the subject is also the topic, as is often the case, it ends up in the first position, preserving SOV word order; otherwise, the subject will be placed second. For example, the English sentence 'I hit the man' could be rendered in Comanche with the components in either of the following two orders: 'I' (topic) 'man' (object) 'hit' (an aspect marker) - the standard SOV word order - or 'man' (object and topic) 'I' 'hit' (an aspect marker) - an <a href="/wiki/Object%E2%80%93subject%E2%80%93verb" class="mw-redirect" title="Object–subject–verb">OSV</a> word order, which accentuates the role of the man who was hit.<sup id="cite_ref-Charney1993_11-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Charney1993-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Switch_reference">Switch reference</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Comanche_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Switch reference"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Like other Numic languages, Comanche has <a href="/wiki/Switch-reference" title="Switch-reference">switch-reference</a> markers to handle subordination.<sup id="cite_ref-Charney1993_11-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Charney1993-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> This refers to markers which indicate whether or not a subordinate verb has the same or different subject as the main verb, and in the case of Comanche, also the temporal relation between the two verbs.
</p><p>When the verb of a <a href="/wiki/Dependent_clause" title="Dependent clause">subordinate clause</a> has a different subject from the verb of the <a href="/wiki/Independent_clause" title="Independent clause">main clause</a>, and the time of the verbs is simultaneous, the subordinate verb is marked with <i>-ku</i>, and its subject is marked as if it were an object. When the time of the verbs is not simultaneous, the subordinate verb is marked with one of several affixes depending on the <a href="/wiki/Length_(phonetics)" title="Length (phonetics)">duration</a> of the subordinate verb and whether it refers to an action which occurred before that described by the main verb or one which occurred after.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Comanche_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: In popular culture"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the 1956 film <i><a href="/wiki/The_Searchers_(film)" class="mw-redirect" title="The Searchers (film)">The Searchers</a></i>, starring <a href="/wiki/John_Wayne" title="John Wayne">John Wayne</a>, there are several badly pronounced Comanche words interspersed, such as <i>nawyecka</i> (<span title="Comanche-language text"><i lang="com">nooyʉka</i></span> 'move camp around') and <i>timoway</i> (<span title="Comanche-language text"><i lang="com">tʉmʉʉ</i></span> 'buy, trade').
</p><p>In the 1963 film <i><a href="/wiki/McLintock!" title="McLintock!">McLintock!</a></i>, also starring <a href="/wiki/John_Wayne" title="John Wayne">John Wayne</a>, McLintock (Wayne) and Chief Puma (Michael Pate) speak Comanche several times throughout the film.
</p><p>In a 2013 <i><a href="/wiki/Boston_Globe" class="mw-redirect" title="Boston Globe">Boston Globe</a></i> article, linguist Todd McDaniels of <a href="/wiki/Comanche_Nation_College" title="Comanche Nation College">Comanche Nation College</a> commented on <a href="/wiki/Johnny_Depp" title="Johnny Depp">Johnny Depp</a>'s attempts to speak the Comanche language in the film <i><a href="/wiki/The_Lone_Ranger_(2013_film)" title="The Lone Ranger (2013 film)">The Lone Ranger</a></i>, saying, "The words were there, the pronunciation was shaky but adequate."<sup id="cite_ref-mcdaniels_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mcdaniels-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the 2016 film <i><a href="/wiki/The_Magnificent_Seven_(2016_film)" title="The Magnificent Seven (2016 film)">The Magnificent Seven</a></i> two of the titular characters, a Comanche warrior named Red Harvest and Sam Chisholm, an African-American warrant officer, speak Comanche to each other.
</p><p>In the 2019 TV series <i><a href="/wiki/The_Son_(TV_series)" title="The Son (TV series)">The Son</a></i>, the main character, Eli McCullough, lives with a tribe of Comanche natives, who speak in Comanche to each other and later to him.
</p><p>The 2022 movie <i><a href="/wiki/Prey_(2022_film)" title="Prey (2022 film)">Prey</a></i>, set in the early 18th century, is the first feature film to have a full Comanche language dub.<sup id="cite_ref-autoCo_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autoCo-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
