					<div id="mw-content-text" class="mw-body-content"><div class="mw-content-ltr mw-parser-output" lang="en" dir="ltr"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Languages spoken by people of the Andaman Islands</div>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1033289096">.mw-parser-output .hatnote{font-style:italic}.mw-parser-output div.hatnote{padding-left:1.6em;margin-bottom:0.5em}.mw-parser-output .hatnote i{font-style:normal}.mw-parser-output .hatnote+link+.hatnote{margin-top:-0.5em}</style><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Not to be confused with <a href="/wiki/Great_Andamanese_languages" title="Great Andamanese languages">Great Andamanese languages</a>.</div><p>The <b>Andamanese languages</b> are the languages spoken by the indigenous <a href="/wiki/Andamanese_peoples" title="Andamanese peoples">Andamanese peoples</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Andaman_Islands" title="Andaman Islands">Andaman Islands</a> in the Indian Ocean. It contains two known language families, <a href="/wiki/Great_Andamanese_languages" title="Great Andamanese languages">Great Andamanese</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ongan_languages" title="Ongan languages">Ongan</a>, as well as two presumed but unattested languages, <a href="/wiki/Sentinelese_language" title="Sentinelese language">Sentinelese</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jangil" title="Jangil">Jangil</a>. 
</p><p>Although all of the languages in the Andaman Islands were once thought to be the same language family, it is now widely accepted that Great Andamanese and Ongan have no <a href="/wiki/Genealogical_relationship_(linguistics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Genealogical relationship (linguistics)">genealogical relationship</a>. Thus, the term "Andamanese languages" is a strictly geographic label that covers several unrelated languages. 
</p><p>Because Sentinelese and Jangil are unattested, almost nothing is known about their relationships with the other Andamanese languages. 
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">Classification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andamanese_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Classification"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The attested Andamanese languages fall into two genetically unrelated families:<sup id="cite_ref-Manoh1_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Manoh1-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Abbi_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Abbi-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1116488514">.mw-parser-output .treeview ul{padding:0;margin:0}.mw-parser-output .treeview li{padding:0;margin:0;list-style-type:none;list-style-image:none}.mw-parser-output .treeview li li{background:url("https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/f2/Treeview-grey-line.png")no-repeat 0 -2981px;padding-left:21px;text-indent:0.3em}.mw-parser-output .treeview li li:last-child{background-position:0 -5971px}.mw-parser-output .treeview li.emptyline>ul>.mw-empty-elt:first-child+.emptyline,.mw-parser-output .treeview li.emptyline>ul>li:first-child{background-position:0 9px}</style><div class="treeview">
<ul><li><b>Andamanese</b>
<ul><li><b><a href="/wiki/Great_Andamanese_languages" title="Great Andamanese languages">Great Andamanese</a></b>: A moribund language family once spoken by <a href="/wiki/Great_Andamanese" title="Great Andamanese">Great Andamanese people</a>. Today, the first language of most Great Andamanese people is <a href="/wiki/Hindi" title="Hindi">Hindi</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> There are 10 Great Andamanese languages:
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Aka-Bea_language" title="Aka-Bea language">Aka-Bea</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Akar-Bale_language" title="Akar-Bale language">Akar-Bale</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/A-Pucikwar_language" title="A-Pucikwar language">A-Pucikwar</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Oko-Juwoi_language" title="Oko-Juwoi language">Oko-Juwoi</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Aka-Kol_language" title="Aka-Kol language">Aka-Kol</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Aka-Kede_language" title="Aka-Kede language">Aka-Kede</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Aka-Jeru_language" title="Aka-Jeru language">Aka-Jeru</a>, three speakers in 2020<sup id="cite_ref-:2_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Aka-Bo_language" title="Aka-Bo language">Aka-Bo</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Aka-Kora_language" title="Aka-Kora language">Aka-Kora</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Aka-Cari_language" title="Aka-Cari language">Aka-Cari</a></li></ul></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Ongan_languages" title="Ongan languages">Ongan</a></b>: Two languages, <a href="/wiki/Jarawa_language_(Andaman_Islands)" title="Jarawa language (Andaman Islands)">Jarawa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Onge_language" title="Onge language">Onge</a>, spoken by roughly 670 people in 2020.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup></li></ul></li></ul>
</div>
<p>In addition, there are two unattested languages: 
</p>
<ul><li><b><a href="/wiki/Sentinelese_language" title="Sentinelese language">Sentinelese</a></b>: Because the <a href="/wiki/Sentinelese" title="Sentinelese">Sentinelese people</a> refuse contact with outsiders, their language and exact population are unknown. There are likely at least 50 speakers, and perhaps upwards of 250. <a href="/wiki/Anvita_Abbi" title="Anvita Abbi">Anvita Abbi</a> (2020) believes that Sentinelese is most likely Ongan.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Jangil" title="Jangil">Jangil</a></b>: Extinct by 1920s. No ethnically Jangil people survive today. <a href="/wiki/Maurice_Vidal_Portman" title="Maurice Vidal Portman">Maurice Vidal Portman</a> observed that Jangil shared similarities with Jarawa, and believed that they were at one point the same language.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>The languages of the Andaman Islands have frequently been assumed to be from the same single Andamanese language family. However, the similarities between Great Andamanese and Ongan are so far mainly of a typological <a href="/wiki/Morphology_(linguistics)" title="Morphology (linguistics)">morphological</a> nature, with little common vocabulary. Abbi (2009) demonstrated that the Andaman Islands have two unrelated language families: Great Andamanese and Ongan.<sup id="cite_ref-Abbi_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Abbi-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>Blevins (2007) summarizes,
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r996844942">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>a relationship between Jarawa and Onge and languages of the Great Andaman group is not widely accepted. Radcliffe-Brown (1914:40) found only seven potential cognates between Onge and Bea/Jeru, and noted that the difference between Onge and the Great Andaman languages "is such that it would not be possible from consideration of the vocabulary alone to prove that they belonged to the same language stock." [...] Abbi (2006:93) is agnostic, stating that "current linguistic analysis does not, with any certainty, indicate any genetic relationship between Great Andamanese and the other two languages." The only positive evidence offered in support of this relationship is a listing of 17 word pairs as proposed cognates in Manoharan (1989:16667). There are several problems with Manoharan's proposal [such as semantic mismatches and failing to identify loans. ...] Given evidence that shows these languages have been in contact, and the scarcity of data available at present on Great Andaman languages, there remains no persuasive evidence of a family relationship between Jarawa-Onge and the Great Andaman languages. [...] Greenberg (1971:810) is unconvinced of the relation between Great Andaman and Onge-Jarawa, agreeing with Radcliffe-Brown (1914) that "there are very few vocabulary resemblances between this language [Onge] and those of Great Andaman and the only real point of contact is typological.  A few citations from Onge have been included in the general Indo-Pacific vocabulary, but both its special relationship to the languages of the rest of the Andamans and its assignment to Indo-Pacific must be considered highly provisional."</p></blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Controversial_classifications">Controversial classifications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andamanese_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Controversial classifications"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Joseph_Greenberg" title="Joseph Greenberg">Joseph Greenberg</a> proposed that the Great Andamanese languages are related to western <a href="/wiki/Papuan_languages" title="Papuan languages">Papuan languages</a> as members of a phylum he called <a href="/wiki/Indo-Pacific_languages" title="Indo-Pacific languages">Indo-Pacific</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Greenb_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Greenb-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> but this is not generally accepted by other linguists. <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Wurm" title="Stephen Wurm">Stephen Wurm</a> states that the lexical similarities between Great Andamanese and the <a href="/wiki/West_Papuan_languages" title="West Papuan languages">West Papuan</a> and certain languages of <a href="/wiki/Timor" title="Timor">Timor</a> "are quite striking and amount to virtual formal identity […] in a number of instances", but considers this to be due to a linguistic <a href="/wiki/Substrata_(linguistics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Substrata (linguistics)">substratum</a> rather than a direct relationship.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>Blevins (2007) proposes that the Ongan languages are related to <a href="/wiki/Austronesian_languages" title="Austronesian languages">Austronesian</a> in an <a href="/wiki/Austronesian%E2%80%93Ongan_languages" title="Austronesian–Ongan languages">Austronesian–Ongan</a> family, for which she has attempted to establish regular sound correspondences.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The proposed connection between Austronesian and Ongan has not been supported by Austronesianists, and <a href="/wiki/Robert_Blust" title="Robert Blust">Robert Blust</a> (2014) finds that Blevins' conclusions are not supported by her data: Of her first 25 reconstructions, none are reproducible using the comparative method, and Blust concludes that the grammatical comparison does not hold up. Blust, in addition, cites non-linguistic (such as cultural, archaeological, and biological) evidence against Blevins' hypothesis.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andamanese_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Andamanese_peoples" title="Andamanese peoples">Andamanese peoples</a></div>
<p>The indigenous peoples of the Andaman Islands have lived there for thousands of years. Although the existence of the islands and their inhabitants was long known to maritime powers and traders of the <a href="/wiki/South_Asia" title="South Asia">South</a>– and <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast–Asia</a> region, contact with these peoples was highly sporadic and very often hostile. As a result, almost nothing is recorded of them or their languages until the mid-18th century. 
</p><p>By the late 18th century, when the <a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">British</a> first established a colonial presence on the Andaman Islands, there were an estimated 5,000 <a href="/wiki/Great_Andamanese" title="Great Andamanese">Great Andamanese</a> living on <a href="/wiki/Great_Andaman" title="Great Andaman">Great Andaman</a> and surrounding islands, comprising 10 distinct tribes with distinct but closely related languages. 
</p><p>From the 1860s onwards, the British established a <a href="/wiki/Penal_colony" title="Penal colony">penal colony</a> on the islands, which led to the subsequent arrival of mainland settlers and indentured labourers, mainly from the <a href="/wiki/Indian_subcontinent" title="Indian subcontinent">Indian subcontinent</a>. This coincided with the massive population reduction of the Andamanese due to outside diseases. 
</p><p>One of the first accounts in English of the languages was by the early phonetician <a href="/wiki/Alexander_John_Ellis" title="Alexander John Ellis">Alexander John Ellis</a>, who presented to the Philological Society on the South Andamanese languages on his retirement. This presentation was later adapted into a <i>Report of Researches into the Language of the South Andaman Island</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>By the beginning of the 20th century most of these populations were greatly reduced in numbers, and the various linguistic and tribal divisions among the Great Andamanese effectively ceased to exist, despite a census of the time still classifying the groups as separate.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Their linguistic diversity also suffered as the surviving populations intermingled with one another, and some also intermarried with <a href="/wiki/Karen_people" title="Karen people">Karen (Burmese)</a> and <a href="/wiki/Demographics_of_India" title="Demographics of India">Indian</a> settlers.
</p>
<figure class="mw-halign-right" typeof="mw:File"><a href="/wiki/File:Andamanese_comparative_distribution.png" class="mw-file-description"><img alt="Precontact and current distribution of Anamanese languages" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Andamanese_comparative_distribution.png/440px-Andamanese_comparative_distribution.png" decoding="async" width="440" height="339" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Andamanese_comparative_distribution.png/660px-Andamanese_comparative_distribution.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/c2/Andamanese_comparative_distribution.png 2x" data-file-width="737" data-file-height="567" /></a><figcaption></figcaption></figure>
<p>By the latter part of the 20th century, the majority of Great Andamanese languages had become extinct.
</p><p>At the start of the 21st century only about 50 or so individuals of Great Andamanese descent remained, resettled to a single small island (<a href="/wiki/Strait_Island" title="Strait Island">Strait I.</a>). About half of these individuals speak what may be considered a modified version (or <a href="/wiki/Creole_language" title="Creole language">creole</a>) of Great Andamanese, based mainly on <a href="/wiki/Aka-Jeru_language" title="Aka-Jeru language">Aka-Jeru</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Abbi_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Abbi-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> This modified version has been called "Present Great Andamanese" by some scholars,<sup id="cite_ref-Abbi06_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Abbi06-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Burenh_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Burenh-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> but also may be referred to simply as "Jero" or "Great Andamanese". <a href="/wiki/Hindi" title="Hindi">Hindi</a> increasingly serves as their primary language, and is the only language for around half of them.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Ongan languages survive mainly because of the greater isolation of the peoples who speak them. This isolation has been reinforced by an outright hostility towards outsiders and extreme reluctance to engage in contact with them by South Andamanese tribes, particularly the <a href="/wiki/Sentinelese_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Sentinelese people">Sentinelese</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jarawa_(Andaman_Islands)" class="mw-redirect" title="Jarawa (Andaman Islands)">Jarawa</a>. The Sentinelese have been so resistant that their language remains entirely unknown to outsiders.
</p>
