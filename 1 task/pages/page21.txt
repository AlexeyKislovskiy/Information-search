<p>The <b>Chadic languages</b> form a branch of the <a href="/wiki/Afroasiatic_languages" title="Afroasiatic languages">Afroasiatic language family</a>. They are spoken in parts of the <a href="/wiki/Sahel" title="Sahel">Sahel</a>. They include 150 languages spoken across northern <a href="/wiki/Nigeria" title="Nigeria">Nigeria</a>, southern <a href="/wiki/Niger" title="Niger">Niger</a>, southern <a href="/wiki/Chad" title="Chad">Chad</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Central_African_Republic" title="Central African Republic">Central African Republic</a>, and northern <a href="/wiki/Cameroon" title="Cameroon">Cameroon</a>. By far the most widely spoken Chadic language is <a href="/wiki/Hausa_language" title="Hausa language">Hausa</a>, a <i><a href="/wiki/Lingua_franca" title="Lingua franca">lingua franca</a></i> of much of inland Eastern <a href="/wiki/West_Africa" title="West Africa">West Africa</a>, particularly Niger and the northern half of Nigeria.
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Composition">Composition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chadic_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Composition"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Paul_Newman_(linguist)" title="Paul Newman (linguist)">Paul Newman</a> (1977) classified the languages into the four groups which have been accepted in all subsequent literature. Further subbranching, however, has not been as robust; <a href="/wiki/Roger_Blench" title="Roger Blench">Roger Blench</a> (2006), for example, only accepts the A/B bifurcation of East Chadic.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Kujarg%C3%A9_language" title="Kujargé language">Kujargé</a> has been added from Blench (2008), who suggests Kujargé may have split off before the breakup of Proto-Chadic and then subsequently became influenced by East Chadic.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Subsequent work by Joseph Lovestrand argues strongly that Kujarge is a valid member of East Chadic. The placing of Luri as a primary split of West Chadic is erroneous. Bernard Caron (2004) shows that this language is South Bauchi and part of the Polci cluster.
</p>
<ul><li><b><a href="/wiki/West_Chadic_languages" title="West Chadic languages">West Chadic</a></b>. Two branches, which include</li></ul>
<dl><dd>(A) the <a href="/wiki/Hausa_language" title="Hausa language">Hausa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ron_languages" title="Ron languages">Ron</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bole_languages" class="mw-redirect" title="Bole languages">Bole</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Angas_languages" title="Angas languages">Angas languages</a>; and</dd>
<dd>(B) the <a href="/wiki/Bade_languages" title="Bade languages">Bade</a>, <a href="/wiki/Warji_language" title="Warji language">Warji</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Zaar_languages" class="mw-redirect" title="Zaar languages">Zaar languages</a>.</dd></dl>
<ul><li><b><a href="/wiki/Biu%E2%80%93Mandara_languages" title="Biu–Mandara languages">Biu–Mandara</a></b> (Central Chadic). Three branches, which include</li></ul>
<dl><dd>(A) the <a href="/wiki/Bura_language" title="Bura language">Bura</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kamwe_language" title="Kamwe language">Kamwe</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bata_language" title="Bata language">Bata</a> languages, among other groups;</dd>
<dd>(B) the <a href="/wiki/Buduma_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Buduma language">Buduma</a> and <a href="/wiki/Musgu_language" title="Musgu language">Musgu</a> languages; and</dd>
<dd>(C) <a href="/wiki/Gidar_language" title="Gidar language">Gidar</a></dd></dl>
<ul><li><b><a href="/wiki/East_Chadic_languages" title="East Chadic languages">East Chadic</a></b>. Two branches, which include</li></ul>
<dl><dd>(A) the <a href="/wiki/Tumak_language" title="Tumak language">Tumak</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nancere_language" title="Nancere language">Nancere</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Kera_language" title="Kera language">Kera</a> languages; and</dd>
<dd>(B) the <a href="/wiki/Dangal%C3%A9at_language" title="Dangaléat language">Dangaléat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mokilko_language" title="Mokilko language">Mukulu</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sokoro_language" title="Sokoro language">Sokoro</a> languages</dd></dl>
<ul><li><b><a href="/wiki/Masa_languages" title="Masa languages">Masa</a></b></li>
<li>? <i><b><a href="/wiki/Kujarg%C3%A9_language" title="Kujargé language">Kujargé</a></b></i></li></ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin">Origin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chadic_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Origin"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<figure class="mw-halign-right" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Afro_asiatic_peoples_nigeria.png" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/Afro_asiatic_peoples_nigeria.png/200px-Afro_asiatic_peoples_nigeria.png" decoding="async" width="200" height="163" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/Afro_asiatic_peoples_nigeria.png/300px-Afro_asiatic_peoples_nigeria.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/Afro_asiatic_peoples_nigeria.png/400px-Afro_asiatic_peoples_nigeria.png 2x" data-file-width="2846" data-file-height="2313" /></a><figcaption>Main Chadic-speaking peoples in Nigeria.</figcaption></figure>
<figure class="mw-halign-right" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Hausa_language_map.png" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Hausa_language_map.png/200px-Hausa_language_map.png" decoding="async" width="200" height="253" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Hausa_language_map.png/300px-Hausa_language_map.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Hausa_language_map.png/400px-Hausa_language_map.png 2x" data-file-width="3571" data-file-height="4519" /></a><figcaption>Hausa-speaking areas in Nigeria and Niger.</figcaption></figure>
<p>Modern genetic studies of Northwestern Cameroonian Chadic-speaking populations have observed relatively high frequencies 
(~23%) of the <a href="/wiki/Haplogroup_R1b" title="Haplogroup R1b">Y-chromosome Haplogroup R1b</a> in these populations (the R1b-V88<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> variant). This paternal marker is common in parts of West Eurasia, but otherwise rare in Africa. Cruciani et al. (2010) thus proposed that the Proto-Chadic speakers during the mid-<a href="/wiki/Holocene" title="Holocene">Holocene</a> (~7,000 years ago) migrated from the <a href="/wiki/Levant" title="Levant">Levant</a> to the Central <a href="/wiki/Sahara" title="Sahara">Sahara</a>, and from there settled in the <a href="/wiki/Lake_Chad" title="Lake Chad">Lake Chad</a> Basin.<sup id="cite_ref-Cruciani2010_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cruciani2010-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>D'Atanasio et al. (2018)  claim that R1b-V88 originated in Europe about 12,000 years ago and crossed to North Africa by about 8000 years ago; it may formerly have been common in southern Europe, where it has since been replaced by waves of other haplogroups, leaving remnant subclades almost exclusively in Sardinia. It first radiated within Africa likely between 7 and 8 000 years ago – at the same time as trans-Saharan expansions within the unrelated haplogroups E-M2 and A-M13 – possibly due to population growth allowed by humid conditions and the adoption of livestock herding in the Sahara. R1b-V1589, the main subclade within R1b-V88, underwent a further expansion around 5500 years ago, likely in the Lake Chad Basin region, from which some lines recrossed the Sahara to North Africa.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Loanwords">Loanwords</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chadic_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Loanwords"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Chadic languages contain many Nilo-Saharan loanwords from either the <a href="/wiki/Songhay_languages" title="Songhay languages">Songhay</a> or <a href="/wiki/Maban_languages" title="Maban languages">Maban</a> branches, pointing to early contact between Chadic and Nilo-Saharan speakers as Chadic was migrating west.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Although <a href="/wiki/Adamawa_languages" title="Adamawa languages">Adamawa languages</a> are spoken adjacently to Chadic languages, interaction between Chadic and Adamawa is limited.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
