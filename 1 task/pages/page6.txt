<p><b>Ayoreo</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Zamucoan" class="mw-redirect" title="Zamucoan">Zamucoan</a> language spoken in both <a href="/wiki/Paraguay" title="Paraguay">Paraguay</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bolivia" title="Bolivia">Bolivia</a>. It is also known as <b>Morotoco</b>, <b>Moro</b>, <b>Ayoweo</b>, <b>Ayoré</b>, and <b>Pyeta Yovai</b>. However, the name "Ayoreo" is more common in Bolivia, and "Morotoco" in Paraguay. It is spoken by <a href="/wiki/Ayoreo_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Ayoreo people">Ayoreo</a>, an indigenous ethnic group traditionally living on a combined <a href="/wiki/Hunter-gatherer" title="Hunter-gatherer">hunter-gatherer</a> and farming lifestyle.
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">Classification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ayoreo_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Classification"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ayoreo is classified as a <a href="/wiki/Zamucoan" class="mw-redirect" title="Zamucoan">Zamucoan</a> language, along with <a href="/wiki/Chamacoco_language" title="Chamacoco language">Chamacoco</a>.  Extinct Guarañoca may have been a dialect.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geographic_distribution">Geographic distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ayoreo_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geographic distribution"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ayoreo is spoken in both <a href="/wiki/Paraguay" title="Paraguay">Paraguay</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bolivia" title="Bolivia">Bolivia</a>, with 3,100 speakers total, 1700 of those in Paraguay and 1,400 in Bolivia. Within Paraguay, Ayoreo is spoken in the <a href="/wiki/Chaco_Department" class="mw-redirect" title="Chaco Department">Chaco Department</a> and the northern parts of the <a href="/wiki/Alto_Paraguay_Department" title="Alto Paraguay Department">Alto Paraguay Department</a>. In Bolivia, it is spoken in the <a href="/wiki/Cordillera_Province_(Bolivia)" title="Cordillera Province (Bolivia)">Cordillera Province</a>, in the <a href="/wiki/Santa_Cruz_Department_(Bolivia)" title="Santa Cruz Department (Bolivia)">Santa Cruz Department</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Grammar">Grammar</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ayoreo_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Grammar"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The prototypical constituent order is <a href="/wiki/Subject-verb-object" class="mw-redirect" title="Subject-verb-object">subject-verb-object</a>, as seen in the following examples (Bertinetto 2009:45-46):
</p>

<p>Ayoreo is a fusional language.<sup id="cite_ref-lang_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lang-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Verbs agree with their subjects, but there is no tense-inflection.<sup id="cite_ref-verb_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-verb-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Consider the following paradigm, which has prefixes marking person and suffixes marking number (Bertinetto 2009:29):
</p>

</td></tr>

</td></tr>
<tr>

</td></tr></tbody></table>

</p>

<p>Ayoreo is a mood-prominent language.<sup id="cite_ref-lang_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lang-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Nouns can be divided into possessable and non-possessable; possessor agreement is expressed through a prefixation.<sup id="cite_ref-prefix_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-prefix-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The syntax of Ayoreo is characterized by the presence of <a href="/wiki/Parataxis" title="Parataxis">para</a>-<a href="/wiki/Hypotaxis" title="Hypotaxis">hypotactical</a> structures.<sup id="cite_ref-sint_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sint-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
