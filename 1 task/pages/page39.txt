<p><b>Avestan</b> (<span class="rt-commentedText nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt" lang="en-fonipa"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ə/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;about&#39;">ə</span><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="&#39;v&#39; in &#39;vie&#39;">v</span><span title="/ɛ/: &#39;e&#39; in &#39;dress&#39;">ɛ</span><span title="&#39;s&#39; in &#39;sigh&#39;">s</span><span title="&#39;t&#39; in &#39;tie&#39;">t</span><span title="/ən/: &#39;on&#39; in &#39;button&#39;">ən</span></span>/</a></span></span> <a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key"><i title="English pronunciation respelling">ə-<span style="font-size:90%">VESS</span>-tən</i></a>)<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> is an umbrella term for two <a href="/wiki/Iranian_languages#Old_Iranian" title="Iranian languages">Old Iranian languages</a>, <b>Old Avestan</b> (spoken in the 2nd to 1st millennium BCE) and <b>Younger Avestan</b> (spoken in the 1st millennium BCE). They are known only from their conjoined use as the scriptural language of <a href="/wiki/Zoroastrianism" title="Zoroastrianism">Zoroastrianism</a>; the <a href="/wiki/Avesta" title="Avesta">Avesta</a>  serves as their namesake. Both are early <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Iranian_languages" title="Eastern Iranian languages">Eastern Iranian languages</a> within the <a href="/wiki/Indo-Iranian_languages" title="Indo-Iranian languages">Indo-Iranian language branch</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Indo-European_languages" title="Indo-European languages">Indo-European language family</a>. Its immediate ancestor was the <a href="/wiki/Proto-Iranian_language" title="Proto-Iranian language">Proto-Iranian language</a>, a sister language to the <a href="/wiki/Proto-Indo-Aryan_language" title="Proto-Indo-Aryan language">Proto-Indo-Aryan language</a>, with both having developed from the earlier <a href="/wiki/Proto-Indo-Iranian_language" title="Proto-Indo-Iranian language">Proto-Indo-Iranian language</a>; as such, Old Avestan is quite close in both grammar and lexicon to <a href="/wiki/Vedic_Sanskrit" title="Vedic Sanskrit">Vedic Sanskrit</a>, the oldest preserved <a href="/wiki/Indo-Aryan_languages" title="Indo-Aryan languages">Indo-Aryan language</a>.
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1134653256">.mw-parser-output .side-box{margin:4px 0;box-sizing:border-box;border:1px solid #aaa;font-size:88%;line-height:1.25em;background-color:#f9f9f9;display:flow-root}.mw-parser-output .side-box-abovebelow,.mw-parser-output .side-box-text{padding:0.25em 0.9em}.mw-parser-output .side-box-image{padding:2px 0 2px 0.9em;text-align:center}.mw-parser-output .side-box-imageright{padding:2px 0.9em 2px 0;text-align:center}@media(min-width:500px){.mw-parser-output .side-box-flex{display:flex;align-items:center}.mw-parser-output .side-box-text{flex:1}}@media(min-width:720px){.mw-parser-output .side-box{width:238px}.mw-parser-output .side-box-right{clear:right;float:right;margin-left:1em}.mw-parser-output .side-box-left{margin-right:1em}}</style><style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1092331828">@media(min-width:720px){.mw-parser-output .contains-special-characters{width:22em}}</style><div class="side-box metadata side-box-right contains-special-characters noprint selfref"><link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1126788409">

<p>The Avestan text corpus was composed in the ancient Iranian <a href="/wiki/Satrap" title="Satrap">satrapies</a> of <a href="/wiki/Arachosia" title="Arachosia">Arachosia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aria_(region)" title="Aria (region)">Aria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bactria" title="Bactria">Bactria</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Margiana" title="Margiana">Margiana</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> corresponding to the entirety of present-day <a href="/wiki/Afghanistan" title="Afghanistan">Afghanistan</a> as well as parts of <a href="/wiki/Tajikistan" title="Tajikistan">Tajikistan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Turkmenistan" title="Turkmenistan">Turkmenistan</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Uzbekistan" title="Uzbekistan">Uzbekistan</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Yaz_culture" title="Yaz culture">Yaz culture</a><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> of <a href="/wiki/Bactria%E2%80%93Margiana_Archaeological_Complex" title="Bactria–Margiana Archaeological Complex">Bactria–Margiana</a> has been regarded as a likely archaeological reflection of the early "<a href="/wiki/Iranian_peoples#Eastern_Iranian_peoples" title="Iranian peoples">Eastern Iranian</a>" <a href="/wiki/Avestan_period" title="Avestan period">culture</a> that is described in the <a href="/wiki/Avesta" title="Avesta">Zoroastrian Avesta</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Genealogy">Genealogy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Avestan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Genealogy"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Avestan and <a href="/wiki/Old_Persian" title="Old Persian">Old Persian</a> are the two attested languages comprising <a href="/wiki/Old_Iranian" class="mw-redirect" title="Old Iranian">Old Iranian</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Hoffmann_EIr_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoffmann_EIr-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> and while Avestan was localized in the northeastern parts of <a href="/wiki/Greater_Iran" title="Greater Iran">Greater Iran</a><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;f 1&#93;</a></sup> according to <a href="/w/index.php?title=Paul_Maximilian_Tedesco&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Paul Maximilian Tedesco (page does not exist)">Paul Maximilian Tedesco</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;">&#160;&#91;<a href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paul_Maximilian_Tedesco" class="extiw" title="de:Paul Maximilian Tedesco">de</a>&#93;</span> (1921), other scholars have favored regarding Avestan as originating in eastern parts.<sup id="cite_ref-Hoffmann_EIr_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoffmann_EIr-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Gnoli_EIr_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gnoli_EIr-6">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Scholars traditionally classify Iranian languages as "old", "middle" and "new" according to their age, and as "eastern" or "western" according to geography, and within this framework Avestan is classified as <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Iranian_languages" title="Eastern Iranian languages">Eastern</a> Old Iranian. But the east–west distinction is of limited meaning for Avestan, as the linguistic developments that later distinguish Eastern from Western Iranian had not yet occurred. Avestan does not display some typical (South-)Western Iranian innovations already visible in Old Persian, and so in this sense, "eastern" only means "non-western".<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Old Avestan is closely related to <a href="/wiki/Old_Persian" title="Old Persian">Old Persian</a> and largely agrees morphologically with <a href="/wiki/Vedic_Sanskrit" title="Vedic Sanskrit">Vedic Sanskrit</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The ancestor of <a href="/wiki/Pashto" title="Pashto">Pashto</a> was close to the language of the <a href="/wiki/Gathas" class="mw-redirect" title="Gathas">Gathas</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Forms_and_stages_of_development">Forms and stages of development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Avestan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Forms and stages of development"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Avestan language is attested in roughly two forms, known as "Old Avestan" (or "Gathic Avestan") and "Younger Avestan". Younger Avestan did not evolve from Old Avestan; the two differ not only in time, but they are also different dialects.
Every Avestan text, regardless of whether originally composed in Old or Younger Avestan, underwent several transformations. <a href="/wiki/Karl_Hoffmann_(linguist)" title="Karl Hoffmann (linguist)">Karl Hoffmann</a> traced the following stages for Avestan as found in the extant texts. In roughly chronological order:
</p>
<ul><li>The natural language of the composers of the <i><a href="/wiki/Gathas" class="mw-redirect" title="Gathas">Gathas</a></i>, the <i><a href="/wiki/Yasna_Haptanghaiti" title="Yasna Haptanghaiti">Yasna Haptanghaiti</a></i>, the four sacred prayers (<i>Y.</i> 27 and 54).</li>
<li>Changes precipitated by slow chanting</li>
<li>Changes to Old Avestan due to transmission by native speakers of Younger Avestan</li>
<li>The natural language of the scribes who wrote grammatically correct Younger Avestan texts</li>
<li>Deliberate changes introduced through "standardization"</li>
<li>Changes introduced by transfer to regions where Avestan was not spoken</li>
<li>Adaptions/translations of portions of texts from other regions</li>
<li>Composition of ungrammatical late Avestan texts</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Phonetic_notation" class="mw-redirect" title="Phonetic notation">Phonetic notation</a> of the Avestan texts in the <a href="/wiki/Avestan_alphabet#History" title="Avestan alphabet">Sasanian archetype</a></li>
<li>Post-Sasanian deterioration of the written transmission due to incorrect pronunciation</li>
<li>Errors and corruptions introduced during copying</li></ul>
<p>Many phonetic features cannot be ascribed with certainty to a particular stage since there may be more than one possibility. Every phonetic form that can be ascribed to the Sasanian archetype on the basis of critical assessment of the manuscript evidence must have gone through the stages mentioned above so that "Old Avestan" and "Young Avestan" really mean no more than "Old Avestan and Young Avestan of the <a href="/wiki/Sassanid_Empire" class="mw-redirect" title="Sassanid Empire">Sasanian period</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-Hoffmann_EIr_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoffmann_EIr-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dating">Dating</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Avestan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Dating"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1033289096">.mw-parser-output .hatnote{font-style:italic}.mw-parser-output div.hatnote{padding-left:1.6em;margin-bottom:0.5em}.mw-parser-output .hatnote i{font-style:normal}.mw-parser-output .hatnote+link+.hatnote{margin-top:-0.5em}</style><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Zarathustra#Date" class="mw-redirect" title="Zarathustra">Zarathustra §&#160;Date</a></div>
<p>The Avestan language is only known from the Avesta and otherwise unattested. As a result, there is no external evidence on which to base the time frame during which the Avestan language was spoken and all attempts have to rely on internal evidence. Such attempts were often based on the life of <a href="/wiki/Zarathustra" class="mw-redirect" title="Zarathustra">Zarathustra</a> as the most distinct event in the <a href="/wiki/Avestan_period" title="Avestan period">Avestan period</a>. Zarathustra was traditionally based in the 6th century BCE meaning that Old Avestan would have been spoken during the early <a href="/wiki/Achaemenid_empire" class="mw-redirect" title="Achaemenid empire">Achaemenid period</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Given that a substantial time must have passed between Old Avestan and Young Avestan, the latter would have been spoken somewhere during the <a href="/wiki/Hellenistic_period" title="Hellenistic period">Hellenistic</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Parthian_empire" class="mw-redirect" title="Parthian empire">Parthian period</a> of Iranian history.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>However, more recent scholarship has increasingly shifted to an earlier dating.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> The literature presents a number of reasons for this shift, based on both the Old Avestan and the Young Avestan material. As regards Old Avestan, the <a href="/wiki/Gatha_(Zoroaster)" title="Gatha (Zoroaster)">Gathas</a> show strong linguistic and cultural similarities with the <a href="/wiki/Rigveda" title="Rigveda">Rigveda</a>, which in turn is assumed to represent the second half of the second millennium BCE.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> As regards Young Avestan, texts like the <a href="/wiki/Yasht" title="Yasht">Yashts</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Vendidad" title="Vendidad">Vendidad</a> are <a href="/wiki/Avestan_geography" title="Avestan geography">situated</a> in the eastern parts of <a href="/wiki/Greater_Iran" title="Greater Iran">Greater Iran</a> and lack any discernible <a href="/wiki/Achaemenid_Empire" title="Achaemenid Empire">Persian</a> or <a href="/wiki/Median_Empire" class="mw-redirect" title="Median Empire">Median</a> influence from Western Iran.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> This is interpreted such that the bulk of this material, which has been produced several centuries after Zarathustra, must still predate the sixth century BCE.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> As a result, more recent scholarship often assumes that the major parts of the Young Avestan texts mainly reflect the first half of the first millennia BCE,<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> whereas the Old Avestan texts of Zarathustra may have been composed around 1000 BCE<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> or even as early as 1500 BCE.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Alphabet">Alphabet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Avestan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Alphabet"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Avestan_alphabet" title="Avestan alphabet">Avestan alphabet</a></div>
<p>The script used for writing Avestan developed during the 3rd or 4th century AD. By then the language had been extinct for many centuries, and remained in use only as a liturgical language of the Avesta canon. As is still the case today, the liturgies were memorized by the priesthood and recited by rote.
</p><p>The script devised to render Avestan was natively known as <i><a href="/wiki/Avestan_alphabet" title="Avestan alphabet">Din dabireh</a></i> "religion writing". It has 53 distinct characters and is written right-to-left. Among the 53 characters are about 30 letters that are&#160;– through the addition of various loops and flourishes&#160;– variations of the 13 graphemes of the <a href="/wiki/Pahlavi_scripts" title="Pahlavi scripts">cursive Pahlavi script</a> (i.e. "Book" Pahlavi) that is known from the post-Sassanian texts of Zoroastrian tradition. These symbols, like those of all the Pahlavi scripts, are in turn based on <a href="/wiki/Aramaic_script" class="mw-redirect" title="Aramaic script">Aramaic script</a> symbols. Avestan also incorporates several letters from other writing systems, most notably the vowels, which are mostly derived from Greek minuscules. A few letters were free inventions, as were also the symbols used for punctuation. Also, the Avestan alphabet has one letter that has no corresponding sound in the Avestan language; the character for <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/l/</span> (a sound that Avestan does not have) was added to write <a href="/wiki/Pazend" title="Pazend">Pazend</a> texts.
</p><p>The Avestan script is <a href="/wiki/Alphabet" title="Alphabet">alphabetic</a>, and the large number of letters suggests that its design was due to the need to render the orally recited texts with high phonetic precision. The correct enunciation of the liturgies was (and still is) considered necessary for the prayers to be effective.
</p><p>The Zoroastrians of India, who represent one of the largest surviving Zoroastrian communities worldwide, also transcribe Avestan in <a href="/wiki/Br%C4%81hm%C4%AB_script" class="mw-redirect" title="Brāhmī script">Brahmi</a>-based scripts. This is a relatively recent development first seen in the <abbr title="circa">c.</abbr><span style="white-space:nowrap;">&#8201;12th century</span> texts of Neryosang Dhaval and other Parsi Sanskritist theologians of that era, which are roughly contemporary with the oldest surviving manuscripts in Avestan script. Today, Avestan is most commonly typeset in the <a href="/wiki/Gujarati_script" title="Gujarati script">Gujarati script</a> (<a href="/wiki/Gujarati_language" title="Gujarati language">Gujarati</a> being the traditional language of the Indian Zoroastrians). Some Avestan letters with no corresponding symbol are synthesized with additional diacritical marks, for example, the <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/z/</span> in <i>zaraθuštra</i> is written with <i>j</i> with a dot below.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Phonology">Phonology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Avestan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Phonology"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Avestan_phonology" title="Avestan phonology">Avestan phonology</a></div>
<p>Avestan has retained voiced sibilants, and has fricative rather than aspirate series. There are various conventions for transliteration of Dīn Dabireh, the one adopted for this article being:
</p><p>Vowels:
</p>
<dl><dd>a ā ə ə̄ e ē o ō å ą i ī u ū</dd></dl>
<p>Consonants:
</p>
<dl><dd>k g γ x xʷ č ǰ t d δ θ t̰ p b β f</dd>
<dd>ŋ ŋʷ ṇ ń n m y w r s z š ṣ̌ ž h</dd></dl>
<p>The glides <i>y</i> and <i>w</i> are often transcribed as <i>ii</i> and <i>uu</i>, imitating Dīn Dabireh orthography. The letter transcribed <i>t̰</i> indicates an allophone of <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/t/</span> with <a href="/wiki/No_audible_release" title="No audible release">no audible release</a> at the end of a word and before certain <a href="/wiki/Obstruent" title="Obstruent">obstruents</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
