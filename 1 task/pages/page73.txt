					<div id="mw-content-text" class="mw-body-content"><div class="mw-content-ltr mw-parser-output" lang="en" dir="ltr"><style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1033289096">.mw-parser-output .hatnote{font-style:italic}.mw-parser-output div.hatnote{padding-left:1.6em;margin-bottom:0.5em}.mw-parser-output .hatnote i{font-style:normal}.mw-parser-output .hatnote+link+.hatnote{margin-top:-0.5em}</style><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">This article is about the form of language in general. For particular languages, see <a class="mw-selflink-fragment" href="#List_of_notable_pidgins">Pidgin §&#160;List of notable pidgins</a>. For the type of bird, see <a href="/wiki/Pigeon" class="mw-redirect" title="Pigeon">pigeon</a>. For the IM client, see <a href="/wiki/Pidgin_(software)" title="Pidgin (software)">Pidgin (software)</a>. For other uses, see <a href="/wiki/Pidgin_(disambiguation)" class="mw-disambig" title="Pidgin (disambiguation)">Pidgin (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Simplified language</div>
<p>A <b>pidgin</b><sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> <span class="rt-commentedText nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt" lang="en-fonipa"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="&#39;p&#39; in &#39;pie&#39;">p</span><span title="/ɪ/: &#39;i&#39; in &#39;kit&#39;">ɪ</span><span title="/dʒ/: &#39;j&#39; in &#39;jam&#39;">dʒ</span><span title="/ɪ/: &#39;i&#39; in &#39;kit&#39;">ɪ</span><span title="&#39;n&#39; in &#39;nigh&#39;">n</span></span>/</a></span></span>, or <b>pidgin language</b>, is a grammatically simplified means of communication that develops between two or more groups of people that do not have a <a href="/wiki/Language" title="Language">language</a> in common: typically, its <a href="/wiki/Vocabulary" title="Vocabulary">vocabulary</a> and <a href="/wiki/Grammar" title="Grammar">grammar</a> are limited and often drawn from several languages. It is most commonly employed in situations such as <a href="/wiki/Trade" title="Trade">trade</a>, or where both groups speak languages different from the language of the country in which they reside (but where there is no common language between the groups). <a href="/wiki/Linguist" class="mw-redirect" title="Linguist">Linguists</a> do not typically consider pidgins full or complete languages.
</p><p>Fundamentally, a pidgin is a simplified means of linguistic communication, as it is constructed impromptu, or by convention, between individuals or groups of people. A pidgin is not the <a href="/wiki/Native_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Native language">native language</a> of any speech community, but is instead learned as a second language.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A pidgin may be built from words, sounds, or body language from a multitude of languages as well as <a href="/wiki/Onomatopoeia" title="Onomatopoeia">onomatopoeia</a>. As the <a href="/wiki/Lexicon" title="Lexicon">lexicon</a> of any pidgin will be limited to core vocabulary, words with only a specific meaning in the <a href="/wiki/Lexifier" title="Lexifier">lexifier</a> language may acquire a completely new (or additional) meaning in the pidgin.
</p><p>Pidgins have historically been considered a form of <i><a href="/wiki/Patois" title="Patois">patois</a></i>, unsophisticated simplified versions of their lexifiers, and as such usually have low <a href="/wiki/Prestige_(sociolinguistics)" title="Prestige (sociolinguistics)">prestige</a> with respect to other languages.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> However, not all simplified or "unsophisticated" forms of a language are pidgins. Each pidgin has its own norms of usage which must be learned for proficiency in the pidgin.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A pidgin differs from a <a href="/wiki/Creole_language" title="Creole language">creole</a>, which is the <a href="/wiki/First_language" title="First language">first language</a> of a speech community of <a href="/wiki/Native_speaker" class="mw-redirect" title="Native speaker">native speakers</a> that at one point arose from a pidgin. Unlike pidgins, creoles have fully developed vocabulary and patterned grammar. Most linguists believe that a creole develops through a process of <a href="/wiki/Nativization" title="Nativization">nativization</a> of a pidgin when children of speakers of an acquired pidgin learn it and use it as their native language.
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pidgin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Pidgin</i> derives from a <a href="/wiki/Chinese_Language" class="mw-redirect" title="Chinese Language">Chinese</a> pronunciation of the English word <i>business</i>, and all attestations from the first half of the nineteenth century given in the third edition of the <i><a href="/wiki/Oxford_English_Dictionary" title="Oxford English Dictionary">Oxford English Dictionary</a></i> mean "business; an action, occupation, or affair" (the earliest being from 1807). The term <i>pidgin English</i> ('business English'), first attested in 1855, shows the term in transition to referring to language, and by the 1860s the term <i>pidgin</i> alone could refer to Pidgin English. The term came to be used in a more general linguistic sense to refer to any simplified language by the late 19th century.<sup id="cite_ref-oed.com_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oed.com-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-etymonline_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-etymonline-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A popular <a href="/wiki/False_etymology" title="False etymology">false etymology</a> for <i>pidgin</i> is English <i><a href="/wiki/Pigeon" class="mw-redirect" title="Pigeon">pigeon</a></i>, a bird sometimes used for carrying brief written messages, especially in times prior to modern telecommunications.<sup id="cite_ref-oed.com_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oed.com-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Terminology">Terminology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pidgin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Terminology"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The word <i>pidgin</i>, formerly also spelled <i>pigion</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-etymonline_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-etymonline-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> used to refer originally to <a href="/wiki/Chinese_Pidgin_English" title="Chinese Pidgin English">Chinese Pidgin English</a>, but was later generalized to refer to any pidgin.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> <i>Pidgin</i> may also be used as the specific name for local pidgins or <a href="/wiki/Creole_language" title="Creole language">creoles</a>, in places where they are spoken. For example, the name of the creole language <a href="/wiki/Tok_Pisin" title="Tok Pisin">Tok Pisin</a> derives from the English words <i>talk pidgin</i>. Its speakers usually refer to it simply as "pidgin" when speaking English.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Likewise, <a href="/wiki/Hawaiian_Creole_English" class="mw-redirect" title="Hawaiian Creole English">Hawaiian Creole English</a> is commonly referred to by its speakers as "Pidgin".
</p><p>The term <i>jargon</i> has also been used to refer to pidgins, and is found in the names of some pidgins, such as <a href="/wiki/Chinook_Jargon" title="Chinook Jargon">Chinook Jargon</a>. In this context, linguists today use <i>jargon</i> to denote a particularly rudimentary type of pidgin;<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> however, this usage is rather rare, and the term <i><a href="/wiki/Jargon" title="Jargon">jargon</a></i> most often refers to the words particular to a given profession.
</p><p>Pidgins may start out as or become trade languages, such as <a href="/wiki/Tok_Pisin" title="Tok Pisin">Tok Pisin</a>. Trade languages can eventually evolve into fully developed languages in their own right such as <a href="/wiki/Swahili_language" title="Swahili language">Swahili</a>, distinct from the languages they were originally influenced by. Trade languages and pidgins can also influence an established language's <a href="/wiki/Vernacular" title="Vernacular">vernacular</a>, especially amongst people who are directly involved in a trade where that pidgin is commonly used, which can alternatively result in a <a href="/wiki/Regional_dialect" class="mw-redirect" title="Regional dialect">regional dialect</a> being developed.
</p>

<p>Pidgins are usually less morphologically complex but more syntactically rigid than other languages, and usually have fewer morphosyntactic irregularities than other languages.
</p><p>Characteristics shared by most pidgins:
</p>
<ul><li>Typologically most closely resemble <a href="/wiki/Isolating_languages" class="mw-redirect" title="Isolating languages">isolating languages</a></li>
<li>Uncomplicated <a href="/wiki/Clause" title="Clause">clausal</a> structure (e.g., no <a href="/wiki/Subordinate_clause" class="mw-redirect" title="Subordinate clause">embedded</a> clauses, etc.)</li>
<li>Reduction or elimination of <a href="/wiki/Syllable_coda" class="mw-redirect" title="Syllable coda">syllable codas</a></li>
<li>Reduction of consonant clusters or breaking them with <a href="/wiki/Epenthesis" title="Epenthesis">epenthesis</a></li>
<li>Elimination of <a href="/wiki/Aspiration_(linguistics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Aspiration (linguistics)">aspiration</a> or <a href="/wiki/Sound_changes" class="mw-redirect" title="Sound changes">sound changes</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Monophthongization" title="Monophthongization">Monophthongization</a> is common, employment of as few basic vowels as possible, such as <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[a,<span class="wrap"> </span>e,<span class="wrap"> </span>i,<span class="wrap"> </span>o,<span class="wrap"> </span>u]</span></li>
<li>Lack of <a href="/wiki/Morphophonology" title="Morphophonology">morphophonemic variation</a></li>
<li>Lack of <a href="/wiki/Tone_(linguistics)" title="Tone (linguistics)">tones</a>, such as those found in <a href="/wiki/Niger-Congo_languages" class="mw-redirect" title="Niger-Congo languages">Niger-Congo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Austroasiatic_languages" title="Austroasiatic languages">Austroasiatic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sino-Tibetan_languages" title="Sino-Tibetan languages">Sino-Tibetan</a> language families and in various families of the <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_languages_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous languages of the Americas">indigenous languages of the Americas</a></li>
<li>Lack of <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_tense" title="Grammatical tense">grammatical tense</a>; use of separate words to indicate tense, usually preceding the <a href="/wiki/Verb" title="Verb">verb</a></li>
<li>Lack of <a href="/wiki/Conjugation_(grammar)" class="mw-redirect" title="Conjugation (grammar)">conjugation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Declension" title="Declension">declension</a> or <a href="/wiki/Agreement_(linguistics)" title="Agreement (linguistics)">agreement</a></li>
<li>Lack of <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_gender" title="Grammatical gender">grammatical gender</a> or <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_number" title="Grammatical number">number</a>, commonly supplanted by <a href="/wiki/Reduplication" title="Reduplication">reduplication</a> to represent <a href="/wiki/Plurals" class="mw-redirect" title="Plurals">plurals</a> and <a href="/wiki/Superlatives" class="mw-redirect" title="Superlatives">superlatives</a>, and other parts of speech that represent the concept being increased and clear indication of the gender of animated objects.</li>
<li>Lack of clear <a href="/wiki/Parts_of_speech" class="mw-redirect" title="Parts of speech">parts of speech</a> or word categorization; common use and derivation of new vocabulary through <a href="/wiki/Conversion_(word_formation)" title="Conversion (word formation)">conversion</a>, e.g. <a href="/wiki/Nominalization" title="Nominalization">nominalization</a>, <a href="/wiki/Verbification" class="mw-redirect" title="Verbification">verbification</a>, adjectivization etc.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pidgin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Development"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The initial development of a pidgin usually requires:
</p>
<ul><li>prolonged, regular contact between the different language communities</li>
<li>a need to communicate between them</li>
<li>an absence of (or absence of widespread proficiency in) a widespread, accessible <a href="/wiki/Interlanguage" title="Interlanguage">interlanguage</a></li></ul>
<p>Keith Whinnom (in <a href="#CITEREFHymes1971">Hymes (1971)</a>) suggests that pidgins need three languages to form, with one (the superstrate) being clearly dominant over the others.
</p><p>Linguists sometimes posit that pidgins can become <a href="/wiki/Creole_language" title="Creole language">creole languages</a> when a generation of children learn a pidgin as their first language,<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
a process that regularizes speaker-dependent variation in grammar. Creoles can then replace the existing mix of languages to become the native language of a community (such as the <a href="/wiki/Chavacano_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Chavacano language">Chavacano language</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Leone_Krio_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Sierra Leone Krio language">Krio</a> in <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Leone" title="Sierra Leone">Sierra Leone</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tok_Pisin" title="Tok Pisin">Tok Pisin</a> in <a href="/wiki/Papua_New_Guinea" title="Papua New Guinea">Papua New Guinea</a>). However, not all pidgins become creole languages; a pidgin may die out before this phase would occur (e.g. the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Lingua_Franca" title="Mediterranean Lingua Franca">Mediterranean Lingua Franca</a>).
</p><p>Other scholars, such as <a href="/wiki/Salikoko_Mufwene" title="Salikoko Mufwene">Salikoko Mufwene</a>, argue that pidgins and creoles arise independently under different circumstances, and that a pidgin need not always precede a creole nor a creole evolve from a pidgin. Pidgins, according to Mufwene, emerged among trade colonies among "users who preserved their native vernaculars for their day-to-day interactions". Creoles, meanwhile, developed in settlement colonies in which speakers of a European language, often <a href="/wiki/Indentured_servants" class="mw-redirect" title="Indentured servants">indentured servants</a> whose language would be far from the standard in the first place, interacted extensively with non-European <a href="/wiki/Slave" class="mw-redirect" title="Slave">slaves</a>, absorbing certain words and features from the slaves' non-European native languages, resulting in a heavily <a href="/wiki/Basilect" class="mw-redirect" title="Basilect">basilectalized</a> version of the original language. These servants and slaves would come to use the creole as an everyday vernacular, rather than merely in situations in which contact with a speaker of the superstrate was necessary.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
