<p><b>Boro</b><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> (बर&#x2bc; or बड़ो <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="bo-Latn-fonipa"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Tibetan" title="Help:IPA/Tibetan">&#91;bɔɽo&#93;</a></span>), also rendered <b>Bodo</b>,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> is a <a href="/wiki/Sino-Tibetan_languages" title="Sino-Tibetan languages">Sino-Tibetan language</a> spoken primarily by the <a href="/wiki/Boro_people" title="Boro people">Boros</a> of <a href="/wiki/Northeast_India" title="Northeast India">Northeast India</a> and the neighboring nations of <a href="/wiki/Nepal" title="Nepal">Nepal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bangladesh" title="Bangladesh">Bangladesh</a>. It is an <a href="/wiki/Official_language" title="Official language">official language</a> of the Indian state of <a href="/wiki/Assam" title="Assam">Assam</a>, predominantly spoken in the <a href="/wiki/Bodoland_Territorial_Region" title="Bodoland Territorial Region">Bodoland Territorial Region</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> It is also one of the twenty-two languages listed in the <a href="/wiki/Languages_with_official_status_in_India#Eighth_Schedule_to_the_Constitution" title="Languages with official status in India">Eighth Schedule</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_India" title="Constitution of India">Constitution of India</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Since 1975 the language has been written using the <a href="/wiki/Devanagari" title="Devanagari">Devanagari</a> script. It was formerly written using <a href="/wiki/Latin_script" title="Latin script">Latin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Nagari" class="mw-redirect" title="Eastern Nagari">Eastern-Nagari</a> scripts. Some scholars have suggested that the language used to have its own now lost script known as Deodhai.
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boro_language_(India)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As result of socio-political awakenings and movements launched by different Boro organisations since 1913, the language was introduced in 1963 as a medium of instruction in the primary schools in Boro dominated areas. Today, the Boro language serves as a medium of instruction up to the secondary level and it is an associated official language in the state of Assam. Boro language and literature have been offered as a post-graduate course at the <a href="/wiki/Gauhati_University" title="Gauhati University">University of Guwahati</a> since 1996. There are a large number of Boro books on poetry, drama, short stories, novels, biography, travelogues, children's literature, and literary criticism. Though there exists different dialects, the Western Boro dialect <i>Swnabari</i> form used around <a href="/wiki/Kokrajhar_district" title="Kokrajhar district">Kokrajhar district</a> has emerged as the standard.<sup id="cite_ref-RCILTS,_IIT_Guwahati_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RCILTS,_IIT_Guwahati-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Writing_system_and_script_movement">Writing system and script movement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boro_language_(India)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Writing system and script movement"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It is reported that the Boro and the <a href="/wiki/Dimasa_language" title="Dimasa language">Dimasa</a> languages used a script called <i>Deodhai</i> that is no longer attested.<sup id="cite_ref-bhatta64_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bhatta64-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Latin_script" title="Latin script">Latin script</a> was used first to write down the language, when a prayer book was published in 1843, and then extensively used by Endle beginning 1884 and in 1904, when the script was used to teach children. The first use of the Assamese/Bengali script occurred in 1915 (<i>Boroni Fisa o Ayen</i>) and the first magazine, <i>Bibar</i> (1924-1940) was tri-lingual in Boro, Assamese and Bengali, with Boro written in Assamese/Bengali script. In 1952, the Bodo Sahitya Sabha decided to use the Assamese script exclusively for the language.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>  In 1963 Boro was introduced in schools as a medium of instruction, in which Assamese script was used.<sup id="cite_ref-harvcol&#124;Sarmah&#124;2014&#124;p=1336_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-harvcol|Sarmah|2014|p=1336-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>   Into the 1960s the Boro language was predominantly written in Assamese/Bengali script, though the Christian community continued to use Latin for Boro.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-harvcol&#124;Sarmah&#124;2014&#124;p=1336_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-harvcol|Sarmah|2014|p=1336-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Boro_Script_Movement">Boro Script Movement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boro_language_(India)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Boro Script Movement"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>With the <a href="/wiki/Assamese_Language_Movement" title="Assamese Language Movement">Assamese Language Movement</a> in Assam peaking in the 1960s the Boro community felt threatened and decided to not use the Assamese script.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> After a series of proposals and expert committees the <a href="/wiki/Bodo_Sahitya_Sabha" title="Bodo Sahitya Sabha">Bodo Sahitya Sabha</a> reversed itself in 1970 and unanimously decided to adopt the <a href="/wiki/Latin_script" title="Latin script">Latin script</a> for the language in its 11th annual conference.<sup id="cite_ref-harvcol&#124;Sarmah&#124;2014&#124;p=1336_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-harvcol|Sarmah|2014|p=1336-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The BSS submitted this demand to the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Assam" title="Government of Assam">Assam Government</a> in 1971, which was rejected on the grounds that the Latin script was of foreign origin.  This instigated a movement for the Latin script which became a part of the movement for a separate state, <i><a href="/wiki/Bodoland_Territorial_Region" title="Bodoland Territorial Region">Udayachal</a></i>, then led by the Plains Tribe Council of Assam (PTCA).  In this context, the Boro leaders were advised by the Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Indira_Gandhi" title="Indira Gandhi">Indira Gandhi</a> to choose any Indian script other than Latin.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> In defiance of the Assam Government the BSS, in April 1974, went ahead and published <i>Bithorai</i>, a Boro textbook, in Latin script and asked school teachers to follow it.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Retaliating against the unilateral decision, the Assam Government withheld grants to schools using the Latin script.  This triggered a phase of active movement that was joined by the All Bodo Students' Union (ABSU) and the PTCA.  This led to a critical situation in November 1974 when fifteen volunteers of the movement died in a police firing, and many others were injured.  Unable to resolve the issue, the Assam Government referred the matter to the Union Government.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>  In the discussion, the Union Government suggested <a href="/wiki/Devanagari_script" class="mw-redirect" title="Devanagari script">Devanagari script</a> as the solution to the problem, which the BSS accepted in the Memorandum of Understanding in April 1975, and adopted later year in the Annual Conference.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>  This ended the Boro Script Movement.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Final_Acceptance_of_Devanagari_script">Final Acceptance of Devanagari script</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boro_language_(India)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Final Acceptance of Devanagari script"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Bodo_language_textbooks.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Bodo_language_textbooks.jpg/220px-Bodo_language_textbooks.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Bodo_language_textbooks.jpg/330px-Bodo_language_textbooks.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Bodo_language_textbooks.jpg/440px-Bodo_language_textbooks.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4000" data-file-height="3000" /></a><figcaption>Boro-language textbooks for secondary schools written in Devanagari script</figcaption></figure>
<p>The Devanagari script for Boro was an unexpected development and it was not immediately accepted by the wider Boro community.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>  The BSS failed to implement the use of the Devanagari script, and writers continued to use the Assamese/Bengali and Latin scripts.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>  In 1982, ABSU included the demand of the Latin script in Boro schools in its charter of Demands.  Following an expert committee report, constituted by BSS, the Bodoland Autonomous Council adopted a resolution to use Latin script in its territory, which the Assam Government too accepted.
</p><p>Nevertheless, in the discussion with the <a href="/wiki/Bodo_Liberation_Tigers" class="mw-redirect" title="Bodo Liberation Tigers">Bodo Liberation Tigers</a>, the Union Government demanded the implementation of the earlier agreement with the BSS on the use of the Devanagari script if the Boro language was to be included in the Eighth Schedule of the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Constitution" class="mw-redirect" title="Indian Constitution">Indian Constitution</a>.  Following this, the ABSU and the BSS <a href="/wiki/Surrendered" class="mw-redirect" title="Surrendered">surrendered</a> and agreed to use the Devanagari script exclusively, and the matter was settled.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dialects">Dialects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boro_language_(India)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Dialects"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Kiryu (2012) suggests that the language of the Meche people and the Boro of Bengal are western dialects whereas the dialects in Assam are the eastern dialects of Boro.  The western dialects differ in phonology and grammar but are mutually intelligible.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> The Kokrajhar variety of the eastern dialects has been promoted as standard, at least for the eastern dialects.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Phonology">Phonology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boro_language_(India)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Phonology"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The Boro language has a total of 30 phonemes: 6 vowels, 16 consonants, and 8 diphthongs—with a strong prevalence of the <a href="/wiki/High_back_unrounded_vowel" class="mw-redirect" title="High back unrounded vowel">high back unrounded vowel</a> /ɯ/.  The Boro language use tones to distinguish words. There are three different tones: high, medium and low. The difference between high and low tones is apparent and quite common.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boro_language_(India)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Education"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Boro is a compulsory subject till class 10 in tribal areas of Assam who do not want to study Assamese. The subject is mandatory in all schools including those under the <a href="/wiki/Central_Board_of_Secondary_Education" title="Central Board of Secondary Education">Central Board of Secondary Education</a> (CBSE) and Kendriya Vidyalaya Sangathan (KVS). The legislation was passed in the assembly in August 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sample_text">Sample text</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boro_language_(India)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Sample text"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The following is a sample text in Boro, of Article 1 of the <a href="/wiki/Universal_Declaration_of_Human_Rights" title="Universal Declaration of Human Rights">Universal Declaration of Human Rights</a>, with a transliteration (IAST) and transcription (<a href="/wiki/International_Phonetic_Alphabet" title="International Phonetic Alphabet">IPA</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dt>Boro in Devanagari Script</dt>
<dd>गासै सुबुं आनो उदांयै मान सनमान आरो मोनथाय लाना जोनोम लायो। बिसोरो मोजां- गाज्रि सान्नो हानाय गोहो आरो सोलो दं। बिसोरो गावखौनो गाव बिदा फंबाय बायदि बाहाय लायनांगौ।</dd></dl>
<dl><dt>Transliteration (<a href="/wiki/ISO_15919" title="ISO 15919">ISO 15919</a>)</dt>
<dd><i>Gāsai subuṁ āno udāṁyai mān sanmān āro monthāy lānā jonom lāyo. Bisorā mojāṁ-gājri sānno hānāy goho āro solo daṁ. Bisoro gāvkhauno gāv bidā phaṁbāy bāydi bāhāy lāynāṁgau.</i></dd></dl>
<dl><dt>Boro in Latin script</dt>
<dd><i>Gaswi subung anw udangwi man sanman arw mwnthai lana jwnwm layw. Biswrw mwjang-gajri sannw hanai gwhw arw swlw dong. Biswrw gaokhwunw gao bida phongbai baidi bahai lainangwu.</i></dd></dl>
<dl><dt>Translation (grammatical)</dt></dl>
<dl><dd>All human beings are born free and equal in dignity and rights. They are endowed with reason and conscience and should act towards one another in a spirit of brotherhood.</dd></dl>
