<p><b>Dungan</b> (<span class="rt-commentedText nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt" lang="en-fonipa"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="&#39;d&#39; in &#39;dye&#39;">d</span><span title="/ʊ/: &#39;u&#39; in &#39;push&#39;">ʊ</span><span title="/ŋ/: &#39;ng&#39; in &#39;sing&#39;">ŋ</span><span title="/ɡ/: &#39;g&#39; in &#39;guy&#39;">ɡ</span><span title="/ɑː/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;father&#39;">ɑː</span><span title="&#39;n&#39; in &#39;nigh&#39;">n</span></span>/</a></span></span> or <span class="rt-commentedText nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt" lang="en-fonipa"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="&#39;d&#39; in &#39;dye&#39;">d</span><span title="/ʌ/: &#39;u&#39; in &#39;cut&#39;">ʌ</span><span title="/ŋ/: &#39;ng&#39; in &#39;sing&#39;">ŋ</span><span title="/ɡ/: &#39;g&#39; in &#39;guy&#39;">ɡ</span><span title="/ə/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;about&#39;">ə</span><span title="&#39;n&#39; in &#39;nigh&#39;">n</span></span>/</a></span></span>) is a <a href="/wiki/Sinitic_languages" title="Sinitic languages">Sinitic language</a><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;note 1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> spoken primarily in <a href="/wiki/Kazakhstan" title="Kazakhstan">Kazakhstan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kyrgyzstan" title="Kyrgyzstan">Kyrgyzstan</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Dungan_people" title="Dungan people">Dungan people</a>, an ethnic group related to the <a href="/wiki/Hui_people" title="Hui people">Hui people</a> of <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>. Although it is derived from the <a href="/wiki/Central_Plains_Mandarin" title="Central Plains Mandarin">Central Plains Mandarin</a> of <a href="/wiki/Gansu" title="Gansu">Gansu</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shaanxi" title="Shaanxi">Shaanxi</a>, it is written in <a href="/wiki/Cyrillic_script" title="Cyrillic script">Cyrillic</a> (or <a href="/wiki/Xiao%27erjing" title="Xiao&#39;erjing">Xiao'erjing</a>) and contains loanwords and archaisms not found in other modern varieties of Mandarin.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dungan_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Dungan people of <a href="/wiki/Kazakhstan" title="Kazakhstan">Kazakhstan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kyrgyzstan" title="Kyrgyzstan">Kyrgyzstan</a> (with smaller groups living in other post-Soviet states) are the descendants of several groups of the <a href="/wiki/Hui_people" title="Hui people">Hui people</a> that migrated to the region in the 1870s and the 1880s after the defeat of the <a href="/wiki/Dungan_Revolt_(1862%E2%80%931877)" title="Dungan Revolt (1862–1877)">Dungan revolt</a> in Northwestern China. The Hui of Northwestern China (often referred to as "Dungans" or "Tungani" by the 19th-century western writers as well as by members of <a href="/wiki/Turkic_peoples" title="Turkic peoples">Turkic nationalities</a> in China and Central Asia) would normally speak the same Mandarin dialect as the Han people in the same area<sup id="cite_ref-g393_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-g393-4">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> (or in the area from which the particular Hui community had been resettled). At the same time, due to their unique history, their speech would be rich in <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Islamic</a> or Islam-influenced terminology, based on loanwords from Arabic, Persian and Turkic languages, as well as translations of them into Chinese.<sup id="cite_ref-g393_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-g393-4">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The Hui traders in the bazaars would be able to use Arabic or Persian numbers when talking between themselves, to keep their communications secret from Han bystanders.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> While not constituting a separate language, these words, phrases and turns of speech, known as <i>Huihui hua</i> (<span title="Dungan-language text"><span lang="dng">回回話</span></span>, "Hui speech"), served as markers of group identity.<sup id="cite_ref-g393_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-g393-4">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> As early 20th century travellers in Northwestern China would note, "the <a href="/wiki/Mohammedan" title="Mohammedan">Mohammedan</a> Chinese have to some extent a vocabulary and always a style and manner of speech, all their own".<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>As the Dungans in the Russian Empire — and even more so in the Soviet Union — were isolated from China, their language experienced significant influence from the <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a> and the Turkic languages of their neighbors.
</p><p>In the Soviet Union, a written standard of the Dungan language was developed, based on a dialect of the <a href="/wiki/Gansu" title="Gansu">Gansu Province</a>, rather than the Beijing base of <a href="/wiki/Standard_Chinese" title="Standard Chinese">Standard Chinese</a>. The language was used in the schools in Dungan villages. In the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet</a> time there were several school textbooks published for studying the Dungan language, a three volume Russian–Dungan dictionary (14,000 words), the Dungan–Russian dictionary, <a href="/wiki/Linguistics" title="Linguistics">linguistics</a> monographs on the language and books in Dungan. The first Dungan-language newspaper was established in 1932; it continues publication today in weekly form.
</p><p>When <a href="/wiki/Dru_C._Gladney" title="Dru C. Gladney">Dru C. Gladney</a>, who had spent some years working with the Hui people in China, met with Dungans in <a href="/wiki/Almaty" title="Almaty">Almaty</a> in 1988, he described the experience as speaking "in a hybrid Gansu dialect that combined Turkish and Russian lexical items".<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mutual_intelligibility_with_Mandarin_dialects">Mutual intelligibility with Mandarin dialects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dungan_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Mutual intelligibility with Mandarin dialects"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There is a varying degree of mutual intelligibility between Dungan and various Mandarin dialects. <a href="/wiki/Central_Plains_Mandarin" title="Central Plains Mandarin">Central Plains Mandarin</a> varieties are understood by Dungans. On the other hand, Dungan speakers like <a href="/wiki/Iasyr_Shivaza" title="Iasyr Shivaza">Iasyr Shivaza</a> and others have reported that people who speak the <a href="/wiki/Beijing_dialect" title="Beijing dialect">Beijing Mandarin dialect</a> can understand Dungan, but Dungans could not understand Beijing Mandarin.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Demographics">Demographics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dungan_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Demographics"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Dungan is spoken primarily in <a href="/wiki/Kyrgyzstan" title="Kyrgyzstan">Kyrgyzstan</a>, with speakers in <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kazakhstan" title="Kazakhstan">Kazakhstan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Uzbekistan" title="Uzbekistan">Uzbekistan</a> as well. The Dungan ethnic group are the descendants of refugees from China who migrated west into Central Asia.
</p><p>According to the Soviet census statistics from 1970 to 1989, the Dungan maintained the use of their ethnic language much more successfully than other minority ethnic groups in Central Asia; however, in the post-Soviet period, the proportion of Dungans speaking the Dungan language as their native language appears to have fallen sharply.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Grammar">Grammar</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dungan_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Grammar"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Classifiers">Classifiers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dungan_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Classifiers"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Chinese_classifier" title="Chinese classifier">Chinese classifier</a></div>
<p>Chinese varieties usually have different <a href="/wiki/Classifier_(linguistics)" title="Classifier (linguistics)">classifiers</a> for different types of nouns, with northern varieties tending to have fewer classifiers than southern ones. <span title="Chinese-language text"><span lang="zh"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%80%8B" class="extiw" title="wikt:個">個</a></span></span> (<span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA" title="Help:IPA">&#91;kə&#93;</a></span>) is the only classifier found in the Dungan language, though not the only <a href="/wiki/Measure_word" title="Measure word">measure word</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Phonology">Phonology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dungan_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Phonology"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In basic structure and vocabulary, the Dungan language is not very different from <a href="/wiki/Mandarin_Chinese" title="Mandarin Chinese">Mandarin Chinese</a>, specifically a variety of <a href="/wiki/Zhongyuan_Mandarin" class="mw-redirect" title="Zhongyuan Mandarin">Zhongyuan Mandarin</a> (not Lan-Yin Mandarin) spoken in the southern part of the province of Gansu and the western part of the valley of <a href="/wiki/Guanzhong" title="Guanzhong">Guanzhong</a> in the province of <a href="/wiki/Shaanxi" title="Shaanxi">Shaanxi</a>. Like other <a href="/wiki/Varieties_of_Chinese" title="Varieties of Chinese">Chinese varieties</a>, Dungan is <a href="/wiki/Tone_(linguistics)" title="Tone (linguistics)">tonal</a>. There are two main dialects, one with 4 tones and the other, considered standard, with 3 tones in the final position in phonetic words and 4 tones in the nonfinal position.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Vocabulary">Vocabulary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dungan_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Vocabulary"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Basilect" class="mw-redirect" title="Basilect">basilects</a> of Gansu/Shaanxi Mandarin and Dungan are largely <a href="/wiki/Mutual_intelligibility" title="Mutual intelligibility">mutually intelligible</a>; Chinese journalists conversant in one of those Mandarin dialects report that they can make themselves understood when communicating with Dungan speakers. However, even at the level of basic vocabulary, Dungan contains many words not present in modern Mandarin dialects, such as <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arabic_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Turkic_languages" title="Turkic languages">Turkic</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Persian_language" title="Persian language">Persian</a> <a href="/wiki/Loanwords" class="mw-redirect" title="Loanwords">loanwords</a>. Furthermore, Dungan contains some archaic <a href="/wiki/Qing_dynasty" title="Qing dynasty">Qing dynasty</a>-era Chinese vocabulary. Because of this, some Dungan vocabulary may sound old-fashioned to Chinese people. For example, they refer to a President as an "<a href="/wiki/Emperor_of_China" title="Emperor of China">Emperor</a>" (Хуаңды/皇帝, <i>huan'g-di</i>) and call government offices <i><a href="/wiki/Yamen" title="Yamen">yamen</a></i> (ямын/衙門, <i>ya-min</i>), a term for mandarins' offices in ancient China.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Furthermore, the <a href="/wiki/Acrolect" class="mw-redirect" title="Acrolect">acrolects</a> of Dungan and Gansu/Shaanxi Mandarin have diverged significantly due to time and cultural influences. During the 20th century, translators and intellectuals introduced many <a href="/wiki/Neologism" title="Neologism">neologisms</a> and <a href="/wiki/Calque" title="Calque">calques</a> into the Chinese language, especially for political and technical concepts. However, the Dungan, cut off from the mainstream of Chinese discourse by orthographic barriers, instead borrowed words for those same concepts from Russian, with which they came into contact through government and higher education. As a result of these borrowings, the equivalent standard Chinese terms are not widely known or understood among the Dungan.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Writing_system">Writing system</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dungan_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Writing system"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The modern Dungan language is the only spoken Chinese that is written in the Cyrillic alphabet, as they lived under Soviet rule. It is a Russian-based alphabet plus five special letters: <a href="/wiki/%D3%98" class="mw-redirect" title="Ә">Ә</a>, <a href="/wiki/%D2%96" class="mw-redirect" title="Җ">Җ</a>, <a href="/wiki/%D2%A2" class="mw-redirect" title="Ң">Ң</a>, <a href="/wiki/%D2%AE" class="mw-redirect" title="Ү">Ү</a> and <a href="/wiki/%D0%8E" class="mw-redirect" title="Ў">Ў</a>. As such, it differs somewhat from the <a href="/wiki/Cyrillization_of_Chinese" title="Cyrillization of Chinese">Palladius System</a> that is normally used in Russia to write Chinese in Cyrillic.
</p>

<dl><dt>Note</dt></dl>
<ul><li>The letters ъ and ь are only used to write Russian loanwords and tone markings on children's primers dictionaries<sup id="cite_ref-omniglot.com_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-omniglot.com-10">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>Dungan is unique in that it is one of the few <a href="/wiki/Varieties_of_Chinese" title="Varieties of Chinese">varieties of Chinese</a> that is not normally written using <a href="/wiki/Chinese_characters" title="Chinese characters">Chinese characters</a>. Though it may be seen written in Chinese characters, this writing system is now considered obsolete, much like the Arabic-script Xiao’erjing. Originally, the Dungan, who were <a href="/wiki/Muslim" class="mw-redirect" title="Muslim">Muslim</a> descendants of the <a href="/wiki/Hui_people" title="Hui people">Hui</a>, wrote their language in an <a href="/wiki/Arabic_script" title="Arabic script">Arabic</a>-based alphabet known as <a href="/wiki/Xiao%27erjing" title="Xiao&#39;erjing">Xiao'erjing</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> banned all Arabic scripts in the late 1920s<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2022)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>, which led to a <a href="/wiki/Latin_script" title="Latin script">Latin</a> orthography based on <a href="/wiki/Ya%C3%B1alif" title="Yañalif">Yañalif</a>. The Latin orthography lasted until 1940, when the Soviet government <a href="/wiki/Cyrillisation_in_the_Soviet_Union" title="Cyrillisation in the Soviet Union">promulgated</a> the current <a href="/wiki/Cyrillic_script" title="Cyrillic script">Cyrillic</a>-based system. Xiao'erjing is now virtually extinct in Dungan society, but it remains in limited use by some Hui communities in China.
</p><p>The writing system is based on the standard 3-tone dialect. Tone marks or numbering do not appear in general-purpose writing, but are specified in dictionaries, even for <a href="/wiki/Loanword" title="Loanword">loanwords</a>. The tones are specified using the soft sign, hard sign, or none.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Literature">Literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dungan_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Literature"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A number of books in Dungan language, including textbooks, Dungan-Russian and Russian-Dungan dictionaries, a Dungan etymological dictionary, collections of folk tales, original and translated fiction and poetry have been published in Kyrgyzstan. Usual print runs were no more than a few hundred copies. A newspaper in Dungan has been published as well.
</p><p>Works of the Dungan poet <a href="/wiki/Iasyr_Shivaza" title="Iasyr Shivaza">Yasir Shiwaza</a> have been translated into Russian, Standard Chinese and a number of other languages, with print runs in some of them been much higher than in the original Dungan. English translations of some of them, along with the original Dungan text, are available in the book by S. Rimsky-Korsakoff (1991).
</p>
