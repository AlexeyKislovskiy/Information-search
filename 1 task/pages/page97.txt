<p><b>Shor</b> (<a href="/wiki/Exonym_and_endonym" class="mw-redirect" title="Exonym and endonym">endonym</a>: шор тили, тадар тили) is a <a href="/wiki/Turkic_languages" title="Turkic languages">Turkic language</a> spoken by about 2,800 people in a region called <a href="/wiki/Mountain_Shoriya" class="mw-redirect" title="Mountain Shoriya">Mountain Shoriya</a>, in the <a href="/wiki/Kemerovo_Oblast" title="Kemerovo Oblast">Kemerovo Province</a> in Southwest <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a>, although the entire Shor population in this area is over 12000 people. Presently, not all ethnic Shors speak Shor and the language suffered a decline from the late 1930s to the early 1980s. During this period the Shor language was neither written nor taught in schools. However, since the 1980s and 1990s there has been a Shor language revival. The language is now taught at the <a href="/wiki/Novokuznetsk" title="Novokuznetsk">Novokuznetsk</a> branch of the <a href="/wiki/Kemerovo_State_University" title="Kemerovo State University">Kemerovo State University</a>.
</p><p>Like other <a href="/wiki/Siberian_Turkic_languages" title="Siberian Turkic languages">Siberian Turkic languages</a>, Shor has borrowed many roots from <a href="/wiki/Mongolian_language" title="Mongolian language">Mongolian</a>, as well as words from <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>. The two main dialects are Mrassu and Kondoma, named after the rivers in whose valleys they are spoken. From the point of view of classification of Turkic languages, these dialects belong to different branches of Turkic: According to the reflexes of the Proto-Turkic (PT) intervocalic -d- in modern languages (compare PT *adak, in modern Turkic languages meaning 'foot' or 'leg'), the Mrassu dialect is a -z- variety: azaq, the Kondoma dialect is a -y- variety: ayaq. This feature normally distinguishes different branches of Turkic which means that the Shor language has formed from different Turkic sources.
</p><p>Each Shor dialect has subdialectal varieties. The Upper-Mrassu and the Upper-Kondoma varieties have developed numerous close features in the course of close contacts between their speakers in the upper reaches of the Kondoma and Mrassu rivers.
</p><p>The Mrassu dialect served as a basis for literary Shor language both in the 1930s and in the 1980s when the written form of the Shor language was revitalized after a break (of almost 50 years) in its written history. However, the Kondoma dialect norms are also largely accepted. The <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Academy_of_Sciences" class="mw-redirect" title="Soviet Academy of Sciences">Academy</a> grammar of Shor, published in 1941, was written on the basis of the Mrass dialect by <a href="/wiki/Nadezhda_Petrovna_Dyrenkova" title="Nadezhda Petrovna Dyrenkova">Dyrenkova</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Shor was first written with a <a href="/wiki/Cyrillic_script" title="Cyrillic script">Cyrillic</a> alphabet introduced by <a href="/wiki/Russian_Orthodox" class="mw-redirect" title="Russian Orthodox">Christian</a> <a href="/wiki/Missionaries" class="mw-redirect" title="Missionaries">missionaries</a> in the middle of the 19th century. After a number of changes, the modern Shor alphabet is written in another modified Cyrillic alphabet.
</p><p>In 2005, to highlight the endangered status of the language, Gennady Kostochakov published a book of poems in Shor, entitled "I am the Last Shor Poet".<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> In 2017, a Shor translation of <i><a href="/wiki/Alice%27s_Adventures_in_Wonderland" title="Alice&#39;s Adventures in Wonderland">Alice's Adventures in Wonderland</a></i> by Liubovʹ Arbaçakova was published.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Writing_system">Writing system</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shor_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Writing system"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shor_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: History"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Before the 19th century the Shor language had remained unwritten; in the 1870s Orthodox missionaries made the first effort to create a Cyrillic Shor alphabet. In spite of all the efforts by the missionaries, the percentage of literacy among the native population increased very slowly — by the beginning of the 20th century they constituted only about 1% of the Shors.
</p><p>The Shor written language had its 'golden age' in the 1920s. In 1927, a second attempt was made to create a Shor alphabet based on Cyrillic. In 1932-1933, Fedor Cispijakov wrote and published a new primer based on the Latin alphabet. This however considerably complicated the process of learning; thus in 1938, the same author together with Georgij Babuskin created a new variant of the primer based on the Cyrillic alphabet, of which several editions have been published since then.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Missionary_alphabet">Missionary alphabet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shor_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Missionary alphabet"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The first book written in the Shor language was published in 1885.
It used a modified <a href="/wiki/Russian_alphabet" title="Russian alphabet">Russian alphabet</a> (excluding Ё ё, Ф ф, Щ щ, and Ѣ ѣ) with additional letters Ј ј, Ҥ ҥ, Ӧ ӧ, and Ӱ ӱ.
</p><p>In 1927 an official alphabet was adopted, being the Russian alphabet (excluding Ё ё and ъ) with additional letters Ј ј, Ҥ ҥ, Ӧ ӧ, and Ӱ ӱ.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Latin_alphabet">Latin alphabet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shor_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Latin alphabet"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A Latin alphabet for the Shor language was introduced in 1930: A a, B в, C c, D d, Ə ə, F f, G g, Ƣ ƣ, I i, J j, K k, Q q, M m, N n, Ꞑ ꞑ, O o, Ө ө, P p, R r, S s, T t, U u, V v, Ş ş, Z z, Ƶ ƶ, L l, Ь ь, Y y, Į į.
</p><p>The order of the letters was later changed to correspond with alphabets for other languages in the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>, the letter Ә ә was replaced with E e, and the letter Į į was dropped.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_alphabet">Modern alphabet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shor_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Modern alphabet"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1938 the Latin alphabet was replaced with a Cyrillic one. It used the Russian alphabet with additional letters Ӧ ӧ, Ӱ ӱ, and Нъ нъ.
After reforms in 1980 it reached its present form: А а, Б б, В в, Г г, Ғ ғ, Д д, Е е, Ё ё, Ж ж, З з, И и, Й й, К к, Қ қ, Л л, М м, Н н, Ң ң, О о, Ӧ ӧ, П п, Р р, С с, Т т, У у, Ӱ ӱ, Ф ф, Х х, Ц ц, Ч ч, Ш ш, Щ щ, Ъ ъ, Ы ы, Ь ь, Э э, Ю ю, Я я.
</p>
