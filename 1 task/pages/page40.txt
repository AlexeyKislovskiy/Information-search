<p>The <b>Uru–Chipaya</b> family is an indigenous <a href="/wiki/Language_family" title="Language family">language family</a> of <a href="/wiki/Bolivia" title="Bolivia">Bolivia</a>.
</p><p>The speakers were originally fishermen on the shores of <a href="/wiki/Lake_Titicaca" title="Lake Titicaca">Lake Titicaca</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lake_Poop%C3%B3" title="Lake Poopó">Lake Poopó</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Desaguadero_River_(Bolivia_and_Peru)" title="Desaguadero River (Bolivia and Peru)">Desaguadero River</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Chipaya_language" title="Chipaya language">Chipaya</a> has over a thousand speakers and sees vigorous use in the native community, but all other <a href="/wiki/Uru_people" title="Uru people">Uru</a> languages or dialects are extinct.
</p><p>Loukotka (1968) also lists the <a href="/wiki/Chango_people" title="Chango people">Chango</a> language, once spoken on the coast of Chile from <a href="/wiki/Huasco" title="Huasco">Huasco</a> to <a href="/wiki/Cobija" title="Cobija">Cobija</a> in <a href="/wiki/Antofagasta_Province" title="Antofagasta Province">Antofagasta Province</a>. The population has since been Araucanized.<sup id="cite_ref-Loukotka_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Loukotka-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Stark (1972) proposed a <a href="/wiki/Maya%E2%80%93Yunga%E2%80%93Chipayan_languages" title="Maya–Yunga–Chipayan languages">Maya–Yunga–Chipayan</a> macrofamily linking <a href="/wiki/Mayan_languages" title="Mayan languages">Mayan</a> with Uru–Chipaya and <a href="/wiki/Mochica_language" title="Mochica language">Yunga</a> (Mochica).<sup id="cite_ref-Stark-1972_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stark-1972-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Language_contact">Language contact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Uru%E2%80%93Chipaya_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Language contact"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Jolkesky (2016) notes that there are lexical similarities with the <a href="/wiki/Kunza_language" title="Kunza language">Kunza</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pukina_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Pukina language">Pukina</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pano_languages" class="mw-redirect" title="Pano languages">Pano</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jaqi_languages" class="mw-redirect" title="Jaqi languages">Jaqi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kechua_languages" class="mw-redirect" title="Kechua languages">Kechua</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mapudungun_languages" class="mw-redirect" title="Mapudungun languages">Mapudungun</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Moseten-Tsimane_languages" class="mw-redirect" title="Moseten-Tsimane languages">Moseten-Tsimane</a> language families due to contact.<sup id="cite_ref-Jolkesky-2016_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jolkesky-2016-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
