<p><b>Kerek</b> (Russian: <span lang="ru">Керекский язык</span>) is an extinct language in <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a> of the northern branch of the <a href="/wiki/Chukotko-Kamchatkan_languages" title="Chukotko-Kamchatkan languages">Chukotko-Kamchatkan languages</a>. On historical linguistic grounds it is most closely related to <a href="/wiki/Koryak_language" title="Koryak language">Koryak</a> (both languages have a <a href="/wiki/Phonemic_differentiation#Phonemic_mergers" class="mw-redirect" title="Phonemic differentiation">merger</a> of the proto-Chuktotian <a href="/wiki/Phonemes" class="mw-redirect" title="Phonemes">phonemes</a> /*ð/ and /*r/ with /*j/). The next closest relative is Chukchi (/*ð/ and /*r/ are merged, but not /*j/).
</p><p>In 1997 there were still two speakers remaining, but by 2005 the language was considered extinct.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> According to the 2010 census,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> there were 10 people claiming Kerek as their native language, believed to only consist of partial speakers and non-speakers who claim the language as part of their ethnic heritage. Over the 20th century many members of the <a href="/wiki/Kereks" title="Kereks">Kerek ethnic group</a> <a href="/wiki/Language_shift" title="Language shift">shifted</a> to <a href="/wiki/Chukchi_language" title="Chukchi language">Chukchi</a>, the language of the majority ethnic group in the area, but now most <a href="/wiki/Chukchi_people" title="Chukchi people">Chukchis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kereks" title="Kereks">Kereks</a> speak Russian.
</p><p>Kerek is an agglutinative language, meaning that the morphemes build on each other to have different meanings.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
