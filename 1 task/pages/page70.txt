The <b>Chukotko-Kamchatkan</b> or <b>Chukchi–Kamchatkan languages</b> are a <a href="/wiki/Language_families_and_languages" class="mw-redirect" title="Language families and languages">language family</a> of extreme northeastern <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a>. Its speakers traditionally were indigenous hunter-gatherers and reindeer-herders. Chukotko-Kamchatkan is <a href="/wiki/Endangered_language" title="Endangered language">endangered</a>. The Kamchatkan branch is <a href="/wiki/Moribund_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Moribund language">moribund</a>, represented only by <a href="/wiki/Western_Itelmen" class="mw-redirect" title="Western Itelmen">Western Itelmen</a>, with only 4 or 5 elderly speakers left. The Chukotkan branch had close to 7,000 speakers left (as of 2010, the majority being speakers of <a href="/wiki/Chukchi_language" title="Chukchi language">Chukchi</a>), with a reported total ethnic population of 25,000.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup></p><figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Chukotko-kamchatkan_language_family_tree.png" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/Chukotko-kamchatkan_language_family_tree.png/220px-Chukotko-kamchatkan_language_family_tree.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="87" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/Chukotko-kamchatkan_language_family_tree.png/330px-Chukotko-kamchatkan_language_family_tree.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/Chukotko-kamchatkan_language_family_tree.png/440px-Chukotko-kamchatkan_language_family_tree.png 2x" data-file-width="1370" data-file-height="542" /></a><figcaption>The language family tree of the Chukotko-Kamchatkan languages.</figcaption></figure>
<p>While the family is sometimes grouped <a href="/wiki/Typology_(linguistics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Typology (linguistics)">typologically</a> and geographically as <a href="/wiki/Paleosiberian_languages" class="mw-redirect" title="Paleosiberian languages">Paleosiberian</a>, no external genetic relationship has been widely accepted as proven. The most popular such proposals have been for links with <a href="/wiki/Eskimo%E2%80%93Aleut" class="mw-redirect" title="Eskimo–Aleut">Eskimo–Aleut</a>, either alone or in the context of a wider grouping.
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Alternative_names">Alternative names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chukotko-Kamchatkan_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Alternative names"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Less commonly encountered names for the family are <b>Chukchian</b>, <b>Chukotian</b>, <b>Chukotan</b>, <b>Kamchukchee</b> and <b>Kamchukotic</b>. Of these, <i>Chukchian</i>, <i>Chukotian</i> and <i>Chukotan</i> are ambiguous, since the three terms are sometimes used to refer specifically to the family's northern branch; the last two names are portmanteau words referring to both branches.
</p><p>In addition, <b>Luorawetlan</b> (also spelled <b>Luoravetlan</b>) has been in wide use since 1775 as a name for the family, although it is properly the self-designation of one of its constituent languages, <a href="/wiki/Chukchi_language" title="Chukchi language">Chukchi</a>. 
</p>
<figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Chukotko-Kamchatkan_map.svg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/Chukotko-Kamchatkan_map.svg/220px-Chukotko-Kamchatkan_map.svg.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="336" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/Chukotko-Kamchatkan_map.svg/330px-Chukotko-Kamchatkan_map.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/Chukotko-Kamchatkan_map.svg/440px-Chukotko-Kamchatkan_map.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="427" data-file-height="652" /></a><figcaption>Map of the Chukotko-Kamchatkan languages. Languages with labels accompanied by a <a href="/wiki/Dagger_(typography)" class="mw-redirect" title="Dagger (typography)">dagger</a> (†) are extinct.</figcaption></figure>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Languages">Languages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chukotko-Kamchatkan_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Languages"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Chukotko-Kamchatkan family consists of two distantly related <a href="/wiki/Dialect_cluster" class="mw-redirect" title="Dialect cluster">dialect clusters</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chukotkan_languages" title="Chukotkan languages">Chukotkan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kamchatkan_languages" title="Kamchatkan languages">Kamchatkan</a>. Chukotkan is considered anywhere from three to five languages, whereas there is only one surviving Kamchatkan language, <a href="/wiki/Itelmen_language" title="Itelmen language">Itelmen</a>.
</p><p>The relationship of the Chukotkan languages to Itelmen is at best distant, and has been met with only partial acceptance by scholars.
</p><p>All the Chukotko-Kamchatkan languages are under pressure from <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>. Almost all speakers are bilingual in Russian, and few members of the ethnic groups associated with the languages born after 1970 speak any language but Russian.
</p><p>The accepted classification is this:
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1116488514">.mw-parser-output .treeview ul{padding:0;margin:0}.mw-parser-output .treeview li{padding:0;margin:0;list-style-type:none;list-style-image:none}.mw-parser-output .treeview li li{background:url("https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/f2/Treeview-grey-line.png")no-repeat 0 -2981px;padding-left:21px;text-indent:0.3em}.mw-parser-output .treeview li li:last-child{background-position:0 -5971px}.mw-parser-output .treeview li.emptyline>ul>.mw-empty-elt:first-child+.emptyline,.mw-parser-output .treeview li.emptyline>ul>li:first-child{background-position:0 9px}</style><div class="treeview">

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relation_to_other_language_families">Relation to other language families</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chukotko-Kamchatkan_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Relation to other language families"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Chukotko-Kamchatkan languages have no generally accepted relation to any other language family. There are several theories about possible relationships to existing or hypothetical language families.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Paleosiberian">Paleosiberian</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chukotko-Kamchatkan_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Paleosiberian"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Chukotko-Kamchatkan languages are sometimes classified among the <a href="/wiki/Paleosiberian_languages" class="mw-redirect" title="Paleosiberian languages">Paleosiberian languages</a>, a catch-all term for language groups with no identified relationship to one another that are believed to represent remnants of the language map of Siberia prior to the advances of <a href="/wiki/Turkic_languages" title="Turkic languages">Turkic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tungusic_languages" title="Tungusic languages">Tungusic</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Eurasiatic">Eurasiatic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chukotko-Kamchatkan_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Eurasiatic"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Joseph_Greenberg" title="Joseph Greenberg">Joseph Greenberg</a> identifies Chukotko-Kamchatkan (which he names Chukotian) as a member of <a href="/wiki/Eurasiatic_languages" title="Eurasiatic languages">Eurasiatic</a>, a proposed <a href="/wiki/Macrofamily" title="Macrofamily">macrofamily</a> that includes <a href="/wiki/Indo-European_languages" title="Indo-European languages">Indo-European</a>, <a href="/wiki/Altaic_languages" title="Altaic languages">Altaic</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Eskimo%E2%80%93Aleut_languages" class="mw-redirect" title="Eskimo–Aleut languages">Eskimo–Aleut</a>, among others. Greenberg also assigns <a href="/wiki/Nivkh_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Nivkh language">Nivkh</a> and <a href="/wiki/Yukaghir_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Yukaghir language">Yukaghir</a>, sometimes classed as "Paleosiberian" languages, to the Eurasiatic family.
</p><p>While the Eurasiatic hypothesis has been well received by <a href="/wiki/Nostratic_languages" title="Nostratic languages">Nostraticists</a> and some <a href="/wiki/Indo-European_studies" title="Indo-European studies">Indo-Europeanists</a>, it remains very controversial. Part of the reason is that the Eurasiatic hypothesis rests on <a href="/wiki/Mass_comparison" title="Mass comparison">mass comparison</a> of lexemes, grammatical formatives, and vowel systems (see Greenberg 2000–2002), rather than on the prevailing view that regular sound correspondences that are linked to a wide array of lexemes and grammatical formatives are the only valid means to establish <a href="/wiki/Genetic_relationship_(linguistics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Genetic relationship (linguistics)">genetic relationship</a> (see for instance Baldi 2002:2–19).
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Murray_Gell-Mann" title="Murray Gell-Mann">Murray Gell-Mann</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ilia_Peiros" title="Ilia Peiros">Ilia Peiros</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Georgiy_Starostin" title="Georgiy Starostin">Georgiy Starostin</a> group Chukotko-Kamchatkan languages and <a href="/wiki/Nivkh_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Nivkh language">Nivkh</a> with <a href="/wiki/Almosan" class="mw-redirect" title="Almosan">Almosan</a> instead of Eurasiatic.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Uralo-Siberian">Uralo-Siberian</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chukotko-Kamchatkan_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Uralo-Siberian"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1998, <a href="/wiki/Michael_Fortescue" title="Michael Fortescue">Michael Fortescue</a>, a specialist in <a href="/wiki/Eskimo%E2%80%93Aleut_languages" class="mw-redirect" title="Eskimo–Aleut languages">Eskimo–Aleut</a> as well as in Chukotko-Kamchatkan, argued for a link between <a href="/wiki/Uralic_languages" title="Uralic languages">Uralic</a>, Yukaghir, Chukotko-Kamchatkan, and Eskimo–Aleut<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> calling this proposed grouping <a href="/wiki/Uralo-Siberian_languages" title="Uralo-Siberian languages">Uralo-Siberian</a>.
</p>
<h3><span id="Chukotko-Kamchatkan.E2.80.93Amuric"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Chukotko-Kamchatkan–Amuric">Chukotko-Kamchatkan–Amuric</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chukotko-Kamchatkan_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Chukotko-Kamchatkan–Amuric"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1033289096">.mw-parser-output .hatnote{font-style:italic}.mw-parser-output div.hatnote{padding-left:1.6em;margin-bottom:0.5em}.mw-parser-output .hatnote i{font-style:normal}.mw-parser-output .hatnote+link+.hatnote{margin-top:-0.5em}</style><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Chukotko-Kamchatkan%E2%80%93Amuric_languages" title="Chukotko-Kamchatkan–Amuric languages">Chukotko-Kamchatkan–Amuric languages</a></div>
<p>In 2011, Fortescue instead suggested that <a href="/wiki/Nivkh_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Nivkh language">Nivkh</a> (Gilyak, Amuric), another Paleo-Siberian language, is related to Chukotko-Kamchatkan on the basis of morphological, typological, and lexical evidence as the closest relative of Chukotko-Kamchatkan<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> and came to prefer an interpretation of the similarities to Uralo-Siberian through language contact.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Together, Chukotko-Kamchatkan and Nivkh could form a larger Chukotko-Kamchatkan-Amuric language family, and their common ancestor might have been spoken 4000 years ago.
</p>
