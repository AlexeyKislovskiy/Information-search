<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1066479718"><table class="infobox" style="background:#fff6d9"><tbody><tr><th scope="row" class="infobox-label" style="background:#fee8ab">People</th><td class="infobox-data"><a href="/wiki/Mari_people" title="Mari people">Mari</a></td></tr><tr><th scope="row" class="infobox-label" style="background:#fee8ab">Language</th><td class="infobox-data"><a class="mw-selflink selflink">Marij</a></td></tr><tr><th scope="row" class="infobox-label" style="background:#fee8ab">Country</th><td class="infobox-data"><a href="/wiki/Mari_El" title="Mari El">Mari El</a></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>The <b>Mari language</b> (Mari: <span title="Mari (Russia)-language text"><span lang="chm">марий йылме</span></span>, <i>marij jylme</i>; <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru">марийский язык</span>, <i>mariyskiy yazyk</i>), formerly known as the <b>Cheremiss language</b>, spoken by approximately 400,000 people, belongs to the <a href="/wiki/Uralic_languages" title="Uralic languages">Uralic</a> language family. It is spoken primarily in the <a href="/wiki/Mari_Republic" class="mw-redirect" title="Mari Republic">Mari Republic</a> (Mari: <span title="Mari (Russia)-language text"><span lang="chm">Марий Эл</span></span>, <i>Marij El</i>, i.e., 'Mari land') of the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Federation" class="mw-redirect" title="Russian Federation">Russian Federation</a> as well as in the area along the <a href="/wiki/Vyatka_River" class="mw-redirect" title="Vyatka River">Vyatka river</a> basin and eastwards to the <a href="/wiki/Ural_Mountains" title="Ural Mountains">Urals</a>. Mari speakers, known as the <a href="/wiki/Mari_people" title="Mari people">Mari</a>, are found also in the <a href="/wiki/Tatarstan" title="Tatarstan">Tatarstan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bashkortostan" title="Bashkortostan">Bashkortostan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Udmurtia" title="Udmurtia">Udmurtia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Perm_Krai" title="Perm Krai">Perm</a> regions.
</p><p>Mari is the titular and official language of its republic, alongside <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>.
</p><p>The Mari language today has three standard forms: <a href="/wiki/Hill_Mari" class="mw-redirect" title="Hill Mari">Hill Mari</a>, <a href="/wiki/Northwestern_Mari_language" title="Northwestern Mari language">Northwestern Mari</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Meadow_Mari" class="mw-redirect" title="Meadow Mari">Meadow Mari</a>. The latter is predominant and spans the continuum Meadow Mari to Eastern Mari from the Republic into the Ural dialects of <a href="/wiki/Bashkortostan" title="Bashkortostan">Bashkortostan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sverdlovsk_Oblast" title="Sverdlovsk Oblast">Sverdlovsk Oblast</a> and <a href="/wiki/Udmurtia" title="Udmurtia">Udmurtia</a>), whereas the former, Hill Mari, shares a stronger affiliation with the Northwestern dialect (spoken in the <a href="/wiki/Nizhny_Novgorod_Oblast" title="Nizhny Novgorod Oblast">Nizhny Novgorod Oblast</a> and parts of the <a href="/wiki/Kirov_Oblast" title="Kirov Oblast">Kirov Oblast</a>). Both language forms use modified versions of <a href="/wiki/Cyrillic_script" title="Cyrillic script">Cyrillic script</a>. For the non-native, Hill Mari, or Western Mari, can be recognized by its use of the special letters "ӓ" and "ӹ" in addition to the shared letters "ӱ" and "ӧ", while Eastern and Meadow Mari utilize a special letter "ҥ".
</p><p>The use of two "variants", as opposed to two "languages", has been debated: Maris recognize the unity of the ethnic group, and the two forms are very close, but distinct enough to cause some problems with communication.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ethnonym_and_glottonym">Ethnonym and glottonym</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mari_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Ethnonym and glottonym"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Mari language and people were known as "Cheremis" (<a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru">черемисы, черемисский язык</span>, <i>cheremisy</i>, <i>cheremisskiy yazyk</i>). In medieval texts the variant forms Sarmys and Tsarmys are also found, as well as <a href="/wiki/Tatar_language" title="Tatar language">Tatar</a>: <span lang="tt-Cyrl">Чирмеш</span>, <small>romanized:&#160;</small><span title="Tatar-language romanization"><i lang="tt-Latn">Çirmeş</i></span>; and <a href="/wiki/Chuvash_language" title="Chuvash language">Chuvash</a>: <span lang="cv">Ҫармӑс</span>, <i>Śarmăs</i> before the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Revolution_of_1917" class="mw-redirect" title="Russian Revolution of 1917">Russian Revolution</a>. The term <i>Mari</i> comes from the Maris' autonym <span title="Mari (Russia)-language text"><span lang="chm">марий</span></span> (<span title="Mari (Russia)-language text"><i lang="chm-Latn">mari</i></span>).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sociolinguistic_situation">Sociolinguistic situation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mari_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Sociolinguistic situation"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Most Maris live in rural areas with slightly more than a quarter living in cities. In the republic's capital, <a href="/wiki/Yoshkar-Ola" title="Yoshkar-Ola">Yoshkar-Ola</a>, the percentage of Maris is just over 23 percent. At the end of the 1980s (per the 1989 census) Maris numbered 670,868, of whom 80% (542,160) claimed Mari as their first language and 18.8% did not speak Mari. In the Mari Republic, 11.6% claimed Mari was not their first language. In a survey by the Mari Research Institute more than three quarters of Maris surveyed considered Mari language to be the most crucial marker of ethnic identity, followed by traditional culture (61%) and common historical past (22%), religion (16%), character and mentality (15%) and appearance (11%) (see Glukhov and Glukhov for details). A gradual downward trend towards assimilation to Russian has been noted for the Communist period: the 1926 census indicated more than 99% of Maris considered Mari their first language, declining to less than 81% in 1989. Some qualitative evidence of a reversal in recent years has been noted.
</p><p>There was no state support for Mari language in <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Russia" class="mw-redirect" title="Imperial Russia">Imperial Russia</a>, and with the exception of some enthusiasts and numerous ecclesiastical texts by the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Orthodox_Church" title="Russian Orthodox Church">Russian Orthodox Church</a>, there was almost no education in Mari language. After the <a href="/wiki/October_Revolution" title="October Revolution">October Revolution</a>, there was a period of support of all lesser national cultures in the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>, but eventually <a href="/wiki/Russification" title="Russification">Russification</a> returned. While the development of Mari literary language continued, still, only elementary-school education was available in Mari in the Soviet period, with this policy ending in village schools in the 1970–1980s. The period of <a href="/wiki/Glasnost" title="Glasnost">glasnost</a> and <a href="/wiki/Perestroika" title="Perestroika">perestroika</a> in the 1990s opened opportunities for a revival of efforts expand the use of Mari in education and the public sphere. In the 1990s, the Mari language, alongside Russian, was proclaimed in the republican constitution to be an official language of Mari El. By the beginning of the 21st century, Mari language and literature was taught in 226 schools. At the History and Philology Department of the Mari State University and the <a href="/wiki/Nadezhda_Konstantinovna_Krupskaya" class="mw-redirect" title="Nadezhda Konstantinovna Krupskaya">Krupskaya</a> Teachers' Training Institute (Yoshkar-Ola), more than half of the subjects are taught in Mari.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dialects">Dialects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mari_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Dialects"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<figure class="mw-halign-right" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Mari_language_dialect_map.svg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Mari_language_dialect_map.svg/400px-Mari_language_dialect_map.svg.png" decoding="async" width="400" height="267" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Mari_language_dialect_map.svg/600px-Mari_language_dialect_map.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Mari_language_dialect_map.svg/800px-Mari_language_dialect_map.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="697" data-file-height="466" /></a><figcaption>The four main dialects of Mari. <style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r981673959">.mw-parser-output .legend{page-break-inside:avoid;break-inside:avoid-column}.mw-parser-output .legend-color{display:inline-block;min-width:1.25em;height:1.25em;line-height:1.25;margin:1px 0;text-align:center;border:1px solid black;background-color:transparent;color:black}.mw-parser-output .legend-text{}</style><div class="legend"><span class="legend-color mw-no-invert" style="background-color:chocolate; color:black;">&#160;</span>&#160;Hill Mari</div> <link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r981673959"><div class="legend"><span class="legend-color mw-no-invert" style="background-color:#d7d700; color:black;">&#160;</span>&#160;Northwestern Mari</div> <link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r981673959"><div class="legend"><span class="legend-color mw-no-invert" style="background-color:violet; color:black;">&#160;</span>&#160;Meadow Mari</div> <link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r981673959"><div class="legend"><span class="legend-color mw-no-invert" style="background-color:royalblue; color:white;">&#160;</span>&#160;Eastern Mari</div></figcaption></figure>
<p>The principal division between Mari varieties is the West and the East. According to the Soviet linguist Kovedyaeva (1976:9-15, 1993:163-164) the Mari macrolanguage is divided into four main dialects:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Hill_Mari" class="mw-redirect" title="Hill Mari">Hill Mari</a>, spoken mainly on the right upper bank of the Volga River around <a href="/wiki/Kozmodemyansk" title="Kozmodemyansk">Kozmodemyansk</a> (hence the name), but also on the left bank and in the mouth of <a href="/wiki/Vetluga_River" class="mw-redirect" title="Vetluga River">Vetluga</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Northwestern_Mari_language" title="Northwestern Mari language">Northwestern Mari</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Meadow_Mari" class="mw-redirect" title="Meadow Mari">Meadow Mari</a>, spoken on the left Volgan bank on the central and eastern plain ("meadow") of Mari El around the republican capital, <a href="/wiki/Yoshkar-Ola" title="Yoshkar-Ola">Yoshkar-Ola</a>.</li>
<li><i>Eastern Mari</i> is scattered to the east of Mari El from <a href="/wiki/Vyatka_River" class="mw-redirect" title="Vyatka River">Vyatka</a> through <a href="/wiki/Kama_River" class="mw-redirect" title="Kama River">Kama</a> to <a href="/wiki/Ufa_River" class="mw-redirect" title="Ufa River">Ufa</a>.</li></ul>
<p>Each main dialect is divided into their own smaller local subdialects. Only Hill and Meadow Mari have their own literary written standard varieties, based on the dialects of Kozmodemyansk and Yoshkar-Ola respectively.
</p><p>Eastern and Meadow Mari are often united as a Meadow-Eastern <a href="/wiki/Supra-dialect" class="mw-redirect" title="Supra-dialect">supra-dialect</a>. Northwestern Mari is transitional between the Hill and Meadow dialects, and its phonology and morphology are closer to Hill Mari.
</p>

<p>The schwa <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ə/</span> and its fronted counterpart are usually transcribed in <a href="/wiki/Finno-Ugric_transcription" class="mw-redirect" title="Finno-Ugric transcription">Finno-Ugric transcription</a> as <i>ə̑</i> (reduced mid unrounded vowel) and <i>ə</i> (reduced front unrounded vowel) respectively. </p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Word_prosody">Word prosody</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mari_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Word prosody"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Stress is not phonemic in Mari, but a dynamic stress system is exhibited phonetically, the stressed syllable being higher in pitch and amplitude and greater in length than an unstressed syllable. Generally, there is one prominent syllable per word and prominence may be found in any syllable of the word. Post- and prefixes behave as <a href="/wiki/Clitic" title="Clitic">clitics</a>, i.e., they do not have their own stress. For example, пӧ́рт (<i>pört</i>, "house") гыч (<i>gəč</i>, "out of") (<span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ˈpørt<span class="wrap"> </span>ɣɤt͡ʃ]</span>); or му́ро (<i>muro</i>, "song") дене (<i>dene</i>, "with") (<span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ˈmuro<span class="wrap"> </span>ðene]</span>).
</p>

<li>Palatalisation is marked in different ways. A <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;у&#x27e9;</span> following a palatalised consonant is written as <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;ю&#x27e9;</span>, and <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;а&#x27e9;</span> following a palatalised consonant is written as <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;я&#x27e9;</span>. If the vowel following a palatalised consonant is an е or an и, palatalisation is not marked at all. In other cases, the <a href="/wiki/Soft_sign" title="Soft sign">soft sign</a> ь is used to mark palatalisation.</li>
<li>The modified Cyrillic letter for the velar nasal (<span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">ŋ</span>) combines the Cyrillic letter <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;Н н&#x27e9;</span> with and <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;Г г&#x27e9;</span>, where the rightmost post of Н is conflated with the vertical post of <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;Г&#x27e9;</span>: <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;Ҥ, ҥ&#x27e9;</span>. Although Hill Mari has this sound too, this character is only used in Meadow Mari.</li>
<li>In Russian loanwords and after nasals, <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/b<span class="wrap"> </span>d<span class="wrap"> </span>ɡ/</span> are voiced stops. Word-finally and before a consonant, there is free variation between voiced fricatives (<span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[β<span class="wrap"> </span>ð<span class="wrap"> </span>ɣ]</span>) and voiceless stops <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[p<span class="wrap"> </span>t<span class="wrap"> </span>k]</span>.</li></ol>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Phonological_processes">Phonological processes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mari_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Phonological processes"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Like several other Uralic languages, Mari has <a href="/wiki/Vowel_harmony" title="Vowel harmony">vowel harmony</a>. In addition to front/back harmony, Mari also features round/unround harmony. If the stressed vowel in the word is <a href="/wiki/Roundedness" title="Roundedness">rounded</a>, then the suffix will contain a rounded vowel: for example, кӱтӱ́ ([kyˈty] 'herd') becomes кӱтӱ́штӧ ([kyˈtyʃtø], 'in the herd'); if the stressed vowel is unrounded, then the suffix will contain an unrounded vowel: ки́д ([kid], 'hand') becomes ки́дыште ([ˈkidəʃte], 'in the hand'). If the stressed vowel is back, then the suffix will end in a back vowel: агу́р ([aˈgur], 'whirlpool') becomes агу́рышто ([aˈgurəʃto], 'in the whirlpool').<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Declension">Declension</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mari_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Declension"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Like other <a href="/wiki/Uralic_languages" title="Uralic languages">Uralic languages</a>, Mari is an <a href="/wiki/Agglutinating_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Agglutinating language">agglutinating language</a>. It lacks grammatical gender, and does not use articles.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Case">Case</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mari_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Case"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Meadow Mari has 9 <a href="/wiki/Productivity_(linguistics)" title="Productivity (linguistics)">productive</a> <a href="/wiki/Declension" title="Declension">cases</a>, of which 3 are <a href="/wiki/Locative_case" title="Locative case">locative cases</a>. The usage of the latter ones is restricted to inanimate objects.
</p><p>Many cases, aside from their basic function, are used in other situations, such as in expressions of time.
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Nominative_case" title="Nominative case">Nominative</a>, used for subjects, predicatives and for other grammatical functions.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Genitive_case" title="Genitive case">Genitive</a>, is used for possessive constructions.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Dative_case" title="Dative case">Dative</a>, the indirect object's case.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Accusative_case" title="Accusative case">Accusative</a>, the direct object's case.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Comitative_case" title="Comitative case">Comitative</a>, used when a subject or an object can be split up into parts, or in adverbials expressing the involvement of an object in an action.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Comparative_case" title="Comparative case">Comparative</a>, used to express the likeness to something.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Inessive_case" title="Inessive case">Inessive</a>, used to state where something is.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Illative_case" title="Illative case">Illative</a>, used to state where something is going.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Lative_case" title="Lative case">Lative</a>, used to express into what something is going.</li></ul>

<p>If a locative statement was to be made about an animate object, postpositions would be used.
</p><p>Additionally, terms denoting family members have <a href="/wiki/Vocative_case" title="Vocative case">vocative</a> forms. These are, however, not created with a specific paradigm, and only exist in a few pre-defined cases.
</p><p>Hill Mari has these cases, plus the <a href="/wiki/Abessive_case" title="Abessive case">abessive case</a> (of the form -де), which is used to form <a href="/wiki/Adverbial" title="Adverbial">adverbials</a> stating without the involvement or influence of which an action happens.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Number">Number</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mari_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Number"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Mari, though an agglutinative language, does not have a separate <a href="/wiki/Morpheme" title="Morpheme">morpheme</a> to signify <a href="/wiki/Plural" title="Plural">plurality</a>. There are three particles, which are attached to the end of words with a hyphen, used to signify plural.
</p>
<ul><li>-влак (<i>-vlak</i>) – Standard plural form.</li>
<li>-шамыч (<i>-šamõč</i>) – Alternative standard plural, used in many dialects. There is no difference in meaning between these two.</li>
<li>-мыт (<i>mət</i>) – Sociative plural. Used to signify a group of people: the members of a family, a person and their family and friends.</li></ul>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Additional_suffixes">Additional suffixes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mari_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Additional suffixes"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Additional particles, falling into none of the categories above, can be added to the very end of a word, giving it some additional meaning. For example, the suffix -ат <i>(-at</i>), means 'also' or 'too'.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conjugation">Conjugation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mari_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Conjugation"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Morphologically, <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_conjugation" title="Grammatical conjugation">conjugation</a> follows three <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_tense" title="Grammatical tense">tenses</a> and three <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_moods" class="mw-redirect" title="Grammatical moods">moods</a> in Meadow Mari.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Conjugation_types">Conjugation types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mari_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Conjugation types"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In Meadow Mari, words can conjugate according to two conjugation types. These differ from each other in all forms but the infinitive and the third-person plural of the imperative. Unfortunately, the infinitive is the form denoted in dictionaries and word lists. It is, thus, necessary to either mark verb infinitives by their conjugation type in word lists, or to include a form in which the conjugation type is visible—usually, the first-person singular present, which ends in -ам (or -ям) for verbs in the first declination, and in -ем (or -эм) for second-declination verbs.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tense">Tense</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mari_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Tense"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The three tenses of Mari verbs are:
</p>
<ol><li><a href="/wiki/Present_tense" title="Present tense">Present</a>: The present tense is used for present and future actions, for states of being and for habitual actions, among others.</li>
<li>First <a href="/wiki/Preterite" title="Preterite">preterite</a>: The first preterite is used to express observed, recent actions.</li>
<li>Second preterite: The second preterite is used for actions that are in the more-distant past.</li></ol>
<p>Additional tenses can be formed through <a href="/wiki/Periphrasis" title="Periphrasis">periphrasis</a>.
</p>
<ul><li>First periphrastic <a href="/wiki/Imperfect" title="Imperfect">imperfect</a></li>
<li>Second periphrastic imperfect</li>
<li>First periphrastic <a href="/wiki/Perfect_(grammar)" title="Perfect (grammar)">perfect</a></li>
<li>Second periphrastic perfect</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mood">Mood</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mari_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Mood"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The moods are:
</p>
<ol><li><a href="/wiki/Indicative_mood" class="mw-redirect" title="Indicative mood">Indicative</a>: The indicative is used to express facts and positive beliefs. All intentions that a particular language does not categorize as another mood are classified as indicative. It can be formed in all persons, in all times.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Imperative_mood" title="Imperative mood">Imperative</a>: The imperative expresses direct commands, requests, and prohibitions. It only exists in the present tense, and exists in all persons but the first person singular.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Desiderative_mood" title="Desiderative mood">Desiderative</a>: The desiderative is used to express desires. It can be formed for all persons, in the present tense and in the two periphrastic imperfect.</li></ol>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Negation">Negation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mari_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Negation"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Negation in Mari uses a 'negative verb', much like <a href="/wiki/Finnish_language" title="Finnish language">Finnish</a> does. The negative verb is more versatile than the negative verb in Finnish (see <a href="/wiki/Finnish_grammar" title="Finnish grammar">Finnish grammar</a>), existing in more grammatical tenses and moods. It has its own form in the present indicative, imperative and desiderative, and in the first preterite indicative. Other negations are periphrastic.
</p><p>The negation verb in its corresponding form is put in front of the negated verb in its second-person singular (the stem-only form), much as it is in Finnish and <a href="/wiki/Estonian_language" title="Estonian language">Estonian</a>.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Infinitive_forms">Infinitive forms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mari_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Infinitive forms"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Verbs have two <a href="/wiki/Infinitive" title="Infinitive">infinitive</a> forms: the standard infinitive and the necessive infinitive, used when a person must do something. The person needing to do something is put in the dative in such a situation.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Word_order">Word order</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mari_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Word order"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Word order in Mari is <a href="/wiki/Subject%E2%80%93object%E2%80%93verb" class="mw-redirect" title="Subject–object–verb">subject–object–verb</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-8">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> This means that the object appears directly before the predicate.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-8">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Word order in Mari is affected by information structure.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-8">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> However, the position of the verb is not affected.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-8">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The focus position is directly before the verb. <sup id="cite_ref-:0_8-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-8">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Subjects, objects, adverbial, and secondary predicate can appear in this position.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_8-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-8">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The examples below quoted in Saarinen (2022)<sup id="cite_ref-:0_8-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-8">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> show the different elements that can appear in the focus position.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nominal_predication">Nominal predication</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mari_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Nominal predication"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Two nouns can be put against each other to form nominal predication.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> According to Saarinen (2022) both nouns and adjectives appear in the nominative case and do not agree with the subject in number  in nominal predication.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_8-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-8">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Saarinen (2022) notes that when the sentence is in the indicative mood with 3sg, a <a href="/wiki/Copula_(linguistics)" title="Copula (linguistics)">copula</a> is not used.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_8-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-8">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> However, a <a href="/wiki/Copula_(linguistics)" title="Copula (linguistics)">copula</a> is obligatory and appears clause-final and in other persons, tenses, and moods.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_8-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-8">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Verbal_predication">Verbal predication</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mari_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Verbal predication"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Saarinen (2022) points out that the object is marked with the accusative in transitive clauses.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_8-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-8">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> However, the object can appear in the nominative case in non-finite constructions.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_8-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-8">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> When the clause is ditransitive, the direct object appears in the accusative case and the indirect takes the dative case.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_8-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-8">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> However, Saarinen (2022) notes that in dialects and with verbs such as йӱкты- /jyktə-/ ‘water’ and пукшы- /pukʃə-/ ‘feed’ both objects appear in the accusative case.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_8-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-8">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
