After the <a href="/wiki/Alaska_Purchase" title="Alaska Purchase">Alaska Purchase</a>, English-speaking missionaries from the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> developed a written version of the language with the <a href="/wiki/Latin_alphabet" title="Latin alphabet">Latin alphabet</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tlingit_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The history of Tlingit is poorly known, mostly because there is no written record until the first contact with Europeans around the 1790s.  Documentation was sparse and irregular until the early 20th century. The language appears to have spread northward from the <a href="/wiki/Ketchikan,_Alaska" title="Ketchikan, Alaska">Ketchikan</a>–<a href="/wiki/Saxman,_Alaska" title="Saxman, Alaska">Saxman</a> area towards the <a href="/wiki/Chilkat_Peninsula" title="Chilkat Peninsula">Chilkat</a> region since certain conservative features are reduced gradually from south to north. The shared features between the <a href="/wiki/Eyak_language" title="Eyak language">Eyak language</a>, found around the <a href="/wiki/Copper_River_(Alaska)" title="Copper River (Alaska)">Copper River</a> delta, and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tongass_Tlingit&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Tongass Tlingit (page does not exist)">Tongass Tlingit</a>, near the <a href="/wiki/Portland_Canal" title="Portland Canal">Portland Canal</a>, are all the more striking for the distances that separate them, both geographic and linguistic.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">Classification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tlingit_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Classification"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Tlingit is currently classified as a distinct and separate branch of Na-Dene, an <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_languages_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous languages of the Americas">indigenous language family of North America</a>. <a href="/wiki/Edward_Sapir" title="Edward Sapir">Edward Sapir</a> (1915) argued for its inclusion in the Na-Dené family, a claim that was subsequently debated by <a href="/wiki/Franz_Boas" title="Franz Boas">Franz Boas</a> (1917), <a href="/wiki/Pliny_Earle_Goddard" title="Pliny Earle Goddard">P.E. Goddard</a> (1920), and many other prominent linguists of the time.
</p><p>Studies in the late 20th century by (Heinz-)Jürgen Pinnow (1962, 1968, 1970, int. al.) and <a href="/wiki/Michael_E._Krauss" title="Michael E. Krauss">Michael E. Krauss</a> (1964, 1965, 1969, int. al.) showed a strong connection to <a href="/wiki/Eyak_language" title="Eyak language">Eyak</a> and hence to the <a href="/wiki/Athabaskan_languages" title="Athabaskan languages">Athabaskan languages</a>.
</p><p>Sapir initially proposed a connection between Tlingit and <a href="/wiki/Haida_language" title="Haida language">Haida</a>, but the debate over Na-Dene gradually excluded Haida from the discussion. Haida is now considered an <a href="/wiki/Language_isolate" title="Language isolate">isolate</a>, with some borrowing from its long proximity with Tlingit. In 2004, the Haida linguist John Enrico presented new arguments and reopened the debate. <a href="/wiki/Victor_Golla" title="Victor Golla">Victor Golla</a> writes in his 2011 <i>California Native Languages</i>, "John Enrico, the contemporary linguist with the deepest knowledge of Haida, continues to believe that a real, if distant, genetic relationship connects Haida to Na-Dene[.]"<sup id="cite_ref-Golla2011_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Golla2011-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geographic_distribution">Geographic distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tlingit_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Geographic distribution"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Tlingit language is distributed from near the mouth of the <a href="/wiki/Copper_River_(Alaska)" title="Copper River (Alaska)">Copper River</a> down the open coast of the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Alaska" title="Gulf of Alaska">Gulf of Alaska</a> and throughout almost all of the islands of the <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Archipelago" title="Alexander Archipelago">Alexander Archipelago</a> in <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Alaska" title="Southeast Alaska">Southeast Alaska</a>. It is characterized by four or five distinct dialects, but they are mostly mutually intelligible. Almost all of the area where the Tlingit language is endemic is contained within the modern borders of <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a>.
</p><p>The exception is an area known as "Inland Tlingit" that extends up the <a href="/wiki/Taku_River" title="Taku River">Taku River</a> and into northern <a href="/wiki/British_Columbia" title="British Columbia">British Columbia</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Yukon" title="Yukon">Yukon</a> around <a href="/wiki/Atlin_Lake" title="Atlin Lake">Atlin Lake</a> (<i>Áa Tleen</i> "Big Lake") and <a href="/wiki/Teslin_Lake" title="Teslin Lake">Teslin Lake</a> (<i>Desleen</i> &lt; <i>Tas Tleen</i> "Big Thread") lake districts, as well as a concentration around <a href="/wiki/Bennett_Lake" title="Bennett Lake">Bennett Lake</a> at the end of the <a href="/wiki/Chilkoot_Trail" title="Chilkoot Trail">Chilkoot Trail</a> (<i>Jilkhoot</i>). Otherwise, Tlingit is not found in <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>. Tlingit legend tells that groups of Tlingit once inhabited the <a href="/wiki/Stikine_River" title="Stikine River">Stikine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nass_River" title="Nass River">Nass</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Skeena_River" title="Skeena River">Skeena</a> river valleys during their migrations from the interior. There is a small group of speakers (some 85) in <a href="/wiki/Washington_(state)" title="Washington (state)">Washington</a> as well.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_and_revitalization_efforts">Use and revitalization efforts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tlingit_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Use and revitalization efforts"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Golla (2007) reported a decreasing population of 500 speakers in Alaska. The <a href="/wiki/First_Peoples%27_Cultural_Council" title="First Peoples&#39; Cultural Council">First Peoples' Cultural Council</a> (2014) reported 2 speakers in Canada out of an ethnic population of 400.
</p><p>As of 2013, Tlingit courses are available at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Alaska_Southeast" title="University of Alaska Southeast">University of Alaska Southeast</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ktoo201310_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ktoo201310-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> In April 2014, Alaska HB 216 recognized Tlingit as an official language of Alaska, lending support to <a href="/wiki/Language_revitalization" title="Language revitalization">language revitalization</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dialects">Dialects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tlingit_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Dialects"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Tlingit is divided into roughly five major dialects, all of which are essentially mutually intelligible:
</p>
<ul><li>The Northern dialect is also called the Yakutat (<i>Yakhwdaat</i>) dialect, after its principal town and is spoken in an area south from <a href="/wiki/Lituya_Bay" title="Lituya Bay">Lituya Bay</a> (<i>Litu.aa</i>) to <a href="/wiki/Frederick_Sound" title="Frederick Sound">Frederick Sound</a>.</li>
<li>The Transitional dialect, a two-tone dialect like the Northern dialect but has phonological features of the Southern, is historically spoken in the villages of <a href="/wiki/Petersburg,_Alaska" title="Petersburg, Alaska">Petersburg</a> (<i>Gántiyaakw Séedi</i> "Steamboat Canyon"), <a href="/wiki/Kake,_Alaska" title="Kake, Alaska">Kake</a> (<i>Khéixh' </i> "Daylight"), and <a href="/wiki/Wrangell,_Alaska" title="Wrangell, Alaska">Wrangell</a> (<i>Khaachxhana.áak'w</i> "Khaachxhan's Little Lake"), and in the surrounding regions although it has almost disappeared.</li>
<li>The similarly-moribund Southern dialects of <i>Sanya</i> and <i>Heinya</i> are spoken from <a href="/wiki/Sumner_Strait" title="Sumner Strait">Sumner Strait</a> south to the Alaska-Canada border, excepting <a href="/wiki/Annette_Island" title="Annette Island">Annette Island</a>, which is the reservation of the <a href="/wiki/Tsimshian" title="Tsimshian">Tsimshian</a>, and the southern end of <a href="/wiki/Prince_of_Wales_Island_(Alaska)" title="Prince of Wales Island (Alaska)">Prince of Wales Island</a>, which is the land of the Kaigani <a href="/wiki/Haida_people" title="Haida people">Haida</a> (<i>K'aayk'aani</i>).</li>
<li>The <a href="/w/index.php?title=Inland_Tlingit&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Inland Tlingit (page does not exist)">Inland Tlingit</a> dialect is spoken in Canada around <a href="/wiki/Atlin_Lake" title="Atlin Lake">Atlin Lake</a> and <a href="/wiki/Teslin_Lake" title="Teslin Lake">Teslin Lake</a>.</li>
<li>The Tongass Tlingit dialect was once spoken in the Cape Fox area south of <a href="/wiki/Ketchikan,_Alaska" title="Ketchikan, Alaska">Ketchikan</a> but recently died with its last speakers in the 1990s.</li></ul>
<p>The various dialects of Tlingit can be classified roughly into two-tone and three-tone systems. Tongass Tlingit, however, has no tone but a four-way <a href="/wiki/Register_(phonology)" title="Register (phonology)">register</a> contrast between short, long, glottalized, and "fading" vowels. (In the last type, the onset of the vowel is articulated normally but the release is <a href="/wiki/Murmured_voice" class="mw-redirect" title="Murmured voice">murmured</a>, essentially a rapid opening of the glottis once articulation is begun.)
</p><p>The tone values in two-tone dialects can be predicted in some cases from the three-tone values but not the reverse. Earlier, it was hypothesized that the three-tone dialects were older and that the two-tone dialects evolved from them. However, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jeff_Leer&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Jeff Leer (page does not exist)">Jeff Leer</a>'s discovery of the Tongass dialect in the late 1970s has shown<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> that the Tongass vowel system is adequate to predict the tonal features of both the two-tone and three-tone dialects, but none of the tonal dialects could be used to predict vocalic feature distribution in Tongass Tlingit. Thus, Tongass Tlingit is the most conservative of the various dialects of Tlingit, preserving contrasts which have been lost in the other dialects.
</p><p>The fading and glottalized vowels in Tongass Tlingit have also been compared with similar systems in the <a href="/wiki/Coast_Tsimshian_dialect" title="Coast Tsimshian dialect">Coast Tsimshian dialect</a>. However, Krauss and Leer (1981, p.&#160;165) point out that the fading vowels in Coastal Tsimshian are the surface realization of <a href="/wiki/Underlying_representation" title="Underlying representation">underlying</a> sequences of vowel and glottalized sonorant, <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">VʔC</span>. That is in contradistinction to the glottal modifications in Tongass Tlingit, which Leer argues are symmetric with the modifications of the consonantal system. Thus, a fading vowel <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">V̤</span> is symmetric<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (September 2015)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> with an aspirated consonant <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">Cʰ</span>, and a glottalized vowel <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">Vʔ</span> is symmetric with an ejective (glottalized) consonant <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">Cʼ</span>. That implies that the two systems have no familial relationship. Leer (1978) speculated that the maintenance of the pretonal system in Tongass Tlingit was caused by the proximity of its speakers around the Cape Fox area near the mouth of the <a href="/wiki/Portland_Canal" title="Portland Canal">Portland Canal</a> to speakers of Coastal Tsimshian, just to the south.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Phonology">Phonology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tlingit_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Phonology"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Tlingit has a complex <a href="/wiki/Phonology" title="Phonology">phonological</a> system compared to <a href="/wiki/Indo-European_languages" title="Indo-European languages">Indo-European languages</a> such as English or <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a>. It has an almost complete series of <a href="/wiki/Ejective" class="mw-redirect" title="Ejective">ejective</a> consonants accompanying its stop, fricative, and affricate consonants. The only missing consonant in the Tlingit ejective series is <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ʃʼ]</span>. The language is also notable for having several <a href="/wiki/Lateral_consonant" title="Lateral consonant">laterals</a> but no voiced <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[l]</span> and for having no <a href="/wiki/Labial_consonant" title="Labial consonant">labials</a> in most dialects, except for <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[m]</span> and <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[p]</span> in recent English <a href="/wiki/Loanword" title="Loanword">loanwords</a>.
</p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Nasal_stop" class="mw-redirect" title="Nasal stop">Nasal consonants</a> <a href="/wiki/Assimilation_(linguistics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Assimilation (linguistics)">assimilating</a> with <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/n/</span> and the <a href="/wiki/Velar_consonant" title="Velar consonant">velar</a> and <a href="/wiki/Uvular_consonant" title="Uvular consonant">uvular</a> <a href="/wiki/Stop_consonant" class="mw-redirect" title="Stop consonant">plosives</a> is common among Tlingit-speakers of all dialects. For example, the sequence <i>ng</i> (<span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/nk/</span>) is often heard as <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ŋk]</span> and <i>ngh</i> (<span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/nq/</span>) as <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ɴq]</span>. Native speakers in a teaching position may admonish learners when they produce these assimilated forms, deriding them as "not Tlingit" or "too English", but it is common to later hear such speakers producing those forms themselves. It is uncertain whether this assimilation is autochthonous or if it arose from contact with English, but the former is more likely from a purely articulatory perspective.
</p><p>Young speakers and second-language learners are increasingly making a <a href="/wiki/Voice_(phonetics)" title="Voice (phonetics)">voiced/unvoiced</a> distinction between consonants, rather than the traditional <a href="/wiki/Aspiration_(phonetics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Aspiration (phonetics)">unaspirated/aspirated</a> distinction. That is because of the influence of English, which makes a similar distinction. For speakers who make the voiced/unvoiced distinction, the distribution is symmetrical with the unaspirated/aspirated distinction among other speakers.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Ian_Maddieson" title="Ian Maddieson">Maddieson</a>, Smith, and Bessel (2001) note that all word final non-ejective stops are phonemically unaspirated. That contrasts with the orthography that typically represents them as aspirated stops: <i>t</i> <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[tʰ]</span> for the more accurate <i>d</i> <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[t]</span>. There is a wide variation in ordinary speech, ranging from unreleased <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[t̚]</span> to a very delayed aspiration <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[tːʰ]</span>. However, the underlying phoneme is certainly unaspirated <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/t/</span> since it is consistently produced when the word is suffixed. The orthography usually but not always reflects that: <i>hít</i> "house" is written <i>(du) hídi</i> "(his) house" when marked with the possessive suffix <i>-ÿí</i>. It is possible but has not been verified that aspirated and unaspirated stops are collapsed into a single phoneme word-finally.
</p><p>Maddieson and colleagues also confirm that the <a href="/wiki/Ejective_consonant" title="Ejective consonant">ejective</a> <a href="/wiki/Fricative_consonant" class="mw-redirect" title="Fricative consonant">fricatives</a> in Tlingit are in fact true ejectives, despite the widely-held assumption that ejective fricatives are not actually phonetically ejective but are as a sequence of fricative and glottal stop. In Tlingit, at least, the articulation of ejective fricatives includes complete closure of the glottis before frication begins, and the larynx is raised in the same manner as with ejective stops.
</p><p>Characteristically, the ejective fricatives in Tlingit feature a much smaller aperture for frication than is found in ordinary fricatives. That articulation provides increased resistance to counter the continual loss of dynamic airstream pressure. Also, ejective fricatives appear to include tightening of the pharyngeal muscles, which reduces the diameter of the air column and so further increases pressure. That pharyngeal constriction is not true pharyngealization, however, since the diameter is still greater than what is found in pharyngealized consonants in other languages.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vowels">Vowels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tlingit_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Vowels"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Tlingit has eight <a href="/wiki/Vowel" title="Vowel">vowels</a>, four vowels further distinguished formally by <a href="/wiki/Vowel_length" title="Vowel length">length</a>. However, the length distinction is often in terms of <a href="/wiki/Tenseness" title="Tenseness">tenseness</a> rather than length, particularly in rapid speech. For the Northern dialect, the dominant spoken dialect of Tlingit and the standard for written Tlingit, every vowel may take either high or low <a href="/wiki/Tone_(linguistics)" title="Tone (linguistics)">tone</a>; in the orthography high tone is indicated by an <a href="/wiki/Acute_accent" title="Acute accent">acute accent</a> (<i>áa</i>) and low tone is unmarked (<i>aa</i>). The Southern and Transitional dialects have a mid tone which is unmarked and additional low tone which is marked by a <a href="/wiki/Grave_accent" title="Grave accent">grave accent</a> (<i>àa</i>).
</p><p>The Inland Tlingit orthography does not use vowel digraphs. Instead, short high vowels are marked with an acute accent, long high vowels are marked with a <a href="/wiki/Circumflex" title="Circumflex">circumflex</a>, and long low vowels are marked with a grave accent. Short low vowels are unmarked. Coastal Tlingit &lt;áa&gt; and &lt;aa&gt; are Inland &lt;â&gt; and &lt;à&gt; respectively. Coastal &lt;éi&gt; and &lt;ei&gt; are Inland &lt;ê&gt; and &lt;è&gt;, Coastal &lt;ée&gt; and &lt;ee&gt; are Inland &lt;î&gt; and &lt;ì&gt;, and Coastal &lt;óo&gt; and &lt;oo&gt; are Inland &lt;û&gt; and &lt;ù&gt;.

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Writing_system">Writing system</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tlingit_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Writing system"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1033289096">.mw-parser-output .hatnote{font-style:italic}.mw-parser-output div.hatnote{padding-left:1.6em;margin-bottom:0.5em}.mw-parser-output .hatnote i{font-style:normal}.mw-parser-output .hatnote+link+.hatnote{margin-top:-0.5em}</style><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Tlingit_alphabet" title="Tlingit alphabet">Tlingit alphabet</a></div>
<p>Until the late 1960s, Tlingit was written exclusively in phonetic transcription in the works of linguists and anthropologists except for a little-known Cyrillic alphabet used for publications by the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Orthodox_Church" title="Russian Orthodox Church">Russian Orthodox Church</a>. A number of amateur anthropologists doing extensive work on the Tlingit had no training in linguistics and so left numerous samples in vague and inconsistent transcriptions, the most famous being <a href="/wiki/George_T._Emmons" title="George T. Emmons">George T. Emmons</a>. However, such noted anthropologists as <a href="/wiki/Franz_Boas" title="Franz Boas">Franz Boas</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_R._Swanton" title="John R. Swanton">John R. Swanton</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Frederica_de_Laguna" title="Frederica de Laguna">Frederica de Laguna</a> have transcribed Tlingit in various related systems that feature accuracy and consistency but sacrifice readability.
</p><p>Two problems ensue from the multiplicity of transcription systems used for Tlingit. One is that there are many of them, thus requiring any reader to learn each individual system depending on what sources are used. The other is that most transcriptions made before Boas's study of Tlingit have numerous mistakes in them, particularly because of misinterpretations of the short vowels and ejective consonants. Accuracy of transcription can be increased by checking against similar words in other systems, or against a modern work postdating Naish and Story's work in the 1960s.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Grammar">Grammar</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tlingit_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Grammar"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Tlingit grammar at first glance appears to be highly <a href="/wiki/Fusional_language" title="Fusional language">fusional</a>, but this is an incorrect assumption. There are predictable processes by which the basic phonetic shapes of individual morphemes are modified to fit various phonological requirements. These processes can be described with a regular language, and such descriptions are given here on a per morpheme basis by giving rule schemas for the context sensitive phonological modification of base morphemes. Analyzing all the possible combinations of morphemes and phonological contexts in Tlingit and constructing a regular language to describe them is a daunting but tractable task.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Despite not being a fusional language, Tlingit is still highly <a href="/wiki/Synthetic_language" title="Synthetic language">synthetic</a> as an <a href="/wiki/Agglutinating_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Agglutinating language">agglutinating language</a>, and is even <a href="/wiki/Polysynthetic_language" title="Polysynthetic language">polysynthetic</a> to some extent. The verb, as with all the Na-Dené languages, is characteristically <a href="/wiki/Incorporation_(linguistics)" title="Incorporation (linguistics)">incorporating</a>. Nouns are in comparison relatively simple, with many being derived from verbs.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Word_order">Word order</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tlingit_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Word order"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Tlingit <a href="/wiki/Word_order" title="Word order">word order</a> is <a href="/wiki/Subject%E2%80%93object%E2%80%93verb" class="mw-redirect" title="Subject–object–verb">SOV</a> when non-<a href="/wiki/Pronominal" class="mw-redirect" title="Pronominal">pronominal</a> <a href="/wiki/Agent_(grammar)" title="Agent (grammar)">agent</a> and <a href="/wiki/Object_(grammar)" title="Object (grammar)">object</a> <a href="/wiki/Phrase" title="Phrase">phrases</a> both exist in the sentence. However, there is a strong urge to restrict the argument of the <a href="/wiki/Verb_phrase" title="Verb phrase">verb phrase</a> to a single non-pronominal <a href="/wiki/Noun_phrase" title="Noun phrase">noun phrase</a>, with any other phrases being extraposed from the verb phrase. If a noun phrase occurs outside of the verb phrase then it is typically represented in the verb phrase by an appropriate pronoun.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nouns">Nouns</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tlingit_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Nouns"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<dl><dd><i>See main article: <a href="/wiki/Tlingit_noun" class="mw-redirect" title="Tlingit noun">Tlingit noun</a></i></dd></dl>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pronominals">Pronominals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tlingit_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Pronominals"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Tlingit has a complex system of <a href="/wiki/Pronoun" title="Pronoun">pronominals</a>, which vary depending on their relationship to the verb. The subject pronominals are incorporated into the verb in its subject slot. The object pronominals are also technically incorporated into the verb (i.e. the verb "complex"), but most are graphically independent. They are divided into three classes, the verbal object, nominal object, and postpositional object. There are also the independent pronominals which are completely separate from the verb and can be used in dependent clauses or in subject or object position.

<p>The numbers in the first column represent the usual concept of person, i.e. first, second, or third. Story and Naish identified a <a href="/wiki/Fourth_person" class="mw-redirect" title="Fourth person">fourth person</a>, but this term is inappropriate since they did not describe a clear separation between the so-called fourth person and the other impersonal pronominals.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2020)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>When analyzing a sentence, the pronominal type is given first, then the form (subject, object, independent) is given following a period. This uniquely represents the pronominal as a two dimensional unit. Thus 1<small>SINGULAR SUBJECT</small> is the first person singular subject pronominal, realized as <i>xhat</i>. The <small>RECIPROCAL</small> does not uniquely identify one of the two reciprocal pronominals, but since they are both phonetically identical as <i>woosh</i>, it is generally unnecessary to uniquely identify them.
</p><p>There is also a notional zeroth person which can be of subject, object, or independent form. This is not realized in Tlingit, instead it is an empty placeholder for analysis.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Subject_pronominals">Subject pronominals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tlingit_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Subject pronominals"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The subject pronominals are all incorporated into the verb. Thus when the subject is represented as a pronominal, the subject position of the sentence is empty.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Object_pronominals">Object pronominals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tlingit_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Object pronominals"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Object pronominals are divided into three classes, the verbal, nominal, and postpositional.
</p><p>The verbal object pronominals function similarly to the subject pronominals in that they preclude an explicit object when used.
</p><p>The nominal object pronominals are similar in some respects to the possessive pronouns of English. They precede a noun and represent the object of the noun, typically implying possession of the noun.
</p><p>Postpositional object pronominals function as objects to which postpositions are attached. They act as the object of a postposition in a manner similar to an ordinary noun suffixed with a postposition.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Directionals">Directionals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tlingit_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Directionals"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Strictly speaking, the Tlingit directionals can be classified as nouns on the basis of their syntactic function. However, they form a distinct semantic set of nouns which indicate direction relative to some stated position. They also show stem variation depending locative suffixation, in particular with the allative suffix <i>-dei</i>. These stem variants also occur with the adverb construction <i>N<sub>1</sub></i>-da-<i>N<sub>2</sub></i>-(i)n "<i>N<sub>2</sub></i> <i>N<sub>1</sub></i>-ward" where <i>N<sub>2</sub></i> is an anatomic noun and <i>N<sub>1</sub></i> is a directional stem.

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Particles">Particles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tlingit_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Particles"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Particles function as neither noun nor verb. They are restricted to positions relative to phrases in the sentence.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Focus_particles">Focus particles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tlingit_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Focus particles"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The focus particles follow the left periphery ("forephrase" per Leer) of a sentence. The Naish-Story term for them is "post-marginals". Many of them may be suffixed with a demonstrative (<i>-yá</i>, <i>-hé</i>, <i>-wé</i>, <i>-yú</i>), and they may also be combined with the interrogative (<i>-gé</i>). Focus particles are stylistically written as separate words, but phonetically, they may be indivisible from the preceding utterance.
</p>
<ul><li><i>sá</i> — wh-question</li>
<li><i>gé</i> — dubitative, unlikelihood, "perhaps", "maybe, "it would seem..."</li>
<li><i>á</i> — focus</li>
<li><i>ágé</i> — interrogative (&lt; <i>á</i> + <i>gé</i>)</li>
<li><i>ásé</i> — discovery, understanding of previously unclear information, "oh, so..."</li>
<li><i>ásgé</i> — second hand information, "I hear...", "they say..." (&lt; <i>ásé</i> + <i>gé</i>)</li>
<li><i>khu.aa</i> — contrastive, "however"</li>
<li><i>xháa</i> – softening, "you see"</li>
<li><i>shágdéi</i> — dubitative, likelihood, "perhaps", "probably"</li>
<li><i>dágáa</i> — emphatic assertion, "indeed", "for sure"</li>
<li><i>shéi</i> — mild surprise</li>
<li><i>gwáa</i>, <i>gu.áa</i> — strong surprise</li>
<li><i>gwshéi</i>, <i>gushéi</i> — rhetorical interrogative, request for corroboration, "I wonder", "perhaps"</li>
<li><i>óosh</i> — hypothetical, "as if", "even if", "if only"</li></ul>
<p>The combination of the focus <i>á</i> with the demonstratives gives the frequently used particles <i>áyá</i> and <i>áwé</i>, and the less common <i>áhé</i> and <i>áyú</i>. Combination of the interrogative <i>ágé</i> with the demonstratives gives the confirmative particles <i>ákwé</i> and <i>ákyá</i> (<i>ák-hé</i> and <i>ákyú</i> are uncommon), used to elicit a yes/no response from the listener.
</p><p>The interrogative <i>ágé</i> also usually contracts to <i>ág</i> before <i>tsú</i> "also": <i>ág tsú</i> "also?" &lt; <i>ágé</i> + <i>tsú</i>.
</p><p>The particle <i>sá</i> is obligatory in forming <a href="/wiki/Interrogative_word" title="Interrogative word">wh-question</a> phrases. It can be combined with a demonstrative, the dubitative, the rhetorical interrogative, and the emphatic assertion:
</p>
<ul><li><i>sáwé</i> (&lt; <i>sá</i> + <i>áwé</i>), <i>sáyá</i>, ... — focused question, "... is that?"</li>
<li><i>sgé</i> (&lt; <i>sá</i> + <i>gé</i>) — dubitative question, "maybe?", "perhaps?"</li>
<li><i>ságwshéi</i> — "I wonder?"</li>
<li><i>sdágáa</i> (&lt; <i>sá</i> + <i>dágáa</i>) — "(what) on earth?", "really?"</li></ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Phrasal_particles">Phrasal particles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tlingit_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Phrasal particles"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Phrasal particles may occur after focus particles that occur with or without demonstrative finals. The following are postphrasal particles, thus they may only occur after the phrase that they modify.
</p>
<ul><li><i>tsá</i> — "only then"</li>
<li><i>tsú</i> — "also"</li>
<li><i>s'é</i> — "first", "really!"</li>
<li><i>déi</i> — "now", "this time"</li>
<li><i>x'wán</i> — "be sure to"</li>
<li><i>tsé</i> — "be sure not to"</li></ul>
<p>Except for <i>x'wán</i> and <i>tsé</i>, the above may occur after the focus particles.
</p><p>The following are prephrasal particles, i.e. they occur before the phrase that they modify. Naish and Story call these "pre-marginals".
</p>
<ul><li><i>ch'a</i> — "just", "the very"</li>
<li><i>ch'as</i> — "only", "just"</li>
<li><i>ch'ú</i> — "even"</li>
<li><i>tlaxh</i> — "very"</li></ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Mobile_particles">Mobile particles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tlingit_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Mobile particles"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>These particles may occur before or after any phrase in a clause.
</p>
<ul><li><i>tlei</i> — "just," "simply," "just then"</li>
<li><i>déi</i> — "already," "by now"</li>
<li><i>tsu</i> — "again", "still", "some more"</li></ul>
<p>Compare the mobile particle <i>tsu</i> with the postphrasal particle <i>tsú</i>. Both the sentence <i>káaxwei <b>tsu</b> eetéenaxh xhat yatee</i> "I need more coffee" and the sentence <i>káaxwei <b>tsú</b> eetéenaxh xhat yatee</i> "I also need coffee" are acceptable. However the sentence <i>*<b>tsú</b> káaxwei eetéenaxh xhat yatee</i> is syntactically inadmissible because the particle <i>tsú</i> is postphrasal, i.e. it cannot precede the phrase it modifies, in this case the noun phrase <i>káaxwei</i>. The corresponding sentence with the <i>tsu</i> particle in front, <i><b>tsu</b> káaxwei eetéenaxh xhat yatee</i>  "I need coffee again/still" is in contrast syntactically acceptable. Thus a Tlingit listener will recognize the <i>tsu</i> particle in a phrase-initial position without confusion but tone is necessary to distinguish it in a phrase-final position. For this reason the <i>tsu</i> particle is often used prephrasally although it is syntactically admitted in either position. Thus the song name <i>Tsu Héidei Shugaxhtootaan</i> could also be <i>héidei tsu shugaxhtootaan</i>, but placing the <i>tsu</i> in front has the advantage of unambiguity, and thus seems more <a href="/wiki/Euphony" class="mw-redirect" title="Euphony">euphonious</a> to native speakers.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Sentence-initial_particles">Sentence-initial particles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tlingit_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Sentence-initial particles"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>These particles may only occur at the front of a sentence. Naish-Story term these "clause marginals".
</p>
<ul><li><i>tléik</i>, <i>l</i> — negative, "not"</li>
<li><i>gwál</i> — dubitative, "perhaps"</li>
<li><i>gu.aal</i> — optative, "hopefully"</li>
<li><i>khaju</i>, <i>xhaju</i> — contrary, "actually", "in fact"</li>
<li><i>khashde</i> — "I thought..."</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tlingit-language_media">Tlingit-language media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tlingit_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Tlingit-language media"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Irish</a> TV series <i><a href="/wiki/An_Klondike" title="An Klondike">An Klondike</a></i> (2015–17), set in Canada in the 1890s, contains Tlingit dialogue.
</p><p>The American comedy-drama <a href="/wiki/Northern_Exposure" title="Northern Exposure">Northern Exposure</a> contains Tlingit dialogue.
</p><p>In 2023, the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Central_Council_of_the_Tlingit_%26_Haida_Indian_Tribes&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Central Council of the Tlingit &amp; Haida Indian Tribes (page does not exist)">Central Council of the Tlingit &amp; Haida Indian Tribes</a> (Tlingit &amp; Haida) announced the release of the first of nine Tlingit-language children's books and animated videos. As a collaborative effort between Tlingit &amp; Haida, the Goldbelt Heritage Foundation, Cedar Group, and illustrators <a href="/wiki/Kelsey_Mata" title="Kelsey Mata">Kelsey Mata</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nick_Alan_Foote&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Nick Alan Foote (page does not exist)">Nick Alan Foote</a>, the project is funded under a three-year grant through the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Education" title="United States Department of Education">United States Department of Education</a>'s Alaska Native Education Program. The first book is titled <i>Kuhaantí (2023)</i> and has set release date of October 27, 2023.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
