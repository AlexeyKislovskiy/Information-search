<p><b>Mam</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Mayan_languages" title="Mayan languages">Mayan language</a> spoken by about half a million <a href="/wiki/Mam_people" title="Mam people">Mam people</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Departments_of_Guatemala" title="Departments of Guatemala">Guatemalan departments</a> of <a href="/wiki/Quetzaltenango_Department" title="Quetzaltenango Department">Quetzaltenango</a>, <a href="/wiki/Huehuetenango_Department" title="Huehuetenango Department">Huehuetenango</a>, <a href="/wiki/San_Marcos_Department" title="San Marcos Department">San Marcos</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Retalhuleu_Department" title="Retalhuleu Department">Retalhuleu</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexican</a> states of <a href="/wiki/Campeche" title="Campeche">Campeche</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chiapas" title="Chiapas">Chiapas</a>. Thousands more make up a Mam diaspora throughout the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>, with notable populations living in <a href="/wiki/Oakland,_California" title="Oakland, California">Oakland, California</a><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a> The most extensive Mam grammar is Nora C. England's <i>A grammar of Mam, a Mayan language</i> (1983), which is based on the <a href="/wiki/San_Ildefonso_Ixtahuac%C3%A1n" title="San Ildefonso Ixtahuacán">San Ildefonso Ixtahuacán</a> dialect of <a href="/wiki/Huehuetenango_Department" title="Huehuetenango Department">Huehuetenango Department</a>.
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">Classification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mam_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Classification"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1033289096">.mw-parser-output .hatnote{font-style:italic}.mw-parser-output div.hatnote{padding-left:1.6em;margin-bottom:0.5em}.mw-parser-output .hatnote i{font-style:normal}.mw-parser-output .hatnote+link+.hatnote{margin-top:-0.5em}</style><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Mayan_languages#Subdivisions" title="Mayan languages">Mayan languages §&#160;Subdivisions</a></div>
<p>Mam is closely related to the <a href="/wiki/Tektitek_language" title="Tektitek language">Tektitek language</a>, and the two languages together form the Mamean sub-branch of the Mayan language family. Along with the Ixilan languages, <a href="/wiki/Awakatek_language" title="Awakatek language">Awakatek</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ixil_language" title="Ixil language">Ixil</a>, these make up the <a href="/wiki/Mamean_languages" title="Mamean languages">Greater Mamean</a> sub-branch, one of the two branches of the Eastern Mayan languages (the other being the <a href="/wiki/Quichean_languages" title="Quichean languages">Greater Quichean</a> sub-branch, which consists of 10 Mayan languages, including <a href="/wiki/K%CA%BCiche%CA%BC_language" title="Kʼicheʼ language">Kʼicheʼ</a>).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dialects">Dialects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mam_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Dialects"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Because contact between members of different Mam communities is somewhat limited, the language varies considerably even from village to village. Nevertheless, mutual intelligibility, though difficult, is possible through practice.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Mam varieties within Mexico and Guatemala are divided into five dialect groups:<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><b>Northern Mam</b> in <a href="/wiki/Campeche" title="Campeche">Campeche</a>, Mexico and southern <a href="/wiki/Huehuetenango_Department" title="Huehuetenango Department">Huehuetenango Department</a>, Guatemala.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Northern Mam is the least conservative group according to <a href="/wiki/Terrence_Kaufman" title="Terrence Kaufman">Terrence Kaufman</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Southern Mam</b> in southern <a href="/wiki/Campeche" title="Campeche">Campeche</a>, Mexico and <a href="/wiki/Quetzaltenango_Department" title="Quetzaltenango Department">Quetzaltenango Department</a>, <a href="/wiki/San_Marcos_Department" title="San Marcos Department">San Marcos Department</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Retalhuleu_Department" title="Retalhuleu Department">Retalhuleu Department</a>, Guatemala.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Central Mam</b> in <a href="/wiki/Chiapas" title="Chiapas">Chiapas</a>, Mexico and <a href="/wiki/San_Marcos_Department" title="San Marcos Department">San Marcos Department</a>, Guatemala.</li>
<li><b>Western Mam</b> in eastern <a href="/wiki/Chiapas" title="Chiapas">Chiapas</a>, Mexico and northwestern <a href="/wiki/San_Marcos_Department" title="San Marcos Department">San Marcos Department</a>, Guatemala.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Tektitek_language" title="Tektitek language">Tektitek language</a> may be mutually intelligible with Western Mam dialects.</li>
<li><b>Soconusco Mam</b> in the <a href="/wiki/Soconusco" title="Soconusco">Soconusco</a> region, <a href="/wiki/Chiapas" title="Chiapas">Chiapas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>In addition to these, the dialects of <a href="/wiki/Chiapas" title="Chiapas">Chiapas, Mexico</a> are characterized by significant grammatical as well as lexical differences from the Guatemalan varieties.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution">Distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mam_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Mam is spoken in 64 communities in four <a href="/wiki/Departments_of_Guatemala" title="Departments of Guatemala">Guatemalan departments</a><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> and numerous communities in <a href="/wiki/Campeche" title="Campeche">Campeche</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chiapas" title="Chiapas">Chiapas, Mexico</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Neighboring languages include <a href="/wiki/Jakaltek_language" title="Jakaltek language">Jakaltek</a> and <a href="/wiki/Q%CA%BCanjob%CA%BCal_language" title="Qʼanjobʼal language">Qʼanjobʼal</a> to the north, <a href="/wiki/Tektitek_language" title="Tektitek language">Tektitek</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mocho%CA%BC_language" title="Mochoʼ language">Qato'k</a> to the west, and <a href="/wiki/Ixil_language" title="Ixil language">Ixil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Awakatek_language" title="Awakatek language">Awakatek</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sipakapense_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Sipakapense language">Sipacapense</a>, and <a href="/wiki/K%CA%BCiche%CA%BC_language" title="Kʼicheʼ language">Kʼicheʼ</a> to the east.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Phonology">Phonology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mam_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Phonology"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Stress">Stress</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mam_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Stress"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Mam has weight sensitive stress assignment.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Primary stress falls on the long vowel in a word if there is one, e.g. <span title="Mam-language text"><i lang="mam">aq'ú:ntl</i></span> 'work'. Words without a long vowel assign primary stress to the vowel preceding the last glottal stop, e.g. <span title="Mam-language text"><i lang="mam">puʔláʔ</i></span> 'dipper'. Words without a long vowel or a glottal stop assign stress to the vowel preceding the last consonant in the root, e.g. <span title="Mam-language text"><i lang="mam">xpicháqʼ</i></span> 'raccoon'. Stress is not assigned to suffixes or enclitics that do not have long vowels or a glottal stop.
</p>

<ul><li>The Mid-central vowel is an allophone of short a, e and u that can occur in the syllable following a stressed long vowel.</li></ul>
<p>Like in many other Mayan languages, <a href="/wiki/Vowel_length" title="Vowel length">vowel length</a> is contrastive, and short and long vowels have different phonemic values and are treated as separate vowels. The long versions of the non-front vowels, transcribed <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;oo&#x27e9;</span>, <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;uu&#x27e9;</span> and <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;aa&#x27e9;</span>, are slightly <a href="/wiki/Compressed_vowel" class="mw-redirect" title="Compressed vowel">compressed</a> and pronounced [o͍ː], [u͍ː] and [ɒ͍ː].
</p><p>In the Todos Santos dialect the vowel structure is somewhat different. While /o/, /a/, and /u/ remain the same as in other varieties, short /e/ has become the diphthong /ɛi/, an audio example of this can be heard here:<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the Todos Santos dialect, the long vowels (distinguished by the doubling of the letter) have evolved into separate sounds altogether. Long /aː/ has become /ɒ/,  long /oː/ has become /øː/ and long /uː/ has become /yː/.
</p><p>In some dialects vowels interrupted by a stop have evolved into individual phonemes themselves, for example in Todos Santos dialect
/oʔ/ (spelled oʼ) has evolved into /ɵʏˀ/ and /oʔo/ (spelled oʼo) has evolved into /ɵʼʉ/.
</p>

<ul><li>Todos Santos Mam has an extended amount of affricate consonants being apical palato-alveolar <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/t̺͡ʃ̺ʰ,<span class="wrap"> </span>t̺͡ʃ̺ʼ,<span class="wrap"> </span>ʃ̺/</span>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>/ɓ/ is realized as <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">&#91;<a href="/wiki/Ejective_consonant" title="Ejective consonant">βʼ</a>&#93;</span><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="Voiced ejectives are not physiologically possible. (May 2023)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> word-finally and when part of a consonant cluster in many dialects. In the Todos Santos dialect it is pronounced as <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">&#91;<a href="/wiki/Voiced_labiodental_fricative" title="Voiced labiodental fricative">v</a>&#93;</span> as part of a consonant cluster and as [βv̻] word finally.
</p>
<dl><dd>Examples: <span title="Mam-language text"><i lang="mam"><b>tzeb</b></i></span> [tsɛβʼ] <i>goat</i>, <span title="Mam-language text"><i lang="mam"><b>kbon</b></i></span> [kβʼɤŋ] <i>small table</i>. In the Todos Santos dialect, <span title="Mam-language text"><i lang="mam"><b>tzeb</b></i></span>  is [tsɛiβv̻] and <span title="Mam-language text"><i lang="mam"><b>kbon</b></i></span> is [kvoŋ] <i>small table</i>.</dd></dl>
<p>/p/ is realized as [pʰ] word-finally and word initially, <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">&#91;<a href="/wiki/Voiceless_bilabial_plosive" title="Voiceless bilabial plosive">p</a>&#93;</span> elsewhere, <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">&#91;<a href="/wiki/Voiceless_bilabial_fricative" title="Voiceless bilabial fricative">ɸ</a>&#93;</span> in a consonant cluster and before short i, o, and u. It is pronounced as [ɸʰ] word finally in certain dialects. [f] is an interchangeable pronunciation of [ɸ].
</p>
<dl><dd>Examples: <span title="Mam-language text"><i lang="mam"><b>piich</b></i></span> [pʰiːt͡ʃ] <i>bird</i>, <span title="Mam-language text"><i lang="mam"><b>txkup</b></i></span> [ʈ͡ʂkʰɯpʰ] or [ʈ͡ʂkʰɯɸʰ] <i>animal</i>, <span title="Mam-language text"><i lang="mam"><b>ptzʼan</b></i></span> [pʰt͡sʼaŋ] or [ɸʰt͡sʼaŋ] <i>sugarcane</i>.</dd></dl>
<p>/ch/ has evolved from /tʃ/ to /sʃ/ in most Mexican dialects and some northern Guatemalan dialects. Sometimes the /t/ sound is still lightly pronounced before the stressed /sʃ/ sound.
</p>
<dl><dd>Example: <span title="Mam-language text"><i lang="mam"><b>choot</b></i></span> [tʃʰoːtʰ] <i>weeds</i> has evolved into [sʃøːtʰ] or [<sup>t</sup>sʃoːtʰ]</dd></dl>
<p>/t/ is realized as [tʰ] word-finally and before another consonant, [t] elsewhere.
</p>
<dl><dd>Examples: <span title="Mam-language text"><i lang="mam"><b>taʼl</b></i></span> [taʔl̥] <i>juice, soup</i>, <span title="Mam-language text"><i lang="mam"><b>chʼit</b></i></span> [t͡ʃʼɪtʰ] <i>bird</i>, <span title="Mam-language text"><i lang="mam"><b>qʼootj</b></i></span> [ʛoːtʰχ] <i>dough</i></dd></dl>
<p>/k/ is realized as [kʰ] word-finally and before another consonant, [k] elsewhere.
</p>
<dl><dd>Examples: <span title="Mam-language text"><i lang="mam"><b>paakiʼl</b></i></span> [pɑːkɪʔl̥] <i>butterfly</i>, <span title="Mam-language text"><i lang="mam"><b>xtook</b></i></span> [ʂtʰoːkʰ] <i>staff</i>, <span title="Mam-language text"><i lang="mam"><b>kjoʼn</b></i></span> [kʰχɤʔŋ] <i>cornfield</i></dd></dl>
<p>/w/ can be pronounced <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">&#91;<a href="/wiki/Voiced_labiodental_approximant" title="Voiced labiodental approximant">ʋ</a>&#93;</span>, <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">&#91;<a href="/wiki/Voiced_labiodental_fricative" title="Voiced labiodental fricative">v</a>&#93;</span>, <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">&#91;<a href="/wiki/Voiceless" class="mw-redirect" title="Voiceless">v̥</a>&#93;</span> or <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">&#91;<a href="/wiki/Voiced_bilabial_fricative" title="Voiced bilabial fricative">β</a>&#93;</span> word initially, <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">&#91;<a href="/wiki/Voiced_labial%E2%80%93velar_approximant" title="Voiced labial–velar approximant">w</a>&#93;</span>, <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">&#91;<a href="/wiki/Voiceless_labial%E2%80%93velar_fricative" title="Voiceless labial–velar fricative">ʍ</a>&#93;</span>, <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">&#91;<a href="/wiki/Voiced_labiodental_approximant" title="Voiced labiodental approximant">ʋ</a>&#93;</span> following a consonant, and <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">&#91;<a href="/wiki/Voiced_labiodental_approximant" title="Voiced labiodental approximant">ʋ</a>&#93;</span>, <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">&#91;<a href="/wiki/Voiced_labiodental_fricative" title="Voiced labiodental fricative">v</a>&#93;</span>, <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">&#91;<a href="/wiki/Laminal_consonant" title="Laminal consonant">v̻ʰ</a>&#93;</span> or <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">&#91;<a href="/wiki/Aspirated_consonant" title="Aspirated consonant">fʰ</a>&#93;</span> word finally. It is freely variable between <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">&#91;<a href="/wiki/Voiced_labial%E2%80%93velar_approximant" title="Voiced labial–velar approximant">w</a>&#93;</span> <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">&#91;<a href="/wiki/Voiced_labiodental_fricative" title="Voiced labiodental fricative">v</a>&#93;</span> <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">&#91;<a href="/wiki/Voiced_labiodental_approximant" title="Voiced labiodental approximant">ʋ</a>&#93;</span> <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">&#91;<a href="/wiki/Voiceless" class="mw-redirect" title="Voiceless">v̥</a>&#93;</span> in all other positions with <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">&#91;<a href="/wiki/Voiced_labiodental_approximant" title="Voiced labiodental approximant">ʋ</a>&#93;</span> being the most common pronunciation. In the Todos Santos dialect, /w/ is realized as either <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">&#91;<a href="/wiki/Voiced_labiodental_fricative" title="Voiced labiodental fricative">v</a>&#93;</span> or <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">&#91;<a href="/wiki/Voiced_labiodental_approximant" title="Voiced labiodental approximant">ʋ</a>&#93;</span> word-initially or between vowels and before another consonant, as <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">&#91;<a href="/wiki/Voiceless_labial%E2%80%93velar_fricative" title="Voiceless labial–velar fricative">ʍ</a>&#93;</span> following a consonant and as <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">&#91;<a href="/wiki/Voiceless" class="mw-redirect" title="Voiceless">v̥</a>&#93;</span> word finally.
</p>
<dl><dd>Examples: <span title="Mam-language text"><i lang="mam"><b>waaj</b></i></span> [ʋɑːχ], [vɑːχ], [v̥ɑːχ], or [βɑːχ] <i>tortilla</i>, <span title="Mam-language text"><i lang="mam"><b>twon</b></i></span> [twɤŋ], [tʍɤŋ], [tʋɤŋ] <i>introversion</i>, <span title="Mam-language text"><i lang="mam"><b>lew</b></i></span> [lɛʋ], [lɛv] [lɛv̥ʰ] [lɛfʰ] <i>care</i>.</dd></dl>
<p>/q/ is realized as <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;qʰ&#x27e9;</span> word-finally and before another consonant, <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;q&#x27e9;</span> elsewhere.
</p>
<dl><dd>Examples: <span title="Mam-language text"><i lang="mam">'<i>muuqin<b></b></i></i></span><i lang="mam"><b>'</b></i> [muːqɪŋ] <i>tortilla</i>, <span title="Mam-language text"><i lang="mam"><b>aaq</b></i></span> [ɑːqʰ] <i>honeycomb</i>, <span title="Mam-language text"><i lang="mam"><b>qloolj</b></i></span> [qʰɺoːlχ] <i>obscurity</i></dd></dl>
<p>/tʼ/ is realized interchangeably as <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;tʼ&#x27e9;</span> and <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;ɗ&#x27e9;</span> word-initially and -finally, after a vowel or before <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;l&#x27e9;</span>.
</p>
<dl><dd>Examples: <span title="Mam-language text"><i lang="mam"><b>tʼrikpuul</b></i></span> [tʼɾɪkʰɸuːl̥] ~ [ɗɾɪkʰpuːl̥] <i>to jump</i>, <span title="Mam-language text"><i lang="mam"><b>chʼuutʼ</b></i></span> [t͡ʃʼuːtʼ] ~ [t͡ʃʼuːɗ] <i>something sharp-pointed</i></dd>
<dd>Examples: <span title="Mam-language text"><i lang="mam"><b>tʼutʼan</b></i></span> [tʼɯtʼaŋ] ~ [ɗɯɗaŋ] <i>wet</i>, <span title="Mam-language text"><i lang="mam"><b>witʼli</b></i></span> [vɪtʼli] ~ [vɪɗli] <i>seated squatting</i></dd></dl>
<p>/n/ is realized as <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;ŋ&#x27e9;</span> before <a href="/wiki/Velar_consonant" title="Velar consonant">velar-</a> and <a href="/wiki/Uvular_consonant" title="Uvular consonant">uvular consonants</a> and word-finally, as <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;ɲ&#x27e9;</span> before <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;j&#x27e9;</span> and as <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;m&#x27e9;</span> before /ɓ/ and /p/, <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;n&#x27e9;</span> elsewhere.
</p>
<dl><dd>Examples: <span title="Mam-language text"><i lang="mam"><b>nim</b></i></span> [nɪm] <i>much</i>, <span title="Mam-language text"><i lang="mam"><b>juun</b></i></span> [χuːŋ] <i>one</i>, <span title="Mam-language text"><i lang="mam"><b>qʼankyoq</b></i></span> [ʛaŋkʲɤqʰ] <i>thunder</i></dd>
<dd>Examples: <span title="Mam-language text"><i lang="mam"><b>saajel</b></i></span> [sɑːŋχel̥] <i>sent</i>, <span title="Mam-language text"><i lang="mam"><b>nyuxh</b></i></span> [ɲɯʃ] <i>my godfather</i></dd>
<dd>Examples: <span title="Mam-language text"><i lang="mam"><b>qanbʼax</b></i></span> [qamɓaʂ] <i>foot</i>, <span title="Mam-language text"><i lang="mam"><b>npwaaqe</b></i></span> [mpwɑːqɛ] <i>my money</i></dd></dl>
<p>/l/ is realized as <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">&#91;<a href="/wiki/Voiceless_alveolar_lateral_approximant" class="mw-redirect" title="Voiceless alveolar lateral approximant">l̥</a>&#93;</span> word-finally, <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">&#91;<a href="/wiki/Voiced_alveolar_lateral_flap" class="mw-redirect" title="Voiced alveolar lateral flap">ɺ</a>&#93;</span> before short vowels and after plosives, bilabial, alveolar and retroflex consonants and [l] elsewhere. 
</p>
<dl><dd>Examples: <span title="Mam-language text"><i lang="mam"><b>luux</b></i></span> [luːʂ] <i>cricket</i>, <span title="Mam-language text"><i lang="mam"><b>loʼl</b></i></span> [ɺoʔl̥] <i>to eat fruits</i>, <span title="Mam-language text"><i lang="mam"><b>wlat</b></i></span> [vɺatʰ] <i>stiff</i>.</dd></dl>
<p><span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;ky&#x27e9;</span> is realized as <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">&#91;<a href="/wiki/Palatalization_(phonetics)" title="Palatalization (phonetics)">kɕʲ</a>&#93;</span> in front of another consonant and <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">&#91;<a href="/wiki/Aspirated_consonant" title="Aspirated consonant">kɕʰ</a>&#93;</span> word finally.  It is pronounced as <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">&#91;<a href="/wiki/Palatalization_(phonetics)" title="Palatalization (phonetics)">kʲ</a>&#93;</span> in all other instances.
</p>
<dl><dd>Examples: <span title="Mam-language text"><i lang="mam"><b>kyjaʼtzan</b></i></span> [kɕʲχaʲʔtsʰaŋ], <span title="Mam-language text"><i lang="mam"><b>kyokleen</b></i></span> [kʲɤkleːŋ]</dd></dl>
<p>/ ʼ / is realized as [ʲʔ] following /a/, /aa/, /e/, /ee/, /i/, /u/, /uu/ and /oo/. The standard pronunciation is simply <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">&#91;<a href="/wiki/Glottal_stop" title="Glottal stop">ʔ</a>&#93;</span> after all vowels however in spoken speech [ʲʔ] is the common pronunciation. A similar trend can be seen in other Eastern Mayan languages. After /o/ it is pronounced as [ʉʔ] and after /ii/ it is pronounced simply as [ʔ]. Following consonants / ʼ / modifies each individual consonant differently as explained in the section above. In the Mam language every word must start with a consonant. In the current orthography initial / ʼ / is not written but if a word ever begins with a vowel, the word is treated as if it begin with a / ʼ /. The initial / ʼ / may be pronounced as either [ʔ] or [ʡ] in free variation.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Syllable_structure">Syllable structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mam_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Syllable structure"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Most roots take the morphological shape CVC.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> The only possible root final consonant cluster is -<i>nC</i>. Syllables can have up to four consonants in a cluster in any position. Most consonant clusters are the result of vowel dropping and morpheme addition.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Morphology">Morphology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mam_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Morphology"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Mam has two sets of agreement markers, known to Mayanists as <b>Set A</b> and <b>Set B</b> markers, which can appear on both nouns and verbs. Mam uses <b>Set A</b> (ergative) markers on nouns to mark possessor agreement and on verbs to cross-reference the transitive subject. Mam uses <b>Set B</b> (absolutive) markers on transitive verbs to cross-reference the object and on intransitive verbs to cross-reference the subject. <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Mam_verb_complex">The Mam verb complex</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mam_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: The Mam verb complex"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Verbs in Mam can include inflection for person, aspect and mode, as well as auxiliaries in the form of directionals.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> The verb complex has distinct forms for transitive and intransitive verb stems depending in part on whether the complex cross-references one or two arguments. The lexical status of the verb complex is ambiguous.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> The inflections with vowels are phonologically independent (indicated by spaces). Transitive verb complexes with directionals have a dependent suffix. Two of England's examples of intransitive and transitive verb complexes are shown below.

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pronouns">Pronouns</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mam_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Pronouns"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Mam has no independent pronouns.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> Rather, pronouns in Mam always exist as <a href="/wiki/Bound_morpheme" class="mw-redirect" title="Bound morpheme">bound morphemes</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Syntax">Syntax</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mam_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Syntax"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Mam has both verbal and non-verbal types of sentences. Verbal sentences have verbal predicates, whereas non-verbal sentences have a stative or a locative/existential predicate.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> Verbal predicates have an aspect marker, while non-verbal predicates do not have aspect marking. Both verbal and non-verbal predicates occur in sentence-initial position unless a focused or topicalized phrase is present.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Verbal_predicates">Verbal predicates</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mam_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Verbal predicates"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Verbal predicates are either transitive or intransitive according to the number of arguments cross-referenced in the verb complex. The number of arguments cross-referenced by the verb complex is not consistent with the transitivity of the verb root or the number of participants in an event. England notes examples of transitive verb roots that only appear in their antipassive or passive forms where they only cross-reference a single participant.

<p>Mam speakers use a higher proportion of intransitive sentences than speakers of other Mayan languages. England and Martin (2003) found a low frequency of transitive sentences in Mam texts. Pye (2017:114–115) found a low use of overt subjects in transitive sentences in adults speaking to children. One adult produced overt subjects in 6% of transitive sentences. The same adult produced overt subjects in 41% of intransitive sentences and produced overt objects in 49% of transitive sentences.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Child_Language">Child Language</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mam_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Child Language"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>An overview of child language acquisition in Mam can be found in Pye (2017). Child language data for Mam challenge many theories of language acquisition and demonstrate the need for more extensive documentation of native American languages.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Children acquiring Mam produce a higher proportion of verbs than children acquiring K’iche’, but a lower proportion of verbs compared to children acquiring Wastek and Chol. They produce a higher proportion of intransitive verbs relative to transitive verbs than children acquiring other Mayan languages (Pye, Pfeiler and Mateo Pedro 2017:22). Their high proportion of relational noun production is tied to their frequent use of intransitive verbs.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The following examples illustrate the children’s use of intransitive verbs to express events with two participants. Ages are shown as (years;months.days). WEN (2;0.2) used the intransitive verb <span title="Mam-language text"><i lang="mam">-kub’</i></span>  ("go_down") in reference to an event of picking coffee. She used the relational noun phrase <span title="Mam-language text"><i lang="mam">t-uʔn-a</i></span> to express the agent in an oblique phrase. CRU (2;5.12) used the intransitive verb <span title="Mam-language text"><i lang="mam">-el</i></span>  ("go_out") in reference to an event of taking out an object. She used the relational noun phrase <span title="Mam-language text"><i lang="mam">w-uʔn-a</i></span> to express the agent. JOS (2;6.17) used the intransitive verb <span title="Mam-language text"><i lang="mam">-b’aj</i></span> ("finish") in reference to finishing a drink. He used the possessive prefix on the noun <span title="Mam-language text"><i lang="mam">k’aʔ</i></span> ("drink")to express the agent. The examples overturn the hypothesis that children tie their use of transitive verbs to object manipulation events.
</p>

<p>Two-year-old Mam children produce the consonants /m, n, p, t, ch, k, ʔ, l, y and w/. They produce /ʔ/ in place of glottalized stops, /p/ in place of /ɓ/, /k/ in place of /ky/ and /q/, /ch/ in place of /tz/ and /tx/, /xh/ in place of /x/, and /l/ in place of /r/. Mam children begin producing ejective consonants after they are three and a half years old.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> The early production of /ch/ and /l/ in Mam, as well as the late production of /s/, overturns predictions that all children have similar phonologies due to articulatory development.
</p><p>The acquisition of morphology in Mam is heavily influenced by prosody. Two-year-old children favor the production of word syllables with primary stress, and most often produce syllables with the form CVC. Children do not consistently produce inflectional prefixes on nouns and verbs before they are four years old, although two-year-olds frequently produce verb suffixes, including the directional suffixes. Their production of the directional suffixes is evidence that two-year-old Mam children understand the complex grammatical constraints on the use of directionals. They distinguish between the use of the directional clitics and directional suffixes in indicative and imperative verbs. Two-year-old Mam speakers omit the person enclitic on nouns and verbs despite its high frequency of use in adult speech.
</p><p>The following examples illustrate WEN’s verb complex production.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> In (1), WEN produced the vowel /a/ from the verb root <span title="Mam-language text"><i lang="mam">-q'a</i></span> ("give"), the imperative suffix -n, and the directional suffix -tz as /xh/. (Many directionals have contracted forms as suffixes.). WEN omitted the person enclitic -a. In (2), WEN produced the progressive prefix n-, the vowel /e/ from the verb root <span title="Mam-language text"><i lang="mam">-el</i></span> ("go out"), a spurious /n/, and the directional suffix -tz as /ch/. The intransitive verb <span title="Mam-language text"><i lang="mam">-el</i></span> belongs to the class of motion verbs that take directional suffixes. Intransitive verbs outside of the class of motion verbs do not take directional suffixes except in imperative contexts. The verb <span title="Mam-language text"><i lang="mam">-el</i></span> contracts with the directional suffix -tz to produce the stem <span title="Mam-language text"><i lang="mam">-etz</i></span> ("go out to") in adult speech. WEN’s omission of the person enclitic and production of a spurious consonant overturn the hypothesis that children produce forms that are frequent in adult speech.
</p>
<ul><li>WEN (1;9.2)</li></ul>
<pre>      <span title="Mam-language text"><i lang="mam">aanxh!</i></span>
</pre>
<div class="interlinear" style="margin-left:5em"><div style="float: left; margin-bottom: 0.3em;margin-right: 1em;"><p style="margin: 0px;font-style: italic;">0-q’a-n-tzaj-a</p><p style="margin: 0px;"><abbr class="gloss-abbr" title="ergative agreement (Set A)" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-variant-numeric: oldstyle-nums; text-transform: lowercase;">ERG</abbr>.<abbr class="gloss-abbr" title="second person" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-variant-numeric: oldstyle-nums; text-transform: lowercase;"><a href="/wiki/Grammatical_person" title="Grammatical person">2</a></abbr>.<abbr class="gloss-abbr" title="singular number" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-variant-numeric: oldstyle-nums; text-transform: lowercase;"><a href="/wiki/Singular_number" class="mw-redirect" title="Singular number">SG</a></abbr>-give-<abbr class="gloss-abbr" title="imperative" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-variant-numeric: oldstyle-nums; text-transform: lowercase;">IMP</abbr>.<abbr class="gloss-abbr" title="transitive verb" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-variant-numeric: oldstyle-nums; text-transform: lowercase;">TV</abbr>-hither-<abbr class="gloss-abbr" title="person enclitic" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-variant-numeric: oldstyle-nums; text-transform: lowercase;">ENC</abbr></p></div><p style="display: none;">0-q’a-n-tzaj-a</p><p style="display: none;">ERG.2.SG-give-IMP.TV-hither-ENC</p><p style="clear: left;">"Give it!")</p><div style="clear: left; display: block;"></div>
</div>
<ul><li>WEN (1;8.21)</li></ul>
<pre>      <span title="Mam-language text"><i lang="mam">nench.</i></span>
</pre>
<div class="interlinear" style="margin-left:5em"><div style="float: left; margin-bottom: 0.3em;margin-right: 1em;"><p style="margin: 0px;font-style: italic;">n-0-el-tzaj</p><p style="margin: 0px;"><abbr class="gloss-abbr" title="progressive aspect" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-variant-numeric: oldstyle-nums; text-transform: lowercase;"><a href="/wiki/Progressive_aspect" class="mw-redirect" title="Progressive aspect">PROG</a></abbr>-<abbr class="gloss-abbr" title="absolutive agreement (Set B)" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-variant-numeric: oldstyle-nums; text-transform: lowercase;">ABS</abbr>.<abbr class="gloss-abbr" title="third person" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-variant-numeric: oldstyle-nums; text-transform: lowercase;"><a href="/wiki/Grammatical_person" title="Grammatical person">3</a></abbr>.<abbr class="gloss-abbr" title="singular number" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-variant-numeric: oldstyle-nums; text-transform: lowercase;"><a href="/wiki/Singular_number" class="mw-redirect" title="Singular number">SG</a></abbr>-go_out-hither</p></div><p style="display: none;">n-0-el-tzaj</p><p style="display: none;">PROG-ABS.3.SG-go_out-hither</p><p style="clear: left;">"She is going out toward something")</p><div style="clear: left; display: block;"></div>
</div>
<p>The children’s production of the directional suffixes demonstrates their early recognition of the distinction between intransitive and transitive verbs in Mam. This distinction is a core feature of Mam grammar, and underpins the ergative morphology on the verbs and nouns. The semantic diversity of the verbs and positionals overturns the hypothesis that children use prototypical activity scenes as a basis for constructing grammatical categories. The children’s grammatical acumen is best seen in their use of the ergative and absolutive agreement markers on verbs. The children produced the prevocalic allomorphs of the ergative markers in nearly all of their obligatory contexts. They produced the preconsonantal allomorphs of the ergative markers in 20% of their obligatory contexts.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Two-year-old Mam children display a remarkable awareness of the contexts for extending the use of ergative markers to cross-reference the subject of intransitive verbs. Outside of these contexts, they consistently produced absolutive person markers on intransitive verbs. Three Mam children produced ergative person markers on intransitive verbs in half of the obligatory contexts for extended ergativity (Pye, Pfeiler &amp; Mateo Pedro 2013:323). The children’s awareness of the contexts for extended ergative use is all the more remarkable because the contexts are tied to clauses in dependent contexts in which aspect is not overtly marked.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> The following example shows JOS’s use of extended ergative marking (in bold) on the intransitive verb <span title="Mam-language text"><i lang="mam">-ok</i></span> ("go_in") in a purpose clause headed by the adverb <span title="Mam-language text"><i lang="mam">ii</i></span> ("so that"). The children’s production of ergative markers on intransitive verbs in dependent contexts overturns the theory that children link ergative markers to the subjects of transitive verbs in all contexts.
</p>
<ul><li>JOS (2;6.14)</li></ul>
<pre>      <span title="Mam-language text"><i lang="mam">i <b>t</b>ok mahʔ.</i></span>
</pre>
<div class="interlinear" style="margin-left:5em"><div style="float: left; margin-bottom: 0.3em;margin-right: 1em;"><p style="margin: 0px;font-style: italic;">i</p><p style="margin: 0px;">so_that</p></div><div style="float: left; margin-bottom: 0.3em;margin-right: 1em;"><p style="margin: 0px;font-style: italic;"><b>t</b>-ok</p><p style="margin: 0px;"><b><abbr class="gloss-abbr" title="ergative agreement (Set A)" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-variant-numeric: oldstyle-nums; text-transform: lowercase;">ERG</abbr>.<abbr class="gloss-abbr" title="third person" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-variant-numeric: oldstyle-nums; text-transform: lowercase;"><a href="/wiki/Grammatical_person" title="Grammatical person">3</a></abbr>.<abbr class="gloss-abbr" title="singular number" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-variant-numeric: oldstyle-nums; text-transform: lowercase;"><a href="/wiki/Singular_number" class="mw-redirect" title="Singular number">SG</a></abbr></b>-go_in</p></div><div style="float: left; margin-bottom: 0.3em;margin-right: 1em;"><p style="margin: 0px;font-style: italic;">mal...</p><p style="margin: 0px;"><abbr class="gloss-abbr" title="noun classifier" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-variant-numeric: oldstyle-nums; text-transform: lowercase;">CL</abbr></p></div><p style="display: none;">i <b>t</b>-ok mal...</p><p style="display: none;">so_that <b>ERG.3.SG</b>-go_in CL</p><p style="clear: left;">"So that it is put..."</p><div style="clear: left; display: block;"></div>
</div>
<p>Mam two-year-olds produce sentences with a predicate-initial word order. The children, like adults, rarely produce the subject argument in transitive sentences. The Mam children show an ergative pattern of argument production that similar to the adult pattern.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> The children produced subject arguments in 7 percent or fewer of sentences with transitive verbs. The children produced subject arguments in 40 percent of sentences with intransitive verbs, and produced object arguments in 45 percent of sentences with transitive verbs.
</p><p>The acquisition data for Mam and other Mayan languages have profound implications for language acquisition theory. Children demonstrate an early proficiency with verb inflection in languages with a rich morphology and where the language’s prosodic structure highlights the morphology. The Mam children’s use of directionals and extended ergative marking shows that two-year-olds are capable of using complex affixes appropriately in their obligatory contexts. This morphology accounts for the language-specific look of the children’s early utterances and guides its development in later stages.
</p>
