<p><b>Shetland dialect</b> (also variously known as <b>Shetlandic</b>;<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> <b>broad</b> or <b>auld</b> <b>Shetland</b> or <b>Shaetlan</b>;<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> and referred to as <b>Modern Shetlandic Scots</b> (MSS) by some linguists) is a <a href="/wiki/Dialect" title="Dialect">dialect</a> of <a href="/wiki/Insular_Scots" title="Insular Scots">Insular Scots</a> spoken in <a href="/wiki/Shetland" title="Shetland">Shetland</a>, an <a href="/wiki/Archipelago" title="Archipelago">archipelago</a> to the north of mainland <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>. It is derived from the <a href="/wiki/Scots_language" title="Scots language">Scots</a> dialects brought to Shetland from the end of the fifteenth century by Lowland Scots, mainly from <a href="/wiki/Fife" title="Fife">Fife</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lothian" title="Lothian">Lothian</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> with a degree of Norse influence<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> from the <a href="/wiki/Norn_language" title="Norn language">Norn language</a>, which is an extinct <a href="/wiki/North_Germanic_languages" title="North Germanic languages">North Germanic language</a> spoken on the islands until the late 18th century.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Consequently, Shetland dialect contains many words of Norn origin. Many of them, if they are not place-names, refer to e.g. seasons, weather, plants, animals, places, food, materials, tools, colours, parts of boats.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Like <a href="/wiki/Mid_Northern_Scots" class="mw-redirect" title="Mid Northern Scots">Doric</a> in North East Scotland, Shetland dialect retains a high degree of autonomy due to geography and isolation from southern dialects. It has a large amount of unique vocabulary but as there are no standard criteria for distinguishing languages from dialects, whether or not Shetland dialect is a separate language from Scots is much debated.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Phonology">Phonology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shetland_dialect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Phonology"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>"Shetland dialect speakers generally have a rather slow delivery, pitched low and with a somewhat level intonation".<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Consonants">Consonants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shetland_dialect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Consonants"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>By and large, consonants are pronounced much as in other <a href="/wiki/Modern_Scots" title="Modern Scots">Modern Scots</a> varieties. Exceptions are:
The <a href="/wiki/Dental_fricative" title="Dental fricative">dental fricatives</a> <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ð/</span> and <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/θ/</span> may be realised as <a href="/wiki/Alveolar_plosive" class="mw-redirect" title="Alveolar plosive">alveolar plosives</a> <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[d]</span> and <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[t]</span> respectively,<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> for example <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[tɪŋ]</span> and <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ˈmɪdər]</span> rather than <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[θɪŋ]</span>, or <a href="/wiki/Th-debuccalization" class="mw-redirect" title="Th-debuccalization">debuccalised</a> <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[hɪŋ]</span> and <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[hɪn]</span>, (thing) and <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ˈmɪðər]</span> <i>mither</i> (mother) as in <a href="/wiki/Central_Scots" title="Central Scots">Central Scots</a>. The <i>qu</i> in <i>quick</i>, <i>queen</i> and <i>queer</i> may be realised <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[xʍ]</span> rather than <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[kw]</span>, initial <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/tʃ/</span> <i>ch</i> may be realised <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ʃ]</span> and the initial <a href="/wiki/Consonant_cluster" title="Consonant cluster">cluster</a> <i>wr</i> may be realised <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[wr]</span> or <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[wər]</span>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Vowel length is by and large determined by the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Vowel_Length_Rule" class="mw-redirect" title="Scottish Vowel Length Rule">Scottish Vowel Length Rule</a>, although there are a few exceptions.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Orthography">Orthography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shetland_dialect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Orthography"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>To some extent a bewildering variety of spellings have been used to represent the varied pronunciation of the Shetland dialect varieties.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Latterly the use of the <a href="/wiki/Apologetic_apostrophe" title="Apologetic apostrophe">apologetic apostrophe</a> to represent 'missing' English letters has been avoided.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> On the whole the literary conventions of Modern Scots are applied, if not consistently, the main differences being:
</p>
<ul><li>The <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/d/</span> and <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/t/</span> realisation of what is usually <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ð/</span> and <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/θ/</span> in other Scots dialects are often written <i>d</i> and <i>t</i> rather than <i>th</i>; "thing" and "there" written "<span title="Scots-language text"><i lang="sco">ting</i></span>" and "<span title="Scots-language text"><i lang="sco">dere</i></span>".</li>
<li>The <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/xʍ/</span> realisation of the <i>qu</i> in <i>quick</i>, <i>queen</i> and <i>queer</i> is often written <i>wh</i>; "queer" is written "<span title="Scots-language text"><i lang="sco">wheer</i></span>".</li>
<li>The <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ʃ/</span> realisation of initial <i>ch</i>, usually <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/tʃ/</span> in other Scots dialects, is often written <i>sh</i>; "chair" is written "<span title="Scots-language text"><i lang="sco">shair</i></span>".</li>
<li>The letters <i>j</i> and <i>k</i> are used rather than <i>y</i> and <i>c</i>, influenced by Norse spelling. The letter <i>j</i> is often used to render the <a href="/wiki/Semivowel" title="Semivowel">semivowel</a> <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/j/</span> of the letter <i>y</i>, especially for the <a href="/wiki/Palatalization_(sound_change)" title="Palatalization (sound change)">palatalised</a> consonants in words such as <i>Yule</i> in English— rendered <span title="Scots-language text"><i lang="sco">Yuil</i></span> in Scots— which becomes written <span title="Scots-language text"><i lang="sco">Jøl</i></span> in Shetland dialect (for the additional change of the Scots <i>ui</i> to <i>ø</i> in this word, see below).</li>
<li>Literary Scots <span title="Scots-language text"><i lang="sco">au</i></span> and <span title="Scots-language text"><i lang="sco">aw</i></span> (vowel 12 and sometimes vowel 17) are often represented by <span title="Scots-language text"><i lang="sco">aa</i></span> in written Shetland dialect.</li>
<li>Literary Scots <span title="Scots-language text"><i lang="sco">ui</i></span> and <span title="Scots-language text"><i lang="sco">eu</i></span> (vowel 7) are often represented by <span title="Scots-language text"><i lang="sco">ü</i></span>, <span title="Scots-language text"><i lang="sco">ö</i></span>, or <span title="Scots-language text"><i lang="sco">ø</i></span> influenced by Norse spelling.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Grammar">Grammar</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shetland_dialect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Grammar"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The grammatical structure of Shetland dialect generally follows that of <a href="/wiki/Modern_Scots" title="Modern Scots">Modern Scots</a>, with traces of Norse (Norn) and those features shared with <a href="/wiki/Standard_English" title="Standard English">Standard English</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Articles">Articles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shetland_dialect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Articles"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The definite article <i>the</i> is pronounced <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[də]</span> often written <i>da</i> in dialect writing. As is usual in Scots, Shetland dialect puts an article where Standard English would not:<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><i>gyaan ta da kirk/da scole in da Simmer</i>-- 'go to church/school in summer'
<i>da denner is ready</i>	                     'dinner is ready'
<i>hae da caald</i>		             'have a cold'
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nouns">Nouns</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shetland_dialect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Nouns"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Nouns in Shetland dialect have grammatical gender beside natural gender.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> Some nouns which are clearly considered neuter in English are masculine or feminine, such as <i>spade</i> (m), <i>sun</i> (m), <i>mön</i> (f), <i>kirk</i> (f). This can also apply to dummy constructions, e.g. <i>what time is he?</i> In a study comparing <a href="/wiki/Sullom_Voe_Terminal" title="Sullom Voe Terminal">pre-oil</a> Shetland dialect usage from oral history recordings and contemporary speech from interviews, the gender system in Shetland dialect was found to be a stable feature of modern dialect usage, and is not tied to use alongside "traditional lexical items".<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The plural of nouns is usually formed by adding -s, as in Standard English. There are a few irregular plurals, such as <i>kye</i>, 'cows' or <i>een</i>, 'eyes'.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pronouns">Pronouns</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shetland_dialect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Pronouns"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Shetland dialect also distinguishes between personal pronouns used by parents when speaking to children, old persons speaking to younger ones, or between familiar friends or equals<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> and those used in formal situations and when speaking to superiors.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> (See <a href="/wiki/T%E2%80%93V_distinction" title="T–V distinction">T–V distinction</a>)
</p><p>The familiar forms are <i>thoo</i> (thou), pronounced <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[duː]</span>, often written <i>du</i> in dialect writing; <i>thee</i>, pronounced <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[di(ː)]</span>, often written <i>dee</i> in dialect writing; <i>thy</i>, pronounced <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[daɪ]</span>, often written <i>dy</i> in dialect writing; and <i>thine</i>, pronounced <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[daɪn]</span>, often written <i>dine</i> in dialect writing; contrasting with the formal forms <i>you</i>, <i>you</i>, <i>your</i> and <i>yours</i>, respectively.
</p><p>The familiar <i>du</i> takes the singular form of the verb: <i>Du is, du hes</i> ('you are, you have').
</p><p>As is usual in Scots, the <a href="/wiki/Relative_pronoun" title="Relative pronoun">relative pronoun</a> is <i>that</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> also meaning who and which, pronounced <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[dat]</span> or <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ət]</span>, often written <i>dat</i><sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> or <i>'at</i> in dialect writing,<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> as in 
<i>da dog at bet me...</i> – 'the dog that bit me...'
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Verbs">Verbs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shetland_dialect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Verbs"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As is usual in Scots, the past tense of <a href="/wiki/Germanic_weak_verb" title="Germanic weak verb">weak verbs</a> is formed by either adding -<i>ed</i>, -<i>it</i>, or -<i>t</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> as in <i>spoot</i>, <i>spootit</i> (move quickly).
</p><p>The auxiliary verb <i>ta be</i> 'to be', is used where Standard English would use 'to have':<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> <i>I'm written</i> for 'I have written'.
</p><p><i>Ta hae</i> 'to have', is used as an auxiliary with the modal verbs <i>coud</i> ('could'), <i>hed</i> ('had'), <i>micht</i> ('might'), <i>most</i> ('must'), <i>sood</i> ('should'), and <i>wid</i> ('would') and then reduced to <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ə]</span>, often written <i>a</i> in dialect writing:<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> <i>Du sood a telt me</i>, 'you should have told me'.
</p><p>As is usual in Scots, auxiliary and monosyllabic verbs can be made negative by adding -<i>na</i>:<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> <i>widna</i>, 'would not'. Otherwise, the Scots negative has <i>no</i> where standard English has 'not'.
</p>
