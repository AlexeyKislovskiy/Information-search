<p><b>Aimaq</b> or <b>Aimaqi</b> (<a href="/wiki/Persian_language" title="Persian language">Persian</a>: <span lang="fa" dir="rtl">ایماقی</span>, <small><a href="/wiki/Romanization_of_Persian" title="Romanization of Persian">romanized</a>:&#160;</small><span title="Persian-language romanization"><i lang="fa-Latn">Aimāqi</i></span>) is the dominant eastern <a href="/wiki/Persian_language" title="Persian language">Persian</a> <a href="/wiki/Ethnolect" title="Ethnolect">ethnolect</a> spoken by the <a href="/wiki/Aimaq_people" title="Aimaq people">Aimaq people</a> in central northwest <a href="/wiki/Afghanistan" title="Afghanistan">Afghanistan</a> (west of the <a href="/wiki/Hazarajat" title="Hazarajat">Hazarajat</a>) and eastern <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>. It is close to the <a href="/wiki/Dari" title="Dari">Dari</a> varieties of Persian.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The Aimaq people are thought to have a 5–15% literacy rate.<sup id="cite_ref-e25_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-e25-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Phonology">Phonology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aimaq_dialect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Phonology"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Phonetically, as one of the eastern Persian dialects, the Aimaq dialect resembles a formal or classical form of <a href="/wiki/Persian_language" title="Persian language">Persian</a>.
</p><p>Vowels:
</p>
<ul><li>The "majhul" vowels <i>ē</i> / <i>ī</i> and <i>ō</i> / <i>ū</i> are still kept separate, whereas in <a href="/wiki/Western_Persian" class="mw-redirect" title="Western Persian">western Persian</a> they are merged as <i>ī</i> and <i>ū</i> respectively. For instance, the identically written words شیر  'lion' and 'milk' are in western Persian both pronounced [šīr], but in Aimaq [šēr] for 'lion' and [šīr] for 'milk'. The long vowel in زود 'quick' and زور 'strong' is realized as [ū] in western Persian, in contrast, these words are pronounced as [zūd] and [zōr] respectively by Aimaq speakers.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Diphthong" title="Diphthong">diphthongs</a> of early Classical Persian <i>aw</i> (as <i>ow</i> in Engl. <i>cow</i>) and <i>ay</i> (as <i>i</i> in English <i>ice</i>) have in Aimaq become <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ow]</span> (as in Engl. <i>low</i>) and <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ej]</span> (as in Engl. <i>day</i>). Dari, on the other hand, is more archaic, e.g. نوروز 'Persian New Year' is realized as <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[nowrūz]</span> in Iranian, and <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[nawrōz]</span> in Aimaq, and نخیر 'no' is uttered as <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[naχejr]</span> in Iranian, and as <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[naχajr]</span> in Aimaq.</li>
<li>The high short vowels [i] and [u] tend to be lowered in western Persian to [e] and [o].</li>
<li><span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/æ/</span><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="pronunciations above use &#91;a&#93;, not &#91;æ&#93; (January 2015)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> and <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/e/</span> are in Aimaq kept separate in word-final positions, unlike western Persian, where <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/æ/</span> has <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[e]</span> as a word-final allophone.</li></ul>
<p>Consonants:
</p>
<ul><li>Aimaq still retains the (classical) bilabial pronunciation <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[w]</span> of the labial consonant و, which is realized as a <a href="/wiki/Voiced_labiodental_fricative" title="Voiced labiodental fricative">voiced labiodental fricative</a> <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[v]</span> in western Persian. <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[v]</span> is found in Aimaq as an allophone of <i>f</i> before voiced consonants.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Voiced_uvular_stop" class="mw-redirect" title="Voiced uvular stop">voiced uvular stop</a> <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ɢ/</span> (ق) and <a href="/wiki/Voiced_velar_fricative" title="Voiced velar fricative">voiced velar fricative</a> <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/ɣ/</span> (غ) are still kept separate in Aimaq. They have coincided in western Persian (probably under the influence of Turkic languages like <a href="/wiki/Azeri_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Azeri language">Azeri</a> and <a href="/wiki/Turkmen_language" title="Turkmen language">Turkmen</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
