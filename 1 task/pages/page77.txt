<p><b>Mura</b> is a language of <a href="/wiki/Amazonas_(Brazilian_state)" title="Amazonas (Brazilian state)">Amazonas</a>, Brazil. It is most famous for <a href="/wiki/Pirah%C3%A3_language" title="Pirahã language">Pirahã</a>, its sole surviving dialect. Linguistically, it is typified by <a href="/wiki/Agglutinative" class="mw-redirect" title="Agglutinative">agglutinativity</a>, a very small <a href="/wiki/Phoneme" title="Phoneme">phoneme inventory</a> (around 11 compared to  around 44 in English), <a href="/wiki/Whistled_speech" class="mw-redirect" title="Whistled speech">whistled speech</a>, and the use of <a href="/wiki/Tone_(linguistics)" title="Tone (linguistics)">tone</a>.
</p><p>In the 19th century, there were an estimated 30,000–60,000 Mura<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (April 2018)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>. It is now spoken by only 300 Pirahã people in eight villages.
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dialects">Dialects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mura_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Dialects"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Since at least Barboza Rodrigues (1892) [reference?], there have been three ethnic names commonly listed as dialects of Mura, or even as Muran languages. The names are:
</p>
<ul><li><b>Bohurá</b>, or <i>Buxwaray</i>, the original form of the name 'Mura'; spoken on the <a href="/wiki/Autaz-mirim_River" title="Autaz-mirim River">Autaz River</a><sup id="cite_ref-Loukotka_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Loukotka-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Pirah%C3%A3_language" title="Pirahã language">Pirahã</a></b>, or <i>Pirahá, Pirahán</i>, the name the remaining dialect goes by</li>
<li><b>Yahahí</b>, also spelled <i>Jahahi</i>; spoken on the <a href="/wiki/Branco_River" title="Branco River">Branco River</a><sup id="cite_ref-Loukotka_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Loukotka-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>On the basis of a minuscule amount of data, it would appear that Bohurá (Mura proper) was <a href="/wiki/Mutually_intelligible" class="mw-redirect" title="Mutually intelligible">mutually intelligible</a> with Pirahã; however, for Yahahí there exists only ethnographic information, and it can be assumed they spoke the same language as other Mura. <a href="/wiki/Jo%C3%A3o_Barbosa_Rodrigues" title="João Barbosa Rodrigues">Rodrigues</a> describes the Yahahí as having come from the Branco river, a tributary of the right bank of the upper <a href="/wiki/Dos_Marmelos_River" title="Dos Marmelos River">Marmelos river</a>. The last Yahahí are said to have joined the Pirahã.
</p><p>The Mura/Bohurá endonym is <span title="Pirahã-language text"><i lang="myp">Buhuraen</i></span>, according to Barboza Rodrigues (1892), or <span title="Pirahã-language text"><i lang="myp">Buxivaray</i></span> ~ <span title="Pirahã-language text"><i lang="myp">Buxwarahay</i></span>, according to Tastevin (1923). This was pronounced <i>Murá</i> by their neighbors, the <a href="/wiki/Tor%C3%A1_language" title="Torá language">Torá</a> and <a href="/wiki/Matanawi_language" title="Matanawi language">Matanawi</a>. In his vocabulary, Rodrigues lists <i>Bohura</i> for the people and <i>bhurai-ada</i> "Mura language" for the language, from the Mura of the Manicoré River; Tastevin has <i>Bohurai</i> and <i>bohuarai-arase</i> for the same. They also record,
</p>
<dl><dd><span title="Pirahã-language text"><i lang="myp">nahi buxwara araha</i></span> "That one is Mura"</dd>
<dd><span title="Pirahã-language text"><i lang="myp">yane abahi araha buxwarái</i></span> "We are all Mura"</dd></dl>
<p>(Caution: these words need to be confirmed. The scanned text of Nimuendaju (1948) at the link has several errors, such as <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;c&#x27e9;</span> for <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;e&#x27e9;</span>, <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;h&#x27e9;</span> for <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;b&#x27e9;</span>, and <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;d&#x27e9;</span> for <span class="nowrap">&#x27e8;á&#x27e9;</span>.)
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Genealogical_relations">Genealogical relations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mura_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Genealogical relations"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Mura is often proposed to be related to <a href="/wiki/Matanawi_language" title="Matanawi language">Matanawí</a>. Kaufman (1994) also suggests a connection with <a href="/wiki/Huarpe_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Huarpe language">Huarpe</a> in his <i>Macro-Warpean</i> proposal.
</p>
