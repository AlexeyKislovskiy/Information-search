<p>The <b>Ainu languages</b> (<span class="rt-commentedText nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt" lang="en-fonipa"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="/aɪ/: &#39;i&#39; in &#39;tide&#39;">aɪ</span><span title="&#39;n&#39; in &#39;nigh&#39;">n</span><span title="/uː/: &#39;oo&#39; in &#39;goose&#39;">uː</span></span>/</a></span></span> <a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key"><i title="English pronunciation respelling"><span style="font-size:90%">EYE</span>-noo</i></a>),<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> sometimes known as <b>Ainuic</b>, are a small <a href="/wiki/Language_family" title="Language family">language family</a>, often regarded as a <a href="/wiki/Language_isolate" title="Language isolate">language isolate</a>, historically spoken by the <a href="/wiki/Ainu_people" title="Ainu people">Ainu people</a> of northern <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a> and neighboring islands.
</p><p>The primary varieties of Ainu are alternately considered a group of closely related languages<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> or divergent <a href="/wiki/Dialect#Dialect_or_language" title="Dialect">dialects</a> of a single <a href="/wiki/Language_isolate" title="Language isolate">language isolate</a>. The only surviving variety is <a href="/wiki/Ainu_language" title="Ainu language">Hokkaido Ainu</a>, which <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a> lists as <a href="/wiki/Atlas_of_the_World%27s_Languages_in_Danger" title="Atlas of the World&#39;s Languages in Danger">critically endangered</a>. <a href="/wiki/Sakhalin_Ainu_language" title="Sakhalin Ainu language">Sakhalin Ainu</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kuril_Ainu_language" title="Kuril Ainu language">Kuril Ainu</a> are now extinct. <a href="/wiki/Toponymy" title="Toponymy">Toponymic</a> evidence suggests Ainu was once spoken in northern <a href="/wiki/Honshu" title="Honshu">Honshu</a> and that much of the historically attested extent of the family was due to a relatively recent expansion northward. No genealogical relationship between Ainu and any other language family has been demonstrated, despite numerous attempts.
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Varieties">Varieties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ainu_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Varieties"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Recognition of the different varieties of Ainu spoken throughout northern Japan and its surrounding islands in academia varies. <span title="Japanese-language text"><span lang="ja-Latn" style="font-style: normal;">Shibatani</span></span> (1990:9) and <span title="Polish-language text"><span lang="pl" style="font-style: normal;"><a href="/wiki/Bronis%C5%82aw_Pi%C5%82sudski" title="Bronisław Piłsudski">Piłsudski</a></span></span> (1998:2) both speak of "Ainu languages" when comparing the varieties of language spoken in <a href="/wiki/Hokkaido" title="Hokkaido">Hokkaidō</a> and Sakhalin; however, <a href="#CITEREFVovin1993">Vovin (1993)</a> speaks only of "dialects". Refsing (1986) says Hokkaidō and Sakhalin Ainu were not <a href="/wiki/Mutually_intelligible" class="mw-redirect" title="Mutually intelligible">mutually intelligible</a>. <span title="Japanese-language text"><span lang="ja-Latn" style="font-style: normal;">Hattori</span></span> (1964) considered Ainu data from 19 regions of Hokkaidō and Sakhalin, and found the primary division to lie between the two islands.
</p>
<h3><span id="Hokkaid.C5.8D_Ainu"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Hokkaidō_Ainu">Hokkaidō Ainu</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ainu_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Hokkaidō Ainu"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Hokkaid%C5%8D_Ainu" class="mw-redirect" title="Hokkaidō Ainu">Hokkaidō Ainu</a> clustered into several dialects with substantial differences between them: the 'neck' of the island (<a href="/wiki/Oshima_Subprefecture" title="Oshima Subprefecture">Oshima County</a>, data from <span title="Japanese-language text"><span lang="ja-Latn" style="font-style: normal;"><a href="/wiki/Oshamanbe,_Hokkaid%C5%8D" class="mw-redirect" title="Oshamanbe, Hokkaidō">Oshamambe</a></span></span> and <span title="Japanese-language text"><span lang="ja-Latn" style="font-style: normal;"><a href="/wiki/Yakumo,_Hokkaid%C5%8D" class="mw-redirect" title="Yakumo, Hokkaidō">Yakumo</a></span></span>); the "classical" Ainu of central Hokkaidō around <span title="Japanese-language text"><span lang="ja-Latn" style="font-style: normal;"><a href="/wiki/Sapporo" title="Sapporo">Sapporo</a></span></span> and the southern coast (<span title="Japanese-language text"><span lang="ja-Latn" style="font-style: normal;"><a href="/wiki/Iburi_Subprefecture" title="Iburi Subprefecture">Iburi</a></span></span> and <span title="Japanese-language text"><span lang="ja-Latn" style="font-style: normal;"><a href="/wiki/Hidaka_Subprefecture" title="Hidaka Subprefecture">Hidaka</a></span></span> counties, data from <span title="Japanese-language text"><i lang="ja-Latn"><a href="/wiki/Noboribetsu,_Hokkaid%C5%8D" class="mw-redirect" title="Noboribetsu, Hokkaidō">Horobetsu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biratori,_Hokkaid%C5%8D" class="mw-redirect" title="Biratori, Hokkaidō">Biratori</a>, Nukkibetsu</i></span> and <span title="Japanese-language text"><span lang="ja-Latn" style="font-style: normal;"><a href="/wiki/Niikappu,_Hokkaid%C5%8D" class="mw-redirect" title="Niikappu, Hokkaidō">Niikappu</a></span></span>; historical records from <a href="/wiki/Ishikari_Subprefecture" title="Ishikari Subprefecture">Ishikari County</a> and Sapporo show that these were similar); <span title="Japanese-language text"><span lang="ja-Latn" style="font-style: normal;"><a href="/wiki/Samani,_Hokkaid%C5%8D" class="mw-redirect" title="Samani, Hokkaidō">Samani</a></span></span> (on the southeastern cape in <span title="Japanese-language text"><span lang="ja-Latn" style="font-style: normal;">Hidaka</span></span>, but perhaps closest to the northeastern dialect); the northeast (data from <span title="Japanese-language text"><span lang="ja-Latn" style="font-style: normal;"><a href="/wiki/Obihiro,_Hokkaid%C5%8D" class="mw-redirect" title="Obihiro, Hokkaidō">Obihiro</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kushiro,_Hokkaid%C5%8D" class="mw-redirect" title="Kushiro, Hokkaidō">Kushiro</a></span></span> and <span title="Japanese-language text"><span lang="ja-Latn" style="font-style: normal;"><a href="/wiki/Bihoro,_Hokkaid%C5%8D" class="mw-redirect" title="Bihoro, Hokkaidō">Bihoro</a></span></span>); the north-central dialect (<a href="/wiki/Kamikawa_Subprefecture" title="Kamikawa Subprefecture">Kamikawa County</a>, data from <span title="Japanese-language text"><span lang="ja-Latn" style="font-style: normal;"><a href="/wiki/Asahikawa,_Hokkaid%C5%8D" class="mw-redirect" title="Asahikawa, Hokkaidō">Asahikawa</a></span></span> and <span title="Japanese-language text"><span lang="ja-Latn" style="font-style: normal;"><a href="/wiki/Nayoro,_Hokkaid%C5%8D" class="mw-redirect" title="Nayoro, Hokkaidō">Nayoro</a></span></span>) and <span title="Japanese-language text"><span lang="ja-Latn" style="font-style: normal;"><a href="/wiki/S%C5%8Dya_Subprefecture" title="Sōya Subprefecture">Sōya</a></span></span> (on the northwestern cape), which was closest of all Hokkaidō varieties to Sakhalin Ainu. Most texts and grammatical descriptions we have of Ainu cover the Central Hokkaidō dialect.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Kuril_Ainu">Kuril Ainu</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ainu_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Kuril Ainu"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Data on <a href="/wiki/Kuril_Ainu" class="mw-redirect" title="Kuril Ainu">Kuril Ainu</a> is scarce, but it is thought to have been as divergent as Sakhalin and Hokkaidō.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sakhalin_Ainu">Sakhalin Ainu</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ainu_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Sakhalin Ainu"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Sakhalin_Ainu" class="mw-redirect" title="Sakhalin Ainu">Sakhalin Ainu</a>, an eastern coastal dialect of Taraika (near modern Gastello (<a href="/wiki/Poronaysk" title="Poronaysk">Poronaysk</a>)) was quite divergent from the other localities. The Raychishka dialect, on the western coast near modern <a href="/wiki/Uglegorsk,_Sakhalin_Oblast" title="Uglegorsk, Sakhalin Oblast">Uglegorsk</a>, is the best documented and has a dedicated grammatical description. <a href="/wiki/Take_Asai" title="Take Asai">Take Asai</a>, the last speaker of Sakhalin Ainu, died in 1994.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The Sakhalin Ainu dialects had long vowels and a final -h phoneme, which was pronounced <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">&#91;<a href="/wiki/Voiceless_velar_fricative" title="Voiceless velar fricative">x</a>&#93;</span>.
</p><p>Scant data from Western voyages at the turn of the 19th–20th century (<span title="Japanese-language text"><span lang="ja-Latn" style="font-style: normal;">Tamura</span></span> 2000) suggest there was also great diversity in northern Sakhalin, which was not sampled by <span title="Japanese-language text"><span lang="ja-Latn" style="font-style: normal;">Hattori</span></span>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="External_relationships">External relationships</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ainu_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: External relationships"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>No <a href="/wiki/Genetic_relationship_(linguistics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Genetic relationship (linguistics)">genealogical relationship</a> between Ainu and any other language family has been demonstrated, despite numerous attempts. Thus, it is a <a href="/wiki/Language_isolate" title="Language isolate">language isolate</a>. Ainu is sometimes grouped with the <a href="/wiki/Paleosiberian_languages" class="mw-redirect" title="Paleosiberian languages">Paleosiberian languages</a>, but this is only a geographic blanket term for several unrelated language families that were present in easternmost Siberia before the advances of Turkic and Tungusic languages there.
</p><p>A study by Lee and <span title="Japanese-language text"><span lang="ja-Latn" style="font-style: normal;">Hasegawa</span></span> of <a href="/wiki/Waseda_University" title="Waseda University">Waseda University</a> found evidence that the Ainu language and the early Ainu-speakers originated from the Northeast Asian/<a href="/wiki/Okhotsk_culture" title="Okhotsk culture">Okhotsk</a> population, which established themselves in northern Hokkaido and expanded into large parts of <a href="/wiki/Honshu" title="Honshu">Honshu</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Kurils" class="mw-redirect" title="Kurils">Kurils</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Lee_&amp;_Hasegawa_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lee_&amp;_Hasegawa-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Ainu languages share a noteworthy amount of vocabulary (especially fish names) with several Northeast Asian languages, including <a href="/wiki/Nivkh_languages" title="Nivkh languages">Nivkh</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tungusic_languages" title="Tungusic languages">Tungusic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mongolic_languages" title="Mongolic languages">Mongolic</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Chukotko-Kamchatkan_languages" title="Chukotko-Kamchatkan languages">Chukotko-Kamchatkan</a>. While linguistic evidence points to an origin of these words among the Ainu languages, its spread and how these words arrived into other languages will possibly remain a mystery.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The most frequent proposals for relatives of Ainu are given below:
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Altaic">Altaic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ainu_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Altaic"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>John C. Street (1962) proposed linking Ainu, <a href="/wiki/Korean_language" title="Korean language">Korean</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Japanese_language" title="Japanese language">Japanese</a> in one family and <a href="/wiki/Turkic_languages" title="Turkic languages">Turkic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mongolic_languages" title="Mongolic languages">Mongolic</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tungusic_languages" title="Tungusic languages">Tungusic</a> in another, with the two families linked in a common "North Asiatic" family. Street's grouping was an extension of the <a href="/wiki/Altaic_languages" title="Altaic languages">Altaic</a> hypothesis, which at the time linked Turkic, Mongolic, and Tungusic, sometimes adding Korean; today Altaic sometimes includes Korean and rarely Japanese but not Ainu (Georg et al. 1999).
</p><p>From a perspective more centered on Ainu, James Patrie (1982) adopted the same grouping, namely Ainu–Korean–Japanese and Turkic–Mongolic–Tungusic, with these two families linked in a common family, as in Street's "North Asiatic".
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Joseph_Greenberg" title="Joseph Greenberg">Joseph Greenberg</a> (2000–2002) likewise classified Ainu with Korean and Japanese. He regarded "Korean–Japanese-Ainu" as forming a branch of his proposed <a href="/wiki/Eurasiatic_languages" title="Eurasiatic languages">Eurasiatic language family</a>. Greenberg did not hold Korean–Japanese–Ainu to have an especially close relationship with Turkic–Mongolic–Tungusic within this family.
</p><p>The Altaic hypothesis is now rejected by the scholarly mainstream.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Austroasiatic">Austroasiatic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ainu_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Austroasiatic"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Shafer (1965) presented evidence suggesting a distant connection with the <a href="/wiki/Austroasiatic_languages" title="Austroasiatic languages">Austroasiatic languages</a>, which include many of the indigenous languages of Southeast Asia. <a href="#CITEREFVovin1992">Vovin (1992)</a> presented his reconstruction of Proto-Ainu with evidence, in the form of proposed sound changes and cognates, of a relationship with Austroasiatic. In <a href="#CITEREFVovin1993">Vovin (1993)</a>, he still regarded this hypothesis as preliminary.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Language_contact_with_the_Nivkhs">Language contact with the Nivkhs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ainu_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Language contact with the Nivkhs"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Ainu appear to have experienced intensive contact with the <a href="/wiki/Nivkh_people" title="Nivkh people">Nivkhs</a> during the course of their history. It is not known to what extent this has affected the language. Linguists believe the vocabulary shared between Ainu and <a href="/wiki/Nivkh_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Nivkh language">Nivkh</a> (historically spoken in the northern half of Sakhalin and on the Asian mainland facing it) is due to <a href="/wiki/Loanword" title="Loanword">borrowing</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Language_contact_with_the_Japanese">Language contact with the Japanese</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ainu_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Language contact with the Japanese"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Ainu came into extensive contact with the Japanese in the 14th century. Analytic grammatical constructions acquired or transformed in Ainu were probably due to contact with the Japanese language. A large number of Japanese loanwords were borrowed into Ainu and to a smaller extent vice versa.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> There are also a great number of <a href="/wiki/Loanword" title="Loanword">loanwords</a> from the Japanese language in various stages of its development to Hokkaidō Ainu, and a smaller number of loanwords from Ainu into Japanese, particularly animal names such as <span title="Japanese-language text"><i lang="ja-Latn">rakko</i></span> ('sea otter'; Ainu <span title="Ainu (Japan)-language text"><i lang="ain-Latn">rakko</i></span>), <span title="Japanese-language text"><i lang="ja-Latn">tonakai</i></span> ('reindeer'; Ainu <span title="Ainu (Japan)-language text"><i lang="ain-Latn">tunakkay</i></span>), and <span title="Japanese-language text"><i lang="ja-Latn"><a href="/wiki/Shishamo" title="Shishamo">shishamo</a></i></span> (a fish, <i>Spirinchus lanceolatus</i>; Ainu <span title="Ainu (Japan)-language text"><i lang="ain-Latn">susam</i></span>). Due to the low status of Ainu in Japan, many ancient loanwords may be ignored or undetected, but there is evidence of an older substrate, where older Japanese words which have no clear etymology appear related to Ainu words which do. An example is modern Japanese <span title="Japanese-language text"><i lang="ja-Latn">sake</i></span> or <span title="Japanese-language text"><i lang="ja-Latn">shake</i></span>, meaning 'salmon', probably from the Ainu <span title="Ainu (Japan)-language text"><i lang="ain-Latn">sak ipe</i></span> or <span title="Ainu (Japan)-language text"><i lang="ain-Latn">shak embe</i></span> for 'salmon', literally 'summer food'.
</p><p>According to P. Elmer (2019), the Ainu languages are a <a href="/wiki/Contact_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Contact language">contact language</a>, i.e. have strong influences from various Japonic dialects/languages during different stages, suggesting early and intensive contact between them somewhere in the <a href="/wiki/T%C5%8Dhoku_region" title="Tōhoku region">Tōhoku region</a>, with Ainu borrowing a large amount of vocabulary and typological characteristics from early Japonic.<sup id="cite_ref-Elmer_2019_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Elmer_2019-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_proposals">Other proposals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ainu_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Other proposals"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A small number of linguists suggested a relation between Ainu and <a href="/wiki/Indo-European_languages" title="Indo-European languages">Indo-European languages</a>, based on racial theories regarding the origin of the Ainu people. The theory of an Indo-European—Ainu relation was popular until 1960; later linguists dismissed it and concentrated on more local language families.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Tambovtsev (2008) proposes that Ainu is typologically most similar to Native American languages and suggests that further research is needed to establish a genetic relationship between these languages.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ainu_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Geography"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Until the 20th century, Ainu languages were spoken throughout the southern half of the island of <a href="/wiki/Sakhalin" title="Sakhalin">Sakhalin</a> and by small numbers of people in the Kuril Islands. Only the Hokkaido variant survives, with the last speaker of Sakhalin Ainu having died in 1994.
</p><p>Some linguists note that the Ainu language was an important <a href="/wiki/Lingua_franca" title="Lingua franca">lingua franca</a> on Sakhalin. <span title="Japanese-language text"><span lang="ja-Latn" style="font-style: normal;">Asahi</span></span> (2005) reported that the status of the Ainu language was rather high and was also used by early Russian and Japanese administrative officials to communicate with each other and with the indigenous people.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ainu_on_mainland_Japan">Ainu on mainland Japan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ainu_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Ainu on mainland Japan"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<figure class="mw-default-size mw-halign-right" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Historical_expanse_of_Ainu.png" class="mw-file-description"><img alt="A map of Japan and its northernmost territories, colour-coded to display the proposed historical extent of the Ainu language." src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Historical_expanse_of_Ainu.png/220px-Historical_expanse_of_Ainu.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="176" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Historical_expanse_of_Ainu.png/330px-Historical_expanse_of_Ainu.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Historical_expanse_of_Ainu.png/440px-Historical_expanse_of_Ainu.png 2x" data-file-width="632" data-file-height="505" /></a><figcaption>Attested historical extent of Ainu (red) and suspected earlier extent on Honshu (pink). The latter is based on toponymic evidence (red dots) and Matagi villages (purple dots). The western limit is defined by the early eastern limit of the Japanese language, as preserved in <a href="/wiki/Japanese_dialects" title="Japanese dialects">modern Japanese isoglosses</a>.</figcaption></figure>
<p>It is occasionally suggested that Ainu was the language of the indigenous <span title="Japanese-language text"><span lang="ja-Latn" style="font-style: normal;"><a href="/wiki/Emishi" title="Emishi">Emishi</a></span></span> people of the northern part of the main Japanese island of Honshu.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;a&#93;</a></sup> The main evidence for this is the presence of <a href="/wiki/Toponym" class="mw-redirect" title="Toponym">place names</a> that appear to be of Ainu origin in both locations. For example, the <span title="Japanese-language text"><i lang="ja-Latn">-betsu</i></span> common to many northern Japanese place names is known to derive from the Ainu word <span title="Ainu (Japan)-language text"><i lang="ain-Latn">'pet'</i></span> ("river") in Hokkaidō, and the same is suspected of similar names ending in <span title="Japanese-language text"><i lang="ja-Latn">-be</i></span> in northern Honshū and <span title="Japanese-language text"><span lang="ja-Latn" style="font-style: normal;"><a href="/wiki/Ch%C5%ABbu_region" title="Chūbu region">Chūbu</a></span></span>, such as the <span title="Japanese-language text"><span lang="ja-Latn" style="font-style: normal;"><a href="/wiki/Kurobe_River" title="Kurobe River">Kurobe</a></span></span> and <span title="Japanese-language text"><span lang="ja-Latn" style="font-style: normal;"><a href="/wiki/Oyabe_River" title="Oyabe River">Oyabe</a></span></span> rivers in <a href="/wiki/Toyama_Prefecture" title="Toyama Prefecture">Toyama Prefecture</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Other place names in <span title="Japanese-language text"><span lang="ja-Latn" style="font-style: normal;"><a href="/wiki/Kant%C5%8D_region" title="Kantō region">Kantō</a></span></span> and <span title="Japanese-language text"><span lang="ja-Latn" style="font-style: normal;">Chūbu</span></span>, such as <a href="/wiki/Mount_Ashigara" title="Mount Ashigara">Mount Ashigara</a> (<span title="Japanese-language text"><span lang="ja-Latn" style="font-style: normal;">Kanagawa–Shizuoka</span></span>), <span title="Japanese-language text"><span lang="ja-Latn" style="font-style: normal;"><a href="/wiki/Musashi_Province" title="Musashi Province">Musashi</a></span></span> (modern Tokyo), <a href="/wiki/Keta_Jinja_(Takaoka)" class="mw-redirect" title="Keta Jinja (Takaoka)">Keta Shrine</a> (<span title="Japanese-language text"><span lang="ja-Latn" style="font-style: normal;">Toyama</span></span>), and the <a href="/wiki/Noto_Peninsula" title="Noto Peninsula">Noto Peninsula</a>, have no explanation in Japanese, but do in Ainu. The traditional <span title="Japanese-language text"><i lang="ja-Latn"><a href="/wiki/Matagi" title="Matagi">matagi</a></i></span> hunters of the mountain forests of <span title="Japanese-language text"><span lang="ja-Latn" style="font-style: normal;"><a href="/wiki/T%C5%8Dhoku_region" title="Tōhoku region">Tōhoku</a></span></span> retain Ainu words in their hunting vocabulary.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> However, Elmer (2019) has also suggested Japonic etymologies, which supposedly got borrowed into early Ainu and lost in contemporary Japonic dialects.<sup id="cite_ref-Elmer_2019_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Elmer_2019-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The direction of influence and migration is debated. It has been proposed that at least some Jōmon period groups spoke a proto-Ainu language,<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> and that they displaced the <a href="/wiki/Okhotsk_culture" title="Okhotsk culture">Okhotsk culture</a> north from southern Hokkaido when the Ainu fled Japanese expansion into northern Honshu, with the Okhotsk ancestral to the modern <a href="/wiki/Nivkh_people" title="Nivkh people">Nivkh</a> as well as a component of the modern Ainu. However, it has also been proposed that the Ainu themselves can be identified with the Okhotsk culture, and that they expanded south into northern Honshu as well as to the <a href="/wiki/Kamchatka_Peninsula" title="Kamchatka Peninsula">Kamchatka Peninsula</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Lee_&amp;_Hasegawa_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lee_&amp;_Hasegawa-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> or that the Emishi spoke a Japonic language, most closely related to ancient <a href="/wiki/Izumo_dialect" class="mw-redirect" title="Izumo dialect">Izumo dialect</a>, rather than anything related to Ainu, with Ainu-speakers migrating later from Hokkaido to northern Tōhoku. The purported evidence for this are old-Japanese loanwords in the Ainu language, including basic vocabulary, as well as distinctive Japonic terms and toponyms found in Tōhoku and Hokkaido, that have been linked to the Izumo dialect.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
