<p><b>Mopan</b> (or <b>Mopan Maya</b>) is a language that belongs to the Yucatecan branch of the <a href="/wiki/Mayan_languages" title="Mayan languages">Mayan languages</a>. It is spoken by the <a href="/wiki/Mopan_people" title="Mopan people">Mopan people</a> who live in the <a href="/wiki/Pet%C3%A9n_Department" title="Petén Department">Petén Department</a> of <a href="/wiki/Guatemala" title="Guatemala">Guatemala</a> and in the <a href="/wiki/Maya_Mountains" title="Maya Mountains">Maya Mountains</a> region of <a href="/wiki/Belize" title="Belize">Belize</a>. There are between three and four thousand Mopan speakers in Guatemala and six to eight thousand in Belize.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHofling20111_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHofling20111-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The other Yucatecan languages are <a href="/wiki/Yucatec" class="mw-redirect" title="Yucatec">Yucatec</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lacandon_language" title="Lacandon language">Lacandon</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Itza%CA%BC_language" title="Itzaʼ language">Itzaʼ</a>. Mopan began to diverge from the other Yucatecan languages at least one thousand years ago.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHofling20111_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHofling20111-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKaufman1976b_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKaufman1976b-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution">Distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mopan_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Distribution"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Towns where Mopan is prominently spoken include <a href="/wiki/San_Luis,_El_Pet%C3%A9n" class="mw-redirect" title="San Luis, El Petén">San Luis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Popt%C3%BAn" title="Poptún">Poptún</a>, <a href="/wiki/Melchor_de_Mencos" title="Melchor de Mencos">Melchor de Mencos</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Dolores,_El_Pet%C3%A9n" class="mw-redirect" title="Dolores, El Petén">Dolores</a> in Guatemala,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKaufman1976a65_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKaufman1976a65-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> as well as <a href="/wiki/San_Antonio,_Toledo" title="San Antonio, Toledo">San Antonio</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Toledo_District" title="Toledo District">Toledo District</a> of Belize.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMwakikagile2010114_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMwakikagile2010114-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Grammar">Grammar</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mopan_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Grammar"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Word_order">Word order</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mopan_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Word order"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The word order in Mopan is <a href="/wiki/Verb-object-subject" class="mw-redirect" title="Verb-object-subject">verb-object-subject</a> (VOS),<sup id="cite_ref-Ethnologue_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ethnologue-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> although <a href="/wiki/Subject-verb-object" class="mw-redirect" title="Subject-verb-object">subject-verb-object</a> (SVO) is also common.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHofling201120_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHofling201120-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Noun_classifiers">Noun classifiers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mopan_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Noun classifiers"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Mopan has two noun <a href="/wiki/Classifier_(linguistics)" title="Classifier (linguistics)">classifiers</a> that are used to indicate gender. However, use of these classifiers is not typical of <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_gender" title="Grammatical gender">grammatical gender</a>. The two classifiers are <span title="Mopán Maya-language text"><i lang="mop">ix</i></span> (feminine) and <span title="Mopán Maya-language text"><i lang="mop">aj</i></span> (masculine), for example, <span title="Mopán Maya-language text"><i lang="mop">aj much</i></span>, meaning "toad (masculine)". Use of these gender markers is atypical in several respects:<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAudringCorbettFedden2018_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAudringCorbettFedden2018-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>They are not used for most nouns.</li>
<li>Gender is marked only in the noun and does not require agreement elsewhere in the sentence.</li>
<li>Gender marking can sometimes be omitted.</li></ul>
<p>Although the gender markers normally match the <a href="/wiki/Natural_gender" class="mw-redirect" title="Natural gender">natural gender</a> of the referent when denoting people, this is not always the case for non-human referents. For example, "parrot" (<span title="Mopán Maya-language text"><i lang="mop">ix tʼutʼ</i></span>) is typically feminine regardless of the sex of the animal.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAudringCorbettFedden2018_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAudringCorbettFedden2018-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Numerals_and_numeral_classifiers">Numerals and numeral classifiers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mopan_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Numerals and numeral classifiers"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Numerals in Mopan always include a numeral <a href="/wiki/Classifier_(linguistics)" title="Classifier (linguistics)">classifier</a> which is added as a suffix. These classifiers indicate qualities about the referent. For example, round objects are indicated by the suffix <span title="Mopán Maya-language text"><i lang="mop">-kuul</i></span>, while long, thin objects are indicated by the suffix <span title="Mopán Maya-language text"><i lang="mop">-tzʼiit</i></span>. The most commonly used numeral classifiers are <span title="Mopán Maya-language text"><i lang="mop">-pʼeel</i></span>, for inanimate objects, and <span title="Mopán Maya-language text"><i lang="mop">-tuul</i></span>, for people and animals. In all, there are over 70 numeral classifiers used in Mopan Maya.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHofling201125_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHofling201125-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Orthography">Orthography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mopan_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Orthography"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Since the colonial period, Mopan Maya has been written with the <a href="/wiki/Latin_script" title="Latin script">Latin script</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Ethnologue_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ethnologue-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Historically, a wide range of orthographies have been used to represent the language,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDienhart1989171_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDienhart1989171-12">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> although recently, the orthography has been standardized by the <a href="/wiki/Guatemalan_Academy_of_Mayan_Languages" class="mw-redirect" title="Guatemalan Academy of Mayan Languages">Guatemalan Academy of Mayan Languages</a> (ALMG).<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHofling20113–5_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHofling20113–5-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The following table shows some of the orthographies that have been used to represent Mopan Maya:
