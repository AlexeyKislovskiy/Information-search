<p><b><span title="Norwegian-language text"><i lang="no">Riksmål</i></span></b> (<span class="rt-commentedText nowrap"><small>English: </small><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt" lang="en-fonipa"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="&#39;r&#39; in &#39;rye&#39;">r</span><span title="/iː/: &#39;ee&#39; in &#39;fleece&#39;">iː</span><span title="&#39;k&#39; in &#39;kind&#39;">k</span><span title="&#39;s&#39; in &#39;sigh&#39;">s</span><span title="&#39;m&#39; in &#39;my&#39;">m</span><span title="/ɔː/: &#39;au&#39; in &#39;fraud&#39;">ɔː</span><span title="&#39;l&#39; in &#39;lie&#39;">l</span></span>/</a></span></span>, also <span class="rt-commentedText nowrap"><small><a href="/wiki/American_English" title="American English">US</a>: </small><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt" lang="en-fonipa"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="&#39;r&#39; in &#39;rye&#39;">r</span><span title="/ɪ/: &#39;i&#39; in &#39;kit&#39;">ɪ</span><span title="&#39;k&#39; in &#39;kind&#39;">k</span></span>-/</a></span></span>,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> <link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1177148991"><span class="IPA-label IPA-label-small">Urban East Norwegian:</span> <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="no-NO-03-Latn-fonipa"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Norwegian" title="Help:IPA/Norwegian">&#91;ˈrɪ̀ksmoːɫ&#93;</a></span>) is an unofficial written <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_language" title="Norwegian language">Norwegian language</a> form or spelling standard, meaning the <i>National Language</i>, closely related and now almost identical to the dominant form of <a href="/wiki/Bokm%C3%A5l" title="Bokmål">Bokmål</a>, known as <span title="Norwegian-language text"><i lang="no">Moderat Bokmål</i></span>.
</p><p>Both Bokmål and Riksmål evolved from the Danish written language as used in Norway during <a href="/wiki/Denmark%E2%80%93Norway" title="Denmark–Norway">the countries' union</a> and beyond, and from the pronunciation of Danish that became the native language of Norwegian elites by the 18th century. By the late 19th century, the main written language became known as <span title="Danish-language text"><i lang="da">Rigsmål</i></span> in both Denmark and Norway; the written language in Norway remained identical to Danish until 1907, although it was generally known as "Norwegian" in Norway. From 1907, successive spelling reforms gradually introduced some orthographic differences between written Norwegian and Danish. The name <span title="Norwegian-language text"><i lang="no">Riksmål</i></span> was adopted as the official name of the language, to differentiate it from <span title="Norwegian-language text"><i lang="no"><a href="/wiki/Nynorsk" title="Nynorsk">Landsmål</a></i></span> (now Nynorsk); in 1929, the name of the official language was changed to Bokmål.
</p><p>From 1938, spelling reforms introduced by the Labour government met increasing resistance as they were seen as "radical", and language organisations independent of the state started publishing their own spelling standard known as Riksmål. The struggle between <span title="Bokmål-language text"><i lang="nb">Bokmål</i></span> and <span title="Norwegian-language text"><i lang="no">Riksmål</i></span> eventually led to the <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_language_struggle" class="mw-redirect" title="Norwegian language struggle">Norwegian language struggle</a> that was at its most intense in the 1950s and 1960s. As a result the "Language Peace Committee" was appointed by the government, and subsequent reforms have moved <span title="Bokmål-language text"><i lang="nb">Bokmål</i></span> and <span title="Norwegian-language text"><i lang="no">Riksmål</i></span> closer together, to the extent that few differences remain. <span title="Norwegian-language text"><i lang="no">Riksmål</i></span> and Bokmål were clearly separate spelling standards until a major reform of Bokmål in 2005 that (re)introduced numerous <span title="Norwegian-language text"><i lang="no">Riksmål</i></span> forms as part of Bokmål; some subsequent reforms in the 21st century have eradicated most remaining, mostly small differences. National librarian <a href="/wiki/Aslak_Sira_Myhre" title="Aslak Sira Myhre">Aslak Sira Myhre</a> argued in 2017 that <span title="Norwegian-language text"><i lang="no">Riksmål</i></span> in practice has "taken over" Bokmål and peacefully "won" the language struggle.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Riksm%C3%A5l&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After the dissolution of the <a href="/wiki/Denmark%E2%80%93Norway" title="Denmark–Norway">union with Denmark</a> in 1814, <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a> had no national language standard of its own, the written language being <a href="/wiki/Danish_language" title="Danish language">Danish</a>, while the spoken language consisted of numerous <a href="/wiki/Dialect" title="Dialect">dialects</a> – that to some extent were not <a href="/wiki/Mutual_intelligibility" title="Mutual intelligibility">mutually intelligible</a>. The new <a href="/wiki/Union_between_Sweden_and_Norway" title="Union between Sweden and Norway">union partner Sweden</a> had a different language, <a href="/wiki/Swedish_language" title="Swedish language">Swedish</a>, and there was a fear that if no measures were taken, its language would be imposed upon the Norwegians.
</p><p>Hence, prominent Norwegians, such as <a href="/wiki/Henrik_Wergeland" title="Henrik Wergeland">Henrik Wergeland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bj%C3%B8rnstjerne_Bj%C3%B8rnson" title="Bjørnstjerne Bjørnson">Bjørnstjerne Bjørnson</a>, advocated a standardized Norwegian language, to be based on the legacy of the <a href="/wiki/Danish_language" title="Danish language">Danish language</a> as used in Norway by the upper class of <a href="/wiki/Oslo" title="Oslo">Christiania</a> (now Oslo) and other Eastern Norwegian cities. This was proposed by <a href="/wiki/Knud_Knudsen_(linguist)" title="Knud Knudsen (linguist)">Knud Knudsen</a>, a schoolteacher, who had witnessed how schoolchildren struggled with the Danish language they were taught, since it was very different from the spoken language they were used to. However, as late as in 1883 the Danish intellectual <a href="/wiki/Georg_Brandes" title="Georg Brandes">Georg Brandes</a> stated that the language in Norway was Danish, and that the Norwegians did not have a language of their own.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Introduction_by_Knud_Knudsen">Introduction by Knud Knudsen</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Riksm%C3%A5l&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Introduction by Knud Knudsen"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Knud Knudsen presented his Norwegian language in several works from the 1850s until his death in 1895, while the term <i>Riksmaal</i> (<i>aa</i> was a contemporary way of writing <i><a href="/wiki/%C3%85" title="Å">å</a></i>) was first proposed by Bjørnstjerne Bjørnson in 1899 as a name for the Norwegian variety of written Danish as well as spoken Dano-Norwegian. It was borrowed from Denmark where it denoted standard written and spoken Danish. The same year the Riksmål movement became organised under his leadership in order to fight against the growing influence of Nynorsk, eventually leading to the foundation of the <a href="/wiki/Non-governmental_organisation" class="mw-redirect" title="Non-governmental organisation">non-governmental organisation</a> <a href="/wiki/Riksm%C3%A5l_Society" title="Riksmål Society">Riksmålsforbundet</a> in 1907. Bjørnson became its first leader, until his death in 1910.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Spread">Spread</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Riksm%C3%A5l&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Spread"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Riksmål became the chosen language for Norwegian pupils from the latter part of the 19th century, and Norwegian newspapers adapted to the language. However, many Norwegian authors, such as <a href="/wiki/Henrik_Ibsen" title="Henrik Ibsen">Henrik Ibsen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bj%C3%B8rnstjerne_Bj%C3%B8rnson" title="Bjørnstjerne Bjørnson">Bjørnstjerne Bjørnson</a> and <a href="/wiki/Knut_Hamsun" title="Knut Hamsun">Knut Hamsun</a>, did not adhere and continued using Dano-Norwegian. Riksmål got an official writing norm in 1907, and in 1917 a new reform introduced some elements from <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_dialects" title="Norwegian dialects">Norwegian dialects</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nynorsk" title="Nynorsk">Nynorsk</a> as optional alternatives to traditional Dano-Norwegian forms. This was part of an official policy to bring the two Norwegian languages more closely together, intending eventually to merge them into one. These changes met resistance from the Riksmål movement, and <i>Riksmålsvernet</i> (The Society for the Protection of Riksmål) was founded in 1919. They have later been the purist protectors of the traditional riksmål, in opposition to <a href="/wiki/Bokm%C3%A5l" title="Bokmål">Bokmål</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nynorsk" title="Nynorsk">Nynorsk</a>, and especially <a href="/wiki/Samnorsk" title="Samnorsk">Samnorsk</a>.
</p><p>In the 1938 reform of Bokmål introduced more elements from dialects and Nynorsk, and more importantly, many traditional Dano-Norwegian forms were excluded. This so-called <i>radical</i> Bokmål or <i><a href="/wiki/Samnorsk" title="Samnorsk">Samnorsk</a></i> (Common Norwegian) met even stiffer resistance from the Riksmål movement, culminating in the 1950s under the leadership of <a href="/wiki/Arnulf_%C3%98verland" title="Arnulf Øverland">Arnulf Øverland</a>. Riksmålsforbundet organised a parents' campaign against Samnorsk in 1951, and the Norwegian Academy for Language and Literature was founded in 1953. Because of this resistance, the 1959 reform was relatively modest, and the radical reforms were partially reverted in 1981 and 2005.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_status">Current status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Riksm%C3%A5l&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Current status"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Currently, Riksmål denotes the moderate, chiefly<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> pre-1938, unofficial variant of Bokmål, which is still in use and is regulated by the Norwegian Academy and promoted by Riksmålsforbundet. Riksmål has gone through some spelling reforms, but none as profound as the ones that shaped Bokmål. A Riksmål dictionary was published in four volumes in the period 1937 to 1957 by Riksmålsvernet, and two supplementary volumes were published in 1995 by the Norwegian Academy. After the latest Bokmål reforms in 2005, the difference between Bokmål and Riksmål have diminished and they are now comparable to <a href="/wiki/American_and_British_English_differences" class="mw-redirect" title="American and British English differences">American and British English differences</a>, but the Norwegian Academy still upholds its own standard.
</p><p>The daily newspaper <span title="Norwegian-language text"><i lang="no"><a href="/wiki/Aftenposten" title="Aftenposten">Aftenposten</a></i></span> is notable for its use of Riksmål as its standard language. Use of Riksmål is rigorously pursued, even with regard to readers' letters, which are "translated" into the standard.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
