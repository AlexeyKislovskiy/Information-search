<p><b>Tofa</b>, also known as <b>Tofalar</b> or <b>Karagas</b>, is a <a href="/wiki/Endangered_language" title="Endangered language">moribund</a> <a href="/wiki/Turkic_languages" title="Turkic languages">Turkic language</a> spoken in Russia's <a href="/wiki/Irkutsk_Oblast" title="Irkutsk Oblast">Irkutsk Oblast</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Tofalar" title="Tofalar">Tofalars</a>. Recent estimates for speakers run from 93 people<sup id="cite_ref-RC2010_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RC2010-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> to fewer than 40.<sup id="cite_ref-anderson2004_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-anderson2004-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">Classification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tofa_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Classification"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Tofa is most closely related to the <a href="/wiki/Tuvan_language" title="Tuvan language">Tuvan language</a><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The book doesn&#39;t appear to say this, and other sources categorize it as Taiga Sayan Turkic, while most Tuvan dialects are Steppe Sayan Turkic. (September 2023)">failed verification</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> and forms a dialect continuum with it. <b>Tuha</b> and <b>Tsengel Tuvan</b> may be dialects of either Tuvan or Tofa. Tofa shares a number of features with these languages, including the preservation of *d as /d/ (as in <i>hodan</i> "hare" - compare Uzbek <i>quyon</i>) and the development of low <a href="/wiki/Tone_(linguistics)" title="Tone (linguistics)">tones</a> on historically short vowels (as in *<i>et</i> &gt; <i>èt</i> "meat, flesh").
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Alexander_Vovin" title="Alexander Vovin">Alexander Vovin</a> (2017) notes that Tofa and other Siberian Turkic languages, especially Sayan Turkic, have <a href="/wiki/Yeniseian_languages" title="Yeniseian languages">Yeniseian</a> loanwords.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geographic_and_demographic_distribution">Geographic and demographic distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tofa_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geographic and demographic distribution"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<figure class="mw-default-size mw-halign-left" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Tofalaria.png" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/Tofalaria.png/220px-Tofalaria.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="166" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/Tofalaria.png/330px-Tofalaria.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/Tofalaria.png/440px-Tofalaria.png 2x" data-file-width="805" data-file-height="606" /></a><figcaption>Historical Range of Tofalaria</figcaption></figure>
<p>The Tofa, who are also known as the Tofalar or Karagas, are an <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples" title="Indigenous peoples">indigenous people</a> living in southwestern <a href="/wiki/Irkutsk_Oblast" title="Irkutsk Oblast">Irkutsk Oblast</a>, in Russia. The region they inhabit is informally known as <a href="/wiki/Tofalariya" title="Tofalariya">Tofalariya</a>. They are traditionally a nomadic reindeer-herding people, living on or near the Eastern Sayan mountain range. However, reindeer herding has greatly declined since the 20th century, with only one Tofa family continuing the practice as of 2004<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Tofa_language&amp;action=edit">&#91;update&#93;</a></sup>.<sup id="cite_ref-donahoe2004_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-donahoe2004-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Recognized by the former USSR in 1926 as one of the "<a href="/wiki/Indigenous_small-numbered_peoples_of_the_North,_Siberia_and_the_Far_East" class="mw-redirect" title="Indigenous small-numbered peoples of the North, Siberia and the Far East">Small Numbered Minorities of the North</a>," (Russian: <i>коренные малочисленные народы Севера, Сибири и Дальнего Востока)</i> the Tofa have special legal status and receive economic support from Russia. The Tofa population is around 750 people; around 5% of the population spoke Tofa as a first language in 2002, (although that number has likely declined since then, due to the age of the speakers).<sup id="cite_ref-donahoe2004_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-donahoe2004-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-harrison2003_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-harrison2003-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Although the population of Tofalaria appears to be growing, the number of ethnic Tofalar seems to be in decline.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects_of_language_contact">Effects of language contact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tofa_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Effects of language contact"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Language contact—mainly with Russian speakers—has been extensive since 1926, when the Tofa officially received their "Small Numbered Minorities of the North" status from the USSR (Russian: <i>коренные малочисленные народы Севера, Сибири и Дальнего Востока)</i> and underwent significant cultural, social, and economic changes. Most notably, this traditionally nomadic, reindeer-herding people have since become sedentary and reindeer herding has all but vanished among the Tofa.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> In addition to visiting tax collectors and tourists, many other Russians have come to the Sayan mountain range to live. Russian migration and intermarriage also has had an effect, according to a citation by Donahoe: "In 1931, of a total population in Tofalaria of 551, approximately 420 (76%) were Tofa, and the remaining 131 (24%) were non-Tofa, predominantly Russian (Mel'nikova 1994:36 and 231). By 1970, the population in Tofalaria had increased to 1368, of whom 498 (36%) were Tofa, and 809 (59%) were Russian (Sherkhunaev 1975:23)."<sup id="cite_ref-donahoe2004_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-donahoe2004-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>(p.&#160;159) There were approximately 40 speakers of various fluency levels by 2002, and this number has likely continued to decrease in the intervening time.<sup id="cite_ref-harrison2003_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-harrison2003-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Rassadin also indicates that Tofa has a short [<span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">ĭ</span>].<sup id="cite_ref-rassadin1971_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rassadin1971-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> All vowels except [<span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">æ</span>] can be <a href="/wiki/Pharyngealization" title="Pharyngealization">pharyngealized</a> [<span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">◌ˤ</span>]. According to Rassadin<sup id="cite_ref-rassadin1971_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rassadin1971-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> pharyngealization is realized as <a href="/wiki/Creaky_voice" title="Creaky voice">creaky voice</a> [<span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">◌̰</span>]; Harrison and Anderson represent this feature as low tone.
</p><p><br />

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vowel_harmony">Vowel harmony</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tofa_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Vowel harmony"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Many dialects of Tofa exhibit vowel harmony, although this harmony seems to be linked to fluency: as one decreases, so does the other.<sup id="cite_ref-harrison1999_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-harrison1999-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Tofa vowel harmony is progressive and based on two features: backness and rounding, and this occurs both root-internal and in affixes.<sup id="cite_ref-harrison1999_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-harrison1999-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Enclitics do not appear to trigger backness harmony, and rounding harmony in Tofa has been undergoing changes, and may apply inconsistently. In some cases this may be due to opaque rules resulting in an apparent "disharmony", especially among speakers of the younger generation.<sup id="cite_ref-anderson2004_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-anderson2004-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The complications surrounding Tofa vowel harmony may also be due to fluctuations from language endangerment.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> In general, Russian loanwords do not appear to conform to vowel harmony.<sup id="cite_ref-harrison1999_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-harrison1999-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Given the increasing quantity of these loanwords, leveling may also be a factor in the inconsistent application of vowel harmony.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Morphology_and_syntax">Morphology and syntax</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tofa_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Morphology and syntax"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Tofa is an <a href="/wiki/Agglutinative_language" title="Agglutinative language">agglutinative</a> language with a few auxiliary verbs.<sup id="cite_ref-anderson2004_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-anderson2004-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The bare stem of a verb is only used in the singular imperative; other categories are marked by suffixation, including the singular imperative negative.<sup id="cite_ref-anderson2004_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-anderson2004-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The Tofa suffix /<i>--sig</i>/ is an especially unusual derivational suffix in that it attaches to any noun to add the meaning 'smelling of + [NOUN]' or 'smelling like + [NOUN]'.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Grammatical number in Tofa includes singular, plural, dual inclusive ('you and me'), and plural inclusive, tense includes the present and past, and aspect includes the perfective and imperfective. Historically suffixes conformed to Tofa vowel harmony rules, but that appears to be changing. <p>Tofa has letters that are not present in the Russian alphabet: <a href="/wiki/%D2%92" class="mw-redirect" title="Ғ">Ғ</a>ғ <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ɣ]</span>, <a href="/wiki/%D3%98" class="mw-redirect" title="Ә">Ә</a>ә <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[æ]</span>, <a href="/wiki/Dotted_I_(Cyrillic)" title="Dotted I (Cyrillic)">Ii</a> <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ĭ]</span>, <a href="/wiki/%D2%9A" class="mw-redirect" title="Қ">Қ</a>қ <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[q]</span>, <a href="/wiki/%D2%A2" class="mw-redirect" title="Ң">Ң</a>ң <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ŋ]</span>, <a href="/wiki/%D3%A8" class="mw-redirect" title="Ө">Ө</a>ө <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[œ]</span>, <a href="/wiki/%D2%AE" class="mw-redirect" title="Ү">Ү</a>ү <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[y]</span>, <a href="/wiki/%D2%BA" class="mw-redirect" title="Һ">Һ</a>һ <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[h]</span>, and <a href="/wiki/%D2%B6" class="mw-redirect" title="Ҷ">Ҷ</a>ҷ <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="und-Latn-fonipa" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[d͡ʒ]</span>. Additionally, the letter ъ is sometimes used after a vowel to mark pharyngealization [ˤ], as in эът [ʔɛˤt̪] "meat".
