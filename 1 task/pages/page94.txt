<p>The <b>East Papuan languages</b> is a defunct proposal for a <a href="/wiki/Language_family" title="Language family">family</a> of <a href="/wiki/Papuan_languages" title="Papuan languages">Papuan languages</a> spoken on the islands to the east of <a href="/wiki/New_Guinea" title="New Guinea">New Guinea</a>, including <a href="/wiki/New_Britain" title="New Britain">New Britain</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Ireland_(island)" title="New Ireland (island)">New Ireland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bougainville_Island" title="Bougainville Island">Bougainville</a>, <a href="/wiki/Solomon_Islands" title="Solomon Islands">Solomon Islands</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Santa_Cruz_Islands" title="Santa Cruz Islands">Santa Cruz Islands</a>. There is no evidence that these languages are related to each other, and the Santa Cruz languages are no longer recognized as Papuan.
</p><p>All but two of the starred languages below (<a href="/wiki/Yele_language" title="Yele language">Yélî Dnye</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sulka_language" title="Sulka language">Sulka</a>) make a gender distinction in their pronouns. Several of the heavily Papuanized Austronesian languages of New Britain do as well. This suggests a pre-Austronesian <a href="/wiki/Sprachbund" title="Sprachbund">language area</a> in the region.
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_the_proposal">History of the proposal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=East_Papuan_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History of the proposal"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The East Papuan languages were proposed as a family by linguist <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Wurm" title="Stephen Wurm">Stephen Wurm</a> (1975) and others. However, their work was preliminary, and there is little evidence that the East Papuan languages actually have a <a href="/wiki/Genetic_relationship_(linguistics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Genetic relationship (linguistics)">genetic relationship</a>. For example, none of these fifteen languages marked with asterisks below share more than 2–3% of their basic vocabulary with any of the others. Dunn and colleagues (2005) tested the reliability of the proposed 2–3% <a href="/wiki/Cognate" title="Cognate">cognates</a> by randomizing the vocabulary lists and comparing them again. The nonsense comparisons produced the same 2–3% of "shared" vocabulary, demonstrating that the proposed cognates of the East Papuan languages, and even of proposed families within the East Papuan languages, are as likely to be due to chance as to any genealogical relationship. Thus in a conservative classification, many of the East Papuan languages would be considered <a href="/wiki/Language_isolate" title="Language isolate">language isolates</a>.
</p><p>Since the islands in question have been settled for at least 35&#160;000 years, their considerable linguistic diversity is unsurprising. However, <a href="/wiki/Malcolm_Ross_(linguist)" title="Malcolm Ross (linguist)">Malcolm Ross</a> (2001; 2005) has presented evidence from comparing <a href="/wiki/Pronoun" title="Pronoun">pronouns</a> from nineteen of these languages that several of the lower-level branches of East Papuan may indeed be valid families. This is the classification adopted here. For Wurm's more inclusive classification, see the <a href="/wiki/Glottolog" title="Glottolog">Glottolog</a> page <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://glottolog.org/resource/reference/id/135107">here</a>.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Austronesian_languages_formerly_classified_as_East_Papuan">Austronesian languages formerly classified as East Papuan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=East_Papuan_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Austronesian languages formerly classified as East Papuan"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Wurm classified the three languages of the <a href="/wiki/Santa_Cruz_Islands" title="Santa Cruz Islands">Santa Cruz</a> and <a href="/wiki/Reef_Islands" title="Reef Islands">Reef Islands</a> as an additional family within East Papuan. However, new data on these languages, along with advances in  the reconstruction of <a href="/wiki/Proto-Oceanic_language" title="Proto-Oceanic language">Proto-Oceanic</a>, has made it clear that they are in fact <a href="/wiki/Austronesian_languages" title="Austronesian languages">Austronesian</a>: 
</p>
<ul><li><b><a href="/wiki/Reefs_%E2%80%93_Santa_Cruz_languages" title="Reefs – Santa Cruz languages">Reefs – Santa Cruz languages</a></b>: <a href="/wiki/Santa_Cruz_language" title="Santa Cruz language">Santa Cruz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nanggu_language" title="Nanggu language">Nanggu</a>, <a href="/wiki/%C3%84iwoo_language" title="Äiwoo language">Äiwoo</a></li></ul>
<p>Similarly, Wurm had classified the extinct <a href="/wiki/Kazukuru_language" title="Kazukuru language">Kazukuru language</a> and its possible sister languages of <a href="/wiki/New_Georgia" title="New Georgia">New Georgia</a> as a sixth branch of East Papuan. However, in a joint 2007 paper, Dunn and Ross argued that this was also Austronesian.
</p>
